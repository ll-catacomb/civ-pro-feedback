## Slide 4: When is Preclusion Implicated?

Look for: lawsuit #1 and then lawsuit #2.

**Steps when you see lawsuit #2:**
- Are we in the same court system? If we have two different jurisdictions → intersystem.
- Can we use claim preclusion?
- Can we use issue preclusion?

**Examples of questions/prompts:**
- How could a party use the result in lawsuit #1 in lawsuit #2?
- What defenses could a party make in lawsuit #2?

**Speaker notes:** General background to preclusion: Preclusion — trying to incentivize parties to litigate things in lawsuit #1 so that we don't need a lawsuit #2. Prevents a litigant who had an obligation to assert a theory of relief in lawsuit #1 that has gone to judgment in the trial court from asserting that theory of relief in a subsequent lawsuit #2. At the same time, we want to be cognizant of due process, and make sure you are not precluded if you weren't a party to the initial lawsuit. The preclusion tests help us balance these interests. Preclusion will likely come up on the exam. The moment you see lawsuit #2, immediately think preclusion. Make sure to do your claim preclusion and your issue preclusion analysis separately. First, ask if there's claim preclusion, go through the elements, do the claim preclusion analysis. THEN ask if there's issue preclusion, go through elements, do the issue preclusion analysis.

## Slide 5: Claim Preclusion — Overview

- **Step 0:** (A) Did the court in L1 have jurisdiction over the claim made in L2? (B) Did the court limit the preclusive effect of its decision ("dismissing without prejudice")?
- **Step 1:** Same claim
- **Step 2:** Same parties (or parties in privity)
- **Step 3:** Judgment that is **final** and on the **merits**

**Speaker notes:** Same as Slide 4 notes. (General background on preclusion and exam approach — see Slide 4.)

## Slide 6: Claim Preclusion — Step 0 (Stealth Factors)

**Ability to adjudicate claim in lawsuit #1.**
- Court #1 must have had the **ability** to adjudicate the theory of relief that someone is trying to preclude in lawsuit #2.
- Could you have brought this claim in lawsuit #1? Check for SMJ, PJ, and venue to make sure the court in lawsuit #1 could have heard the claim.
- Ex = *Marrese v. Orthopedic Surgeons*: state court in L1 couldn't adjudicate federal antitrust claims.
- Watch out for this with agencies.

**Rendering court limited claim preclusive effect of its decision.**
- "Dismissal without prejudice."

**Speaker notes:** Can think of these as step "zero" — step 0(a) and step 0(b), can check before getting into entire claim preclusion analysis. Ability to adjudicate — if state court could not have exercised SMJ over a Sherman Act claim (because it's exclusive federal jurisdiction), then party will not be precluded from bringing a Sherman Act claim in lawsuit #2. If you could NOT have brought claim in lawsuit #1, due to lack of SMJ, PJ, etc. then a party is NOT precluded from bringing claim in lawsuit #2. Limiting preclusive effect — court in lawsuit #1 reduces the claim preclusive effects of its judgment by dismissing without prejudice. If Court #1 dismissed without prejudice, then you are allowed to refile. NOT precluded. So in summary, if either (1) court #1 could not have heard the claim, or (2) court #1 dismissed without prejudice, then you will NOT be claim precluded. If neither of these factors are present, proceed to claim preclusion test.

## Slide 7: Claim Preclusion — Same Claim

**Same Claim**

- **Test** = same transaction or occurrence (*Heyward Robinson*)
  - Whether the facts are related in time, space, origin, or motivation.
  - Whether they form a convenient trial unit.
  - Whether the parties would have expected the two issues to have been tried together.

**Two exceptions:**

1. **Markets that involve divisible interests ("bond exception").**
   - When we want a market for two things that would require them to be separate and separable.
   - Ex: bond market (*Cromwell v. County of Sac*) or real estate (Prof. Greiner's interpretation of *Gonzales*).

2. **Installment contracts.**
   - Acceleration clause (*Jones v. Morris Plan Bank*) vs. no acceleration clause.

**Speaker notes:** Exception re divisible interests: Two things are purchased in the same T or O, but we want a market for the two things separately (if bonds were not separable, we couldn't have a bond market — would all be claim precluded). Each bond is its own separate claim. Bond market → *Cromwell v. County of Sac*: no claim preclusion even though in privity with other bondholder and there is final judgment, because each bond is its own individual promise of payment. Real estate → Prof. Greiner thinks *Gonzales* got this wrong: was actually not part of the same claim because we want real estate plots to be sold individually. Exception re installment contracts: If the person who needs to make payments does not pay, when you are seeking to collect, must sue for all outstanding payments due at that time (not future payments). Acceleration clause → all payments become immediately due; must sue for everything. No acceleration clause → sue for missed and past due payments only (not future payments).

## Slide 8: Claim Preclusion — Same Party

**Same parties (or parties in privity)**
- Ordinarily means same legal entity.
- *Gonzales* plaintiffs were not in privity (even though many similarities).

**Two types of privity:**

1. **Substantial control** — A nonparty substantially controlled conduct/litigation of parties in lawsuit #1. Ex: single shareholder of a company.
2. **Successor in legal interest** — Ex: if you sell the right to collect on debt.

**Speaker notes:** Example for substantial control: Sole owner of a corporation ("close" corporation). Lawsuit by corporation for damages, and then lawsuit by owner for loss in value of shares. Owner is in privity with the corporation because the owner exercised substantial control (or could have exercised, had a right to exercise). Claim preclusion applies. Successor in interest: Ex = credit card debt; debt purchaser #2 brings lawsuit to collect, then debt purchaser #3 cannot also bring the same lawsuit. Easements can also be successor in interest.

## Slide 9: Claim Preclusion — Final & On the Merits

**Judgment that is (1) final and (2) on the merits**

| | Final? |
|---|---|
| Judgment entered by trial court | Yes |
| Full summary judgment followed by final judgment | Yes |

| | On the merits? |
|---|---|
| SMJ, PJ, venue, etc. | Not on the merits |
| Jury verdicts, dismissal for failure to state a claim, SJ | On the merits |
| Failure to respond to discovery, failure to prosecute, default judgment for failure to appear | On the merits |
| SOL; partial summary judgment | Unsettled — jurisdictions differ |

**Speaker notes:** Final: don't care if case is still on appeal; final only with respect to trial court judgment. On the merits: plaintiff's failure to move lawsuit forward — that is on the merits. Why? We don't want people to be able to refile because we have already given them an opportunity to litigate.

## Slide 11: Issue Preclusion — Same Issue

**Same Issue**
- Issues are fairly narrow and they are factual.
- Can be a mixed question of fact and law (e.g., was conduct negligent).
- Most often arise within an element of a cause of action or defense.

**Speaker notes:** We do not use issue preclusion to preclude relitigation of legal issues (we have stare decisis for that). Ex = in *Cromwell*, holder-in-due-course defense is not the same issue as the issues litigated in lawsuit #1. Does not mean transaction or occurrence — issue is much narrower. You are not obligated to raise all potential issues that you possibly could have in lawsuit #1. Policy rationales: We don't want plaintiff/defendant raising any and all possible issues — not efficient. The parties might not know about a certain issue at the time. Negligence is a good example of an issue, IE was the driver negligent in the car accident — if that's been decided in a former lawsuit, can't relitigate that in a subsequent lawsuit.

---

## Slide 12: Issue Preclusion — Actually Litigated

**Actually Litigated**
- Was the issue something that the parties raised in court and fought about?
- Ex = *Cromwell*.

**Speaker notes:** Issue was raised, submitted for determination, and determined. In *Cromwell*, didn't raise holder-in-due-course defense in lawsuit #1, so cannot issue preclude that in lawsuit #2. There could be the same issue across different causes of action — IE did you own the bond could be the basis of several causes of action.

---

## Slide 13: Issue Preclusion — Necessary to the Decision

**Necessary to the decision**
- The issue must matter for the judgment that the court issues.
- Ex = *Rios v. Davis*, not necessary to the decision.

**General jury verdicts:**

| Court | Rule |
|---|---|
| Federal rule | Cannot issue preclude. (*Russell v. Place*) |
| Some state courts | Allow for issue preclusion. |

**Speaker notes:** *Rios* explanation: Because the jury found that Davis was negligent, he could not recover any money (because of contributory negligence). As a result, the finding that Rios was also negligent was not necessary to the decision — it would not have changed the outcome. General jury verdict: Does not distinguish/specify which grounds were the basis of the verdict. Federal rule: Cannot issue preclude based on general jury verdicts with multiple independent grounds because we don't know which issues were necessary to the decision (*Russell v. Place*). Some state courts: Assumption that the verdict means the court ruled in favor of the winning party on all issues litigated — losing party lost on all issues.

## Slide 14: Issue Preclusion — Mutuality

| Mutuality Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Strict mutuality | Same parties + same procedural posture | *Cromwell* |
| Flexible mutuality | Same parties + different procedural position | *Rios v. Davis* |
| Federal rule | Distinguishes offensive vs. defensive nonmutual issue preclusion; generally frowns upon **offensive**, generally okay with **defensive** | *Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore* |
| No mutuality (New York) | Not an element of issue preclusion | — |

**Speaker notes:** Note — JG will tell you what mutuality requirement a state requires; keep track of this on the exam! Strict mutuality: non-mutual offensive/defensive doesn't apply. Flexible mutuality: non-mutual offensive/defensive doesn't apply. Federal rule: discussed on next slide. No mutuality (NY): not an element — non-mutual offensive/defensive both allowed.

## Slide 15: Issue Preclusion — Offensive vs. Defensive Nonmutual Preclusion

**Offensive**
- Same defendant in both lawsuits.
- When the defendant loses on an issue in lawsuit #1, and plaintiff is trying to use issue preclusion in lawsuit #2.
- **Exception:** when plaintiff in lawsuit #2 could not have joined lawsuit #1.

**Defensive**
- Same plaintiff in both lawsuits.
- When the plaintiff loses on an issue in lawsuit #1, defendant in lawsuit #2 can use issue preclusion.

**Policy rationales:** Incentive structure.

**Speaker notes:** Federal rule: Offensive = same defendant (or in privity) in both lawsuits; defendant lost on an issue in lawsuit #1; plaintiff #2 wants to use issue preclusion in lawsuit #2. Exception: when plaintiff #2 could not have joined lawsuit #1 (federal courts generally do not allow otherwise). Defensive = same plaintiff (or in privity) in both lawsuits; plaintiff lost on an issue in lawsuit #1; defendant #2 can use issue preclusion; federal courts generally allow. Policy rationales: Defensive — incentivizes a plaintiff to sue all applicable defendants in one lawsuit, because if they lose, they won't be able to relitigate the issue against a different defendant (efficient). Offensive — incentivizes the second plaintiff to "sit out" of lawsuit #1 (take a wait-and-see approach), which is not efficient. BUT if plaintiff #2 could not have joined lawsuit #1, courts will allow offensive nonmutual estoppel. Ex = *Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore* (plaintiff could not have joined SEC litigation). Other possible reasons = if plaintiff would have destroyed complete diversity.

## Slide 16: Issue Preclusion — Stealth Rules

**Stealth Rules**
1. Only applies to the losing party (or parties in privity with losing party).
2. Judgment of sufficient quality/finality — judgment entered by a trial court. Partial SJ, followed by settlement and dismissal: make policy arguments for and against.
3. Losing party must have had sufficient opportunity/incentive to litigate.

**Speaker notes:** You only get issue precluded if you LOST on the issue in lawsuit #1. What if there is partial SJ on one issue, then the parties settle and dismiss? For: SJ would rarely be revisited or revised, we want to avoid continuing litigation. Against: SJ are subject to revision at any time, doesn't seem very final. Full/fair opportunity: For example, if discovery rules limited you from bringing in an important document, a party wasn't able to call a key witness — a party must be given one full and fair opportunity to litigate an issue.

## Slide 17: Principles of Intersystem Preclusion

*[On exam, do this first! Unless L1 and L2 are heard by the exact same court.]*

**Driving question:** What happens if the subsequent court's preclusion laws differ from the rendering court's preclusion laws?

**DO NOT do Erie/HCOL.**

**Examples:**
- SOL issue is versus isn't on the merits for claim preclusion.
- Mutuality rules for issue preclusion.

**Foundational premises:**
- If the rendering court **would** preclude, the subsequent court **must** preclude.
- If the rendering court **would not** preclude, **open question** as to whether the subsequent court can or cannot. Here, always cite *Hart*!

**Workflow:**
1. Find your place in the chart — what preclusion law governs?
2. Determine how that law applies to the facts at hand.
3. Cite the source of law.

## Slide 23: Class Certification and Class Types

**Class must satisfy the prerequisite elements under FRCP 23(a):**
- Numerosity
- Commonality
- Typicality
- Representativeness

**Class must qualify as one (or more) of the "types" of classes under FRCP 23(b):**
- FRCP 23(b)(1)(A); FRCP 23(b)(2) — Injunction
- FRCP 23(b)(1)(B) — Individual Lawsuit Adversity
- FRCP 23(b)(3) — Damages

**Other rules:**
- Notice and opt-out (depends on class type)
- FRCP 23(e) — Settlement: any class member can object to a proposed settlement as unfair.

## Slide 24: Class Certification — Prerequisites: Numerosity

**Numerosity**
- Class is so numerous that joinder of all members is impractical.
- Below 20 = no; above 40 = "numerosity safezone."
- Between 20–40 depends on other factors, such as:
  - Plaintiffs are geographically dispersed.
  - Individual claims have smaller AIC.

**Speaker notes:** For 20–40 range: more likely to certify if plaintiffs are geographically dispersed (harder to use simple joinder) and if AIC are relatively small individually (less likely to do individual joinder). Warning: if unnamed plaintiffs must exit class because they have no specific IPJ under *Bristol-Myers Squibb* rule, you may lose numerosity.

---

## Slide 25: Class Certification — Prerequisites: Commonality

**Commonality**
- Questions of law or fact common to the class — common litigation **answers** to all of their claims.
- *Walmart* standard: plaintiffs must demonstrate that class members have "suffered the same injury."
- Insufficient to say "all suffered violation of same legal provision (e.g., Title VII)" — must have suffered in the same way.
- HCOL can raise issues for commonality.
- Note: if FRCP 23(b)(3) "Damages" lawsuit, **predominance swallows commonality**.

**Speaker notes:** Are there common litigation answers to all claims? Were the class members all wronged for the same reason, harmed by the same scheme in the same way? *Walmart* standard: high bar — need the same answer, not just the same question. Normally, must point to some specific policy or practice engaging each individual class member; lacking a policy/allowing discretion is not itself a policy. Things to consider: Did proposed members have different injuries? Were their contracts the same? HCOL: federal courts use HCOL rules of the state in which they are sitting (*Klaxon*). If laws of different applicable states differ, that might defeat commonality (different elements, defenses, or damages). *Shutts*: if the forum state wants to apply its own law to all claims in a class action, must have sufficient contacts linking defendant and subject matter to the forum state.

---

## Slide 26: Class Certification — Prerequisites: Typicality

**Typicality**
- Claims or defenses of the representative parties are typical of the claims or defenses of the class.

**Speaker notes:** Named plaintiffs' theory of relief is sufficiently close to the theory of relief asserted by unnamed class members. Enough congruence between claims. When named plaintiff pushes hard to get a maximum recovery, that will benefit the unnamed class members — incentive alignment.

---

## Slide 27: Class Certification — Prerequisites: Representativeness

**Representativeness**
- The representative parties will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class.
- Becomes an issue if there are conflicts of interests among the class members.

**Speaker notes:** Conflict of interest — ex: if some class members want money immediately, and others want it available later. *Amchem* asbestos poisoning case: some plaintiffs were presently sick and wanted immediate relief; other plaintiffs were showing symptoms but weren't sick yet and didn't want to exhaust the money. Representativeness was lacking because interests were not aligned: injured claimants who faced current medical expenses wanted relief, whereas exposure-only claimants wanted an inflation-protected fund for the future.

---

## Slide 28: Class Types — Injunction (FRCP 23(b)(1)(A); FRCP 23(b)(2))

**In general:** qualifies if defendant's conduct is common to all class members such that an injunction would be an appropriate remedy.

- **23(b)(1)(A):** When a defendant may not be able to comply with conflicting injunctions that could come from a series of individual lawsuits.
- **23(b)(2):** Defendant acted in a way that applied "generally to the class" so it makes sense to give injunctive relief for the class as a whole.

**Notice and opt-out:** Does not require that individual class members be notified or given the opportunity to opt-out at certification. Court "**may**" direct notice per FRCP 23(c)(2)(A).

**Speaker notes:** 23(b)(1)(A): injunctive remedy in lawsuit A could be in opposition to injunctive remedy in lawsuit B, so defendant could not comply with terms of both. 23(b)(2): *Wal-Mart* — this rule does not include actions where each class member is entitled to different remedies (i.e., different injunctive or declaratory relief from the defendant).

---

## Slide 29: Class Types — Individual Lawsuit Adversity (FRCP 23(b)(1)(B))

**FRCP 23(b)(1)(B)**
- Adjudication of certain individuals' lawsuits could adversely affect other class members.
- Ex: when a limited fund is involved, like an insurance policy — the policy could run out of money before plaintiffs who file later are paid.

**Notice and opt-out:** Does not require notice or opt-out. Court "**may**" direct notice per FRCP 23(c)(2)(A).

**Speaker notes:** Same as Slide 28 notes.

---

## Slide 30: Class Types — Damages (FRCP 23(b)(3))

**FRCP 23(b)(3)**

If class action is seeking damages, it must meet:
1. **Predominance** — essentially a heightened requirement of commonality; common issues of law and fact must predominate over issues that are not common.
2. **Superiority** — class device must be deemed superior to individual adjudications.

**Notice and opt-out:** Reasonable efforts must be made to give notice; class members must be provided opportunity to opt-out. Court "**must**" provide notice per FRCP 23(c)(2)(b).

**Speaker notes:** Predominance: common issues of law and fact must predominate. Superiority: presumption that individual plaintiffs controlling their own lawsuits is better — but class action is superior when individual claims would return very small damages, when plaintiffs are geographically diverse, etc. Notice and opt-out: requires individual notice (mail or email); must provide opportunity to opt-out.

## Slide 31: Class Actions and SMJ

**AUJ:** AUJ cause of action common to all class members (*Parklane Hosiery*).

**DJ — Complete Diversity:** *Ben Hur* — look at the diversity of only the **named plaintiff** and defendant. Domicile of unnamed class members is irrelevant.

**AIC:** *Exxon* — as long as one plaintiff is completely diverse and has sufficient AIC, can use §1367 to assert supplemental jurisdiction over class members that do not meet the AIC requirement (both named and unnamed).
- Applies when suing a **single** defendant.
- "Contamination theory."

**Speaker notes:** DJ complete diversity: Court lacks SMJ if named plaintiff and defendant are not completely diverse (*Strawbridge*). Supports concept of class as single entity — we only care about citizenship of the named plaintiff. AIC: Does NOT work for multiple defendants (Rule 20 almost always applies; under 1367(b) ouster, cannot bring claims because destroys requirements of §1332). *Snyder v. Harris*: no stacking AIC in class action — distinct claims in a class action cannot be added together to satisfy AIC requirement. *Exxon* Rule: With a diversity anchoring claim (named plaintiff completely diverse and AIC sufficient), AIC-deficient plaintiffs do not "contaminate" the anchoring claim under 1367(a). Why? Purpose of AIC rule was to ensure the case was significant enough for federal court — that purpose is still met even if unnamed parties are AIC deficient.

## Slide 32: Class Actions and PJ

**General PJ** over defendant: plaintiffs could sue defendant in defendant's home state (i.e., domicile, place of incorporation, or PPOB).

**Specific PJ** over defendant: when claims of only some class members have minimum contacts, can the court exercise PJ over the defendant for claims that lack minimum contacts?

| View | Description | Supporting Cases |
|---|---|---|
| **View 1: Class as simple joinder on steroids** | Apply *Bristol-Myers Squibb*: courts cannot exercise PJ over a defendant with respect to claims that lack minimum contacts. | *Shutts* (cannot treat class as aggregate unit for HCOL purposes) |
| **View 2: Certification creates a new legal entity** | If the class is a single legal entity, it is harmed in many locations (where any plaintiffs suffered injury). Courts could exercise PJ in any of those locations. | *Ben Hur* (treats class as single entity — only care about citizenship of named plaintiff) |

**Speaker notes:** For claims with minimum contacts, walk through specific PJ rules (long arm statute, minimal contacts, fairness factors). Resident vs. nonresident class member plaintiffs: question is whether there is PJ over the defendant for claims of nonresident class members that lack minimum contacts. Ex = *Bristol-Myers Squibb*, simple joinder case. View 1: BMS applies to state courts, and Rule 4(k)(1)(A) says federal courts can only exercise PJ when state courts can — federal courts also follow this rule (piggy-back). View 2: If there is a scheme that occurs across different states, could argue defendant perpetrated the scheme in all of the states. Analogous to a trust.

## Slide 33: Class Actions and PJ — Unnamed Plaintiff Class Members

**PJ and unnamed plaintiff class members.**

*Shutts* — We are not concerned about PJ over unnamed class members. There are sufficient procedural safeguards in place to protect the interests of the unnamed class members (FRCP 23 prerequisites). State analogues to FRCP 23 prerequisites.

**Speaker notes:** Rule 23 prerequisites for class action certification; there is a lawyer in the courtroom representing their interests. If we have state law analogues to these Rule 23 prerequisites (commonality, typicality, etc.) that's fine to satisfy sufficient procedural protections.

## Slide 34: Class Actions and Venue

Walk through the statute! Consider only named plaintiffs (*Ben Hur*, *Exxon*).

- **28 U.S.C. § 1391(b)(1)** — a district where any defendant **resides** if all defendants reside in the same state. For corporations, anywhere where the corporation is subject to PJ.
- **28 U.S.C. § 1391(b)(2)** — a district where **substantial part of events/omissions** occurred. Where did the events occur that harmed the class members?
- **28 U.S.C. § 1391(b)(3)** — Fall-back.
