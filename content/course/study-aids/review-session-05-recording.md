## Part I: Appellate Jurisdiction

### Overview

Appellate jurisdiction is a threshold question: before asking whether you will *win* on appeal, you must ask whether you have the *right* to seek appellate review at all. There are three sections of the U.S. Code that confer appellate jurisdiction: **§ 1291**, **§ 1292**, and **§ 1651**. On an exam, it is useful to organize your answer around these three sections.

---

### § 1291 — Final Judgment Rule

§ 1291 confers appellate jurisdiction in essentially three circumstances, all involving some form of final disposition.

#### Category 1: The Whole Case Is Over

The most straightforward case: the trial court has entered final judgment and everyone has gone home. This is quintessential § 1291 jurisdiction — yes, you can appeal.

#### Category 2: Final Judgment as to One Claim (*Sears*)

If a case has multiple claims and the trial court dismisses one while the others remain pending, you may still have appellate jurisdiction over the dismissed claim. The test from *Sears* <!-- corrected: "Sears" → *Sears, Roebuck & Co. v. Mackey* --> asks:

1. Has the court **explicitly entered judgment** with respect to that particular claim? and
2. Has the court **explicitly determined there is no just reason for delay**?

On an exam, Professor Greiner <!-- corrected: "Griner"/"grounder" → "Greiner" throughout --> will typically tell you that the court entered final judgment on a claim and ask whether immediate appeal is available. The "no just reason for delay" factor does not do much work in most exam contexts, but you should mention it.

> **Example of a "just reason for delay":** Suppose a trial court grants judgment as a matter of law (directed verdict) on one claim mid-trial while the jury is still deliberating on two other claims. An appellate court might say: the jury will reach its verdict within days — just do it all at once. That could constitute a just reason for delay.

This category also implicates **Rule 54(b)**: § 1291 is the source of jurisdiction, but Rule 54(b) is what allows the court to enter final judgment on fewer than all claims.

#### Category 3: The *Cohen* Exception (Collateral Order Doctrine)

The *Cohen* exception <!-- corrected: *Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp.* --> allows interlocutory appeal of orders that are not final dispositions of the whole case or of a particular claim, but rather of *collateral* issues — issues severable from the merits.

**Four elements (all required):**

1. **Final disposition of a claimed right** — there has been a definitive ruling on an issue. In *Cohen*, the court ordered the plaintiff to post a litigation bond: a final ruling on whether the bond was required.

2. **Collateral to the merits** — the issue must be completely severable from the underlying claims. Whether a party must post a litigation bond has nothing to do with whether they will prevail on the merits of the lawsuit.

3. **Unreviewable on later appeal** — this is the factor that does the most work, analogous to irreparable harm in the preliminary injunction context. In *Cohen*, once a party has been ordered to buy a litigation bond and complies, a later appellate ruling that the bond was not required comes too late — the bond has already been purchased. Courts almost always invoke this factor to *deny* interlocutory review.

4. **Stealth factor (yes-or-no legal question)** — the issue must be a clean yes-or-no legal question, not a request for the appellate court to interpret a statute or resolve a factual dispute. "Do I have to post a litigation bond?" is yes-or-no. "What does this statute mean?" is not.

> **Note on class certification:** Denial of class certification is sometimes allowed interlocutory review despite not clearly satisfying all *Cohen* factors, because class certification is so important and case-dispositive in practice. This is an example of courts treating the doctrine flexibly.

---

### § 1292 — Interlocutory Appeals

§ 1292 provides two routes to interlocutory appeal. This is easier than § 1291.

#### § 1292(a)(1) — Injunctions

Any order pertaining to an injunction — creating, dissolving, or modifying one — is immediately appealable. Note: *denials* of injunctions may not qualify under this provision (Professor Greiner has flagged this in class).

#### § 1292(b) — Double Discretion (Certified Questions)

The trial court may certify a question for immediate appellate review. This is called "double discretion" because it is discretionary at both levels:

- **First discretion:** The trial court judge decides whether to certify the issue. There is no obligation to do so.
- **Second discretion:** After certification, the appellate court decides whether to take up the question.

**Three factors courts consider for certification:**

1. **Controlling question of law** — will this question determine the outcome of the case or a significant part of it?
2. **Substantial ground for differing views** — is this a genuinely contested legal question on which reasonable courts disagree?
3. **Material advancement of the litigation** — will resolving the issue now save significant time and expense?

These factors overlap and are fairly abstract; they give judges broad discretion. On an exam, go through all three.

> **Example:** In *Trump v. United States*, <!-- corrected: *Trump v. United States* --> the question of presidential immunity at the district court level was a classic candidate for § 1292(b) certification: controlling question of law, substantial ground for differing views (the D.C. Circuit and Supreme Court ultimately disagreed), and would materially advance the litigation. As it happened, the court did not certify; the D.C. Circuit took the case up anyway given its constitutional importance.

**Exam tips:**
- Certification must be **in writing** — if the facts say the judge certified orally, that is a defect worth noting (Professor Greiner has given bonus points for catching this).
- After certification, the party has **10 days** to file an application for appeal with the appellate court. Missing this deadline forfeits the right.

---

### § 1651 — Mandamus

Mandamus almost never succeeds. Think of it as the last resort — asking to speak to the manager. It is available only when no other route to appellate review exists.

There are two flavors within § 1651:

**Irreparable harm (emergency mandamus):** The party must show:
1. There is an **emergency** — irreparable harm is imminent, and the appellate court must intervene now.
2. The party is **clearly and indisputably correct** on the law — not just probably right; obviously right.
3. **Consider other parties and the government's interest** — Professor Greiner has described this as essentially asking: would it be a good idea? This factor is highly discretionary.

**Supervisory writ:** Granted when the trial court judge is misbehaving — potentially requiring a pattern of misbehavior. The *La Buy* case <!-- corrected: "Labai case" → *La Buy v. Howes Leather Co.* --> is an example: the judge was delegating all judicial work to a special master, far outside the bounds of proper judicial conduct. For supervisory writs, consider the *Cheney* factors <!-- corrected: "cheney" → *Cheney v. United States District Court* --> in addition to the standard mandamus analysis.

> **Note:** The policy animating §§ 1292(b) and 1651 is the same: appellate courts should not be flooded with piecemeal appeals. Both routes are reserved for exceptional circumstances.

## Part II: Multi-Party Litigation and Supplemental Jurisdiction

### Why This Matters on the Exam

Multi-party litigation is one of the most heavily tested topics in this course. It is complex in its own right and implicates subject matter jurisdiction, personal jurisdiction, venue, and preclusion all at once. On an exam with multiple plaintiffs, multiple defendants, and third-party defendants who themselves have claims against the plaintiff, you must **draw diagrams** — if you do not, you will get lost.

### Four Dimensions to Track on Every Exam

As you read the facts, note:
1. **Choice of law** — which state's law applies to each issue?
2. **Venue / transfer** — where was the case filed, and has it been transferred?
3. **Multi-party litigation** — who is suing whom, under what joinder rule?
4. **Preclusion** — where was any prior lawsuit, and which preclusion rules apply?

### Core Principles

**Every claim in a multi-party case requires its own independent analysis** of subject matter jurisdiction, personal jurisdiction, and venue. The fact that the court has personal jurisdiction over a defendant as to one claim does not mean it has personal jurisdiction as to every claim against that defendant.

**The same transaction or occurrence standard is very broad.** The standard articulated in *Hayward v. Robinson* is that two claims arise from the same transaction or occurrence when there is a logical relationship between them. Professor Greiner consistently emphasizes that this standard almost always will be satisfied — do not spend much time on it on the exam; just identify something and move on.

**Compulsory claims:** If a claim is compulsory and you fail to bring it, you are barred from bringing it later. Note: Rule 19 (compulsory joinder of parties) is not covered in this course. What *is* covered is the compulsory counterclaim rule (Rule 13(a)).

---

### Joinder Rules

#### Rule 13 — Counterclaims and Cross-Claims

Rule 13 applies to all counterclaims, including counterclaims by plaintiffs. For example, if a third-party defendant (TPD) files a claim against the original plaintiff, and the plaintiff wishes to counterclaim against TPD, Rule 13 applies to that counterclaim just as it does to a defendant's counterclaim against the plaintiff.

#### Rule 14 — Impleader (Third-Party Defendants)

A defendant may implead a third-party defendant if doing so shifts liability from the defendant to that third party. Classic examples: impleading an insurer, or a raw materials supplier who sold defective components.

#### Rule 20 — Permissive Joinder

Rule 20 is the primary joinder rule. Two requirements:
1. Claims arise from the **same transaction or occurrence** (almost always satisfied).
2. There is a **common question of law or fact** (Professor Greiner says this always exists; just identify something).

**Critical point:** When you add a second defendant to a case, *both* defendants are joined via Rule 20 — not just the newly added one. This has significant consequences for the § 1367(b) analysis (see below).

#### Kitchen Sink Rule (Rule 18)

Once a party has been properly joined to the case, the plaintiff may bring *any* other claim against that party, even if that claim does not arise from the same transaction or occurrence. This is different from Rule 20 joinder.

---

### Supplemental Jurisdiction — § 1367

**§ 1367(a):** If a court has original jurisdiction over one claim (the "anchoring claim"), it may exercise supplemental jurisdiction over all other claims that are part of the same case — i.e., share a common nucleus of operative fact (*Gibbs* <!-- corrected: *United Mine Workers v. Gibbs* -->).

**§ 1367(b):** Limits supplemental jurisdiction when the anchoring claim's only basis for original jurisdiction is **diversity** (§ 1332). Specifically, § 1367(b) bars supplemental jurisdiction over:

- Claims **by plaintiffs** against parties joined via **Rules 14, 19, 20, or 24**, when those claims would be inconsistent with § 1332 (i.e., would destroy complete diversity or fail the amount-in-controversy requirement); and
- Claims **by plaintiffs joined via Rules 19 or 24** against any defendant, when those claims would be inconsistent with § 1332.

**Key rule:** § 1367(b) only applies to **plaintiffs**. Claims by defendants — including third-party defendants' claims against original plaintiffs — are not subject to § 1367(b). A third-party defendant who is not diverse from the original plaintiff can still sue that plaintiff via supplemental jurisdiction, because TPD is a defendant.

However, if the *plaintiff* wishes to counterclaim against a non-diverse TPD, that counterclaim is a claim *by a plaintiff*, and § 1367(b) applies. Since the parties are not diverse and there is no arising-under jurisdiction, that counterclaim would be dismissed for lack of subject matter jurisdiction.

---

### Contamination Theory (*Exxon Mobil*)

<!-- corrected: "exon" → *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Services* throughout -->

This is one of the more counterintuitive rules in the course and a favorite exam topic.

**The apparent gap in § 1367(b):** Consider a case with P1 (NY) suing D (CA) for $100,000 — a valid diversity claim. P2 (CA) then joins via Rule 20 with a claim against D (CA) for $50,000. By the literal text of § 1367(b):

- § 1367(b) bars claims *by plaintiffs* against defendants joined via *Rules 14, 19, 20, or 24* — but D here is **not** joined via any of those rules; D was already the defendant.
- § 1367(b) also bars claims by plaintiffs joined via Rules *19 or 24* — but P2 joined via **Rule 20**, not 19 or 24.

So by the text, § 1367(b) would not oust P2's claim against D, even though P2 and D are not diverse. This appears to be a drafting error, which the Supreme Court acknowledged in *Exxon Mobil*.

**Contamination theory (the *Exxon Mobil* holding):** Even though § 1367(b) does not textually apply, the Supreme Court held that P2's non-diverse claim is still ousted — through **§ 1367(a)**, not § 1367(b). The theory (which the TA describes as a legal fiction): P2's claim, by destroying complete diversity between the parties, "contaminates" the original jurisdiction the court has over the anchoring claim (P1 v. D). Without that original jurisdiction, there is no anchoring claim, and therefore no supplemental jurisdiction for P2's claim to attach to.

**On an exam:** If you oust this claim via § 1367(b), you get no credit. You must identify it as ousted via § 1367(a) and contamination theory.

**Critical distinction — amount in controversy vs. diversity:**

*Exxon Mobil* draws a sharp line:

| P2's deficiency | Result |
|----------------|--------|
| Not diverse from D (same state) | Claim is **ousted** via contamination theory |
| Diverse from D, but AIC < $75,000 | Claim is **allowed** (AIC deficiency does not trigger contamination) |

The Court's justification: diversity is about fairness (contaminated by non-diversity); amount in controversy is about importance (the importance of the first claim is not diminished by the second claim's low value).

**The second-defendant twist:** This is where things get even more complex. In the single-defendant scenario above, P2's claim with an AIC deficiency *is* allowed. But the moment you add a second defendant (D2), **both D1 and D2 are joined via Rule 20**. Now P2's claim against D1 or D2 is a claim by a plaintiff against a party joined via Rule 20 — and § 1367(b) *does* apply. Under § 1367(b), an AIC deficiency is just as problematic as a diversity deficiency. So once there are two defendants, the Exxon AIC exception disappears.

**Class actions (exception to contamination):** In class actions, *Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur v. Cauble* <!-- corrected: "supreme tribe of ben hur" → *Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur v. Cauble* --> holds that only the **named plaintiffs** must be diverse from the defendants and satisfy amount in controversy. Unnamed class members can get in via supplemental jurisdiction even if they would break diversity or fail AIC. Contamination theory does not apply in class actions.

### Practical Exam Guidance for Multi-Party Litigation

When drawing out a multi-party diagram, for each arrow (i.e., each claim), track:

1. **Subject matter jurisdiction** — arising under (§ 1331), diversity (§ 1332), or supplemental (§ 1367)?
2. **Joinder rule** — how did this party enter the case (Rule 13, 14, 20, 24)?
3. **§ 1367(b) applicability** — is this a claim by a plaintiff, and is the anchor diversity-only?
4. **Personal jurisdiction** — specific or general; analyzed claim-by-claim.
5. **Domicile and AIC** — note these as you go; you may need them for supplemental jurisdiction analysis.

Sometimes the anchoring claim's basis for jurisdiction will be unclear (could be arising under, could be diversity). In that case, work through both possibilities: "If the anchoring claim is arising under, then... If the anchoring claim is diversity only, then..."

## Part III: Intervention (Rule 24)

Intervention is relatively straightforward. The test comes from *Smuck v. Hobson*. <!-- corrected: "Smock" → *Smuck v. Hobson* -->

**Three required elements:**

1. **The would-be intervenor has an interest** in the subject matter of the litigation.
2. **The outcome of the case could affect that interest** — the litigation could as a practical matter impair the intervenor's ability to protect that interest.
3. **The interest is not adequately represented** by the existing parties — i.e., the current parties are not sufficiently incentivized to protect the intervenor's interests. If existing parties already have aligned interests, intervention may be denied.

Cite *Smuck* and work through these elements. They are treated as requirements, not merely factors.

> **Note:** Every new claim added through intervention also requires its own subject matter jurisdiction, personal jurisdiction, and venue analysis.

## Part IV: Selected Q&A

### On *Cohen* Elements

**Q: Do you need all four Cohen factors?**
Yes — the stealth factor and all others must be satisfied.

**Q: Can you give more examples of "final disposition of a claimed right"?**
*Cohen* itself is the cleanest example: the court's order that the plaintiff *must* post a litigation bond is a final disposition of the plaintiff's claimed right *not* to post the bond. The cases on the slides illustrate both where the exception applies and where it does not.

### On § 1292(b) Double Discretion

**Q: Who would certify a question for interlocutory review?**
Either party can ask the trial judge to certify. The judge has discretion to certify sua sponte or at a party's request.

**Q: On an exam, how do you handle § 1292(b) when there's no indication the judge certified anything?**
Say: "There is no evidence the judge certified this question for immediate review under § 1292(b), but if she had — or if we wanted to argue she should — the factors would be..." and then work through the three factors.

**Q: Is the risk of flooding appellate courts a *Cohen* stealth factor?**
You can think of it that way. It is a policy consideration that cuts across all three routes to appellate jurisdiction and helps explain why interlocutory appeals are generally disfavored.

### On *Shaffer v. Heitner* and In Rem Jurisdiction

**Q: Does *Shaffer v. Heitner* <!-- corrected: "Schaefer's Heitner" → *Shaffer v. Heitner* --> apply to in rem jurisdiction?**

*Shaffer* held that all assertions of personal jurisdiction must satisfy minimum contacts. *Burnham v. Superior Court* <!-- corrected: *Burnham v. Superior Court* --> later suggested that at least tag jurisdiction (physical presence) may not require a minimum contacts analysis, casting doubt on whether *Shaffer*'s "all" means all.

For in rem (as opposed to quasi in rem): yes, *Shaffer* technically applies, but minimum contacts will almost always be satisfied. If you are litigating title to land, the land has contacts with the forum state by definition, and the subject matter of the litigation — settling title — is inherently connected to the forum. So the answer is yes, minimum contacts is required, but it will virtually always exist in the in rem context.

### On FRCP 4 and Electronic Service

FRCP 4 is best understood as incorporating state law rules on service of process into the federal rules, and also providing independent federal procedures for service. Electronic service is generally disfavored. The analysis of notice should center on *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co.* <!-- corrected: "malayne" → *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co.* --> and the "reasonably calculated to provide notice" standard. You do not need to go deep into FRCP 4 on an exam.

### On Switching Anchoring Claims

**Q: Can you switch your anchoring claim mid-lawsuit? E.g., if P1's claim against D1 was diversity-based, but later P2 joins with an arising-under claim against D1 — can that become the new anchor?**

Yes. Courts evaluate subject matter jurisdiction on an ongoing basis and must find it wherever it exists. If a valid arising-under claim is added to the case, all other claims can potentially anchor to it via § 1367(a) — and § 1367(b) will not apply, because § 1367(b) is only triggered when the anchoring claim's sole basis is diversity.
