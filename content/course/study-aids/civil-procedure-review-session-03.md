## Lead-Up to *Erie*

### *Swift v. Tyson*

**Background:** Swindle (started by Norton and Keith) eventually leads to Swift suing Tyson in NY federal court based on diversity. Rules of Decision Act (28 U.S.C. § 1652) directs federal courts to apply the "laws of the several states." Under NY state common law, the original swindle relieved Tyson of the obligation to pay. But Justice Story thinks the better rule is the "holder in due course" doctrine, under which Tyson had to pay Swift and sue the swindlers himself.

**Issue:** Does the federal court have to apply NY state common law (under which Tyson would not have to pay), because "laws of the several states" in § 1652 encompasses state common law?

**Holding:** No. State common law does not constitute the "law of the several states." Federal courts aren't bound to follow state common law, and can consider general common-law principles and create a federal body of common law decisions in cases where there is a conflict between state and federal law.

### *Black & White Taxicab & Transfer Co. v. Brown & Yellow Taxicab & Transfer Co.*

**Background:** Brown & Yellow Taxicab (KY) forms an agreement with a railroad giving them exclusive privilege of soliciting passengers at the station. This agreement is invalid under KY state common law. Competitor Black & White Taxicab (KY) begins soliciting passengers at the station. Brown & Yellow reincorporated in TN <!-- uncertain: original may read "TE" — likely "TN" (Tennessee) --> to invoke diversity jurisdiction and sue the competitor, hoping to take advantage of federal common law under which the agreement was valid.

**Issue:** Can Brown & Yellow obtain an injunction under federal common law after reincorporating?

**Holding:** Yes. The federal court in KY granted Brown & Yellow an injunction under federal common law, and the judgment was affirmed on appeal.

**Why this is bad:** Don't want people to be able to manufacture diversity to seek better legal rules when existing law is unfavorable to them.

## *Erie RR v. Tompkins*

**Facts:** Tompkins was injured while walking on a footpath parallel to Erie Railroad tracks in PA. Lawsuit was filed against Erie R.R. in federal court. Under PA state common law, Tompkins would be precluded from recovering because he would be treated as a trespasser (to whom no duty of care would be owed). The federal district court concluded that the railroad did have an ordinary duty of care, so Tompkins could recover.

**Issue:** Does the federal district court have to apply PA state common law (under which Tompkins cannot recover), or can the court create its own federal general common law?

**Holding:** There is no federal general common law. *Swift* is overturned: federal courts must apply state law for substantive matters (when sitting in diversity or when a state law is otherwise before federal courts) and federal law for procedural matters. The "laws of the several states" under § 1652 encompasses state common law.

## Policy Justifications for *Erie*

1. **What is law?**
   - There is no pre-existing "natural" law to be discovered.

2. **Statutory (28 U.S.C. § 1652)**
   - State common law necessarily qualifies as "laws of the several states" of § 1652.

3. **Constitutional**
   - **Federalism**
     - States should be primary regulators of behavior.
     - We care more about vertical uniformity (state court ↔ federal court in same jurisdiction) than horizontal uniformity (federal court in one jurisdiction ↔ federal court in another jurisdiction).
   - **Separation of Powers**
     - Federal courts shouldn't make up rules — that is the legislature's role.
   - **Equal Protection**
     - Difference in substantive law for in-state and out-of-state plaintiffs violates equal protection.

4. **Federal court prudence**
   - In the *Swift* regime, federal courts were displacing substantive state law (torts, property, contracts, etc.).
   - This wasn't working well: harder to regulate behavior, lack of a uniform system within the same jurisdiction.

## Erie Tests

### Step 1: When to Apply?

- Any time there is a conflict between state and federal law (diversity, supplemental jurisdiction, or any other reason state law is before a federal court).
- Substantive → state law. Procedural → federal law.
- How to tell if something is substantive or procedural? Apply the tests below.

### Step 2: Determine Type of Law at Issue

- Constitution; Statute; FRCP; Federal Common Law.

### Step 3: Apply Four *Erie* Tests

- (a) *York* (outcome-determinative)
- (b) *Hanna* Majority — Scalia approach
- (c) *Hanna* Majority — Ginsburg approach
- (d) Harlan Primary Conduct

## The *York* Test

**Rule:** If the choice is outcome-determinative, use state law.

**What does "outcome-determinative" mean?**
- If we use state law, one result would occur. If we use federal law, another result would occur. Therefore, the choice is outcome-determinative.
- The choice determines the outcome of the whole case.
- **Examples:** statute of limitations; rule that requires a supplemental affidavit with the complaint.

**Exception:** *York* addressed only choices that are determinative for the outcome of the entire case.
- Preliminary injunction, class certification — not determinative to the outcome of the entire suit, just one part of the case.

## *Hanna* Majority Test — Constitution

1. **Validity:** Presume that the Constitution is valid.

2. **Applicability:**
   - **Scalia:** Follow the plain textual meaning of the relevant constitutional provision.
   - **Ginsburg:** Although potentially more flexible than Scalia, unlikely to find the constitutional provision inapplicable.

<!-- page: 11 -->

## *Hanna* Majority Test — Federal Statute

1. **Validity**
   - Constitutional provision that gives Congress the power to pass the statute.
   - Common constitutional provisions:
     - Article III, § 2 (gives Congress power to regulate federal courts)
     - Necessary and Proper Clause (gives Congress power to enact housekeeping laws)
     - Commerce Clause (gives Congress power to regulate interstate commerce)

2. **Applicability**
   - **Scalia:** Focus on the plain text of the statute.
     - *Example:* *Stewart v. Ricoh* dissent — no textual support for the proposition that 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a) covers forum selection clauses because the statute doesn't say "forum selection clause." As a result, it's inapplicable and we should use state law because of the Rules of Decisions Act (§ 1652).
   - **Ginsburg:** Possible creative interpretation that narrows the federal statute or makes it inapplicable, but still relatively deferential to the statute.

<!-- page: 12 -->

## *Hanna* Majority Test — FRCP

1. **Validity**
   - Rules Enabling Act (28 U.S.C. § 2072) empowers SCOTUS to create the FRCP.
   - FRCP must not "abridge, enlarge, or modify state substantive right." § 2072(b).
   - *Sibbach*: A FRCP does not "abridge, enlarge, or modify" a substantive right if it "really regulates procedure."
   - (Mnemonic: "Rules Enabling Act, *Sibbach*, really regulates procedure.")

2. **Applicability**
   - **Scalia:** Plain text interpretation. *Example:* *Hanna*.
   - **Ginsburg:** Attempt to find creative interpretation that renders federal law inapplicable.
     - *Examples:* *Walker* and *Shady Grove* dissent.
     - Possible ways federal law could be inapplicable:
       1. Federal law is silent on the issue.
       2. Federal law sets the floor/a minimum; state law adds additional requirements.

## *Hanna* Majority Test — Federal Common Law

**Framework:** Apply the Twin Aims of *Erie*:
1. Inequitable administration of the law
2. Forum shopping

**Scalia and Ginsburg approach the issue the same way:** Apply state law when applying federal law would invite forum-shopping and lead to inequitable administration of the law.

**Example:** Assignment 2, 2023
- Federal common law provides that choice-of-law clauses are enforceable.
- Both PA and NY law renders such clauses invalid.
- Using federal common law would lead to outcome-determinative differences, resulting in inequitable administration of the law and forum-shopping → use state law.

## Harlan Primary Conduct Test

- **Issue affects primary conduct** → substantive → state law applies.
- **Issue does not affect primary conduct** → procedural → federal law applies.

**Primary conduct** (pre-litigation conduct; behavior in daily life):
- Where a company decides to incorporate, where an individual buys products, etc.
- *Black & White Taxicab* — reincorporated to try to take advantage of different laws.
- *Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp.* — state law says that if you want to file a shareholders' derivative lawsuit, you must produce a litigation bond.

**Not primary conduct** (issues that lawyers care about; post-litigation conduct):
- Size of paper.
- Tagging (*Hanna*).

**Ambiguous:**
- Statute of limitations — could possibly impact investment strategies.
