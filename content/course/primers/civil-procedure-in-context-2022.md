## Civil Procedure (and Civil Lawsuits) Overview

Civil procedure is the set of rules governing the process of filing and litigating a civil case: one in which a person or a group of people sue each other (as opposed to criminal cases, where the government prosecutes an individual). Civil cases cover a wide range of topics, including:

- **Contracts**: Failure to perform terms of a contract (employment, contractor, sales)
- **Property**: Ownership disagreements, disputes over zoning, repair responsibilities
- **Tort claims**: Car accidents, assault, negligence, trespass, product liability
- **Family law**: Divorce, child support, child custody, adoption
- **Landlord/tenant cases**: Evictions, returning security deposits
- **Probate cases**: Wills and conservatorships (managing assets of someone still alive)
- **Juvenile cases**: Juvenile delinquency and juvenile dependency
- **Small claims**: Lawsuits with a damages cutoff (up to $25,000, depending on the state)
- **Other cases include**: Name changes, elder abuse, copyright, civil harassment, etc. Civil cases in federal court also arise when the government is a plaintiff (e.g. suing someone for student loan repayment) or the government is a defendant (e.g. someone is suing for their social security benefits).

### Comparing Civil vs. Criminal Law

| | Civil Law | Criminal Law |
|---|---|---|
| **Parties** | Private, non-state actors: individuals, groups of individuals, or organizations. | The government is always a party in a criminal case, bringing a lawsuit against an individual or organization. |
| **Burden of Proof** | Typically, the standard of proof is "preponderance of the evidence." | The criminal standard of proof is "beyond a reasonable doubt," a higher standard than in civil cases. |
| **Remedies** | The remedy sought in civil litigation is typically compensation (money) paid to the party who brought the lawsuit, although it can be an injunction (mandated action or inaction). | The most likely penalty is fines (paid to the government) or incarceration. The injured party (victim) does not ordinarily receive compensation. |
| **Role of incarceration and commitment** | Incarceration is uncommon but still possible in civil cases, and parties do not seek it as a remedy. It can happen when a court holds a party in "contempt," i.e., the court finds that a party (or a lawyer) has not complied with a court order. Civil commitment in a non-carceral setting is also possible in cases when an individual is found to be a danger to themselves or others. | Incarceration is common in criminal cases, and one party (the prosecution) often seeks it as a remedy. |
| **Role of injured party** | In a civil case, the injured party (or their estate or family) ordinarily must sue for the lawsuit to occur. | In criminal cases, the case can proceed without the injured party's consent or participation. |
| **Purpose** | The most commonly cited purposes of the civil system are to settle disputes between parties, help individuals protect their rights, and ensure people are "made whole" after harms to them. | The most commonly cited purposes of the criminal system are to deter certain types of behavior, incapacitate people deemed dangerous, and signal unacceptable action. |
| **Right to an attorney** | There is no federal constitutional right to an attorney in civil cases. | The constitution guarantees a right to legal counsel in serious criminal cases. |

## In Practice

Civil litigation occurs on both the federal and state court level. 98% of civil litigation occurs in state courts. Though it is difficult to get data on the civil legal system as a whole, some snapshots and studies appear below, for context.

**Who uses the civil system?**

- 47% of U.S. households said they had a civil legal problem in the past 12 months (excluding traffic tickets). These were most commonly: taking on rights or responsibility for a family member, dispute with purchases, issue with credit or debt, family breakup, illness, and employment issues.
- 71% of low-income households experienced at least one civil legal problem in the past year, most frequently problems with health care, housing conditions, disability access, veterans benefits, and domestic violence.
- 86% of civil legal problems reported by low-income Americans received inadequate or no legal assistance.

**What types of cases are most common?**

One study looking at non-domestic civil cases in 10 urban counties (covering 152 courts and 925,000 cases) found that:

- 67% were contract cases (37% were about debt and 29% were landlord/tenant);
- 16% were disputes valued at $12,000 or less (small claims);
- 9% were other agency appeals;
- 7% were tort cases; and
- 1% were real property cases (concerning land, houses, etc.).

Another estimate found that 47% of the civil cases in state courts are contract disputes, 18% were small claims, 11% probate, 4% tort, and 20% all other civil cases.

**How far do cases progress?**

- Today, approximately 1% of all civil cases filed in federal court are resolved by trial. In other words, 99% do not reach trial.
- Currently the average number of merit trials per district court judgeship is four per year (three jury, and one "bench" (meaning that the judge heard the evidence and decided the facts)).
- The paucity of trials has not always been the case. The rate of trials in federal court is at its lowest point in the last 55 years. When the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure were promulgated in 1938, 20% of cases were resolved by trial. In 1962, it was down to 12%.
- In state court, the trial rate is less than or equal to the 1% trial rate at federal court.
- One study estimated that, of cases brought:
  - 4% went to bench or jury trial;
  - 33% of cases were dismissed (potentially following a settlement);
  - 10% of cases were recorded as a settlement;
  - 46% of cases had another kind of judgment (many of which were default judgments after a party failed to appear or perform some other court-ordered action); and
  - 1% were decided through summary judgment.

**What damages were awarded?**

The same study found that:

- 75% of cases had damages of less than $5,200 awarded.
- Only .2% of cases had damages of more than $500k, and less than .1% exceeded $1M.
- 75% of real property cases had damages awarded of less than $106,000.
- 75% of torts cases had damages awarded of less than $12,200.
- For most litigants, the cost of litigating a case through trial would greatly exceed the monetary value of the case.

**What percentage of parties had lawyers?**

- In more than 75% of all civil cases in the United States, at least one litigant is pro se (representing themselves, rather than having a lawyer).
- In cases involving family law, domestic violence, and small claims, one party lacks representation in 70–98% of the cases. On the other end of the spectrum, in tort claims the party was self-represented only 36% of the time.
- 49% of filings in federal court of appeals are by pro se litigants (including both civil, criminal, and administrative agency appeals).
- On the federal district court level, roughly 27% of cases have one or more pro se litigant. 25% of the cases are a pro se plaintiff, and 2% are a pro se defendant (in other words, of the 220,000 cases, about 80,000 are pro se).
- On both the appellate and district level, the majority of pro se cases were plaintiffs serving time in prison raising civil rights claims. Cases filed by people not serving prison time were civil rights cases, followed by cases about other statutes, contract actions, personal injury, and property.

**How are lawyers paid?**

There are a variety of pay structures for lawyers:

- **Hourly payment:** Many attorneys work on an hourly basis and bill in 6-min intervals. The Bar and state law sometimes loosely regulate rates (such as by saying that rates should not be "unreasonable").
- **Flat fees:** In a flat fee system, the lawyer charges a set amount, or a pre-fixed range, for the entirety of the work. This setup is most common for relatively routine work, such as estates, wills, and divorces.
- **Contingency fees:** In this structure, the lawyer gets paid only if they settle or win the case, and is paid from the damages (money) awarded. A typical contingency fee is about 33% of the damages after settlement, or 40% after trial. Contingency fees are available only in cases where there is the chance of a financial payout at the end, such as personal injury, worker's comp, and accidents. While contingency fees increase the accessibility of the legal system to those who could not afford an upfront payment, they also significantly reduce the damages the plaintiff receives and often increase the lawyer's incentives to settle.
- **Retainers:** Technically, a retainer is an upfront deposit for the attorney to begin working on the case. The lawyer removes their hourly rate from the retainer; any of the retainer not used is returned to the client. The retainer serves as a placeholder, so the individual or business knows they will have legal assistance. However, colloquially people often refer to having a "lawyer on retainer" meaning they have a pre-existing relationship, even if they bill hourly.

The average hourly billable rate for lawyers was $300 per hour in 2021 and has been increasing. Rates for lawyers handling complex matters for corporations can be many times this amount: some Big Law firms charge more than $2000 per hour. Many people who must pay for their own lawyers run into debt pursuing legal action.

**What options are available for those who cannot afford lawyers?**

- The Legal Services Corporation (LSC) is the biggest source of funding for civil legal aid for low-income Americans. Funding is appropriated by Congress and distributed from LSC to 130 local legal aid programs throughout the country.
- An individual must earn less than $14,713 to be eligible for aid from LSC; a family of four has to make less than $30,313.
- Legal Services Corporation aided 1.8 million Americans in 2013 and turned away an equal number. In 2016, Congress appropriated $385 million for LSC, as opposed to the $770 million appropriated annually in the 1980s.
- In many cases, people chose not to pursue legal action. People pursue legal action to resolve unpaid debt only 46% of the time, likely because the cost of legal action exceeds the benefit or is simply infeasible.

## Alternatives to Civil Litigation

**Arbitration:** Arbitration is a private dispute resolution procedure, whereby a party other than a judge or jury makes a binding decision on the dispute. Arbitration is confidential and the final decision is legally enforceable.

- Arbitration is typically used where the parties have previously made a binding agreement to use arbitration, e.g. through a work contract.
- Currently, 54% of private sector employers have mandatory arbitration procedures, representing 60 million workers (or roughly a third of the U.S. workforce).
- Arbitration is also increasingly common in consumer products. 81 of the Fortune 100 companies put boilerplate arbitration clauses into consumer contracts.
- The most common types of cases to be handled in arbitration are breach of fiduciary duty, negligence, breach of contract, and failure to supervise.
- The Federal Arbitration Act makes agreements to arbitrate valid and enforceable. The FAA "preempts" (meaning supersedes) many state laws that would otherwise limit the extent of arbitration.
- Frequently in arbitration, and particularly on consumer product disputes, one of the parties pays the arbitrator, class actions are prohibited, and the results are subject to a non-disclosure agreement. These facts lead to allegations and arguments that arbitration agreements are abusive.

**Mediation:** Mediation is another alternative form of dispute resolution where a trained mediator helps the parties work towards resolving their differences. Unlike arbitration, mediators do not determine the outcome; the parties themselves determine what they'd like the outcome to be.

- Mediation results vary significantly, ranging from 27–63% rate of case resolution.
- Mediation is typically a voluntary process, but it can be court-ordered, most commonly in family law cases, in an attempt to avoid litigation.

## Does CivPro Matter? Examples of Cases Decided on Procedural Grounds

**Jurisdiction: Does the court have the authority to rule on this case?**

*Marbury v. Madison* (1803): Establishing judicial review by holding the court did not have authority to rule on the issue

In this seminal case, judicial nominee Marbury sued Secretary of State Madison directly in the Supreme Court, asking for the court to require Madison to finalize his appointment papers. The court held that while this would be an appropriate action, the Supreme Court did not have the jurisdiction to do so as a court of first review. Though there was a Congressional statute authorizing the Supreme Court to make such rulings, under the Constitution the Supreme Court only had appellate jurisdiction. The court held that the law was wrong, and the Supreme Court did lack subject matter jurisdiction, and dismissed the case. The decision to dismiss the case established judicial review (the Court's authority to determine if Congress has behaved unconstitutionally).

*Dred Scott v. Sandford* (1857): Holding even free African Americans aren't citizens by deciding the court did not have jurisdiction to rule on the case

Dred Scott was an African American man born into slavery, who later traveled with his owner to a free state. He sued for his freedom in federal court. The court was asked to rule on whether Scott's residence in free territory made him free. The majority held, 7-2, that the court could not hear the case because even if free, an African American descended of slaves was not an American citizen. Since Scott was not an American citizen, he was not a citizen of any state. The court dismissed the case for lack of diversity jurisdiction (a type of authority to hear a case that the court has only when two people are citizens of different states). Not content to stop there, the court also limited Congress's power to regulate slavery.

**Motion to Dismiss: When should a lawsuit be thrown out for not being plausible enough?**

*Trump v. Pennsylvania* (2020): Election challenge dismissed for failure to state a claim persuasive enough that the court felt it was worth hearing.

After the 2020 Presidential election, the Trump campaign initiated a series of lawsuits challenging states' decisions to certify Biden as election winner. In one such case, the Third Circuit Court of Appeals dismissed the Trump campaign's challenge to a district court's decision about Pennsylvania's election for failure to state a claim. Citing *Twombly* and *Iqbal* (which you will learn in this class) the court noted that the Trump campaign only made conclusive statements ("a substantial portion of the … votes should not have been counted") but included no factual support for those statements. Without facts, the campaign's complaint was not plausible enough even to be heard. The court dismissed it without a trial or further investigation; the same pattern played out in many other states.

**Choice of Law: When a legal matter spans multiple states, but the state's laws are in conflict, which laws should be used?**

*Depp v. Heard* (2022): The location of the lawsuit may have been key to Johnny Depp's success

In this case, Johnny Depp sued Amber Heard for $50 million, alleging defamation after she published a Washington Post op-ed accusing him of abuse. While both actors live in California, the lawsuit took place in Virginia. Analysis suggests that Depp sued in Virginia rather than California because California had strong protection for First Amendment rights, which would have made Heard's speech more likely to be legal. In addition, Depp risked having to pay Heard's legal fees in California. Instead, he sued in Virginia, arguing the location of the Washington Post servers in VA was enough to justify the lawsuit. Virginia is one of just ten states that uses the Restatement 1 *lex loci delicti* rule (you'll learn this soon!) which asks where the harm occurred, rather than analyzing the most important location for the case. The VA judge determined that the "harm" of the publication occurred at the location of the servers, and the lawsuit could proceed in Virginia. Depp ultimately won the case, a less likely outcome if the litigation had been in CA.

**Future Abortion Litigation: Crossing state lines for an abortion will create a choice of law dilemma**

Now that *Roe v. Wade* is overturned, each state can determine the legality of abortion on their own. Imagine in State A abortion is illegal, while in bordering State B abortion is legal. Furthermore, State A also passed a law making it illegal to cross state lines and get an abortion in another state, even if abortion is legal there. Let's imagine a pregnant person crosses from State A to State B to get an abortion. Which states' laws should determine whether their action is legal, State A's law or State B's law? Experts are unsure. This litigation is expected in the coming months and years, and it will be decided on procedural grounds.
