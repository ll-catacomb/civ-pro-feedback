# Overview of 28 U.S.C. § 1367

## Why have supplemental jurisdiction?

Suppose Bart, who uses a wheelchair, goes out to dinner at a new restaurant, Lenny's. When Bart arrives, he finds that the restaurant lacks a wheelchair ramp. Bart tries to hobble up the stairs, but they're icy from a recent snowstorm, causing him to slip, fall, and break his leg. Bart wants to sue Lenny's in federal court for violating the Americans with Disabilities Act, a federal cause of action that creates subject matter jurisdiction under § 1331. But Bart also wants to sue Lenny's for a tort claim under state law. If Bart brought his ADA claim in federal court but had to bring his tort claim in state court, we would need separate trials involving almost identical facts, evidence, and witnesses—a waste of litigants' and courts' resources. It would be much more efficient to let Bart bring both claims together in a single federal trial. Section 1367 enables that.

## The anchoring claim and the supplemental claims: § 1367(a)

In the example above, Bart had two kinds of claims: (1) a claim over which a federal court would have subject matter jurisdiction and (2) other claims that don't create subject matter jurisdiction on their own but that it'd be convenient to try with the claims that do. Section 1367(a) describes this situation. It says we need a "civil action of which the district courts have original jurisdiction"—that is, some claim that would create subject matter jurisdiction under § 1331 or § 1332. We'll call this the "anchoring claim." If we've got an anchoring claim, then "the district courts shall have supplemental jurisdiction over all other claims that are so related to [the anchoring claim] that they form part of the same case or controversy." In our example above, the district court would have subject matter jurisdiction over Bart's ADA claim under § 1331. It could then establish supplemental jurisdiction over his tort claim, allowing the court to hear both claims together.

Question: What does it take for the supplemental claims to be so related to the anchoring claim that they qualify for supplemental jurisdiction? Although *Gibbs* predates § 1367, it illustrates the rule. Tl;dr: "The state and federal claims must derive from a common nucleus of operative fact."

## Adding claims and adding parties

Note that in the example above, Bart was using supplemental jurisdiction to add new claims against a party whom he could already sue in federal court. Now suppose that Carl and Lenny simultaneously beat up Bart—but while Carl's actions only give Bart a cause of action under state law, Lenny's also trigger federal causes of actions. Bart wants to sue Lenny in federal court. Can he use supplemental jurisdiction to sue Carl—a defendant whom he otherwise couldn't sue federally—in the same case? In *Aldinger* and *Finley*, the Court distinguished using supplemental jurisdiction to add new parties from using it to add new claims.

Congress didn't like that, so it got rid of that distinction in § 1367(a): "supplemental jurisdiction shall include claims that involve the joinder . . . of additional parties."

## Limiting what parties can be added through supplemental jurisdiction: § 1367(b)

But § 1367(b) keeps an important limit on which parties plaintiffs can add through supplemental jurisdiction. It applies when the anchoring claim only creates supplemental jurisdiction under § 1332 ("[i]n any civil action of which the district courts have original jurisdiction founded solely on section 1332 of this title"). In that case, you can't add a party (at least not using many rules that allow you to join parties) if doing so would destroy one of the requirements of § 1332 ("[T]he district courts shall not have supplemental jurisdiction under subsection (a) over claims by plaintiffs against persons [added through one of the enumerated rules] when exercising supplemental jurisdiction over such claims would be inconsistent with the jurisdictional requirements of section 1332.")

For example, suppose Flanders (a Massachusetts citizen), wants to sue Marge (a Vermont citizen) and Seymour (a Massachusetts citizen) for a state-law tort claim. Flanders couldn't sue them both under § 1332, since there isn't complete diversity. But if he has a crafty lawyer, Flanders might try to evade § 1332's requirements by first suing Marge under § 1332 (because Flanders and Marge are diverse) and then asking the district court to add Seymour using supplemental jurisdiction. Section 1367(b) says you can't dodge the complete-diversity requirement with this two-step.

### § 1367(b) only limits party joinder by plaintiffs

Note that § 1367(b) applies only to parties added by plaintiffs: "the district courts shall not have supplemental jurisdiction under subsection (a) over claims by plaintiffs against persons made parties . . . ." So if a defendant is adding a party using supplemental jurisdiction, it doesn't matter if the party's presence would violate the requirements of § 1332. To see § 1367(b) in action, read *Guaranteed Systems*.

## Power and discretion: § 1367(c)

As we've seen, § 1367(a) and § 1367(b) give federal courts the power to exercise supplemental jurisdiction. But federal courts can choose not to use that power in particular cases. Section 1367(c) lists conditions under which federal courts, as a matter of prudence, can decline to exercise supplemental jurisdiction that they otherwise could: "The district courts may decline to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over a claim under subsection (a) if—

(1) the claim raises a novel or complex issue of State law,
(2) the claim substantially predominates over the claim or claims over which the district court has original jurisdiction,
(3) the district court has dismissed all claims over which it has original jurisdiction, or
(4) in exceptional circumstances, there are other compelling reasons for declining jurisdiction."

These factors all come from *Gibbs*, which contains additional factors that Congress chose not to codify in § 1367.

## Wrapping up: the § 1367 checklist

When you're evaluating whether a district court can exercise supplemental jurisdiction, run it through the subsections of § 1367:

**§ 1367(a)**
- Find the anchoring claim
- Does the state-law claim emerge from a common nucleus of operative fact? (*Gibbs*)
  - Yes → proceed
  - No → no supplemental jurisdiction

**§ 1367(b)**
- Does the anchoring claim only create SMJ under § 1332?
  - No, it also creates AUJ → proceed
  - Yes → Is the plaintiff trying to add a party that would destroy the requirements of § 1332?
    - Yes → no supplemental jurisdiction
    - No → proceed

**§ 1367(c)**
- Are any of the enumerated reasons to deny supplemental jurisdiction present?
