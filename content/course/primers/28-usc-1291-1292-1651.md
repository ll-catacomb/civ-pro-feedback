# Overview of §§ 1291, 1292(a)(1), 1292(b), 1651

## Appellate jurisdiction: When can a circuit court hear the appeal?

We started this course with how cases begin—which court system you can file in and where you can file. We then moved to trials—what law will apply, pleadings and motions, dismissal, summary judgment, post-judgment motions. We now move to the next stage in a lawsuit: appeal.

An appeal requires a new court—a federal circuit court—to hear the case. That means this new court must exercise jurisdiction. But under Article III of the Constitution, all lower federal courts—circuit courts included—can only exercise jurisdiction that Congress has granted them. Congress granted that jurisdiction in the statutes here. The question these statutes answer, then, is: When can a circuit court hear an appeal?

## The rule: § 1291

Section 1291 creates the general rule: "The courts of appeals . . . shall have jurisdiction of appeals from all final decisions of the district courts . . . ." In other words, when a district court issues a final decision, you can appeal it using § 1291. Conversely, everything a district court does that isn't a final decision can't be appealed using § 1291.

Question: When has a district court issued a "final decision[]" that can be appealed? *Liberty Mutual* and *Atlantic City* address this question. Tl;dr: We'll split cases into the underlying claims (where a claim = theory of relief + facts + parties) and say that a claim has reached a final decision when it's been fully litigated in the district court. Another kind of final decision is described in Rule 54(b)—applied in *Sears* and *Cohen*. Tl;dr: This "Cohen exception" deems a decision final (and thus appealable) if (1) it was unrelated to the merits, (2) it won't be revisited by the district court, and (3) an appeal would be pointless after the rest of the case wraps up.

## The exceptions

The § 1291 "final-order rule" (as interpreted in *Liberty Mutual* and *Atlantic City*) will be our usual test for when a decision can be appealed. But there are exceptions, decisions that are not final but that are nonetheless appealable. The statutes below create appellate jurisdiction in those specific circumstances.

### Injunctions: § 1292(a)(1)

Section 1292(a)(1) says that "orders . . . granting, continuing, modifying, refusing or dissolving injunctions, or refusing to dissolve or modify injunctions" are immediately appealable. That makes sense. If a district court grants an injunction, it has harmed the enjoined party by forcing it to do something it doesn't want to (or forcing it to refrain from doing something it does want to). Meanwhile, if the district court denies an injunction, the party who sought the injunction faces what it claimed would be irreparable harm. Either way, we don't want one party to continue suffering unjustly while the district court decides the rest of the claim.

### Double discretion: § 1292(b)

Sometimes a district court has to make a decision that it's uncertain about and whose answer will shape the rest of the trial. The judge might want immediate guidance from the appellate court so that the remainder of the trial can proceed accordingly. If an appeal had to wait until the end of the lawsuit, the appellate court might reverse and everything that happened after the district-court judge took the wrong turn would be a waste.

Section 1292(b) creates appellate jurisdiction for those circumstances. This appeal has two steps:

The district court judge issues a written finding that (1) there's an important question of law (2) over which there's substantial ground for a difference of opinion so that (3) an immediate appeal might materially advance the lawsuit. As § 1292(b) puts it, "When a district judge, in making in a civil action an order not otherwise appealable . . . shall be of the opinion that such order involves a controlling question of law as to which there is substantial ground for difference of opinion and that an immediate appeal from the order may materially advance the ultimate termination of the litigation, he shall so state in writing in such order." After the trial judge issues such a finding, a party has ten days to ask the circuit court to allow the decision to be appealed.

Then comes step two: The circuit court decides whether to accept the appeal or not. In the language of § 1292(b), "The Court of Appeals which would have jurisdiction of an appeal of such action may thereupon, in its discretion, permit an appeal to be taken from such order, if application is made to it within ten days after the entry of the order."

This provision is known as the "double-discretion doctrine," since the district court has discretion to make the written finding (or not) that allows appeal, and the appellate court then has discretion to take the appeal (or not).

### Writ of mandamus: § 1651

Finally, § 1651 authorizes federal courts to issue writs: "The Supreme Court and all courts established by Act of Congress may issue all writs necessary or appropriate in aid of their respective jurisdictions and agreeable to the usages and principles of law."

The writ we'll study is a writ of mandamus, which a court uses to order a government official to fulfill their duties. If a party is unhappy with a lower court's decision that isn't a final order, the party can seek immediate relief by petitioning an appellate court for a writ of mandamus ordering the lower-court judge to do what the party wants. We'll see a writ of mandamus in *La Buy*.

## Putting it all together

A federal appellate court can't hear an appeal without jurisdiction, which Congress granted in the series of statutes discussed above. They stipulate that appellate courts have jurisdiction:

- Over final orders (§ 1291, as interpreted in *Liberty Mutual* and *Atlantic City*)
- Including the "Cohen exception" (Rule 54(b), applied in *Sears* and *Cohen*)
- Over any decision that a district court makes about an injunction (§ 1292(a)(1))
- Under the double-discretion doctrine (§ 1292(b)), and
- To issue a writ of mandamus (§ 1651).
