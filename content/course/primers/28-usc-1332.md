# Overview of 28 U.S.C. § 1332(a)–(c)

## The second source of subject matter jurisdiction: diversity

Last class, we saw our first source of subject matter jurisdiction: arising-under jurisdiction. But AUJ cases aren't the only ones that federal courts can hear. We'll now see a second source of subject matter jurisdiction: so-called "diversity jurisdiction." As with AUJ, the Constitution says that Congress can grant district courts the power to hear cases with diversity jurisdiction—if Congress wants to. As with AUJ, Congress granted that power. We'll look at the constitutional and statutory provisions below, then discuss some questions that diversity jurisdiction raises (some of which are answered by two cases you're reading for this class, *Strawbridge* and *Mas*).

## The constitutional limit: Article III

As we saw last class, Article III, section 1 authorizes Congress to create district courts and invest them with the "judicial Power of the United States." Article III, section 2 says that the judicial power includes "Cases . . . between Citizens of different States." Putting these sections together, Article III says that Congress can grant district courts the power to hear cases between citizens of different states.

## The statutory grant of diversity jurisdiction: § 1332

Congress granted that authorization in § 1332. For a federal district court to hear a case under § 1332, two requirements must be met:

1. **Amount in controversy:** The amount in controversy between the parties must "exceed[] the sum or value of $75,000," and
2. **Diversity of citizenship:** The parties must be "citizens of different states."

## Amount in controversy

The amount in controversy is the amount of money that the plaintiff could conceivably recover. If Homer sues Ned for $80,000, then there is more than $75,000 in controversy between them. Section 1332 is thus satisfied (as long as Homer and Ned are also citizens of different states). But if Homer sues Smithers for $60,000, the amount in controversy is less than $75,000, and a district court will not have subject matter jurisdiction under § 1332. Note that it doesn't matter how likely Homer is to actually recover more than $75,000—if there's a chance he'll win at least that much, then he meets the AIC requirement.

## Diversity of citizenship

The diversity requirement raises two questions, which we'll take our first swipe at below.

### How diverse do the parties need to be? Complete diversity

Diversity is simple when one plaintiff sues one defendant: Are the plaintiff and defendant citizens of different states? But what happens when you have multiple plaintiffs or multiple defendants? Do you need only one plaintiff to be diverse from only one defendant (what we'll call "minimal diversity")? Or do all plaintiffs need to be diverse from all defendants (what we'll call "complete diversity")?

- **Minimal diversity:** At least one plaintiff is diverse from at least one defendant.
- **Complete diversity:** All plaintiffs are diverse from all defendants.

*Strawbridge v. Curtiss* answers this question. Tl;dr: § 1332 requires complete diversity.

### What state is a party a citizen of? Domicile, etc.

Because § 1332 creates subject matter jurisdiction if the plaintiff(s) and defendant(s) are citizens of different states, we need to figure out what state each party is a citizen of.

#### Corporations

§ 1332(c)(1) gives the answer for corporations: (1) "a corporation shall be deemed to be a citizen of every State . . . by which it has been incorporated" and (2) "of the State . . . where it has its principal place of business." How do we figure out where a corporation's principal place of business is? The Supreme Court established the rule in *Hertz*, discussed in note 2 on p. 283. Tl;dr: A corporation's principal place of business is its "nerve center" where its directors and officers control its decisions. For instance, suppose Burns Corp. is incorporated in Delaware. Its CEO works from Manhattan, which is also where its board of directors meets to make key strategic decisions. Manhattan is Burns Corp.'s "nerve center" and therefore its principal place of business. Burns Corp. is thus a citizen of Delaware and New York.

Because corporations can be citizens of multiple states, to sue a corporation in federal court under § 1332, the plaintiff cannot be a citizen of any state where the corporation is a citizen. In our example above, a federal court would have subject matter jurisdiction under § 1332 over a lawsuit between Burns Corp. and a citizen of Massachusetts (assuming the AIC is met)—but not one between Burns Corp. and a citizen of New York.

#### Natural persons

Where is a natural person a citizen? *Mas v. Perry* illustrates the test. Tl;dr: A natural person is a citizen of their "domicile"—the state where they intend to remain indefinitely. A useful way of finding someone's domicile is:

1. Start when they're born. That state is their domicile.
2. Follow them as they move. For every state that they enter, do they establish an intent to remain indefinitely? If so, this state is now their domicile. If not, their domicile hasn't changed.

For example, you probably don't intend to remain in Massachusetts after law school. That means your domicile probably hasn't changed to Massachusetts. *Mas* turns on a similar situation. What would it take to make Massachusetts your domicile? We'll discuss that as well.
