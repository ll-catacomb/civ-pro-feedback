# Overview of the Rules of Decision Act (28 U.S.C. § 1652)

## Federal court, state law

Suppose a federal court is deciding a state-law case under diversity jurisdiction. What law should the court apply: federal or state? The answer is easy when the Constitution or a federal statute clearly contradicts the state law. The Constitution says that federal law is the supreme law of the land, so in that case the court must apply the federal law. But what if there's no federal law on point? Must the court apply the state law, or can it create its own law (a federal common law) that it thinks represents a better understanding of how the law should work?

## The Rules of Decision Act: § 1652

Congress confronted this problem in the Rules of Decision Act (§ 1652). The Act says, "The laws of the several states [that is, state law], except where the Constitution . . . or Acts of Congress otherwise require or provide [in other words, unless federal law says otherwise], shall be regarded as rules of decision in civil actions in the courts of the United States [i.e., federal courts], in cases where they apply." In plainer language, the Rules of Decision Act says that if there's no on-point federal law, the federal court must apply on-point state law.
