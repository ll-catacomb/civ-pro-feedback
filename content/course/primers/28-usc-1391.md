# Overview of § 1391(a)–(d)

## What's venue?

While personal jurisdiction limits the states where a plaintiff can sue, venue limits the districts within a state where a plaintiff can sue. For example, personal jurisdiction might allow a plaintiff to sue a defendant only in Texas, while venue might allow suit only in the Northern District of Texas. Together, subject matter jurisdiction, personal jurisdiction, and venue pin down which court system can hear a case where.

How do we decide where venue is proper? Section 1391 gives the answers. Section 1391(a) says so itself: "this section shall govern the venue of all civil actions brought in district courts of the United States." We've seen that personal jurisdiction is based on a combination of where a defendant lives (for general personal jurisdiction) and where the defendant did relevant things (for specific personal jurisdiction). Venue works similarly.

## Venue based on residence: § 1391(b)(1)

One basis of venue is where the defendants live. Section 1391(b)(1) says that venue is proper in "a judicial district in which any defendant resides, if all defendants are residents of the State in which the district is located." Suppose you're suing Bart (who resides in the Northern District of California) and Lisa (who resides in the Southern District of California). Since both defendants reside in the same state, venue is proper under § 1391(b)(1) in the district where either defendant resides. You could therefore sue them together in the Northern or Southern Districts of California (but not the Central District of California).

Conversely, if you want to sue Bart (still a resident of the Northern District of California) and Nelson (who resides in the District of Nevada), not all defendants reside in the same state and so you can't use § 1391(b)(1) to lay venue.

### Where does a defendant reside?

This forces us to ask: What does it mean for a defendant to "reside" somewhere? Section 1391(c) answers. A natural person resides in the district where they're domiciled: "a natural person . . . shall be deemed to reside in the judicial district in which that person is domiciled." This requires us to do the same domicile analysis we did for § 1332 but at the district instead of the state level. Corporations and unincorporated associations, meanwhile, reside in the districts that could, if they were states, exercise personal jurisdiction over them: "an entity with the capacity to sue and be sued in its common name under applicable law, whether or not incorporated, shall be deemed to reside, if a defendant, in any judicial district in which such defendant is subject to the court's personal jurisdiction with respect to the civil action in question."

Suppose you want to sue Hibbert Corp., incorporated in Manhattan with its principal place of business in Cambridge. If the Southern District of New York and the District of Massachusetts were states, Hibbert Corp. would be subject to personal jurisdiction there. It therefore resides in those districts, so you could lay venue under § 1391(b)(1) in either one of them.

## Venue based on events: § 1391(b)(2)

Section 1391(b)(2) also allows a plaintiff to lay venue in the district where "a substantial part of the events or omissions giving rise to the claim occurred," or, if the suit is in rem or quasi in rem, where "a substantial part of the property that is the subject of the action is" located. Multiple districts might qualify. For example, if a corporation manufactures a product in the Eastern District of Michigan that injures a user in the Northern District of Texas, a substantial part of the events giving rise to the claim occurred in both districts. The user could therefore lay venue under § 1391(b)(2) in either district.

## Fallback venue: § 1391(b)(3)

What if no district satisfies § 1391(b)(1) or (b)(2)? You can still lay venue under the fallback provision, § 1391(b)(3). In that case, the plaintiff can lay venue in any district in which any defendant is subject to personal jurisdiction: "if there is no district in which an action may otherwise be brought as provided in this section, [a civil action may be brought in] any judicial district in which any defendant is subject to the court's personal jurisdiction with respect to such action."

So, for example, suppose that Edna (who resides in the District of Massachusetts) and Smithers (who resides in the District of Maryland) beat up Skinner on their spring-break trip to Cancun. Edna and Smithers don't reside in the same state, so Skinner can't lay venue under § 1391(b)(1). And the events giving rise to the claim didn't take place in any district of the United States, foreclosing venue under § 1391(b)(2). But Skinner must be able to lay venue somewhere, so § 1391(b)(3) kicks in as a fallback. Under its terms, Skinner could lay venue in either the District of Massachusetts (where Edna is subject to personal jurisdiction) or the District of Maryland (where Smithers is).

But remember that to hear the merits of a case, courts must be able to lay venue and have personal jurisdiction over all defendants. So, for example, if Skinner sued Edna and Smithers in the District of Maryland, venue would be proper for both defendants, but Edna could dismiss for lack of personal jurisdiction. To satisfy both venue and personal jurisdiction, Skinner would need to sue Edna and Smithers separately—Edna in the District of Massachusetts, Smithers in the District of Maryland.
