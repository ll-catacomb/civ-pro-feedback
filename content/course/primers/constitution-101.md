# The Constitution 101

(Or: What you might have, or would have, learned in a U.S. civics class.)

## Overview of the Constitution

The Constitution establishes the most basic laws of the United States. The Constitution is a "superlaw" in that any other kind of law inconsistent with it is invalid (although who gets to decide what is inconsistent with the Constitution is up for debate). The Constitution includes:

- A preamble
- Seven articles
- Twenty-seven amendments
  - Amendments 1–10 make up the Bill of Rights
  - Amendments 11–27 are not part of the Bill of Rights, but they're still important

Some parts of the Constitution are self-executing: their existence creates law. For example, Article III created the Supreme Court, so there had to be a Supreme Court. Congress didn't get to decide whether there would be a Supreme Court (although, as few people know, Congress does set the number of justices that sit on it).

Some parts of the Constitution aren't self-executing: they require legislation to give them effect. For example, Article III grants Congress the authority to *create* lower federal courts, but Congress had to enact legislation to actually create those courts. The first Congress in fact did so, and there have always been lower federal courts. But Congress didn't have to do so.

**Key constitutional structures:**

**Federalism** (implied from structure, including 10th Amendment): federalism is concerned with the relationship and balance of powers between the central (federal) government and the states. Speaking generally, a "federation" or a "confederation" refers to a group of political entities bound together by some kind of governing document. The Constitution is that binding document for the United States: its ratification bound together the pre-existing state governments. But it did more than that (indeed, the major reason the Constitution was adopted was because the Articles of Confederation, which had previously bound the states together, were thought to have created too little centralized structure and power): the Constitution also created a federal government that had the power to act directly upon, and interact directly with, the people. For example, the federal government can tax directly; it doesn't have to ask the states to tax on its behalf.

The Constitution enumerates the specific powers of the federal government it created, such as levying taxes, declaring war, and regulating interstate and foreign commerce. The 10th Amendment specifies that powers the Constitution does not grant to the federal government or prohibit to the state governments are reserved for the state governments. Most thinking people now believe that, despite the 10th Amendment, the federal government's enumerated powers have been interpreted so broadly that there are few interesting constitutionally enforceable limits on the federal government's power to act. So federalism is now thought to be a doctrine of prudence, in the sense that the federal government owes state governments (including state courts) due respect.

A note on the evolution of federalism: originally, the federalist cause was progressive. The Federalist Papers (a set of essays written by James Madison, Alexander Hamilton, and John Jay to persuade the people of New York to ratify the newly proposed Constitution) themselves were an effort to convince people to take a chance on creating a centralized authority that was not dependent on the states. This was a big step at the time. That said, at the time of the adoption of the Constitution in 1787, the state governments arguably retained as much power as did the federal government, perhaps more. Over time, the balance of power between federal and state governments shifted (an enormous shift occurred as a result of the Civil War), as the federal government grew in size and scope beyond anything that anyone could have imagined in 1787. The federalist movement changed with it. Modern federalism is often considered a conservative movement because its proponents (like the Federalist Societies found on most law school campuses) want to strengthen the rights of states relative to the federal government, which they claim is more consistent with the views of the founding fathers.

**Separation of powers** (implied from structure: Articles I, II, III): separation of powers refers to the structure of governance mandated by the Constitution, wherein the three separate branches of the government have separate responsibilities. Article I enumerates the powers (and some limitations) of the legislative branch, Congress. The vesting clause of Article II grants executive power to the President and outlines some specific tasks that fall to him or her as head of the executive branch, including the power/duty to "take care" that the laws Congress passes are faithfully executed. Article III creates the Supreme Court and grants Congress the power to create lower federal courts. As noted above, Congress exercised that power immediately (*i.e.*, the very first Congress created lower federal courts).

The structure of the Constitution (as interpreted by ensuing cases) suggests that branches are to exercise their powers separately and check one another in their interpretations of law. Thus, separation of powers is what drives our system of checks and balances. As was true of federalism, however, our modern understanding of separation of powers has departed so substantially from that envisioned in 1787 that few if any of the founding generation would recognize the doctrine today. The primary development on this score has been the emergence of an enormous administrative state with huge agency structures, some of which have legislative, executive, and adjudicative functions all within a single agency. Some of these agencies are nominally within the Executive Branch. Some of them, called "independent" agencies, are nominally under no branch of government.

**Due Process Clause** (the 5th and 14th Amendments): both the 5th and 14th amendments contain due process clauses. The 5th Amendment covers the federal government, whereas the 14th Amendment refers to the states. These clauses determine that no person shall be "deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law." The two main types of due process rights are procedural due process (standards to ensure fairness in legal procedures, such as requiring a certain amount of notice for parties to lawsuits), and substantive due process (determining the areas into which the government can intervene). An example of a procedural due process right is the right to have the plaintiff (or the state) notify you of judicial proceedings that could affect your rights. An example of a substantive due process right is the right to have an abortion during the first trimester of a pregnancy.

**Equality** norm (the 14th Amendment, 5th Amendment's Due Process Clause): the 14th Amendment includes the Equal Protection Clause, providing that no state shall deprive any person of "equal protection of the laws." An example of the use of the 14th Amendment's equality norm: segregation was deemed acceptable under the Equal Protection Clause in *Plessy v. Ferguson* in 1896. In 1954 the Supreme Court overturned *Plessy* with its decision in *Brown v. Board of Education*, finding that the principle of "separate but equal" violated the Equal Protection Clause.

The Due Process Clause of the 5th Amendment has also been invoked in an equality-ensuring capacity: in *United States v. Windsor*, the Supreme Court found that denying the plaintiff a tax refund on the basis of her legally-recognized marriage to a woman was an unconstitutional deprivation of property rights, violating the Due Process Clause.

Note: an equality norm demands that the law treat "like" fact patterns "alike." But nothing in the Equal Protection Clause (or its 5th Amendment counterpart) specifies the criteria by which fact patterns are to be judged "like" or "different." Deciding whether two fact patterns are "like" or "different" requires a theory external to the text of the Constitution.

## Relevance for Civil Procedure

**The Supreme Court**

Article III created the Supreme Court.

**Creation of Lower Federal Courts**

Article III granted Congress the power to create lower federal courts, and although (as noted above) the provision wasn't self-executing, Congress immediately created lower federal courts. Initially, Congress gave the lower federal courts diversity jurisdiction but not arising under jurisdiction (you'll learn what these terms mean in the first week of Civil Procedure). Congress did not give the lower federal courts what we now think of as arising under jurisdiction until the late 1800s.

**Relationship with State Courts**

While federal courts only have original jurisdiction over a specific subset of cases, the Supreme Court has certiorari power (the discretionary power to hear appeals) over cases that arise through the federal courts *and* cases that arise through the state courts.

**Provisions Providing Congress with Power to Legislate, as Relevant to Civil Procedure**

**Commerce Clause:** Article I contains a clause granting Congress the power to "regulate Commerce with foreign nations, and among the several states, and with the Indian tribes." This clause is the basis of Congress's authority to involve itself in commercial activity.

**Article III, power to create the lower federal courts:** Congress' power to create the lower federal courts implies certain other powers, such as to regulate what happens within those federal courts. For example, in 1934 Congress passed the Rules Enabling Act which established a process eventually resulting in the creation the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (FRCPs). The FRCPs are a set of rules governing civil procedure in the federal court system. Previously, federal courts adopted the rules of procedure of the states in which they sat, but the REA process resulted in standardized procedure across all federal courts. (The significance of this will become apparent when discussing choice of law and the Erie doctrine.)

**Full Faith and Credit Clause:** this provision of Article IV requires states to respect the laws and judgments effectuated in other states. It is particularly relevant when a party seeks to enforce a judgment issued in a first state in the courts of a second state. Because of the FFCC, one state court must respect another state court's judgments, so the law of the state of the court that issued the judgment "follows" the judgment from place to place.

**Necessary and Proper Clause:** this provision of Article I grants Congress the power to make what laws they deem necessary to execute their responsibilities. It is often used in conjunction with the Commerce Clause, *i.e.* justifying New Deal-era reforms about interstate commerce, and even criminal law, *i.e.* promulgating laws around kidnapping by framing it as a form of interstate commerce. Taken in conjunction with the Commerce Clause, the Necessary and Proper Clause allows Congress to regulate purely *intrastate* commerce as long as its rationale for doing so is as a means of regulating *interstate* commerce.
