# Civil Procedure Glossary

## Filing a Civil Suit 101

**Plaintiff** — the party that files the suit in court (there are no plaintiffs in criminal cases)

**Defendant** — the party that is being sued

**Pro Se** — a litigant who is representing himself or herself without an attorney

**Relief** — the redress (whether money damages or a specific type of action) the plaintiff is seeking from the lawsuit

**A party can move the court or file a motion** — a formal request to the court asking for a favorable ruling or order requiring some action to be taken that is beneficial to the movant (i.e., applicant)

**Pleadings** — any formal written statement by a party and filed in court that states the claims or defenses of that particular party (ex. complaint, answer, counter claims). Generally, the pleadings will define the issues that will be argued during the case.

## How Courts Work

**Trial Court** — For the majority of issues, the trial court is the first place where a case will be heard and adjudicated. For this reason, trial courts are referred to as "courts of original jurisdiction" and will hear all civil and criminal cases.

**Appellate Court** — Courts that have the power to review trial court rulings and orders and check for errors. Appellate Courts will "affirm" the trial court's decision, "overturn" it, or "remand" it (send it back to the trial court) for further investigation.

**Appeal** — After a trial court issues a decision, the losing party can formally request a higher court to analyze the decision in the hopes of getting a different outcome.

**Appellant** — party appealing the trial court's decision

**Appellee** — party opposing or responding to the appeal

A judge will write an opinion explaining how a final decision on a case was reached and the legal principles involved.

**Holding** — the court's final decision reached as a matter of law on the specific facts and issue in question. The holding will have binding authority as "precedent" (see below) going forward.

**Dictum** — any discussion in a court opinion that is not directly related to the grounds upon which the court decides the case before it. Dictum is not binding "precedent," but can be used as persuasive authority.

**Precedent** — the holding portion of a court's opinion establishes a "precedent." In a common law judicial system (like that in the United States), later courts consider themselves to be usually bound to follow the rules established in prior precedents, even if they believe one or more of those rules to be wrong (i.e., the prior case to have been wrongly decided).

**Stare decisis** — "To stand by things decided." The presumption in our judicial system that judges are obligated to respect and follow precedent.

**Sua Sponte** — "of one's own will," describes when the court takes an action or makes a decision on its own without a party filing a motion or making a specific request. Ex. a court can dismiss a case at any time "sua sponte" if it finds it does not have proper jurisdiction.

## Naming Convention

Trial Courts name cases with the following convention: Plaintiff v. Defendant.

Some Appellate Courts also use Plaintiff v. Defendant and others use Appellant (which can be either the Plaintiff or Defendant) v. Appellee. The United States Supreme Court uses Petitioner (the party that first asked the Court to review the case) and Respondent (the other party).

Thus, only in a trial court can one be sure of the names of the plaintiff and the defendant by looking at the name of the case. In a trial court, a case name of "*Smith v. Jones*" means that Smith is the plaintiff and Jones is the defendant. In any other court, you don't know because either party could be appealing an issue.

## Federal Court System

An independent court system that can handle both state and federal law claims, but has limited jurisdiction, meaning only some claims can be brought in federal court. (State courts also hear federal and state law claims but have broader authority to hear cases.) Federal judges are appointed by the President with the "Advice and Consent" of the Senate, and can only be impeached (removed from their position) for failing to exhibit "good Behaviour." To impeach a federal judge, the House of Representatives must vote with a simple majority to impeach the judge, and the Senate must hold a trial and vote by supermajority (2/3rd) to convict the judge. (See Article I of Constitution.) State judges can be elected or appointed depending on state laws.

**Federalism** — relationship and balance of power between federal and state governments

**Separation of Powers** — idea that each branch of the government (executive, legislative, and judicial) has a set of unique responsibilities and powers that allows them to check and restrain the powers of other branches to prevent an abuse of power

**Department of Justice (DOJ)** — the DOJ's main responsibility is to represent or defend the government in a criminal or civil suit. US Attorneys (Federal Prosecutors) are part of the DOJ and are responsible for prosecuting federal crimes throughout the country.

**The United States Supreme Court** consists of the Chief Justice and eight associate justices. Parties file petitions asking the Supreme Court to hear their case. SCOTUS will then choose whether or not to grant Certiorari ("Cert") by selecting which cases they will hear in the upcoming term (roughly 75–80 of around 10,000 cases each year). Usually these cases involve complicated issues relating to the Constitution or federal policies, and might be chosen because circuit courts have differed in their resolutions of the issue (this is called a circuit split).

**Petitioner** — the party filing the "petition for certiorari" asking SCOTUS to hear the appeal and review the case

**Respondent** — the party responding to the appeal and petition for certiorari

**US Court of Appeals for the __ Circuit** — This is the intermediate level appellate court in the federal court system. Circuit courts have appellate jurisdiction over the US district courts within their jurisdiction, which means they will hear any appeals filed on decisions made by those district courts judges. Circuit court judges hear cases in panels of three judges and one judge will be responsible for writing the opinion of the court. However, it is possible for there to be a dissenting opinion if a judge on the panel feels the case should have been resolved differently. A majority of judges in a particular circuit can also vote to hear a case *en banc*, which means the case will be heard or reheard by all the judges of the court rather than a panel. This is rare and may only be granted to maintain uniformity of the court's decisions or in cases of exceptional importance.

**US District Court** — This is the trial court of the federal court system. District courts will hear federal civil and criminal cases, but the court must first determine they have proper jurisdiction to hear the case. A state, depending on its size and population, can have a single district court or multiple district courts within it.

## Basic Steps in a Civil Lawsuit

(Details will be fleshed out throughout the semester.)

1. Plaintiff files a complaint outlining the reasons she believes she was "wronged" and the type and amount of relief she is seeking.
2. Court has the opportunity to review the complaint and can dismiss it for a variety of reasons.
3. Plaintiff needs to "serve" the defendant to let him know of the lawsuit by providing him a copy of the complaint and a copy of the summons (order from the court requiring the defendant's presence at court).
4. Defendant responds with the "Answer" which is a formal written statement responding to the complaint by raising objections or defenses.
5. After a scheduling order (court designated deadlines for the next phases of the case) determined by the court, the litigants enter the discovery period to gather evidence, take depositions (attorneys question witness while they are under oath and the whole event is transcribed), and learn information to use against the opposing party.

## Federal Court Structure

```
SCOTUS
  |
US Court of Appeals for the ___ Circuit
  = 12 Regional Circuit Courts (1st–11th + DC Circuit)
    and the Federal Circuit (handles specialized issues like patent disputes)
  |
US District Courts
  = 94 Judicial Districts across the United States
    (states can have a single district court or multiple district courts
    depending on the size and population of the state)
```
