# Overview of 28 U.S.C. § 1738

## Intersystem preclusion

We've seen how preclusion works within a single court system—say, if Lawsuit 1 and Lawsuit 2 both occur in Massachusetts state court. But how does preclusion work across different court systems—say, if Lawsuit 1 was in Massachusetts state court and Lawsuit 2 is in California state court?

## The constitutional provision: The Full Faith and Credit Clause

Article IV, Section 1 of the Constitution says that each state's courts must give full faith and credit to the decisions of other state courts: "Full Faith and Credit shall be given in each State to the . . . judicial Proceedings of every other State." This is called the "Full Faith and Credit Clause."

Notice two things about the clause:

1. It applies to exactly one situation: when the court that rendered the judgment in the first case was a state court and the court in which the second lawsuit is filed is also a state court. ("Full Faith and Credit shall be given in each State to . . . the judicial proceedings every other State . . . .") (emphasis added). We can call this situation state-state. But what happens when Lawsuit 1 is in state court and Lawsuit 2 is in federal court (state-federal)? When Lawsuit 1 is in federal court and Lawsuit 2 is in state court (federal-state)? What about federal-federal? The Full Faith and Credit Clause doesn't say.

2. The Full Faith and Credit Clause gave Congress the power to enact a statute to enforce it: "the Congress may by general Laws prescribe the Manner in which such Acts, Records and Proceedings shall be proved, and the Effect thereof."

## The statute: § 1738

On the second point, Congress did enact a statute: § 1738. This statute says that not just state courts, but every court in the country—federal or state—must give full faith and credit to a state's judicial decisions: the "judicial proceedings [of any state] . . . shall have the same full faith and credit in every court within the United States . . . as they have by law or usage in the courts of such State . . . from which they are taken."

Notice two more things:

1. This statute covers both the state-state and the state-federal situations. It covers the effect of "judicial proceedings of" every state, so the rendering court has to be a state court. But it governs that judgment's effect "in every court within the United States," meaning both state and federal courts. In short, § 1738 governs state-state and state-federal. But it doesn't cover federal-state or federal-federal. What happens in the latter two situations? That's for class. Tl;dr: no constitutional provision or statute applies, so we use federal common law.

2. Section 1738 (unlike Article IV of the Constitution) defines what "full faith and credit" means. It means that a rendering court's judgments must have the same effect "as they have . . . in the courts of such State." So suppose Lawsuit 1 is decided by a Massachusetts state court. If a Massachusetts state court would preclude in Lawsuit 2, then so must every other court in the country (in order to give the Massachusetts judgment "the same full faith and credit" as it would have in Massachusetts). "Full faith and credit," in short, means that a judgment carries at least as much preclusive effect in the subsequent court as it would have in the state whose court rendered the decision. To see § 1738 in action, read *Migra*.

## Questions § 1738 doesn't answer

That leaves a couple of questions open.

As suggested above, what if the rendering court is a federal court, not a state court? We've said that federal common law applies, but what does that common law say? Tl;dr it will depend on whether the federal court that rendered the decision in Lawsuit 1 was sitting in diversity or whether it had AUJ. *Semtek* shows the former rule, *Parklane Hosiery* the latter. We'll talk about this in class.

Could a subsequent jurisdiction give more preclusive effect than the rendering jurisdiction would? For example, suppose Lawsuit 1 was decided in Hawaii state court, while Lawsuit 2 is filed in Florida state court. The Full Faith and Credit Clause and § 1738 say that if a Hawaii court would preclude in Lawsuit 2, then the Florida court must preclude (that's what it means to give "full faith and credit" to the Hawaii judgment). But what if a Hawaii court wouldn't preclude? Can a Florida court still preclude if it wants to? We'll discuss this, too, in class.
