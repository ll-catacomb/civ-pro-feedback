# Notice and Service of Process

This document explains the relationship between the Due Process Clause's notice requirement and the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure's service of process requirements.

The constitutional notice requirement and the statutory service of process requirements concern the same element of a suit: the plaintiff and the state must make some effort to notify the defendant that a suit has been filed against him. This effort to notify must be made before adverse consequences can be imposed on the defendant.

## Constitutional Requirements for Notice

The Due Process Clause requires that parties be afforded procedural due process, including notice and an opportunity to be heard. As the Court wrote in *Mullane*, "An elementary and fundamental requirement of due process…is notice reasonably calculated, under all the circumstances, to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections." *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank*, 339 U.S. 306, 314 (1950). The Due Process Clause does *not* require actual notice to the defendant; it requires only that the plaintiff and the state make reasonable efforts to give notice. Operationalize the *Mullane* standard by considering what the plaintiff would need to do if he/she *actually wanted* to notify the defendant without going to extraordinary effort.

## Rule-Based Requirements for Service of Process

Service of process is the term used to describe the delivery of the legal documents (usually the summons and the complaint) that notify the defendant he/she has been sued and informs him/her that the court is asserting its jurisdiction over the controversy.

For a lawsuit to proceed in a federal court, the plaintiff (sometimes assisted by a court officer, often called a "sheriff") must serve process in accordance with the requirements imposed by statute or rule. If service of process is not made properly yet the defendant finds out about the suit through other means, he/she can move to dismiss the suit for insufficient service of process. Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(5). Keep your eye on this because it's counterintuitive — a court will dismiss a lawsuit, even if the defendant in fact knows about the lawsuit, because the plaintiff failed to meet the service of process rules (which are, in fact, designed to assure that the defendant finds out about the lawsuit).

Rule 4 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure governs the service of process in federal courts. It addresses what documents must be served on the defendant, who may serve the defendant, how and when the papers must be served, and how the service requirement can be waived (which we don't cover):

**Serving process on an individual: Rule 4(e)**

- Under Rule 4(e)(1), an individual may be served in accordance with the rules of the state in which the federal court is located or the state where the service is made. In other words, if the state says it's okay to serve process by some method, then that's fine for the relevant federal court.
- Alternatively, under Rule 4(e)(2), one can serve process by:
  - (A) Delivering copies of the summons and the complaint to the defendant;
  - (B) By leaving copies of those documents at the defendant's dwelling with someone who lives there and is of suitable age;
  - (C) Or by delivering copies to an agent appointed by the defendant or authorized by law to receive service of process.

**Serving process on a corporation, a partnership, or an unincorporated association: Rule 4(h)**

- Rule 4(h)(1) governs if the corporation is served in a U.S. judicial district (i.e., in the country). That's all we study here in Civil Procedure (read the rule if you want to learn about service outside the U.S.).
  - (A) Service must be made in accordance with the state laws where the federal court sits or where process is served. In other words, if the state says it's okay to serve process by some method, then that's fine for the relevant federal court;
  - (B) Or it must be made by the delivery (by hand, like a tag) of the summons and the complaint to an officer, a managing or general agent, or other authorized agent of the corporation. Mail delivery to the defendant may also be necessary, if the statute requires it.

## Relationship Between the Constitutional and Statutory Requirements

As these details show, Rule 4 requires more effort by the plaintiff and the state for a service of process to be valid than what is called for by the Due Process Clause. The Due Process Clause sets the floor, and the Federal Rules substantially heighten the requirements. Put another way, the Constitution establishes a standard of 'reasonable efforts,' and the Federal Rules provide a stricter list of specific dos and don'ts.

See the chart below for two other topic areas in which the relationship between constitutional and statutory requirements is the same in the sense that the Constitution sets a floor, and some statute or rule requires 'more.'

## Service of Process and Personal Jurisdiction

Remember that notice and service of process are different from personal jurisdiction:

- Notice and service of process refer to the efforts that must be made to notify the defendant about the suit against him/her.
- Personal jurisdiction refers to the power of a court over the parties in a case. It gives the court the authority to make judgments to which the parties are bound.

These concepts are sometimes confused because prior to the Great Depression, service of process was one method (and under *Pennoyer*, the only method) of establishing personal jurisdiction. However, the Supreme Court articulated new requirements for personal jurisdiction in 1945 in *International Shoe Co. v. Washington*. *International Shoe Co. v. Washington*, 326 U.S. 310 (1945). The *International Shoe* Court held that a court could exercise jurisdiction over an out-of-state defendant as long as the defendant had sufficient minimum contacts with the state which made the exercise of jurisdiction fair and reasonable. Most courts continued to believe, however, that service of process ("tagging") in a state could still be used to establish personal jurisdiction after *International Shoe*. In *Burnham v. Superior Court*, 495 U.S. 604 (1990), the Court split 4-4-1 (around Justice Stevens) in deciding whether service of process on a nonresident defendant inside the state's geographic area was enough to establish personal jurisdiction.

Note that today, for a suit to proceed, the constitutional and statutory requirements for notice and service of process must be met and the court must have personal jurisdiction. The two requirements may compel the plaintiff to prove different things and it is possible for one to be established without the other.
