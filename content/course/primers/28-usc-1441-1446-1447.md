# Overview of the Removal Statutes (28 U.S.C. §§ 1441(a)–(c), 1446–47)

We've seen that if a case creates subject matter jurisdiction, the plaintiff may file in federal court. But if a plaintiff chooses to file a case with SMJ in state court, can the defendant move it to federal court? Yes—through "removal." Removal doesn't expand subject matter jurisdiction. That is, it doesn't create new kinds of cases that can be heard in federal court. Instead, it ensures that in cases for which there is SMJ, a federal forum is available to both plaintiffs and defendants. The statutes discussed here govern when defendants can remove a case to federal court, how they can remove the case, and what happens if they remove a case they weren't allowed to.

## When can you remove? § 1441(a)–(c)

### Creating removal: § 1441(a)

Section 1441(a) creates the option of removal. It says that if a plaintiff files a case in state court that creates SMJ, then the defendant(s) can remove it to federal court: "any civil action brought in a State court of which the district courts of the United States have original jurisdiction, may be removed by the defendant or the defendants, to the district court."

### Limiting the removal of diversity-only cases: § 1441(b)

Diversity jurisdiction exists to protect out-of-state parties from in-state bias. So if a defendant is from the state whose courts are hearing the case, they have less need to remove the case to federal court. Section 1441(b)(2) accordingly bars in-state defendants from removing cases whose only basis of SMJ is diversity. In diversity-only cases, if any defendant is a citizen of a state where the case is being brought, then the defendants cannot remove the case: "A civil action otherwise removable solely on the basis of the jurisdiction under section 1332(a) of this title may not be removed if any of the parties in interest properly joined and served as defendants is a citizen of the State in which such action is brought."

For example, suppose that Lisa (MA citizen) sues Milhouse (VT citizen) and Ralph (NH citizen) in Vermont state court under a federal cause of action. We have AUJ, so this is not a diversity-only case, so Defendants can remove it to federal court.

But if Lisa were to sue Milhouse and Ralph for breach of contract, a state-law claim, the only basis of SMJ would be diversity. Since Milhouse is a citizen of the state whose courts would hear this case, § 1441(b)(2) bars the defendants from removal.

## How do defendants remove a case? § 1446(a)

So how do defendants remove a case? Section 1446 lays out the requirements. Section 1446(a) says that to remove a case currently in state court, defendants file a notice of removal in district court: "A defendant or defendants desiring to remove any civil action from a State court shall file in the district court of the United States . . . a notice of removal." Once the defendant files this notice, the case is immediately removed.

## What are the requirements for removing a case?

Because the case is immediately removed once a defendant files a notice of removal, if any party thinks the case shouldn't have been removed, they must bring this challenge in the district court. This makes sense. The safeguards that the federal court system creates would be weakened if state courts decided whether you could access a federal court. We therefore want federal courts to decide whether removal was proper.

There might be two reasons why a case shouldn't have been removed:

1. **Jurisdictional reasons:** The case lacked SMJ and so can't be heard in district court.
2. **Procedural reasons:** Even if the case creates SMJ, defendants didn't comply with the procedural requirements for removal.

These procedural requirements are listed in §§ 1446(b)(1), (b)(2), and (c).

### Time limits

**Section 1446(b)(1):** If a case is removable as soon as it is filed. Section 1446(b)(1) says that if a case is removable—that is, if it creates subject matter jurisdiction—as soon as it is filed, defendants must remove the case within 30 days. "The notice of removal of a civil action or proceeding shall be filed within 30 days."

**Section 1446(b)(3):** If a case becomes removable. But sometimes cases only become removable after filing. For example, suppose Krusty (MA citizen) sues Homer (VT citizen) and Marge (MA citizen) in Massachusetts state court under a state-law claim. We don't have complete diversity, so there's no SMJ, so the case isn't removable. But suppose the court dismisses the claim against Marge, leaving Krusty (MA) v. Homer (VT). We now have complete diversity, making the case removable. Section 1446(b)(3) says that in such a case, defendants must remove the case within 30 days after the removable event (here, Marge's dismissal). "[I]f the case stated by the initial pleading is not removable, a notice of removal may be filed within thirty days after receipt by the defendant, through service or otherwise, of a copy of an amended pleading, motion, order or other paper from which it may first be ascertained that the case is one which is or has become removable."

**Section 1446(c):** Time limit on diversity-only cases. If a case becomes removable only because of diversity jurisdiction—as in the last example—then § 1446(c) says the case cannot be removed more than one year after it began. So if, in our example above, Krusty's claim against Marge were dismissed 366 days into the litigation, Homer couldn't remove. "A case may not be removed . . . on the basis of jurisdiction conferred by section 1332 more than 1 year after commencement of the action . . . ."

### Section 1446(b)(2)(A): Defendants' consent

Finally, § 1446(b)(2)(A) says that all defendants must consent to removal. So if (1) Nelson sues Lisa and Maggie, and (2) Maggie doesn't want to remove, then Lisa can't remove their case.

## Challenging an improper removal: § 1447

Suppose a defendant has removed a case pursuant to § 1441. But the removal has violated one of the restrictions in § 1446. How can other parties challenge the removal to get the case back in state court where it belongs? The answers are in § 1447. As we saw in § 1446(a), the case is removed as soon as a defendant files the notice of removal. Challenges to removal will therefore take place before the district court, in the form of motions to remand the case back to state court.

### How long a party has to challenge an improper removal

We've seen that there are two reasons a removal might be improper: lack of SMJ or a procedural defect. How long an objecting party has to challenge an improper removal depends on which of those reasons is present.

**Time limit to raise a procedural defect**

If the problem was procedural, then § 1447(c) requires a party challenging removal to file a motion to remand within 30 days after the case was removed: "A motion to remand the case on the basis of any defect other than lack of subject matter jurisdiction must be made within 30 days after the filing of the notice of removal . . . ."

**(No) time limit to raise a lack of SMJ**

In *Mottley*, we saw that federal courts must consider whether they have SMJ at all times and sua sponte. That includes a case that was removed. Section 1447(c) therefore clarifies that if the removal was improper because of a lack of SMJ, parties can raise that objection at any time, and the court can identify this problem sua sponte and remand on its own. "If at any time before final judgment it appears that the district court lacks subject matter jurisdiction"—notice this doesn't say one of the parties must point this out!— "the case shall be remanded."

### An example

Suppose Barney (MA) sues Moe (VT) and Carl (MA) in Vermont state court under a state-law breach of contract claim. Defendants remove to federal court. There are two problems with this removal:

1. The district court lacks subject matter jurisdiction because there's no AUJ and Barney isn't diverse from Carl, and
2. An in-state defendant removed in violation of § 1441(b)(2) (Moe is a Vermont citizen who removed from Vermont state court).

Barney's lawyer sees the notice of removal as she's leaving the office for a 31-day vacation to Bora Bora—and decides to do nothing. Once she's back in the office, more than 30 days have elapsed since removal. She therefore cannot move to remand on the basis of the procedural defect (removal by an in-state defendant). But she can move to remand for lack of subject matter jurisdiction, which can be raised any time. In fact, because federal courts must examine subject matter jurisdiction sua sponte (as we saw in *Mottley*), Barney's lawyer might return to find that the district court has already noted the lack of subject matter jurisdiction and remanded on its own.
