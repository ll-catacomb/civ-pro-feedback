# Overview of the Rules Enabling Act (28 U.S.C. §§ 2071–72)

## Who makes the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure?

You may have noticed by now that a lot of cases turn on the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. You may have also wondered: Where do the Rules come from? Your answer is in the Rules Enabling Act, of which §§ 2071 and 2072 are part.

The Supreme Court promulgates the Rules, per § 2071(a) ("The Supreme Court and all courts established by Act of Congress may from time to time prescribe rules for the conduct of their business.") and § 2072(a) ("The Supreme Court shall have the power to prescribe general rules of practice and procedure . . . for cases in the United States district courts . . . and courts of appeals.")

## What does it take for a Rule to be valid?

Normally in a democracy, we don't let the Supreme Court pass laws. Even if you read that last sentence and laughed, it's obvious that the Supreme Court couldn't issue a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure raising your taxes or ordering the Marines to invade Canada. So there must be some limits to what kinds of things the Rules can do. What are they?

The limit is § 2072(b): "Such rules shall not abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right."

Questions: What does that mean? And what does it have to do with *Erie*? We'll talk about both questions in class. As for the relationship between the Federal Rules and *Erie*, read *Hanna v. Plumer* and the related cases. Tl;dr: All federal law—including the Rules—overrides state law as the supreme law of the land so long as it's (1) valid and (2) applicable to the case at hand. We've seen that a Rule is valid if it doesn't "abridge, enlarge, or modify any substantive right." When a Rule is applicable is more contested, as we'll discuss in class.
