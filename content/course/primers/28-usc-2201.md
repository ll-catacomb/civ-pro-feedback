# Overview of 28 U.S.C. § 2201(a)

## What's a declaratory judgment?

In most lawsuits, the plaintiff is seeking something from the defendant—say, money damages or a court order compelling the defendant to do (or to refrain from doing) something. But sometimes, a plaintiff only wants a court to declare what the parties' legal rights or duties are. This is called a "declaratory judgment." 28 U.S.C. § 2201(a) authorizes federal courts ("any court of the United States") to issue declaratory judgments ("upon the filing of an appropriate pleading"—i.e., if the plaintiff seeks a declaratory judgment—a federal court "may declare the rights and other legal relations of any interested party seeking such a declaration"). Note that this declaration might be the only thing the plaintiff is seeking (courts can issue declaratory judgments "whether or not further relief is or could be sought").

## What does a declaratory judgment action look like? Why would anyone want one?

Suppose I sell you a car whose gas tank explodes, leaving you with severe burns. I'm worried that you're going to sue me for money damages. We'll call your potential suit against me the "imaginary lawsuit."

I can make the first move by seeking a declaratory judgment—suing you and asking the court to declare that I'm not liable. Why would I do this? Suppose you could sue me in Massachusetts or Vermont (we'll get into where you can sue someone in a later class). And suppose that Vermont law—and only Vermont law—says it was your duty to inspect the car and so I wouldn't be liable. Also suppose that a Vermont court would use Vermont law to decide our case (we'll get to choice of law later in the semester). I might sue you for a declaratory judgment in Vermont, hope the Vermont court declares that I'm not liable, then try to use that declaration to fend off your suit. ("Any such declaration shall have the force and effect of a final judgment . . . .").

## The question: subject-matter jurisdiction

We saw in *Mottley* that arising-under jurisdiction (AUJ) typically depends on whether you're suing me under a federal cause of action. So how does AUJ work in declaratory-judgment suits?

For the answer, read *First Federal Savings & Loan Ass'n v. McReynolds*. Tl;dr we use the following checklist:

1. Find the lawsuit the declaratory-judgment action is seeking to fend off (the "imaginary lawsuit")
2. Would the imaginary lawsuit create AUJ?
   - **Yes** → there's AUJ over the declaratory-judgment suit
   - **No** → there isn't AUJ over the declaratory-judgment suit
