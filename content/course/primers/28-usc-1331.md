# Overview of Article III, §§ 1–2; 28 U.S.C. § 1331

## The constitutional limit of arising-under jurisdiction: Article III

At the Founding, nearly all governing was done by states. That included deciding court cases — if you wanted to sue someone, you could do it in state court. The Framers wanted states to continue to govern most of our lives, so when they created the federal judiciary in the Constitution, they limited its reach. The Constitution doesn't even require there to be any federal courts other than the Supreme Court. Instead, Article III, section 1 authorizes Congress to create lower federal courts — if Congress wants to. As it turned out, Congress did want to, and it immediately created district courts in which you could file a lawsuit. But, thanks to the Framers' federalist vision, district courts can only hear a specific subset of cases. What's in this subset? That's the subject of our first unit, on so-called subject matter jurisdiction.

The Constitution lays out the maximum extent of what cases district courts can decide. Article III, section 1 authorizes Congress to invest district courts with "the judicial power of the United States," while section 2 says that the judicial power includes "all Cases . . . arising under this Constitution [and] the Laws of the United States" (that is, federal laws). Putting those sections together, Article III authorizes Congress to grant district courts authority to decide cases "arising under" under the Constitution or federal law.

Question: What does it mean for a case to "arise under" federal law for purposes of the Constitution? That is, what's the maximum extent of cases that this provision says Congress can authorize district courts to hear? *Osborn* discusses this question (although its discussion is not binding since it's "dictum"). We'll talk about what *Osborn* says in class. The tl;dr version: The Constitution allows Congress to grant district courts jurisdiction that's probably really broad.

## The arising-under jurisdiction Congress could grant

But while the Constitution authorizes Congress to grant district courts the power to hear cases "arising under" federal law, it doesn't require Congress to grant that power. Congress could, if it wanted to, decline to let district courts hear any of these cases (Option 1 in the pictures below). It could also, by passing a statute, grant district courts the power to hear only some of these cases (Option 2). Or it could pass another statute authorizing lower courts to hear all of these cases (Option 3).

- **Option 1:** Congress declines to grant lower federal courts the power to hear any cases the Constitution says "arise under" federal law.
- **Option 2:** Congress grants lower federal courts the power to hear some of the cases the Constitution says "arise under" federal law.
- **Option 3:** Congress grants lower federal courts the power to hear all cases the Constitution says "arise under" federal law.

## The arising-under jurisdiction Congress did grant: § 1331

The statute Congress actually passed is 28 U.S.C. § 1331, which grants the district courts "original jurisdiction" (meaning you can file a lawsuit in this court) over "all civil actions" (i.e., civil lawsuits) "arising under the Constitution [or] laws . . . of the United States."

Question: What does it mean for a case to "arise under" federal law for purposes of § 1331? Is that different from what it means for a case to "arise under" federal law for purposes of the Constitution?

The basic answer is in *Mottley*, with some nuance added in *Smith* and *Grable*. The tl;dr version: According to *Mottley*, Congress went with Option 2 in § 1331, giving district courts only some of the arising-under jurisdiction that the Constitution allows. We'll put together *Osborn*, *Mottley*, *Smith*, and *Grable* in class. We'll see that the picture looks like:

- Constitutionally allowed arising-under jurisdiction (*Osborn*) — broader
- Arising-under jurisdiction under § 1331 (*Mottley*, *Smith*, *Grable*) — narrower subset

When we talk in class, listen for the "well-pleaded complaint rule" that comes out of *Mottley*. In a nutshell, it means that your case "arises under" federal law under § 1331 — allowing you to file it in federal court — only if your right to sue comes from federal law (in the language we'll learn, if you have a federal cause of action). A lot of things that you might know you can sue someone for — like breach of contract, negligence, or trespass — only create the right to sue under state, not federal law (i.e., they're state-law causes of action). That means they don't "arise under" federal law for purposes of § 1331, and so you typically can't use § 1331 to file those lawsuits in federal court. That's a summary of the rule from *Mottley*. *Smith* and *Grable* create an exception. Tl;dr: Even if you're suing a defendant under a state-law cause of action, if a court cannot decide the state-law claim without also deciding an issue of federal law, then you might be able to file in federal court.

## A quick checklist to start putting all these cases together

1. Look at the complaint. Is the plaintiff suing under a federal or state cause of action?
   - **Federal** → AUJ under § 1331 (*Mottley*)
   - **State** → Is the state-law cause of action so intertwined with an issue of federal law that a court cannot decide the state-law cause of action without deciding the federal issue?
     - **Yes** → Maybe AUJ under § 1331 (*Smith*-*Grable* exception)
     - **No** → No AUJ under § 1331
