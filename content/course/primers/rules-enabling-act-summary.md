# Rules Enabling Act Summary

## How the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure Are Amended

Together, 28 U.S.C. § 2071 and § 2072 constitute the Rules Enabling Act of 1934. These provisions empower the Supreme Court to promulgate the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. The Supreme Court delegated the role of drafting the Rules to an Advisory Committee, which drafted the original Rules. Today, advisory committees continue to propose amendments to the Rules in accordance with the process outlined below. This seven-step process by which the Rules are amended looks first like an administrative rulemaking (steps 1–5), then like judge-made common law (step 6), and then legislation (step 7). As we will discuss in class, how one characterizes the rules (rulemaking, common law, or legislation) matters in terms of how one should think about them for Erie purposes.

**Step 1: Initial Consideration by the Advisory Committee**

- Proposed changes to the rules are suggested by judges, clerks of court, lawyers, professors, government agencies, or other individuals and organizations.
- These suggestions are considered by the appropriate advisory committee (appellate, bankruptcy, civil, criminal, or evidence).
- Advisory committees are composed of federal judges, practicing lawyers, law professors, state chief justices, representatives of the Department of Justice, and a reporter (usually a prominent law professor).

**Step 2: Publication and Public Comment**

- Once an advisory committee votes to recommend an amendment to the rules, it must obtain the approval of the Standing Committee to publish the proposed amendment for public comment.
- The Standing Committee is composed of federal judges, practicing lawyers, law professors, state chief justices, representatives of the Department of Justice, and an academic reporter.
- With the Standing Committee's approval, the proposed amendment is published for a 6-month period of public comment.

**Step 3: Consideration of the public comments and final approval by the advisory committee**

- The advisory committee takes another look at the proposed rule changes in light of the public comments. If the advisory committee decides to make a substantial change, it may provide for an additional period of public comment.
- The advisory committee submits its proposed amendment to the Standing Committee.

**Step 4: Approval by the Standing Committee**

- The Standing Committee considers the final recommendations of the advisory committee. If the Standing Committee approves a proposed rule change, it transmits it to the Judicial Conference with a recommendation for approval.

**Step 5: Judicial Conference Approval**

- The Judicial Conference considers proposed amendments to the rules at its annual September session. Amendments it approves are transmitted to the Supreme Court.
- The Judicial Conference is comprised of the chief judge of each judicial circuit, the Chief Judge of the Court of International Trade, and a district judge from each regional judicial circuit. The Chief Justice of the United States is the presiding officer of the Judicial Conference.

**Step 6: Supreme Court approval**

- The Supreme Court has the authority to prescribe the federal rules, subject to a statutory waiting period. 28 U.S.C. §§ 2072, 2075. The Court must transmit proposed amendments to Congress by May 1 of the year in which the amendment is to take effect. 28 U.S.C. §§ 2074, 2075. The Court votes in its usual format to decide what to transmit (meaning a five-justice majority is required).

**Step 7: Congressional review**

- The Congress has a statutory period of at least 7 months to act on any rules prescribed by the Supreme Court. If the Congress does not enact legislation to reject, modify, or defer the rules, they take effect as a matter of law on December 1. 28 U.S.C. §§ 2074, 2075.

## Why Does This Matter?

*Erie*, *Stewart*, *Hanna*, and *Walker* together suggest that courts should treat federal statutes differently from federal common law in diversity cases. The problem is, the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure are part statute, part common law (and part administrative rule). So, how do courts treat the Rules for Erie purposes?

**The Erie Doctrine: No Federal General Common Law**

In *Erie*, the Court held that in diversity cases, federal courts must use state substantive law. "Except in matters governed by the Federal Constitution or by acts of Congress, the law to be applied in any case is the law of the state." The Court specified, "[t]here is no federal general common law. Congress has no power to declare substantive rules of common law applicable in a state… [a]nd no clause in the Constitution purports to confer such a power upon the federal courts."

**Erie and Statutes:**

In *Stewart v. Ricoh*, the Court held that although Alabama state law invalidated forum selection clauses, the Federal Venue Transfer Statute (1404(a)) was valid and applicable, and therefore federal law, not state law, governed whether to grant a motion to transfer induced by a forum selection clause. The rule out of *Stewart* is that a federal statute governs an issue as long as the statute is both applicable (i.e. the statute "covers the point in dispute") and valid (i.e. the statute "represents a valid exercise of Congress' authority under the Constitution"). In practice, almost all federal statutes are found to be valid and applicable.

**Erie and the Rules:**

Because the Rules are partly statutory and partly judge-made law, for Erie purposes the Court treats the Rules with less deference than statutes (see *Stewart*), yet with far greater legitimacy than federal general common law (which *Erie* declares does not exist).

In *Hanna v. Plumer*, the Court held that service of process should be governed by Federal Rule 4(d)(1) because when there is a valid and applicable Federal Rule, that Rule will govern. The court held that *Erie* is not the appropriate test for the validity and applicability of a Federal Rule; rather, the test is whether the Rule is both constitutional and within the scope of the Rules Enabling Act, which provides that a Rule is valid unless it would "abridge, enlarge, or modify any substantive right" (see § 2072(b)). The *Hanna* Court explained, "When a situation is covered by one of the Federal Rules…, the court has been instructed to apply the Federal Rule, and can refuse to do so only if the Advisory Committee, this Court, and Congress erred in their prima facie judgment that the Rule in question transgresses neither the terms of the Enabling Act nor constitutional restrictions."

However, the Court in *Walker v. Armco* construed *Hanna* narrowly, noting that *Hanna* only governs in a situation in which there is a "direct collision" between the Federal Rule and the state law. The *Walker* court held that before conducting a *Hanna* analysis, the preliminary question of whether the Federal Rule is sufficiently broad to control the issue must be answered in the affirmative. In *Walker*, the plaintiff served the defendant outside of the state grace period but inside of the federal grace period, and the Court interpreted the Federal Rules narrowly to avoid conflicting with state law, holding that the state grace period governed because there was no direct clash between state law and the Federal Rule.

The Court's narrow interpretation was that Rule 3 did not apply because that rule only determines the date from which timing requirements of Federal Rules begin to run, but does not apply to state statutes of limitations.
