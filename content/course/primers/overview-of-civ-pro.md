# Civil Procedure, Section 2 (Greiner Section): Course Overview

Professor Jim Greiner[^1]

Version as of August 18, 2011

---

## Subjects And Basic Questions

1. **Federal Court Subject Matter Jurisdiction:** What kind of cases can a federal court (as opposed to a state court) adjudicate?
2. **Horizontal (Between-State) Choice of Law:** When a transaction or occurrence involves facts that occurred in more than one state but the relevant issue is governed by state (as opposed to federal) law, which state's law governs?
3. **Personal Jurisdiction:** Where (geographically) can the lawsuit occur?
4. **Venue:** Within the set of geographic locations housing a court that could exercise personal jurisdiction over the parties, where can the lawsuit occur?
5. **Vertical (Federal Versus State) Choice of Law:** When a federal court sits in diversity, as to what issues does state law govern and as to what issues does federal law govern?
6. **The Complaint: Pleading of Facts:** How much specificity must a plaintiff include in a complaint to survive a motion to dismiss and, thus, to get to discovery?
7. **Relief Pending Litigation:** When is it appropriate for a court to award a plaintiff relief before the plaintiff has proved his/her case?
8. **Procedural Due Process:** What do concerns of fairness require a court to do before imposing adverse consequences upon a party?
9. **Discovery:** What devices are available to parties to gather information and evidence regarding the litigation, and how should courts regulate their use?
10. **Burdens And Summary Judgment:** Now that discovery has been conducted, when is a trial necessary to decide disputed issues of fact?

<!-- page: 2 -->

11. **Right To And Conduct of a Jury Trial:** What issues is a party entitled to have decided by a jury instead of a judge?
12. **Post-Trial Practice:** When is the conduct or result of a trial (particularly a jury trial) sufficiently defective to warrant disregard of the verdict reached, and what remedies are available in such a circumstance?
13. **Appellate Jurisdiction:** At what point in the litigation may a party seek review of a trial court order in an appellate court?
14. **Expanding the Lawsuit:** When is the lawsuit allowed or required to expand beyond the original plaintiff's claims against the original defendant?
15. **Finality:** Once a first lawsuit has gone to final judgment, what claims and issues cannot be litigated in a second lawsuit?
16. **Class Actions:** When will parties present in litigation be allowed to represent those not present, and what are the implications of such representation?

## 1. Federal Court Subject Matter Jurisdiction

**(a) Basic Question:** What kind of cases can a federal court (as opposed to a state court) adjudicate?

**(b) Modules**

- i. Arising under/federal question: when lawsuit presents a federal law issue (in a particular way)
- ii. Diversity: when parties are from different states, and a sufficient amount is at stake
- iii. Supplemental jurisdiction: when state law causes of action are appended to a federal court lawsuit
- iv. Removal/remand jurisdiction: when the litigation begins in state court
- v. Excuses: when a federal court does not exercise jurisdiction even though it appears power to do so has been granted

**(c) Lessons**

- i. Well-pleaded complaint rule (and exceptions)
- ii. How declaratory judgments work
- iii. Constitutional AND statutory grant of jurisdiction needed
- iv. Complete diversity
- v. When subject matter defect can be raised, and by whom
- vi. Domicile versus residency, how domicile changes
- vii. Calculation of amount in controversy
- viii. Power plus discretion in supplemental jurisdiction
- ix. Technical rules governing removal/remand, e.g.,
  - Plaintiff cannot remove

<!-- page: 4 -->

  - Defendant unanimity
  - 30-day time limits
  - What happens if technical rules missed
- x. Rare cases when federal courts can decline to exercise jurisdiction

**(d) Broad Themes**

- i. What sort of questions/cases should be litigated in federal court? Has the answer to this question changed over time?
- ii. What is the proper role of federalism in allocating business between the state and federal courts? Of efficiency concerns?
- iii. When should subject matter jurisdiction be adjudicated?
- iv. When should a federal court be allowed to decline to exercise jurisdiction granted to it?
- v. Why the technical, rule-bound nature of this jurisprudence? (Contrasts are coming.)

## 2. Horizontal (Between-State) Choice of Law

**(a) Basic Question:** When a transaction or occurrence involves facts that occurred in more than one state but the relevant issue is governed by state (as opposed to federal) law, which state's law governs?

**(b) Lessons**

- i. Rules from First Restatement
  - Tort rule: Lex loci delicti
  - Limitations periods (split among states)
- ii. Standards from the Second Restatement
  - Tort presumption
  - Limitations presumption
- iii. Theory underlying each approach

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. What is law? How do we "discover" or "make" it?
- ii. What theories underly the First and Second Restatement approaches?
- iii. What divides substance from procedure?
- iv. Are rules preferable to standards?
- v. Did the "revolution" in choice of law accomplish anything? (Should courts ever pay attention to academics?)

## 3. Personal Jurisdiction (BOTH State and Federal Courts)

**(a) Basic Question:** Where (geographically) can the lawsuit occur?

**(b) Modules/Lessons**

- i. In personam, quasi-in-rem, and in rem
- ii. Territorial approach
- iii. Challenges to territorial approach, resulting legal fictions
- iv. Minimum contacts
  - Contracts, torts & stream of commerce, domestic relations
  - General versus specific
- v. Constitutional AND statutory (long-arm statute) grant of jurisdiction needed
- vi. Federal court exercise of in personam jurisdiction
- vii. Rules governing international exercise, especially by federal courts
- viii. Tag jurisdiction
- ix. In personam jurisdiction and the Internet

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. Why the standard-based approach here (in contrast to subject matter jurisdiction)?
- ii. Does minimum contacts make sense in torts?
- iii. Can the concept of in personam jurisdiction survive global internationalization and the Internet?
- iv. How much role should historical practice play (e.g., in tag jurisdiction)?

## 4. Venue

**(a) Basic Question:** Within the set of geographic locations housing a court that could exercise personal jurisdiction over the parties, where can the lawsuit occur?

**(b) Lessons**

- i. To what federal court may a federal case be transferred
- ii. Choice of law consequences to federal court transfer (1404(a) versus 1406(a))
- iii. Forum non conveniens (between states, international)

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. Do we need the venue doctrine, another geographic limit on the lawsuit, given that we already have the personal jurisdiction doctrine?
- ii. What connects a forum non conveniens dismissal to the Colorado River doctrine from subject matter jurisdiction?

## 5. Vertical (Federal Versus State) Choice of Law

**(a) Basic Question:** When a federal court sits in diversity, as to what issues does state law govern and as to what issues does federal law govern?

**(b) Modules/Lessons**

- i. Replacement of "general law" approach with a focus on the source of law
- ii. Potential sources of the Erie doctrine: constitutional, statutory, prudential
- iii. Where the federal law does and does not govern, particularly with respect to the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure
- iv. Various tests for cases when no validly enacted federal law is directly on point: substance versus procedure, outcome-determinative, twin aims of Erie, affecting primary conduct (as measured by what point in time?)

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. What is "law"? How do we "find" or "make" it?
- ii. How does the substance/procedure division in horizontal choice of law related to the substance/procedure division in vertical choice of law (particular with respect to limitations periods)?
- iii. What are the constitutional limits of congressional power to prescribe rules of decisions for federal court lawsuits?
- iv. When (and why) should we narrowly interpret federal law to avoid a conflict with state law?

## 6. The Complaint: Pleading of Facts

**(a) Basic Question:** How much specificity must a plaintiff include in a complaint to get to discovery?

**(b) Modules/Lessons**

- i. "Code" pleading versus "notice" pleading versus the current "plausibility" standard
- ii. Standard under Rules 8 and 12(b)(6)
- iii. Would the complaint in *Jones v. Clinton* have been dismissed had it been filed today?

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. What are the implications of various approaches the law has taken regarding pleading of facts?
- ii. Is the pleading stage the right place to address crowded dockets?
- iii. Should pleading rules be trans-substantive?
- iv. What strategic considerations should govern the level of specificity in a complaint?

## 7. Relief Pending Litigation (Preliminary Injunctions)

**(a) Basic Question:** When is it appropriate for a court to award a plaintiff relief before the plaintiff has proved his/her case?

**(b) Lessons**

- i. Four elements: likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, balance of the equities, public interest
- ii. Meaning of irreparable harm

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. How much preliminary relief is consistent with fairness to the defendant?
- ii. Should the four elements of a preliminary injunction be prerequisites or factors?
- iii. How can litigators use preliminary injunction requests effectively?

## 8. Procedural Due Process

**(a) Basic Question:** What do concerns of fairness require a court to do before imposing adverse consequences upon a party?

**(b) Modules**

- i. Notice: requirement of, methods of providing
- ii. Opportunity to be heard: when the case involves property rights
- iii. Opportunity to be heard: when the case involves liberty interests

**(c) Lessons**

- i. Standard for evaluating notice
- ii. Extent of duty to follow up when efforts at notice fail
- iii. *Mathews* three-part balancing test
- iv. Factors affecting evaluation of procedures when cases involve property rights versus liberty interests

**(d) Broad Themes**

- i. Are courts institutionally well suited to decide the (empirical?) issues raised by the requirements regarding the notice and opportunity to be heard? Are other institutions better suited?
- ii. Do the standards the Supreme Court has articulated in this area provide sufficient guidance for lower courts as the latter decide cases? Are these standards sufficient to constrain the Supreme Court itself?
- iii. How should we balance fairness to the defendant with systemic needs for quick action?

## 9. Discovery

**(a) Basic Question:** What devices are available to parties to gather information and evidence regarding the litigation, and how should courts regulate their use?

**(b) Lessons**

- i. Case management rules
- ii. Scope of discovery
- iii. Party-driven nature of discovery, and judicial regulation thereof
- iv. Tools: request for production, interrogatories, depositions, requests for admission
- v. The pretrial order

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. How do we balance the values of allowing parties to obtain information needed to prove claims while preventing in terrorem settlements and preserving access to justice among those not millionaires?
- ii. How do we handle "big" cases?
- iii. How can the discovery tools be used effectively? Strategically?

## 10. Burdens And Summary Judgment

**(a) Basic Question:** Now that discovery has been conducted, when is a trial necessary to decide disputed issues of fact?

**(b) Lessons**

- i. Burdens of production versus burdens of persuasion
- ii. The summary judgment standard: no genuine issue of material fact, movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law
- iii. How to apply the standard to facts of cases
- iv. Was the summary judgment grant in *Jones v. Clinton* correct? Is the decision more or less defensible today than it was when the case was decided?

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. What is the proper allocation of power between the judge and the jury in terms of deciding disputed issues of fact?
- ii. How much should we require a party with the burden of proof to provide to reach trial?

## 11. Right To And Conduct of a Jury Trial

**(a) Basic Question:** What issues is a party entitled to have decided by a jury instead of a judge?

**(b) Modules**

- i. Constitutional right to a jury trial
- ii. Issues of fact versus issues of law
- iii. Jury instructions
- iv. Additur and remittitur

**(c) Lessons**

**(d) Broad Themes**

- ii. What sort of remedies should be awarded by a jury instead of a judge?

## 12. Post-Trial Practice

**(a) Basic Question:** When is the conduct or result of a trial (particularly a jury trial) sufficiently defective to warrant disregard of the verdict reached, and what remedies are available in such a circumstance?

**(b) Lessons**

- i. Power to enter judgment as a matter of law despite a jury verdict
- ii. Power to order a new trial despite rationality of a jury verdict
- iii. Power to condition denial of a new trial motion on the reduction, and in some states on the increase, of a jury award
- iv. Rarity of post-judgment relief

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. What justifies the grant of power to a trial judge to overturn a jury verdict and enter judgment in favor of the party the jury thought should lose?
- ii. What justifies requiring the parties to repeat a trial conducted without fatal procedural defects which resulted in a rational jury verdict?
- iii. We often laud the jury as an important facet of our democracy: do we like juries so much because we give them so little to do?
- iv. How do we balance the need for finality with the desire to achieve a legally correct result?

## 13. Appellate Jurisdiction

**(a) Basic Question:** At what point in the litigation may a party seek review of a trial court order in an appellate court?

**(b) Lessons**

- i. Final order requirement
- ii. Collateral order doctrine
- iii. Partial disposition of a case
- iv. Permission & double discretion
- v. Injunctive relief
- vi. The mandamus outlet

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. How do we balance the cost of needing to repeat trial court litigation with the desire to prevent undue interruption of trial court proceedings?
- ii. Should appellate jurisdiction rules be trans-substantive?

## 14. Expanding the Lawsuit

**(a) Basic Question:** When is the lawsuit allowed or compelled to expand beyond the original plaintiff's claims against the original defendant?

**(b) Modules/Lessons**

- i. Counterclaims
- ii. Cross-claims
- iii. Third-party practice
- iv. Intervention

**(c) Broad Themes**

- i. How should we balance the concerns of judicial efficiency and administrability of litigation when a transaction or occurrence involves multiple claims and parties?
- ii. Do we need the rules to provide checks on expanding of the lawsuit beyond those imposed by jurisdictional & venue rules?
- iii. Is the massive amount of discretion we award the trial judge in this area justified?

## 15. Finality

**(a) Basic Question:** Once a first lawsuit has gone to final judgment, what claims and issues cannot be litigated in a second lawsuit?

**(b) Modules**

- i. Claim preclusion
- ii. Issue preclusion
- iii. Preclusion among systems
- iv. Preclusion from administrative agency decisions

**(c) Lessons**

- i. Requirements for triggering claim and issue preclusion when rendering and subsequent courts are the same
- ii. Sources of law governing inter-system preclusion
- iii. When preclusion law of rendering and subsequent courts differs, which governs
- iv. May a subsequent court afford greater preclusive effect to a judgment than a rendering court can
- v. Requirements for triggering claim and issue preclusion when rendering entity is administrative

**(d) Broad Themes**

- i. What role does full faith and credit play in our federalism system?
- ii. What is the nature of the judicial power?
- iii. Should subsequent courts be allowed to afford greater preclusive effect to a judgment than would the rendering court? How does this relate to *Colorado River*?

## 16. Class Actions

**(a) Basic Question:** When are parties present in litigation allowed to represent those not present, and what are the implications of such representation?

**(b) Modules**

- i. Prerequisites to maintaining
- ii. Types of class actions
- iii. Choice of law limits
- iv. Settlement
- v. Implications for finality

**(c) Lessons**

- i. Numerosity, commonality, typicality, representativeness
- ii. Limited fund, common claim, injunctive relief, damages
- iii. Judicial role in overseeing a settlement
- iv. Preclusive effects

**(d) Broad Themes**

- i. What economic principles underpin class actions?
- ii. Are there principal/agent problems with class counsel, problems that can be addressed via trial judge oversight?
- iii. What due process principles do class actions implicate, and how should we apply those principles?
- iv. How can a trial judge adjudicate certification intelligently early in the litigation, i.e., before merits discovery?
