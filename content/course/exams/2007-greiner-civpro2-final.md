## Question 1 (65 points)

Assume that Alabama state law invalidates contractual forum-selection clauses, but that New York law nearly always enforces them. For federal law on the subject, remember Kennedy and O'Connor's concurrence in *Stewart v. Ricoh*. Assume also that Alabama's long-arm statute provides, "Alabama courts may exercise personal jurisdiction over any and all parties when such exercise would be consistent with the Constitution of the United States. Process may be served on any party in any manner consistent with the Constitution of the United States." Assume Alabama still follows the First Restatement regarding choice of law. [By the way, in real life, all of this may be untrue. This is an exam question only. Same goes for the rest of the exam.]

In May, 2006, software engineer Peter Parker, a resident and domiciliary of Mississippi, signed a contract to work for Excellent Software, Inc. ("ESI"). Parker's employment contract had a three-year term at a salary of $150,000.00 per year. The contract included the following boilerplate clause: "Any lawsuit by Employee [meaning Parker] against ESI relating in any way to this contract shall take place in a court located in the jurisdiction of ESI's principal place of business. Any lawsuit against any ESI shareholder relating in any way to this contract shall take place in a court located in the jurisdiction of that shareholder's domicile (if the shareholder is a human being) or in the place of its incorporation (if the shareholder is a corporation)."

At all times relevant, 30% of the voting stock of ESI was owned by the Dong Ah Corporation [no, not the same Dong Ah from the *Thane-Coate* case; I just reused the name], an entity incorporated in the People's Republic of China (and itself wholly owned by another Chinese entity with ties to the Chinese government). Dong Ah's principal place of business was in Albany, New York, where Dong Ah operated a large software facility.

In May of 2006, ESI was a Mississippi corporation with its principal place of business in Mississippisville, a few miles on the Mississippi side of the Mississippi/Alabama border. In June of 2006, however, ESI's chief executive officer, John Boss, proposed to move ESI's headquarters (*i.e.*, principal place of business) to Alabama to take advantage of favorable tax laws. The move required the approval of the majority of the stockholders; Dong Ah voted its shares against the move, but as Dong Ah had only 30% of the shares, it lost the vote, and the move to Alabama proceeded. ESI and all 30 of its employees began operations in Alabamasville, Alabama, a small town just on the Alabama side of the Mississippi/Alabama border. The move was completed by December of 2006, and at that time, Parker began to travel to Alabamasville each day to work.

<!-- page: 2 -->

In February of 2007, Parker traveled with Boss to inspect the Dong Ah facility in Albany. At that time, ESI and Dong Ah were in negotiations to merge; all merger negotiations were taking place in Albany. During this trip, Dong Ah's regional head of operations, Chenxin Zhang, asked Boss to step into a private conference room. Once inside, Zhang told Boss that the merger could not go forward unless ESI fired Parker. When asked for a reason, Zhang replied that Dong Ah had discovered via the Internet that Parker was writing certain software in his spare time that might compete with software Dong Ah was developing, and that "it would be best if he [Parker] didn't find out about what we're doing, which he could if we merged. The people back in China who own my company ordered me to say that you had to get rid of him." Fifteen minutes after this meeting was over, Boss called Parker into the same conference room and fired Parker.

Parker sued ESI and Dong Ah in Alabama state court on April 9, 2007. Against ESI, the claim was state law breach of contract; against Dong Ah, the claim was tortious interference with contractual relations[^1] under state law (refer to this litigation as the "First Case"). Against Dong Ah, Parker requested compensatory and punitive damages totaling $500,000.00. On April 16, an Alabama marshal served a copy of Parker's complaint upon a low-level Dong Ah software engineer who happened to be in Alabamasville inspecting ESI's facility there as part of the merger negotiations. This employee took a copy of the lawsuit back to Albany. On the same day, the marshal served ESI at its Alabamasville location.

On April 17, Parker and ESI settled, and accordingly, on April 23, Parker filed a stipulation dismissing ESI from the Alabama state court litigation.

On April 24, Dong Ah removed the lawsuit to Alabama federal court and on the next day moved "to transfer this case under 28 U.S.C. §§ 1404(a) and 1406(a)" to the Federal District Court for the Northern District of New York (where Albany is located). Over Parker's opposition, Alabama federal judge G. Goblin granted the motion, stating, "This case belongs in New York. Important events happened there. Plus, my docket is really crowded. I don't care which transfer statute applies; the New York court can figure that out." The transfer order was signed in late May, 2007, and the record of the First Case arrived in New York federal district court and was assigned to Judge Mary J. Watson.

On June 4, Parker sued Dong Ah in the Supreme Court of New York (New York's state trial-level court), Albany division, again alleging tortious interference with contractual relations (call this case the "Second Case"). On June 11, Dong Ah removed the Second Case, citing diversity of citizenship, to the Federal District Court for the Northern District of New York. Dong Ah's request to have this case also assigned to Judge Watson was granted the next day.

Shortly thereafter, Dong Ah filed motions to dismiss both cases, in favor of litigation in the People's Republic of China, citing the contractual forum selection clause. Parker opposed, and both parties filed briefs. Judge Watson scheduled a hearing on the motions for August 1, 2007. At the hearing, in addition to arguing against Dong Ah's motions, Parker's attorney

<!-- page: 3 -->

moved to remand the Second Case, saying it had been improperly removed. Dong Ah opposed the remand motion.

On August 2, Judge Watson issued an order remanding the Second Case to the Supreme Court of New York, Albany Division. In the First Case, she issued an order stating:

> I find that the contractual forum selection clause is enforceable. I will not dismiss on this basis, however, because I am worried that Dong Ah will attempt to avoid the merits of dispute in the court system of the People's Republic. Accordingly, I hereby STAY this case. This stay means that the case will remain on my list of active cases, that I will not enter an order of dismissal yet, and I am retaining jurisdiction in case Dong Ah attempts to do any of the following in litigation in the People's Republic of China: plead a statute of limitations defense; avoid any reasonable discovery Parker might request; or take any other action to prevent the court in the People's Republic from adjudicating the merits of this dispute. Should Parker convince me that Dong Ah has in fact done any of the above, I will lift the stay and proceed with this litigation. Otherwise, once a court in the People's Republic adjudicates the merits, I will dismiss the Second Case with prejudice.
>
> It seems like this case would benefit from Second Circuit review; my ruling today is probably controlling with respect to the case, and an appeal would materially advance its ultimate disposition. There is certainly no just reason to delay anything. Anyway, I'm pretty sure the Second Circuit would affirm. This case is a no-brainer.

You are a low-level attorney in the office of Dong Ah's general counsel. Your boss has just received a telephone call from Parker's attorney relating that Parker intends to try to get review of the stay in the First Case in the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit. Your boss would herself like to get review of Judge Watson's remand decision in the Second Case. Your boss asks for a memorandum addressing the following three (a, b, and c) issues:

### Question 1(a). With respect to the Second Case,

**(i) (5 points)** can we get review of Judge Watson's order in the Second Circuit? Why or why not?

**(ii) (5 points)** if we could get review, is the order correct? Why or why not?

### Question 1(b). With respect to the First Case,

**(i) (10 points)** can Parker get review of Judge Watson's stay order in the Second Circuit? What possible bases are there, and which might work?

**(ii) (30 points)** if Parker can get review of Judge Watson's stay order, should the Second Circuit affirm or reverse or what? Analyze in full.

<!-- page: 4 -->

### Question 1(c) (15 points).

[For this subpart, pretend that the Second Case goes back to state court, *i.e.*, that the remand is effectuated.]: Can we make any use of the stay order in the First Case to help us get rid of the Second Case? Explain.

**Note:** If you think the Second Circuit might remand the case for factual findings, discuss what facts the Circuit might order the district court to find/consider.

## Question 2 (35 points)

Throughout the semester, we have discussed various circumstances in which the Constitution and congressional statutes appear to give a federal district court subject matter jurisdiction, and in which in personam jurisdiction is present and venue lies, but in which the district court nevertheless refuses to adjudicate the lawsuit. Identify and describe two such circumstances. Then use them, as well as any others that strike your fancy, as a springboard for discussing the circumstances (if any) in which a federal court's refusal to hear a case that it appears to have the power to adjudicate is legitimate and/or advisable. What policies are important here? Does the distinction between rules and standards matter? Also, how does this issue relate to the question of whether district courts adjudicating putative class actions should have discretion to decline to certify a class even if a case meets all prerequisites of Rule 23(a) and fits within one of the subparts of Rule 23(b)? Should district courts have this discretion re class actions?
