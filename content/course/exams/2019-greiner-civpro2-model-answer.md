Question 1

12(b)(1)/SMJ: Counts I and II assert state law causes of action, with no federal issue embedded, so there
is no FQJ nor Smith-Grable exception. There is complete diversity (HG = MA (PPoB, incorporation),
Droobles = NH) only if Riddle is not domiciled in MA. His time as an undergrad and business school
student was probably not enough to change his domicile from his RI place of birth (Mas), but his
purchase of an apartment, opening a business, and paying taxes in Boston might constitute
simultaneously living in Massachusetts with an intent to stay. If Riddle = MA, then the whole complaint,
including Count II, must be dismissed (Strawbridge). Even if Riddle = RI, however, the suit against
Droobles is AIC deficient ($50K < $75K, 1332), and section 1367 likely does not provide supplemental
jurisdiction. Joinder is proper under FRCP 20 (same T&O in the deepfake scheme, common issue is the
existence of the Riddle-Droobles scheme), and under the same reasoning, HG versus Droobles is part of
the same case or controversy as HG versus Riddle (anchoring claim $950K > $75K). But Count II is a
claim by a plaintiff (HG) against a person made a party under FRCP 20 where the exercise of such
jurisdiction would be AIC deficient and thus inconsistent with 1332, so 1367(b) prohibits supplemental
jurisdiction (Exxon dictum).

12(b)(2)/IPJ: Under FRCP 4(k)(1)(A), with no basis for general IPJ (25% MA sales volume not high enough
under Daimler) or consent IPJ, we look for a MA long-arm (present, limits of the Constitution), minimal
contacts relating Droobles and the alleged deepfake to the state of MA, and the fairness and
reasonableness of MA IPJ exercise (International Shoe). Regarding minimal contacts, 25% of Droobles'
sales are in MA, the highest volume sales state. These are direct sales, showing purposeful availablment
of MA customers, with no intermediary (contrast Nicastro), so this is no stream of commerce case. True,
this lawsuit is not as directly related to those sales as would be a suit based on defective gum sold in
MA. But the complaint concerns, and followed, the use and independent promotion of Droobles'
product in MA, which results from Droobles' direct shipments there. HG, the deepfake's target,
monetizes by operating solely in MA, with Harry's images and postings using MA landmarks to show its
connection to MA, meaning a big part of HG's reputation is in MA. Droobles and Riddle knew and
intended that the harm to HG would occur in MA. True, Droobles has no office, salesforce, advertising,
or business solicitation in MA, and his website merely responds to visitors. He does not accept checks or
monetary transfers from MA (or anywhere else). Regarding fairness and reasonableness, Massachusetts
has an interest in protecting MA companies from intentional business harm. MA is a convenient place
to litigate for Riddle, who does business there, and Droobles, who directly sells business product there.
Evidence of harm (HG records, Harry's testimony) will be in MA. The harm was felt in MA, so even an R2
state like MA could use MA law (see below).

12(b)(3)/venue: Droobles and Riddle are domiciled/reside (1391(c)(1) in different states, so 1391(b)(1)
is unavailable. But venue is likely proper under 1392(b)(2) because the following MA events form a
substantial part of the Counts: Harry's initial endorsement of Droobles' gum, Harry's portion of the
Riddle email exchanges, all of Harry's activities that generate the Internet traffic HG depends on, and the
business harm to HG. 1391(b)(3) is not available because 1392(b)(2) venue is definitely proper in D.R.I

12(b)(6)/Twibal: We might ignore only portions of the paragraph beginning "On information and belief"
as conclusory. The remainder of this detailed complaint alleges the participation in the production
(element 1) of a fake video (element 2) in paras 12-14; injury to business value (element 3) in paras 6-8
and 18-19, and causation in paras 6-11, 18-19. The only hard issue is whether there are the alleged facts
are sufficient to nudge the issue of Droobles' involvement in the deepfake scheme across the line from
merely possible to plausible. But consider the following: Harry has never met either Droobles or Riddle;
the anonymous email describes the Droobles/Riddle's meeting and suggests that the video forming the
basis for the deepfake was taken on Droobles' personal phone at Droobles' personal and explicit
request. Although it's possible Droobles' wasn't involved, it's plausible that he was.

MRCP 4(a)(1)(H): The complaint did not include the separate attachment required by this rule, so if it
applies, the court will dismiss despite the absence of a counterpart requirement under FRCP4. (MRCP
4(a)(1)H) is procedural for HCOL purposes, as it concerns how to pursue lawsuits, so MA HCOL rules,
applicable under Klaxon, would use it). Erie time. Under York, we dismiss under MRCP4 and continue
under FRCP4, so we use MRCP4. Under Harlan in Hannah, knowledgeable lawyers will (or will not)
include the attachment without bothering clients, so with no effect on primary conduct, we use FRCP4.
Under Shady Grove/Scalia, FRCP4 is valid because it really regulates procedure (Sibbach) in that the
issue here concerns the manner and means of how lawsuits proceed, and one can argue that FRCP4's
phrasing "a summons must" contain the listed items means that it need only contain the items in the
detailed list that follows, and we continue. Otherwise, Shady Grove/Scalia resembles Shady
Grove/Ginsburg, and here, an interpretation of FRCP4 as not speaking to the issue of whether a
summons must contain the separate MRCP 4(a)(1)(H) attachment, thus rendering FRCP4 inapplicable, is
available: FRCP 4(a)(1)(A-G) is not exhaustive of what a summons "must" contain.

Question 2: If, as a matter of HCOL, the MA Public Access Defense ("PAD") applies, then a Droobles
FRCP56 motion should succeed. Movant Droobles has the BoP at trial, and he can point to Harry's
deposition testimony to the facts described in paragraphs 6-7 of the complaint to meet his burden of
production that HG has ready access to the public. Even drawing all rational inferences in HG's favor, it
is hard to see how HG could demonstrate a genuine issue of material fact requiring a trial given that
HG's business model depends on public access.

The HCOL analysis suggests that the PAD is available. Under Ferrens, the NH federal district court will
use MA choice of law rules (as the MA federal court would have, Klaxon) if venue was proper in MA and
MA had personal jurisdiction over both defendants, which seems likely, see above. Even if not, NH's R1
HCOL analysis will result in MA substantive law because the last event needed to complete the harm,
the loss of HG's business value, occurred in MA. Thus, MA's R2 analysis presumes MA law. True, key
events did occur elsewhere (the Riddle/Droobles meeting, the video recording), but key events also
occurred in Boston (HG's gum endorsement, the Riddle/Harry email exchange, HG loss of value). MA
(NH) has an interested in protecting HG (Drooles), but this will matter little because MA (NH) law means
a loss for HG (Droobles). A court is unlikely to depart from the R2 presumption of lex loci delicti,
meaning MA law, for any issue, particularly the PAD, which centers on a MA party's access to the
Internet.

Without the PAD, an HG FRCP56 motion will likely succeed. HG, bearing the BoP at trial, will meet its
production burden as follows:

    · The two Harry/Riddle exchanges to suggest Riddle's intention to perpetrate the scheme and
         motive therefor;

    · Harry's uncontradicted testimony stating that the video is fake, and to the other events
         described in the complaint;

    · The FluNetwork report, and the striking similarities in length and language between the
         deepfake video and the video taken in the Droobles' conference room, to suggest how the fake
         was perpetrated; and
    · Evans' uncontradicted testimony showing Droobles' participation in the scheme. Droobles'
         called in Evans and instructed her to record the meeting on his cell phone. A reasonable jury
         must infer Droobles' emailed the video to Riddle with the note "Keep this quiet!!!!". Ergo,
         Droobles knew what he was doing and why.

Drawing all reasonable inferences in Droobles' favor, there is no genuine issue of material fact on the BD
elements and HG is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. Droobles will probably not meet his burden
to produce facts showing a genuine issue of material fact. There is nothing to counteract HG's
production. True, there is no smoking gun evidence showing Droobles and Riddle saying "We agree to
commit a tort against Riddle," no direct evidence of a payment from Riddle to Droobles, or direct
evidence of causal harm, but insisting on that seems fastidious.

Question 4: Droobles can invoke claim preclusion against Harry but not Ginny. We have the same
claim/transaction in the form of the deepfake video, a judgment that is final and "on the merits" in the
dismissal with prejudice. HG and Harry are different parties, but Harry is in privity with HG based on his
controlling share of HG and his (discovery could establish such if necessary) control over the HG lawsuit.
Ginny, however, is not in privity with HG; there is no evidence that she controlled the HG lawsuit, and
she does not have a controlling share of its stock.

Ginny may try to use NMOCE to preclude Droobles from denying that he perpetrated the tort. First, is
the summary judgment followed by the dismissal sufficiently firm to support issue preclusion? On the
one hand, SJ grants are subject to revision at any time. On the other hand, they hardly ever are revised,
and we want to prevent needless relitigation of issues. Second, under Semtek, we should use the
preclusion law of the state in which the rendering federal diversity court sat, but after the transfer, is
that MA or NH? Likely MA, as we likely use the happy court rule to say that with sufficient venue and
IPJ, the transfer is simply a change of courthouse, so MA preclusion rules govern. If so, Ginny can use
NMOCE (same issues, the elements of the BD tort; actually litigated in the SJ motion, subject to the
above; necessary to the judgment from the SJ grant, subject to the above) even though she is a different
party from HG. If NH applies, either because the happy court rule does not (allowing us to look only to
the face of the NH federal court's judgment to know what law to use, or because either IPJ or venue was
lacking in MA), then Ginny cannot use issue preclusion because she is not the same party as HG.

Question 5: This is a potential b(3) class, so we need numerosity, typicality/representativeness,
predominance (which swallows commonality), and superiority. Regarding predominance, all plaintiffs
were harmed (if they were harmed at all) by precisely the same scheme in precisely the same way. The
only difference among their claims is the amounts to which they are entitled, which vary according to
their HG ownership. Everything else consists of common issues. For the same reasons, Harry and
Ginny's claims are the same as those of the other class members, providing
typicality/representativeness. Because the issues are so similar, it has to be better to try them all once.
The only real issue appears to be numerousity, but with 20 unnamed class members spread across three
distant states, and small ($15,000) in controversy for each, numerosity is likely present. Certification
seems likely.
Question 6: The second suggestion would eliminate the distinction between permissive and compulsory
joinder (including counterclaims) currently in FRCP13 & 20. Particularly given the declaratory judgment
statute, so doing would provide sophisticated parties with additional power to compel litigation on all
disputes in that party's chosen forum. How much this will matter is hard to guess. For sophisticated
and reasonably financed opponents, it will probably matter little. Such parties can take advantage of
modern transportation and information transmission mechanisms to render location less important.
Geographic location can sometimes matter to HCOL (Klaxon), but given the similarity in HCOL rules
across the country (see how NY's experience with HCOL resulted in the R2 = R1 except in unusual cases),
this will infrequently. The big divide currently, and likely in the future, is whether litigation occurs in
court at all (as opposed to some kind of arbitration), and if so, whether it occurs in a United States court
using United States law ("US law, who needs it?"). The FRsCP already encourage resolution of as many
disputes as possible in a single lawsuit, and this change seems consistent with that policy, with Rule 42
providing the needed complexity-reducing discretion to the trial judge. Whatever efficiency gains are
result from the litigation, but most likely settlement, of multiple matters would be welcome.

An additional suggestion to expand the preclusion doctrine would be to expand the rules of privity, such
that (say) the Gonzalez district court's decision would have been affirmed. As applied to claim
preclusion, this would create a rule of near-compulsory intervention for anyone who wanted to have a
say in litigation of their interests, when those interests were effectively represented by a party to that
litigation, knowing that if they do not intervene, they will be precluded anyway. As applied to issue
preclusion, this would effectively expand the rules of precedent (which does not require any form of
mutuality) to include factual determinations. This proposal could result in some efficiency gains from
reduced relitigation of claims and issues, particularly on large matters of public interest, such as the
behavior of large corporations, or the legality of government policies. The proposal would, however,
involve acute information costs. Depending on how much privity is expanded, it could result in parties
being bound by litigation about which they had no notice and no prior knowledge, which would
implicate PDP doctrines (Mullane). Parties unsure of whether changes in PDP doctrines were ripe for
relaxation might need to begin to monitor courts closely; information systems regarding the nature of
ongoing litigation are not currently in place for such monitoring. Moreover, large public-interest
litigation might become absurdly unwieldy (think Smuck) unless class action certification rules were
relaxed (we'd need to reverse the trend in Walmart).

Both proposals could involve difficult access to justice costs. The declaratory judgment statute, plus the
inability of low-income parties to use modern methods of transportation and information transmission,
will make it difficult for smaller parties or low-income individuals to combat a sophisticated party's
choice of forum for tort cases (contract matters probably have FSCs anyway). And only sophisticated
parties would have a chance to monitor cases that might affect their interests, as would be required in
by the second proposed change. As is often true, the usefulness of these doctrines depends on which
world we consider, that of sophisticated and reasonably resourced parties or the other 99%.
