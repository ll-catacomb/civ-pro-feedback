5541(c) $80K
      --  Personal jurisdiction, individual claim:
            --  General IPJ over WWW in OR?  Perhaps, major plant located

            --  Specific IPJ over WWW in OR?  Questionable.  Don't know if

      --  Failure to state a claim:  Apply Twiqbal.  Paragraph 5 is
specific, so we assume that WWW provided WAMs to Tonks and to the class

plausibility, it is hard to see what WWW would gain from screwing over its
best customers by posting their private details online.  On the other hand,
something happened to get the images and information up on the website, and
it's hard to believe there's it could be a coincidence that the website
would have those specific people
      --  Transfer:  Is E.D. Cal. a place in which the suit "could have
been brought" under Hoffman?  Yes; General IPJ over WWW there under PPoB,
venue there because it is where WWW "resides."  Re convenience, can't quite
tell yet what's going on in terms of the information posting on the
Internet, but the prototype WAMs were made entirely in Sacramento, and
Sacramento isn't far too far (5 hours).  Medical evidence in Sacramento.
Convenience seems to point pretty heavily to Sacramento.  Don't have to
decide yet whether OR fed ct has IPJ and/or venue, can transfer with the
authority granted under some combination of 1404(a), 1406(a), 1631.

Question 3:  Should the court certify the class?


      We need supplemental jurisdiction.  Under Exxon, we have it assuming
           that Tonks' claim met the AIC, and thus that her claim provides
           an anchoring claim.  We don't care about the citizenship of
           unnamed class members, Ben-Hur, or about their AIC, Exxon.
      Assuming FRCP 23 governs
      --  Numerosity:  30 in the not-clear zone; class members are not too
           geographically disperse.  If HCOL analysis reveals that CA law
           applies to the 28 CA plaintiffs (all contacts as to them are CA,
           so it seems likely), then recovery likely limited to $20,000
           each, too small to incentivize a plaintiffs' atty given what is
           likely to be a somewhat factually complex case (this is not a
           slip-and-fall tort, emotion distress may require expert
           testimony)
      --  Typicality, representativeness, predominance (swallows
           commonality):  it appears that whatever WWW has done (perhaps
           collected information, and posted it), it has done the same
           thing to all of the class members. Only problem may be HCOL. OR
           recognizes Outrage but requires intentionality.  CA and AZ
           recognize Outrage but allow a plaintiff to succeed upon proving
           either recklessness or intentionality.  CA and OR have different
           damages, but both are by formula, so that wouldn't be much of an
           issue (except under Walmart, which suggest that any questions
           that have dissimilar answers can defeat class certification,
           even if those questions are unlikely ever to be disputed).  The
           facts of Tonks' case seem similar to those of the other class
           members, but WWW could argue that emotional distress is an
           inherently personal tort, and each plaintiff would need to show
           that she herself was in fact distressed (even if the damages are
           set by formula).
      --  Superiority:  Honestly, do we think we'll have 30 individual
           lawyers in the room?
      But, does FRCP 23 govern, or the California anti-class statute.
First, a VCOL analysis to see if any state's law governs.  Then, if some
state law governs, a HCOL analysis to say which state.
On the VCOL:  Yes, this is a reprise of Shady Grove, but if we think CA law
might apply as a matter of HCOL, then the twist is the CA legislature's
findings in the EDLAOOCA.
--  York/outcome determinative:  not clear, not really outcome-
determinative of the individual case, but determinative of the class claim
--  Harlan in Hanna:  CA legislature appeared to think that the class
action prohibition is needed so as to alter primary conduct, perhaps court
should defer this finding?
--  Hanna/Stewart, as interpreted by Scalia:  Class action, same issue as
Shady Grove, Scalia does not care what the state law says at all
--  Hanna/Stewart, as interpreted by Ginsburg:  No class action, same issue
as Shady Grove
--  But, Stevens in Shady Grove?  This is the controlling opinion in Shady
Grove, and probably would follow California law if it applied under HCOL.
Reason:  CA legis appeared to make the non-aggregation limit a part of the
state substantive scheme, to under the REA Rule 23 would have to yield to
avoid a conflict with state substantive rights.  So, no class if CA law
applies.

Now, an HCOL analysis:  First, what HCOL rules do we use?  We know we have
to use state HCOL rules, but which state?  That depends on the Happy Court
rule as relates to the transfer.  Case was filed in OR, transferred without
comment, so it depends on if both IPJ and venue were proper in OR.  If both
IPJ and venue are proper, then OR choice of law rules would apply.  In that
case, it doesn't matter whether the issue is characterized as procedural or
substantive; if procedural OR would apply its own law, and if substantive,
OR would apply lex loci delicti, which means OR law because Tonks suffered
her emotional distress injury there.  Either way, no class action
prohibition.  If either IPJ or venue is not proper, then CA COL rules
apply.  Here, it might matter whether the issue is characterized as
procedural or substantive.  If procedural, CA law applies, class actions
prohibited; if substantive, we have a 2d restatement analysis, vague,
unclear, but given the dominance of the class members in CA, CA's interest
in regulating conduct that occurred there, CA's interest in wanting the CA
party (WWW) to win, probably CA law.  Remember that what is procedural for
HCOL purposes can be substantive for VCOL purposes.


Question 4:

      There don't appear to be any historical facts in dispute.  WWW
collected the information.  Fred left it lying around in an insecure way
(unlocked doors, post-it note saying that important information was on it,
lying on his desk and not locked up).  The email shows that Fred and George
knew of the risk they were ignoring.  Alicia stole the usb device, mailed
it to a WWW competitor, and the competitor had an obvious motive to post it
online, which someone apparently did.  Tonks proved emotional distress harm
with her affidavit in the PI litigation (demonstrating that she suffered
PTSD).  Meanwhile, there is no evidence Fred or George they did anything
intentional to cause the publication of the information collected.  Also,
WWW had an economic motive NOT to harm her.
      Applying Celotex, Brennan, first, to Tonks' motion:  Tonks would have
the burden of persuasion at trial.  Has she produced enough evidence that
if unrebutted would compel a jury finding in her favor?  Tonks can prove
that WWW's conduct was reckless, but not intentional.  So whether she
succeeds depends on whether she has to provide intentionality or merely
recklessness.   That turns out to depend on HCOL, because OR substantive
law requires intentionality, and CA substantive law requires only
recklessness.
      Applying Celotex, Brennan, to WWW's cross motion, we get the same
conclusion.  Tonks has the burden at trial, but WWW can affirmatively knock
out an essential element of Tonks' claim if OR law applies, namely, the
issue of intentionality.
      Result:  If HCOL leads to OR substantive law on the issue of
recklessness/intentionality, grant WWW's MSJ and deny Tonks' MSJ.  If HCOL
leads to CA substantive law on the issue of recklessness/intentionality,
deny WWW's MSJ and grant Tonks' MSJ.
      So, how do we handle the HCOL issue?  The first step is to figure out
which HCOL rules apply (  Greiner Happy Court rule (same issue as discussed
above).  If OR had IPJ and venue, then OR choice of law rules, 1st
Restatement, tort not complete until Tonks' felt the injury, which she did
in OR, OR law (and all that implies).  If OR lacked either IPJ or venue, CA
HCOL rules, 2d Restatement.  CA HCOL rules might well lead to CA
substantive law to the issue of liability because most relevant events
occurred in CA, except for the recording itself (fortuitous) and Tonks'
learning of the online posting (which she might have done anywhere because
the information was on the Internet).  Maybe CA COL rules lead to OR law on
damages, but we don't care about that in the context of this motion.
      Even under CA substantive law, WWW might argue that recklessness is
inherently a jury issue, so we'd need a jury to decide it, but that seems
unlikely, given the powerful and undisputed evidence of disregard of a
known risk.

Question 6:

In all jurisdictions' laws, we need same issue, actually litigated, and
necessary to decision.  So deal with those first.

It would appear that we have the same issue.  It's likely that either CA or
AZ substantive law would apply to Bellatrix's lawsuit, but since the two
are identical on the tort of Outrage, we can just assume it's CA.  What law
applied to the summary judgment grant in Tonks' lawsuit?  If it's CA, then
we have the same law as in Bellatrix's lawsuit.  If it's OR, then that
meant that the court found WWW acted intentionally, which would also be
sufficient under CA law.  So we're OK on same issue.  Bonus points for
students who catch the issues that nothing in the fact patterns states that
BL filed an affidavit proving that she did in fact suffer emotional harm,
but no points off for failing to see this.

Actually litigated:  yes, in the summary judgment motion, with affidavits
and discovery, assuming we have a sufficiently final judgment, see below.

Necessary to the decision:  It's not clear that the SJ grant has the
requisite finality to constitute a "decision."  A SJ grant could be revised
at any time before final judgment, and it's not appealable (appealability
isn't required to render a ruling sufficiently final, but its absence is an
indication that the ruling may be non-final).  On the other hand, SJ grants
are hardly ever altered prior to judgment.

Mutuality?  The issue depends on whether the DOR in Tonks' lawsuit was a
happy court, but Bellatrix's motion is likely to fail here.

Initially, assume that DOR in Tonks' lawsuit was had IPJ and venue.  Then
the transfer was merely a change of courtroom, so for preclusion purposes,
the EDCal judgment should be treated as though it was from DOR.  So pretend
the rendering court, then, was the federal DOR sitting in diversity [this
is analogous to the happy court rule].  Under Semtek (and the implication
for a federal subsequent court), we use OR state preclusion law.  OR
requires full mutuality, and Bellatrix was not a party to Tonks' lawsuit,
so she cannot invoke issue preclusion.

Next, assume that the DOR lacked IPJ or venue.  Then the Tonks' judgment
was rendered by a EDCal court sitting in diversity, and under Semtek, we
would look to Cal preclusion law.  Cal IP law = FCL, so we ask whether
Bellatrix could have joined the first lawsuit; if she could have, no IP
under Parklane; if she could not have, then perhaps IP.  Could she have
joined Tonks' lawsuit?  She would not have destroyed complete diversity
(Bellatrix = AZ, WWW = Del, Cal on nerve center), and if there was SMJ in
the Tonks lawsuit (perhaps not, given the AIC restriction ion the
ECLAOOCA), then Bellatrix could have joined under Exxon.  Bellatrix's only
hope is to argue that CA HCOL rules (applicable after the unhappy court
transfer) would mean that the ECLAOOCA anti-joinder provision would apply
(best argument here is that the anti-joinder provision is procedural for
HCOL, so use "forum" = Cal law), and that the ECLAOOCA would apply as a
matter of VCOL.  The VCOL issue is the same as the class action issue in
Question 3; it's just Rule 20 versus the ECLAOOCA instead of Rule 23 versus
the ECLAOOCA.
