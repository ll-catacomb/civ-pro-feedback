Question 1:

Example essay by Greiner

PDP jurisprudence is about whether to provide greater procedural protections, such as a pre-deprivation live hearing (in Matthews) or notice, an opportunity to present evidence (Restatement 82), the requirement of or opportunity to post a bond (Doehr), or a written decision (Vitek).  The point of all this process is to protect substantive rights.  Certain kinds of additional procedural protection may be impossible to achieve without additional procedural complication.  A hearing (Goldberg) provides an opportunity to present critical evidence, but presenting evidence may mean compliance with well-intentioned but complex rules.  Participating in a proceeding that ends with a written decision  (Vitek, R52) might entail proposing findings of fact and conclusions of law as to what the written decision should say, which are hard to do.  And reading a written decision, with no one there to answer questions and explain, is hard to do.   Even putting aside the money required, posting a bond means understanding what a bond is for and how to access the market for one.  If the complication means that those without lawyers cannot use the process, they cannot protect their substantive rights.  The additional process can be self-defeating.  A key point:  All this is before one takes into account any efficiency or cost concerns.  This discussion need not be about efficiency.

In Turner, the court tells us that when the stakes are high enough, a state must either DECREASE procedural complication (difficult to do if PDP trappings are constitutionally demanded) or provide lawyers.  But lots of state-paid lawyers to mitigate the complication are not likely after Lassiter and Turner.  So we need ways to provide procedural protections without inducing procedural complication.  The latter requires a lot of creativity.  Turner mentions court forms, which requires that the court system internalize the idea that it is responsible for assuring access to justice (a task the court system would rather foist on the bar).  Vitek mentions non-lawyer assistance, which bar views as an existential threat. There are plenty of other measures one might try:  specialized courts with inquisitorial questioning; self-help instructional materials; court navigators; law school clinics.  One single answer is unlikely to do the trick.

Question 2:  The Second Circuit should dismiss both appeals for lack of appellate jurisdiction.  Neither the grant of a motion for a new trial nor the denial of a JAMOL motion is a final order because litigation on the merits continues after both.  JJ’s recitation that the issue is important satisfies some of the 1292(b) double discretion grounds, but she specifically found that the key issue was easy, not that there was a substantial ground for a difference of opinion.  There is no injunction, only one claim and no certification for Rule 54(b) purposes, no issue collateral to the merits.  There are no special harm (just continued trial court litigation) for a mandamus.

There being no reason to doubt the authenticity of the D&O emails, the court should have granted JAMOL to Fillius in the issue of when the defendants knew of the high drone accident rate.  The emails were strong documentary proof of their knowledge in early 2012, and defendants self-serving oral testimony was not enough to allow a reasonable jury to rule in their favor.  This case is like Scott.

The new trial grant was within JJ”s discretion.  For the reasons above, the verdict was against the great weight of the evidence.

Question 3:  (I did not teach this, but bonus points if students point out that the judgment is valid in that there is SMJ (diversity) and IPJ (decision not to pursue FAA permit made in Manhattan).)  SS will be claim precluded by the SDNY action.  She is in privity with Fillius; she controlled all of its legal strategies (she controls all decisions for the corporation), so she is the same party.  The shares at issue, and the transaction or occurrence, are the same in the two cases.  The SDNY action was the subject of two trials on the merits.

If SS is not claim precluded, then for issue preclusion, whether can SS use NMOCE against Chudley depends on whether the SDNY court was exercise FQJ and, if so, what preclusion law we use when a court has both FQJ and diversity.  We have the same issue (several of them, such as whether Chudley needed a permit, whether defendants’ conduct was outrageous), necessary to decision (these issues were basis for the SDNY judgment), and actually litigated (two jury trials). The only question is mutuality.  NY state law does not require mutuality, and if SDNY was only sitting in diversity (which SDNY clearly had), Semtek, preclude.  For FCL, under Parklane, it would depend on whether SS could have entered the SDNY lawsuit, there being no problems regarding a full ability to litigate in the SDNY action, or sufficient incentive to do so, or the foreseeability that issue preclusion might attach (I did not emphasize these last factors in class, so these are not required for full credit).

It’s clear that SDNY had diversity jurisdiction, no question, so if it was only diversity, then (i) we would use NY state law, which does not require mutuality, and (ii) in any event, SS could not have intervened as a plaintiff (SDNY defendants are all domiciled in NJ, as is SS, no diversity), so FCL would allow the NMOCE.  Semtek.  Preclude.

If the SDNY was also exercising FQJ, we might use FCL, and SS could have intervened in the SDNY litigation.  Given the near identicality of the claims, joinder of the two claims would be permissible under Rule 20, and if there was FQJ in the SDNY litigation, then there would have been FQJ in the present one, so no SMJ problem.  Did the SDNY have FQJ?  Perhaps, under Smith/Grable.  The case is functionally identical to Smith:  A SH derivative suit, key issue is compliance with a federal statute (whether Chudley had to have a permit to fly its drones), raising important issue that is at least partially legal (depending on the interpretation of the Mail-Only rule) and it doesn’t seem like too many cases would get swept up into federal court.

Do we use state law (Semtek) or FCL when a rendering federal court has both FQJ and diversity?  My guess is that the importance of the federal issues at stake in an FQJ probably would lead the federal courts to opt for a uniform federal rule.

Finally, the NY state court may issue preclude anyway.  Hart.

Question 4

SMJ:  There is diversity jurisdiction over the claim against Chudley (plaintiff = Virginia, Chudley = DE and NY, $2M+ in controversy).  Is there FQJ over the derivative claim against the D&Os?  Same issue as above.  If not, is there diversity over that claim?  No, it’s AIC deficient.  Is there supplemental jurisdiction, using the derivative as an anchoring claim?  There are two problems.  First, is the derivative claim part of the same case or controversy?  For once, it’s not clear.  There is little fact relatedness, although both involve drones in some way, the defendants are not the same.  Second, is there a 1367(b) problem?  It depends on how one reads Exxon; this is the same plaintiff suing two defendants problem I put on the board.  If one reads Exxon to draw a line between complete diversity and AIC, saying that only complete diversity must be preserved, this the exercise of supplemental jurisdiction is OK here.  If one reads Exxon to be about “against a person made a party under Rule 20,” this there is no supplemental jurisdiction here.

IPJ over the D&Os in the derivative claim.  4(k)(1)(A), could a VA state court have exercised IPJ?  Long-arm goes to the limits of the constitution.  Minimum contacts, International Shoe.  On the basis of the complaint, there is no IPJ.  Schaeffer.  There is no allegation that any of the three D&Os have done business or set foot in Va.  They might have taken action that caused a harm to a VA shareholder, but in a company the size of Chudley, that would mean IPJ anywhere.

IPJ over Chudley.  Same law as above.  There is no way to exercise general IPJ because not enough of Chudley’s business is in Va.  Daimler.  What are Chudley’s contacts connecting Virginia and this litigation?  Not many.  Chudley has unrelated contacts in its stores and its business volume and its Va drone traffic, but those don’t count for as much.    The only real contacts among Chudley, Va, and the subject matter of the litigation are the plaintiff’s domicile, the fact that the drone was on its way to a Va location (but never reached it), and the fact that the plaintiff was in VA controlling the drone at the time of the collision (but he could have controlled it from anywhere, and this seems like unilateral activity by the plaintiff, WWV).  Regarding fair play and justice, the collision occurred in WVa, and that’s where the physical evidence would be.  Key Chudley witnesses would be in WVa.  There is no strong reason for the case to be tried in VA.  IPJ seems unlikely.

Venue.  No substantial events occurred in the Eastern (as opposed to the Western) District of Va.  There is no property in the Eastern District.  No defendant resides there.  There is another district in which the case might have been brought (SDNY).  The only connection to the Eastern District is the plaintiff’s residence.

Failure to state a claim:  The specificity in the claim against the D&Os seems impeccable, down to the basis for the allegation that the D&Os knew of the high drone collision rate.  This passes plausibility, and therefore notice, pleading.  Similarly, facts are alleged which support the inference that Chudley’s conduct was negligent under either Va or WVa law in that Chudley failed to investigate its flight routes.

What about the punitive damages demand against Chudley?  Under notice pleading, there is notice given as to the transaction giving rise to the punitive damages demand.  And under Va substantive punitive damages law, the allegation of facts sufficient to support the inference of negligence would be enough.  But are there facts alleged that would nudge the contention that Chudley acted recklessly across the line from plausibility into possibility?  Probably not, if in fact what is required reckless, as opposed to negligent, conduct.  It’s clear that under WVa law, a failure to investigate, by itself, will not give rise to a finding of recklessness.

Thus, the punitive damages portion of the lawsuit will be dismissed if (i) federal (or WVa) plausibility pleading is applied to the complaint, and (ii) WVa substantive law applies.  Erie (Va notice pleading versus federal plausibility pleading) and HCOL (Va versus WVa).  Easier to do Erie first here.

Erie:

Under York, the difference would be outcome determinative of the punitive damages claim, but not the whole case.  Not clear.

Under Harlan in Hanna, if under plausibility pleading large companies who are typically defendants would become more aggressive in their conduct because they perceive less danger in litigation, they can invest more, or if private individuals become more cautious because they cannot succeed in filing lawsuits, then we should use state law to prevent the vertical difference from so changing primary conduct.  If plausibility pleading is all about avoiding high discovery costs and weeding out non-meritorious lawsuits early to preserve litigant and judicial resources, then we can tolerate the difference and should use federal law.

Under the Hanna majority/Scalia, Rule 8(a)(2) and 12(b)(6) are FRCPs that directly speak to the question at issue, and under the broad Sibbach test, they probably “really regulate procedure” and are thus valid under the REA because they speak to how a plaintiff must operate in a lawsuit.

Under the Walker/Ginsburg analysis, it’s hard to see how we can narrowly construe 8(a)(2) and 12(b)(6) here.  One might try an argument that the judicial interpretation of the rules is separate from the rules themselves, but that’s madness, and would suggest that no one has to follow SCOTUS interpretation of rules.  A conflict seems hard to avoid.

HCOL

The prompt says repeatedly that the punitive damages issue is substantive, so we cannot use forum (Va) law by saying punitive damages are procedural.  The pleading standard is clearly procedural given the breadth of things considered procedural in the HCOL context.  So we need an HCOL analysis on the punitive damages issue.  Under Klaxon, we use Va HCOL rules, so we do a second Restatement analysis.  The last event needed to give rise to the lawsuit was the collision, which occurred in WVa, so there is a presumption that we’d use WVa punitive damages law, unless a lot of factors point to Va.  They don’t.  The light contacts among Va and this litigation are discussed above in the IPJ section.  This isn’t a matter of a preexisting relationship among the parties, as per Babcock.  It’s about punitive damages, which are designed to punish and deter bad conduct.  The kind of conduct that should be punished and deterred over WVa forestland is of intense interest to WVa, but of little to Va.

Dividing the claims:  was the D&O claim properly joined to the Chudley claim under Rule 20?  Per above in the supplement jurisdiction discussion, it is unclear that the drone collision is the same transaction or occurrence as the FAA license issue; the two have few facts in common, and there’s little common evidence.  For once, it is hard to find a common issue of law or fact.  One argument might be that the high number of collisions involving Chudley drones should have put it on notice regarding both its need for an FAA license and its need to examine its drone flight paths.  We might move to dismiss or for severance under Rule 42 on the grounds of improper party joinder.
