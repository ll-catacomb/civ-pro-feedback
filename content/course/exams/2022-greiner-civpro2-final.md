The following facts will be used for Questions 3-8 of this exam.

All states relevant to this exam have long-arm statutes providing that their state courts may
exercise jurisdiction to the extent permissible under the U.S. Constitution.
All states relevant to this exam have a tort cause of action for "conversion," also known as theft.
A defendant commits the tort of conversion if they exercise wrongful dominion or control of the
plaintiff's property, assets, or money to the detriment of the rights of the actual owner.
"Property" in this context encompasses digital assets. The last event necessary for the
completion of this tort is the removal of the item converted/stolen from the control of the
plaintiff, so the tort occurs where the item was located at the time of the theft.

All states relevant to this exam have a tort cause of action for negligence. The elements of this

cause of action are that the defendant owes a duty of care to the plaintiff, that the defendant
breached this duty by failing to act with ordinary care under the circumstances, and that the
breach caused quantifiable harm to the plaintiff. In all states relevant to this exam, a plaintiff can
sue a defendant for negligence if the defendant failed to exercise ordinary care in guarding an
item (including a digital item) that the plaintiff entrusted to the defendant's care, where such
failure caused the loss (due to theft or otherwise) of that item. In such cases, the last event
necessary for the completion of the tort is the theft/conversion of the item from the
possession/control from the defendant such that the plaintiff no longer has access to the item. In
other words, in this context, the tort occurs where the item was located at the time of the theft.
In all states relevant to this exam, an affirmative defense exists to a negligence claim, but the
nature of the affirmative defense differs from state to state. In Oklahoma and South Dakota, the
defense is called "contributory negligence," and it specifies that if the defendant persuades the
trier of fact that the plaintiff's own failure to exercise due care contributed in any way to the
plaintiff's loss, the court may not award the plaintiff anything, regardless of whether the
defendant was negligent. In Massachusetts, South Carolina, Delaware, and Texas, the defense is
called "comparative negligence," and it specifies that if a defendant persuades a trier of fact that
the plaintiff's own failure to exercise due care contributed to their loss, the trier of fact reduces
the amount that the plaintiff recovers by the fraction of the loss due to the plaintiff's own failure,
and the court (if it finds the defendant negligent) orders the defendant to pay the remaining
fraction.

Texas, Oklahoma, South Dakota, and South Carolina follow the Second Restatement for
horizontal choice of law. Delaware and Massachusetts follow the First Restatement for
horizontal choice of law.

Texas has four federal district courts. San Antonio is in the Western District of Texas. Dallas is
in the Northern District of Texas.

Oklahoma has three federal district courts. Oklahoma City is in the Western District of
Oklahoma.

California has four federal district courts. San Diego is in the Southern District of California.

South Dakota, South Carolina, and Massachusetts each have only one federal district court.
Delaware and South Carolina follow federal common law in all aspects of preclusion (claim and
issue). In Oklahoma, Texas, South Dakota, and Massachusetts, if two or more separate and
independent bases support a ruling key to a judgment, they are all considered to have been
decided against the losing party for issue preclusion purposes (meaning that these jurisdictions
do not follow the Supreme Court's decision in Russell v. Place).

Sylvania, Inc. was incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Oklahoma
City, OK. Its programmers all work in a massive facility in Oklahoma City, OK. The company
specialized in selling cheap and un-hackable data storage. Sylvania was the first company to use
quantum computing to encrypt its information on its servers, so anyone hacking into its systems
except by way of a user account would obtain only gobbledygook. Users of Sylvania's data

storage could access their data from anywhere in the world. They did so through a username and
a password. Unlike most data storage companies, Sylvania did not require users to choose
passwords of any particular length, nor did passwords have to include a particular mix of
characters (e.g, numbers, letters, and special characters). Also unlike most data storage
companies, Sylvania allowed, but did not require, users to opt to enable two-factor authentication
("2FA") to log into their data storage accounts. In 2FA, a user must provide a username and a
password to initiate the login process; the system then sends a code to the user's cell phone,
which the user enters into the system to complete the login process. Thus, with 2FA, a hacker
attempting to gain access to a user's account would require both knowledge of the user's
username and password as well as possession of the user's cell phone (or some other method of
acquiring and entering the system-generated code).

Sylvania's storage methods were as follows. When a user uploaded data to one of its accounts,
Sylvania used patented technology to split the information into three pieces, none of which could
be used without the other. It stored the three pieces separately on its servers located in Dallas,
TX (in the Northern District of Texas); in Boston, MA (in the District of Massachusetts); and in
Columbia, SC (in the District of South Carolina).

Sylvania did no advertising of any kind. It depended entirely on word of mouth and
recommendations from its users. Users created accounts on its website,
sylvanianotfreedonia.com. Users paid in advance via credit card or cryptocurrency through an
interface on sylvanianotfreedonia.com. Sylvania was especially popular among artists who
created NFTs and other forms of digital crafts, and its customer base spanned the globe with no
particular geographic concentration, but was overwhelmingly European and Asian. It had few if
any US-based customers at the time period relevant to this exam.
