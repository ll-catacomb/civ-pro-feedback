## Question 1 (24 points, two parts)

In September of 2007, Congress passed the "Federal Giveaway Bank Regulation Act of 2007" (the "FGBRA"). The FGBRA created a fund of several billion dollars, controlled by the Secretary of the Treasury, designed to provide temporary loans to banks facing liquidity trouble. Section 301 of the FGBRA provided:

> (a) The Congress intends that any banks obtaining funds under this statute shall pursue liberal lending policies toward small businesses. No bank receiving funds appropriated under this statute shall charge an interest rate on any loan to a small business higher than the rate which the Secretary of the Treasury shall specify.
>
> (b) A business is considered "small" for purposes of this statute if it has less than $5,000,000 in assets.

<!-- page: 3 -->

> (c) Should a bank, after receiving funds under this statute, enter into a loan contract with a small business providing for an interest rate greater than that set by the Secretary, the loan contract shall be deemed automatically amended, as of the date of the execution of the contract, to charge the Secretary's maximum specified rate.

Georgia law defines the word "business" to mean, "in the case of an unincorporated entity, a venture that provides its principals with their primary means of livelihood." South Carolina law defines the word "business" to mean "any economic activity designed, in whole or in part, to earn a profit." Assume South Carolina, North Carolina, and Georgia preclusion law are all identical to federal preclusion law, and that all three states have declaratory judgment statutes identical to 28 U.S.C. § 2201.

In October of 2007, the Secretary of the Treasury announced that the maximum allowable interest rate under the FGBRA would be 10% for the next 24 months.

In November of 2007, Dursley Bank, Inc., a Georgia corporation with its principal place of business in South Carolina, faced a liquidity crisis. It applied for and immediately received a sizeable bridge loan from FGBRA funds.

In December of 2007, two friends, Nicolas Flamel and Severus Snape, decided to try their hands at selling intricately hand-carved, hand-painted, wooden, life-sized Harry Potter figurines that each had started making upon the publication of the first Harry Potter novel in 1997. They decided to operate out of the warehouse they had long rented as a place to keep their figurines. The warehouse, by then bursting at the seams with their creations, was located in Columbia, South Carolina. Because both Flamel and Snape worked full-time at other jobs (Flamel had lived and worked as a geologist in Charlotte, North Carolina for 20 years; Snape had lived and worked as a professor of chemistry in Columbia, South Carolina for an equal time period), they would open the warehouse to show their figurines by appointment only, typically on nights and weekends.

Needing funds for advertising, raw materials, rent payments on the warehouse, etc., each put $100,000 in a joint bank account. They then approached Dursley Bank. In January of 2008, each executed identical contracts (signed at Dursley's offices in South Carolina) for a $300,000 loan from Dursley, to be paid back over ten years, with the interest rate on each loan fixed at 15%. The contract specified that the law of Georgia would govern all of the parties' relationships.

Flamel and Snape had some initial success in sales, but in April of 2008, they fell on hard times, with the recession causing declining sales. That same month, they learned that Dursley had recently received a FGBRA loan, so they demanded that the payments on their loans be adjusted to reflect the Secretary of the Treasury's 10% interest rate. Dursley refused, contending that Flamel and Snape were not operating a "business" within the meaning of the FGBRA, but were rather pursuing a hobby that just happened to generate some money. In August of 2008, unable to make the payments under a 15% interest rate, Flamel and Snape had to liquidate their inventory, selling everything at fire sale prices. As a result, each lost his $100,000 investment.

<!-- page: 4 -->

In December of 2008, Flamel and Snape wrote a letter to Dursley demanding that the bank pay them $100,000 each in damages. Dursley did not reply.

In January of 2009, Flamel sued Dursley (demanding a jury trial) in the United States District Court for the District of South Carolina (South Carolina has only one district), alleging that Dursley's failure to accept lower monthly payments in accordance with the interest rate set by the Secretary of the Treasury under FGBRA constituted a breach of contract, and that this breach had cost Flamel damages in the amount of $100,000. Dursley answered, denying liability, and the parties began discovery.

Dursley's answer asserted that because the word "business" was not defined in the FGBRA, state law should define the word "business." Dursley contended that the parties' contract specified that Georgia state law would govern their relationship, and that Georgia state law (as quoted above) defined a "business" in terms of whether the principals earn their primary livelihoods from the venture, which Flamel clearly did not with the figurines. Dursley also contended that the grant of power to the Secretary to set interest rates in loan contracts was unconstitutional. On these two grounds, in April of 2009, Dursley moved for summary judgment.

In his summary judgment response, Flamel agreed with Dursley that because the word "business" was not defined in the FGBRA, some state's law should provide the definition. But Flamel contended that the choice of law clause in the mortgage contract was not intended to apply to definitions of terms in federal statutes; that because the contractual choice of law clause did not apply, South Carolina law should apply; and that under South Carolina law, the figurine venture was a "business." Flamel also defended the constitutionality of the FGBRA's interest provision.

On June 10 of 2009, South Carolina United States District Judge Hestia Jigger orally denied Dursley's motion for summary judgment, stating:

> I find that the FGBRA and the Secretary's order pursuant to it are constitutional. The parties agree that the law of some state should define the word "business" in the FGBRA, and so I assume they are right. But what state? The key issue in this motion is whether the choice of law clause in the Flamel-Dursley contract picks the relevant state. I don't think the contractual choice of law clause does apply to a federal statute, so I'm denying Dursley's motion for summary judgment. The thing is, if I'm wrong, and the contractual choice of law clause does govern, then Georgia law applies, the case is clearly over, and Dursley should win. This is a tough question, and I could use some help on it from the Fourth Circuit. But I don't think this issue will come up again very often because in most FGBRA-governed transactions, it will be obvious whether borrower is operating a "business."

On June 12, Dursley filed a notice of appeal of Judge Jigger's summary judgment denial, focusing on the issue of whether the choice of law clause applies here, and on the same day, Dursley initiated appeal proceedings in the United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit.

<!-- page: 5 -->

### Part (a): 6 points

**Can the Fourth Circuit hear this appeal, or otherwise consider the issue of whether the contractual choice of law clause applies to the FGBRA?**

While the Dursley Fourth Circuit proceeding was pending, the case before Judge Jigger continued. In August of 2009, after a short trial, a jury found that Dursley had breached its contract with Flamel by refusing to accept payments in accordance with a 10% interest rate and awarded Flamel $100,000. Judge Jigger entered final judgment on the jury verdict, and Dursley appealed this judgment to the Fourth Circuit; this second appeal is also still pending.

In early September of 2009, Snape wrote Dursley a letter again demanding $100,000 in damages. In response, Dursley sued Snape in the South Carolina state court for a declaratory judgment that it owed nothing to Snape. Snape answered and counterclaimed,[^1] asserting breach of contract due to Dursley's failure to reduce the interest rate due on the Snape-Dursley loan in accordance with the FGBRA. Snape's counterclaim demanded $100,000 in damages. Dursley's answer to the Snape counterclaim asserted that under the choice of law clause in the Snape-Dursley contract, Georgia law should define the word "business," and thus that Snape was not engaged in a "business" when he sold his figurines. Dursley also asserted that the grant of power to the Secretary to set interest rates in loan contracts was unconstitutional.

### Part (b): 18 points

**You represent Snape. Snape is worried about the expense of litigation (*i.e.*, your fees), and he wants to see if he can win the lawsuit quickly. How could he try to do so? Is he likely to succeed?**

## Question 2 (22 points total, no divisions/parts)

Assume the following:

- West Virginia Rule of Civil Procedure ("WVRCP") 23 consists of language identical to that of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure ("FRCP") 23. However, the Supreme Court of Appeals of West Virginia (the state's highest court) has held that "[a]ny question as to whether a case should proceed as a class in a doubtful case should be resolved in favor of allowing class certification," and that West Virginia state courts should certify classes in cases in which federal courts, applying FRCP 23, would not do so. Assume that West Virginia follows the First Restatement approach to choice of law. Assume West Virginia's long-arm statute allows its courts to exercise jurisdiction whenever the due process clause would allow them to do so.

- Although New York procedure rules allow class actions, a New York statute provides that no class action may be maintained if any part of the suit seeks recovery of a statutorily imposed penalty. The legislative history of this statute makes clear that the New York legislature was concerned that an overabundance of class action suits, and the threat of such suits, seeking civil penalties was stifling innovation in businesses operating in the state. Assume New York's long-arm statute allows its courts to exercise jurisdiction whenever the due process clause would allow them to do so.

<!-- page: 6 -->

From July of 2006 to January of 2007, as part of a national movement concerning aggressive lending practices, 37 states passed statutes creating a tort called "Mortgage Loan Fraud." All of these statutes had the following language:

> (a) a bank commits Mortgage Loan Fraud when, through misleading representations or undue encouragement, and contrary to the financial interests of a particular borrower, the bank convinces a borrower to engage in a mortgage transaction for a house located in [the relevant state], which results in the borrower's loss of the home to foreclosure.
>
> (b) whether a mortgage is contrary to "financial interests of a particular borrower" is to be assessed using a reasonable borrower standard as applied to the particular financial circumstances of that borrower at the time of the execution of the mortgage. Courts are directed to examine the particular borrower's financial circumstances, including (but not limited to) his/her income and assets, the price of the house, the house's condition, and all other relevant facts.

All states passing statutes creating this tort also passed statutes providing that the tort of Mortgage Loan Fraud was to be the exclusive remedy for fraud relating to a mortgage contract. With respect to the amount of recovery allowed, 23 (of the 37) states passed what became known as the "West Virginia" version of the statute (after the first state to pass this version), which allowed the Mortgage Loan Fraud victim to recover only actual damages from the lender. The other 14 states passed the "New York" version (after the first state to pass this version), which allowed the victim to recover actual damages "and, in addition, a statutory penalty in an amount equal to 1% of the fair market value of the home."

Foolim Mortgage Bank, a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in Manhattan, New York, makes mortgage-backed loans to persons who want to buy homes. Throughout the events described, Foolim maintained cash reserves of several billion dollars. On March 1 of 2007, Foolim announced that it would offer, until March 31 only, an adjustable rate mortgage package called the "Tom Brady Special." The Tom Brady Special was so-called because it was endorsed in a nationwide ad campaign by New England Patriots quarterback Tom Brady.

Boilerplate language in the Tom Brady Special contract included the following words:

> The Interest Rate on this loan shall remain fixed for the first year of the loan. Beginning in Year 2, and for each year thereafter, the Interest Rate for the monthly mortgage payments shall vary according to [a contractually set formula that you should not worry about].

<!-- page: 7 -->

This language was drafted in early 2007 by personnel in Foolim's principal offices in New York. For customers who chose the Tom Brady Special, beginning in Year 2 of the loan, Foolim calculated an updated interest rate each *month*. In July of 2008, Tom Brady Special customers began calling Foolim to complain, stating that they thought (because of language "for each year thereafter" in the boilerplate) that the interest rate would be recalculated at the beginning of each *year* of the mortgage (and remain the same throughout the year). Because the monthly rate Foolim was charging ended up being very high, Foolim's customers had to make substantially larger payments. The increased payments caused some Tom Brady Special customers to lose their homes when Foolim foreclosed on those homes (after the borrowers were unable to pay the higher amounts).

Two of these customers/plaintiffs particularly concern us here. Wanda Vance is a resident and domiciliary of West Virginia. On May 5, 2009, Vance, who had lost her Charlestown, WV home to foreclosure under a Tom Brady Special mortgage, sued Foolim in the United States District Court for the Southern District of West Virginia, alleging that due to the loss of her home, she was owed $100,000 in actual damages under the West Virginia Mortgage Fraud Statute. Ms. Vance sought to represent a class of "all house purchasers who had purchased Tom Brady Special mortgages from Foolim and who had lost their houses to foreclosure on or before May 5, 2009 in states that passed statutes creating the tort of Mortgage Home Fraud." The case was assigned to Judge Bellatrix Lestrange. Document production requests propounded on Foolim during class discovery showed that before May 5, 2009, Foolim had foreclosed on six Tom Brady Special mortgages in West Virginia and 35 nationwide. Vance did not seek discovery regarding the value of the homes or other financial details of the mortgage transactions. On September 28, Vance moved for class certification, but in the motion, Vance informed the court that she and Foolim had agreed to settle the Vance class claims for $1,500,000.

Neville Jorkins is a resident and domiciliary of New Jersey. Jorkins purchased a home in Manhattan, New York using a Foolim Tom Brady Special, thinking he would fix up the place, then retire there. Instead, Jorkins ended up losing the Manhattan place to foreclosure. On August 3, 2009, Jorkins sued Foolim in a New York state court (located in Manhattan), alleging that due to the loss of his home, he was owed $70,000 in actual damages and a $15,000 statutory penalty under the New York Mortgage Fraud Statute. On September 7, after answering, Foolim removed the case to the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York (which covers Manhattan), then moved to transfer the case to the Southern District of West Virginia (because the Vance litigation was already pending there). Jorkins opposed the transfer motion, but it was granted on October 12 without a written or oral explanation, and the Jorkins case was also assigned to Judge Lestrange. A week later, Jorkins filed a motion (i) to transfer the case back to the Southern District of New York, as well as (ii) for leave to amend his complaint[^2] to seek to represent a class of "all house purchasers who had purchased Tom Brady Special mortgages from Foolim and who had lost their houses to foreclosure on or before October 19, 2009 in states that passed statutes that created the tort of Mortgage Home Fraud. The class will seek all available recovery including statutory penalties in states where such penalties are available." Abbreviated discovery showed that on or before October 19, Foolim had foreclosed

<!-- page: 8 -->

eight homes in West Virginia, ten in New York, and 67 nationwide, and that the aggregate value of the 67 homes was $43,550,000 but Jorkins did not seek discovery regarding the value of the homes or other financial details of the mortgage transactions.

You are Judge Lestrange's law clerk. Judge Lestrange wonders whether the Jorkins case is properly in federal court. She asks you for a memorandum addressing how she should rule on the two class certification motions, the Jorkins motion to transfer the Jorkins litigation back to New York, and any other issues you might see. Regarding the Vance case, Judge Lestrange says not to worry about whether the Vance settlement is fair, adequate, and reasonable, as she'll take care of that; just tell her whether she should certify either the Vance class or the Jorkins class (or both). If you think of any additional facts that would be useful to know, Judge Lestrange directs you to tell her what those facts are, so that she can decide whether to request that the parties take additional discovery.

**Note:** You should not consider the Class Action Fairness Act, or the powers of the panel on Multi-District Litigation, in your answer.
