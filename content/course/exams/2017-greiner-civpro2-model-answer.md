Question 1:

The court will dismiss or transfer for lack of venue.

There is likely SMJ via diversity but not AUJ. Gringotts = NY, DE. Sp used to be Delaware, which would
destroy diversity, but her email suggests that, while living in TX, she formed an intent to stay there by
looking for a condo and demanding that her boyfriend move, which is probably enough to change her
domicile to TX. $70K in compensatory damages < $75K, so AIC depends on the HCOL viability of the
$700K punitive damages claim. TX allows the punitive damages for fraud, LA does not. The N.D.TX
(Klaxon) will use the 2d Restatement. The Sp-Gringotts relationship formed in TX at a bank branch
located there. The alleged misconduct, part of a scheme including a TX resident (triggering TX's interest
in regulating misconduct) occurred in TX. True, Sp deposited the money and found out she'd been
damaged while in LA, and her bonds became worthless due to an accident that occurred near LA, but
her location in these events seems gratuitous, due to unrelated travel (and CCC bonds are available in
numerous locations).
Regarding AUJ, there is no federal cause of action (Mottley). Accepting complaint paragraphs 8-9 as
true, Gringotts violated section 9.75, the misstatements will conclusively be deemed material for the
fraud case. Thus, the federal law violation is embedded within the state fraud claim (Smith-Grable). But
Gringotts is unlikely to contest that embedded federal issue; instead, it is likely to defend on the facts
(making the case's issues are likely factual). Plus, there is no federal government agency whose actions
are at issue, and holding that any violation of federal law that became part of a state cause of action
triggered AUJ would sweep too many cases into federal court.

There is no general IPJ (one branch is nowhere near enough of Gringott's business to make it "at home"
in TX, Daimler) but there is easily specific IPJ. FRCP 4(k)(1)(A) plus TX's long-arm going to the limits of
the Constitution result in a focus on whether the Houston negotiation between Sp and Lestrange
constitutes minimal contacts relating Gringotts, the alleged fraud, and TX (yes). Litigation in TX is fair
and reasonable because witnesses (Lestrange, Spinnet) and documents (contract, emails) are there;
because TX has an interest in regulating misconduct in its borders; and because TX law will likely apply.

Venue is improper. No event giving rise to the case occurred in north Texas (1292(b)(2)), nor does
Gringotts reside in the N.D.Tex (1291(b)(1)) because it has no branch or contacts to the N.D.Tex (1291(d,
c(2)). Spinnet could lay venue in S.D.Tex (1291(b)(2)), so no 1291(b)(3).

The complaint does state a claim with sufficient specificity (Twiqbal). We ignore paragraph 16 as
conclusory, but otherwise, there the complaint alleges with specificity via emails that the misstatement
was intentional and false, via Sp's statements that it was material, via Lestrange statements that
reliance was reasonable, and that damages resulted via the investment in risky securities, all nudging
the complaint across the line from possible to plausible.

The disparate signature requirements require a VCOL analysis in this diversity case. Under York, the
difference is outcome determinative (TXRCP 11 requires dismissal for failure to sign every page, FRCP
not so), so we use state law and dismiss. Under Harlan, the difference addresses how lawyers file
complaints not how clients act pre-litigation, so use the federal rule. Under Scalia, the signature
requirement is valid under the REA because it really regulates the manner and means of case processing,
and it is applicable because it speaks to what signatures are required, so use FRCP 11 and do not
dismiss. Under Ginsburg, one can avoid a conflict by interpreting FRCP 11 as requiring a signature but
being silent on multiple signatures, and the Weasley court held that the TXRCP purpose of encouraging
attorneys to review complaints was important, so probably use state law and dismiss.

Because venue is improper, the court must transfer. Even if venue were proper, the court should still
transfer to either the S.D.Tex., the E.D.LA, D.Del., or N.D.N.Y. Under Hoffman, the former two have
specific IPJ (key banking relationship events occurred there) and venue (ditto, 1291(b)(2)), the latter two
have general IPJ (place of incorp & PPoB) and venue (1291(b)(1)). But only for the former two do the
public and private interest factors support transfer. The events, witnesses (only Greyback is outside the
deep South), and documents described above all occurred in those two venues, suggesting convenience
of litigation and an interest in litigating the matter.

So hard to say.

Question 4:
With Sp as a named pla, we have anchoring SMJ (see above) and Ben-Hur/Exxon means no diversity or
AIC problems with unnamed plaintiffs.

Given the Greyback email inducing a common scheme, and the similarity among the statements and the
written contracts used in the various transactions, there will probably be predominance/commonality
(Walmart involved no centrally-induced scheme, rather (according to the majority) the opposite of one),
and because Spinnet is a victim of the Greyback-induced fraud scheme, representativeness and
typicality are probably satisfied. Conflicting state laws on punitives seem of lesser concern. Superiority
seems satisfied given the common policy involved and the geographic dispersion of those affected by it.

But numerosity will be lacking. Per our class discussion, LA cannot exercise IPJ over the cases that arose
outside LA, except Spinnet's (which does have substantial connection to LA) because those cases have
absolutely no contacts to LA. Three LA accounts is not numerous.

Question 6:
Spinnet may renew her FRCP 50 motion. Construing the facts (including credibility determinations)
reasonably in Gringotts's favor, could the jury could reasonably credit Lestrange's self-serving conspiracy
theories? The only element of the cause of action in dispute is whether Lestrange made false
statements to Spinnet, and whether the contract entered into evidence was genuine. The Greyback-
Lestrange emails, which Lestrange concedes came from her computer, are powerful evidence that she
did and that it is. Spinnet's testimony that the contract (with her own signature) is genuine is also
uncontradicted. Lestrange offers no story for who forged her signature or who hacked her computer or
how the hacker could have known who Spinnet (mentioned specifically in the email) was or how Spinnet
made her money, nor does she suggest why a hack occurred. Lestrange's testimony conceded
materiality, and the deposit receipt plus Greyback's and Spinnet's uncontradicted testimony
demonstrates the harm. Scott v. Harris. The R50 motion seems likely to succed.
A Rule 59 motion arguing the verdict is against the great weight of the evidence, in which setting the
trial judge has greater leeway to make credibility calls, is likely to succeed for the same reasons (Yeatts).
