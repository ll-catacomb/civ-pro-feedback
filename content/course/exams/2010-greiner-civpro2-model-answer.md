Question 1(a)

      Since we're in federal court on a state law cause of action, the MD
federal court can exercise IPJ if a MD state court of general jurisdiction
could do so.  FRCP 4(k)(1)(A).  Because MD's long-arm statute allows it to
go to the limits of the federal Constitution, the only IPJ question is
whether the Constitution allows the exercise of IPJ.

      Minimum contacts, International Shoe.  General jurisdiction over Dean
and/or Rita in Maryland is out of the question; from the facts given, it
appears that Dean has no contacts to MD other than the fact that train on
which he was riding was in MD at the time he uttered the Harry-murderer
slander.   Rita has even less.

      Re specific jurisdiction for Dean as to Count I, the contacts are
tenuous, but they probably are sufficient.  As noted above, the action that
gave rise to the tort was committed in MD.  True, the fact that this action
took place in MD was as gratuitous and accidental as was the tag in Grace
v. MacArther (admittedly an SMJ case).  So on the question of Dean's
contacts with MD, the case for IPJ in MD is not strong.  But MD has an
interest in regulating conduct that takes place within its borders, even if
the conduct is committed by transients; if Dean had beaten Harry up instead
of slandering him, for example, few would doubt that MD would have
jurisdiction in a battery suit against Dean.  Note that the fact that this
train is apparently used as a method for MD residents to travel to DC for
the day suggests that Dean might have foreseen that MD residents would hear
his slander.  Further, under the substantive law of tort, the slander was
complete (and Harry entitled to nominal damages) when the train passengers,
many of them MD residents, heard Dean's slander.  Thus, the entirety of the
tort, from action to harm and some portion of any effects of the slander
(thus the entitlement to damages), occurred in MD.  Under the First
Restatement, MD law will thus apply to the tort.  Regarding the needs of
the interstate judicial system and the convenience aspects of the
litigation, because at least some of the witnesses/recipients of the
slander were MD residents, and witnesses to this event are as likely to be
found in MD as anywhere else (the train makes this hard), MD seems a
reasonable place to try the case.  Note that Harry's (plaintiff's) interest
in an MD forum does not appear strong, as he has no connections to MD.  On
the whole, I'd say specific IPJ over Dean.  (Note Calder v. Jones is not
really helpful here because it might support IPJ over Dean in MA but
doesn't really speak to MD.)

      It seems unlikely that the MD state court could exercise IPJ over
Dean as to Count II without a supplemental IPJ doctrine that takes
advantage of an admittedly arguable (see above) exercise of IPJ over Dean
as to Count I.  The contacts among Dean, MD, and the dispute as to the
Harry-thief slander seem tenuous.  The Harry-is-a-thief slander was
separated in time and space from the slander committed in MD.  MD has no
interest in regulating slander committed (and primarily felt in terms of
damage to Harry's reputation) in MA. There is no suggestion that any MD
residents heard, or heard of, the Harry-thief slander.

      Meanwhile, re Count III, there is no evidence that Rita has any
contacts to MD except for a train ride she took that passed through the
state, and Rita's publication of the thief slander had little relationship
to MD.  The only connection to the litigation appears to be that Rita's
awareness that Dean might have different (and false, it turns out)
information about Harry was gained in MD.  And MD has even less of an
interested in regulating Rita's conduct.  Nor does Harry seem to have a
particular need to litigate against Rita in MD.  Re the needs of the
interstate judicial system and the efficiency concerns, there is good
reason to exercise IPJ here (see below, 1(b) answer), but this alone will
probably not be sufficient.

Question 1(b)
      [Note:  this is a policy question, so the following is just one tack
an answer might take.].

      The efficiency arguments for trying the three Counts together seem
fairly strong.  As to Counts I and II, witnesses who could establish Dean's
motive to slander Harry (e.g., Mary Catherine), as well as at least one who
witnessed both slanders (Rita), are in common.  In fact, it would be hard
for a jury to understand the story leading to Count II unless it heard
about Dean's jealousy and his drunken statement on the train.  Similarly,
an issue in the Count III case against Rita would be whether she had any
reason to doubt Dean's reliability as a source, again suggesting that the
facts on the train would need to come out.  Thus, the efficiency arguments
(which constitute a part of the "regular" IPJ analysis when one considers
the "needs of the interstate judicial system") strongly suggest one trial,
which can only take place if the court exercise supplemental IPJ ("SIPJ")
over Dean as to Count II and Rita as to Count III.

      Note that the efficiency arguments presume that there is no other
single court that could hear all claims against both Dean and Rita.  Here,
depending on how widely one interprets Calder, that may not be true; it
could be that a MA court could hear all three claims (the problem would be
IPJ over Dean as to Count I, as the Count I facts have little relationship
to MA, and Dean (at CT resident who goes to school in NH) has, as far as
the problem suggests, no contacts to MA except that he was visiting Boston
on the day that Rita saw him on the street.  If a MA court could exercise
IPJ over all counts, the efficiency argument in this case is less powerful,
although one might still argue for an SIPJ doctrine in general.

      The recitation above suggests that a court might consider the facts
in the two slanders to be the same transaction or occurrence ("T/O"),
because the witnesses and the evidence (particularly motive) are in common
and the facts are somewhat related.  In response to the efficiency
concerns, a court might be tempted to create a doctrine of SIPJ as a matter
of common law (the same way SCOTUS did for supplemental subject matter
jurisdiction ("SSMJ") in Gibbs, even absent a statute).  If so, the
doctrine would probably say that if a court can exercise IPJ over one
defendant as to one claim (claim = Liberty mutual definition), it can
exercise SIPJ over all other claims so related as that arise as part of the
same T/O.  The general argument here, again, would be efficiency, mirroring
SSMJ.

      But there are two reasons for caution here.  First, limits on SSMJ
stem from constitutional and prudential concerns about separation of powers
and court control.  Courts might feel as though they can create new legal
doctrine here with relative freedom.  IPJ, supposedly, involves a right
personal to the defendant, as well as the projection of state power into
other states.  Courts might be less willing to create new law when a
defendant's individual rights are involved, particularly in the absence of
a statute specifying the doctrine of SIPJ.  True, there was no statute
available in Gibbs, either, but statutes matter in IPJ, compare McGee to
Hanson and see also Schaeffer.  Second, one might read Finley in particular
and Owen as well as demonstrating a court wariness to read supplemental
jurisdiction (of any type) as allowing exercise of judicial power over a
PARTY who would not otherwise properly be before the court.  If so, courts
might be willing to create an SIPJ doctrine that would allow exercise of
IPJ over Dean as to Count II, since the court will already exercise IPJ
over Dean as to Count I.  But courts might refuse to create an SIPJ
doctrine that would allow exercise of IPJ over Rita on these fact, because
Rita is not otherwise before the court.  This SIPJ additional-claim-but-not-
additional-party distinction would mirror the holdings in Finley and Owen.
And for this reason it seems the least constitutionally suspect.

      Finally, note that there exist other tools, such as transfer, FNC,
and the joinder rules of FRCP to prevent undue abuse of an expanded
doctrine of SIPJ.  These tools all concern geographic concerns.  The only
problem with relying on them too heavily is that none are of constitutional
magnitude, so if Congress or a state legislature did something goofy (such
as eliminating all of these doctrines), and expanded doctrine of SIPJ might
be dangerous.  One could also engraft an element of discretion over the
exercise of SIPJ, as was true in the SSMJ context in Gibbs, although again,
if there were discretion, it would seem as though Congress could remove it.

Question 1(c)

      It looks as though Rita was properly joined under FRCP 20(a)(2).
Assuming that the analysis above establishes that the Rita and Dean
lawsuits are part of the same T/O, there is an issue of fact in common (the
falsity of the statement that Harry is a thief, among others).  Rita can
move under Rule 42 for separate trials, arguing that the facts of her case
would be better tried separately.  But given the common witnesses and
factual issues, per the discussion above, this is unlikely to work.





Question 2(a)

      The motion in federal court is likely to be denied.  The federal
claim sounds in tort and includes a punitive damages request, the theory
being that the def. knew of previous problems with Deathtrap tires.  The
request for previous incidents on this score is thus directly relevant, and
it seems hard to think of the request as too burdensome.  There might be
privacy issues (medical issues related to crashes), but it sees as though
those could be handled via a privacy protective order if need be.

      The motion in state court is a closer issue.  The claim there is
breach of contract/warranty, so evidence of past incidents is not directly
relevant to the claims or defenses, which is the standard.  Still, the
evidence need not be admissible in itself if it is reasonably likely to
lead to admissible evidence.  Here, information about other incidents of
tire blowouts might lead to evidence about the terrain in which the bikes
were being used (even if CRASH!, Inc. doesn't know that information
itself), which might be relevant to the issue of whether the tires were fit
for "reasonable terrain" as warranted.



Question 2(b)

      The refusal to rule on a motion to dismiss is not a final order.
There has been no entry of any sort of judgment (FRCP 54(b)), nor any sort
of certification (1292(b)), nor any sort of injunction.  A refusal to rule
is not an "order" under the collateral order doctrine.  The only way to
move things along would appear to be to petition the relevant circuit court
for a writ of mandamus forcing the district judge to rule.  This is
unlikely to work; what is the irreparable harm?  If slowness were
irreparable harm, circuit courts would be inundated with mandate petitions.
 Given the district court's control over its own docket, the right to
relief is also far from clear.

under all of the transfer statutes.  Because CRASH!, Inc.'s headquarters is

Question 2(d)

      The only basis for a stay is Colorado River (" CR" ) (none of the
other abstention bases work, plus we didn't cover them in detail in this
class, anyway).  But CR recognizes that the federal court has an
"unflagging obligation" to exercise the jurisdiction given, with the
understanding that the fact of parallel state and federal litigation alone
is not enough.  And there's little more here to suggest a stay.  A major
factor AGAINST a stay is that Piers filed the lawsuit that he's currently
asking to be stayed.  True, he filed it in federal court, but he had to
know that CRASH!, Inc. might remove (per Hertz, the company's principle
place of business is AL, not TX, and it is incorporated in DE, so it can
remove a TX suit.)  If Piers wanted to trap CRASH!, Inc. in state court, he
should have filed first in AL state court (as he eventually did).  The two
suits have progressed about the same distance, and there can't be much of a
convenience difference when both are located in Birmingham.  Only the kind
of judicial chicanery that we saw in Clark v. Lacy could lead to a stay
here, and even there the defendant (not the plaintiff) wanted the stay.
The court should not grant the stay.

      The issue on limitations and FNC depends on choice of law.  On
limitations, York tells us we have to use state law (TX or AL, see below),
but the FNC issue poses an Erie choice as between federal FNC (as
articulated in Piper) and state (TX or AL, see below).  And there are some
serious differences in federal versus state FNC law here, so we have to
choose.

      So, Erie and FNC.  We don't know anything about the judicial system
in Mexico, so we don't know whether the Mexico system provides any remedy
at all, a precondition for FNC dismissal.  And no court would dismiss on
FNC grounds without assuring that the Mexico forum would be open, so any
court would insist on a CRASH!, Inc. waiver of limitations.  Assume that's
all taken care of, let's run through the four Erie tests we know.  FNC is
probably not outcome-determinative in the sense that the York court meant,
i.e., that the FNC ruling mandates (in a legal sense) the end of the
lawsuit, meaning the court could apply federal law.  Under the Hanna
majority, there is no federal constitutional provision, statute, or rule on
point, so we look to the twin aims of Erie.  Here, the difference in FNC
law might well induce a choice of state court over federal court at the
time of filing, leading to forum shopping (although it's hard to see much
of an argument for inequitable administration of laws), suggesting state
law.  Looking to primary conduct (Harlan, Hanna concurrence), it's hard to
see how the conduct of a stateside corporation would change based on the
availability or lack of availability of FNC law with respect to foreign
consumers.  The best argument one can make here is that an extremely
liberal application of FNC law to a corporation that sells most of its
products in a city near the Mexico border might induce the corporation to
adopt a more aggressive business style, since it might know that it will
defend more suits in Mexico, where liability is presumably less likely and
less costly.  One might also argue that the corporation might place a forum
selection clause in its sales contracts.  The first seems a bit of a
stretch, and the second really isn't the sort of primary conduct Harlan was
worried about, so Harlan's concurrence suggests federal law.  In terms of
the accommodation-of-interests approach, one might argue that FNC is not a
particularly strong federal policy; it's judge-made law of venue.  The
federal interest is merely convenience; the state interest (FNC not
available in AL, rarely available in TX) might be in ease of administration
in the sense that the FNC issue will be rarely litigated in TX (never in
AL), so no line-drawing problems, and in preserving the plaintiff's choice
of forum.  It is, concededly, hard to understand how one can accommodate
both the state and the federal interests can be accommodated here, but this
seems like a case in which Justice Ginsburg might say that the federal
interest should simply yield to the state policy choice.

      If federal law, is this a good candidate for FNC dismissal?  A close
call.  We do have a foreign plaintiff, as we did in Piper.  And Mexico law
might govern the tort action (TX = last event necessary, which is the
crash, suggesting Mexico law; less clear under AL 2d Restatement approach,
one might argue that the center of gravity in this case is TX because
that's where the purchase was made). These facts are similar to Piper, and
they point to Mexico.  But the purchase was made in TX, and the evidence
regarding the tire rubber is in AL (apparently).  The plaintiff was the
purchaser (who took the product to Mexico), not someone removed from the
stateside transaction (as was true of the plaintiffs in Piper).  Perhaps
most importantly, the defendant knew that the plaintiff wanted to use the
bike in Mexico and still made the sale with oral and written promises.  My
recommendation is no FNC dismissal.

      If state law, which state?  Under Van Dusen and Ferens, the issue is
whether the Texas state (and thus the Texas federal) court had IPJ over the
def (venue not being an issue in a removed case).  Clearly so.  The def
really does do business in Texas, including the sale in this case.  So this
transfer was under 1404(a), and it came from a court with IPJ and venue.
That means TX choice of law rules apply.  FNC is likely to be characterized
as "procedural" for horizontal choice of law purposes, in which case Texas
would apply its own law.  It would seem really odd for TX to apply Mexican
FNC law to decide whether to dismiss the case in favor of a Mexican suit.
If FNC is characterized as substantive, Mexico FNC law would apply, and we
don't know the content of Mexican FNC law.  Under TX FNC law, given the
location of the CRASH!, Inc. store, neither a TX or an AL forum would be
oppressive, so no FNC dismissal.

      But what about limitations?  Under the reasoning above, TX choice of
law rules apply; we don't know with absolute certainty whether TX considers
limitations periods to be substantive or procedural for choice of law
principles, but we do know that TX follows the First Restatement, and under
it, most of the time SoLs are procedural.  If so, as seems likely, the
court should dismiss under TX's 2-year period.    If substantive, then
Mexican law will apply, and we would not dismiss under Mexico's 3-year
period.

Question 2(e)

      We're looking at preclusion; to begin, the pendency of the appeal
does not affect finality for either issue or claim preclusion.

      The theory on getting the AL state court to dismiss in favor of an
action is Mexico is that the issue of where this suit should be litigated
was decided in the federal court action.  But given AL law absolutely
prohibiting an FNC dismissal, it's hard to see how this issue arises in the
AL state court litigation.  There's no FNC issue to arise in the state
court action because whatever else is true, federal FNC law does not apply
in state court.  We might also have a problem with the necessary-to-the-
decision element of issue preclusion (we have mutuality, so we don't need
to worry about that), given the rulings in-the-alternative.  But the lack
of a  similar issue here is probably dispositve.

      The limitations dismissal is harder.  We need to know whether the
limitations dismissal is on the merits (we have same parties and same
transaction and a final judgment) under somebody's law.  We have to decide
whose law, because the law of the state in which the dismissing federal
court sat (AL) does not consider the dismissal on the merits for claim
preclusion purposes.    Probably Texas again.  The transfer under 1404(a)
(see above) is supposed to be a change of courtroom only.  Semtek didn't
cover this situation because there was no transfer.  If it's just a change
of courtroom with no effect on the law applied, the state in which the
dismissing court "sits" is TX, not AL, and so the dismissal has claim
preclusive effect.



Question 2(f)

      Numerosity is probably met; true, we have only 30 plaintiffs, but
they're spread out geographically, making it harder to sue.  Commonality
will be subsumed within predominance, so see below.  Typicality and
representativeness are probably met; Piers' claim depends on bad rubber,
and if there are other bikes that had bad rubber, this claim would be the
same as those of the other folks.  The problems are predominance and
superiority.  CRASH!, Inc.'s defense is likely to be that terrain in each
of the accidents was not "reasonable."  That requires an assessment of the
terrain at each site, which is certainly not common.  Part of this might
depend on the substantive law of the jurisdiction, meaning that it might be
hard to know whether the reasonableness of the terrain is in fact a defense
to a strict liability claim under these circumstances.  That, again, may
depend on the law of each state (even under Restatement 2 the law of the
state in which the accident happened would be a likely choice for this kind
of issue).  These concerns mitigate against predominance and superiority.
The amount of the claims involved cuts both ways.  $5K is a small claims
court case.  $150K is enough to get a reasonable attorney.  If there are
more of the latter, then it's not clear that the class action is really
needed.
