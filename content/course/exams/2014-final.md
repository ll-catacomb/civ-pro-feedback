In reading this provision, all you need know about the parties' Rule 26(f) conference is that it occurs early in the case, typically within a few weeks of the filing of the complaint. "90 days before the date set for trial" comes much, much later. That is, under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, a party need not disclose the existence of an expert witness, and produce a corresponding expert report, until many months (perhaps even years) after the filing of the complaint. That means no disclosure of the expert report until many months (perhaps years) after the initial disclosures covered by Rule 26(a).

By way of explanation: Fed. Rs. Evid. 702–703, 705 govern the admissibility of "expert testimony." Expert testimony is evidence provided by an "expert witness." An expert witness is someone who, by virtue of specialized training, knowledge, skill, or experience, is able to assist a trier of fact in understanding part of a case that would ordinarily be beyond the trier of fact's ability to adjudicate. A common use of expert testimony is in medical malpractice cases. Medical malpractice is a tort closely related to a standard negligence claim. In a medical malpractice case, a plaintiff alleges that a doctor provided negligent medical care (meaning care below the applicable standard of care), and that she was injured as a result. In such a case, then, a plaintiff might call as a witness a physician from the same specialty as the defendant's to testify that the medical care the defendant provided was negligent and that the negligence proximately caused the plaintiff's injuries. For example, a plaintiff who alleges that her former radiologist failed to diagnose cancer from MRI images might call another radiologist as an expert witness. This expert witness might testify that a radiologist of ordinary skill and exercising ordinary care would have diagnosed cancer from her MRI images.

Expert witnesses pose difficult problems for judges and juries. By definition, their testimony concerns matters that a trier of fact cannot easily evaluate. As a result, the

<!-- page: 5 -->

provisions of Rule 26 that you just read require that an expert witness provide the opposing side with an expert report detailing the basis for the witness's opinions and likely testimony, and they address when the report must be provided. And again, under the Federal Rules, a party need not disclose the existence of an expert witness, and produce a corresponding expert report, until many months (perhaps even years) after the filing of the complaint.

Assume that under a long established Massachusetts statute, a plaintiff cannot succeed in a medical malpractice claim unless she has at least one expert witness to testify that the medical care the defendant provided was negligent, and that she suffered damages as a result of the negligence.[^2] Assume that Massachusetts has a 4-year limitations period for medical malpractice actions.

Assume that the Massachusetts Rules of Civil Procedure were, as of early 2011, identical to the Federal Rules. In March of 2011, however, through a state-level process similar to that of the federal Rules Enabling Act, the following subsection was added to Ma. R. Civ. P. 26:

> **Ma. R. Civ. P. 26(a)(1)(F):** Notwithstanding any other section of this Rule 26 or any other provision in these Rules, in any civil action in the nature of medical malpractice in which the testimony of an expert witness is necessary to establish liability, the plaintiff shall attach to her compliant (at the time of filing) and serve upon the defendant a report of the medical expert witness or witnesses she intends to call to support her claim. The report shall include the items specified in Ma. R. Civ. P. 26(a)(2)(B) [which is identical to Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(a)(2)(B), quoted above]. If a plaintiff fails to comply with this subsection, a court shall grant a motion to dismiss the complaint.

## Question 2

New Jersey uses the first restatement for horizontal choice of law. Both New York and New Jersey have long-arm statutes that go to the limits of the federal constitution. Both states have declaratory judgment statutes identical to the federal declaratory judgment statute. New Jersey substantive (in both the horizontal and vertical choice of law senses) tort law prohibits punitive damages for negligence actions. New York substantive (in both the horizontal and vertical choice of law senses) tort law allows unlimited punitive damages for negligence actions. Both New York and New Jersey have four-year limitations periods for negligence lawsuits.

In the tort law in both New York and New Jersey, there is a doctrine called "negligence per se." Roughly speaking (and sufficient for the purposes of this problem), the doctrine of negligence per se states that an actor is negligent if, without excuse, the actor violates a statute or a regulation that is designed to protect against the type of accident that the actor's conduct causes, and if the accident victim is within the class of persons the statute or regulation is designed to protect.[^3]

New Jersey has only one federal district, the United States Federal District Court for the District of New Jersey. New York has four federal districts; Manhattan is located in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York.

In 2012, the United States Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (the "CFPB"), a federal agency with the power to regulate financial products and transactions involving consumers (but not to create a federal cause of action), promulgated a regulation entitled "Credit Card Default Rates: Duty to Warn." In relevant part, the regulation specified as follows:

> **Credit Card Sold Over the Internet**
>
> **§ 5633:** Any financial institution offering credit cards on an Internet website shall at all times include the following information, in at least 13-

<!-- page: 10 -->

> point typescript and in a clearly visible location, on the webpage in which such offer shall be contained:
>
> (a) The credit limit of the credit card offer; and
>
> (b) The interest rate or rates offered; and
>
> . . . [Other requirements not relevant to this problem]
>
> (h) A statement of the percentage of previous customers who have credit card accounts with the financial institution and whose accounts the financial institution has declared to be in default.

Accompanying the regulation was the following CFPB commentary:

> *The purpose of subpart (h) of section 5633 is to protect consumers. Potential credit card customers should know whether the financial institution from which they are contemplating purchasing credit card services has a high rate of consumer default. Consumers might be able to use this information to evaluate whether the institution's offers seem "too good to be true" or are being marketed to persons who will, in truth, unable to meet the terms of the credit agreements.*

Neither Congress nor the CFPB created a federal private cause of action by which a plaintiff might enforce section 5633.
