Notes on Question 1:  Policy question, lots of different directions in
which students could go.

Rule 126 goes beyond anything required by Bell Atlantic and Iqbal in that
it requires not just that detailed factual allegations be made but that
actual evidence be attached to the complaint.  This rule is wholly
inconsistent with the idea of notice pleading, and goes well beyond code
pleading.  It essentially requires a plaintiff to prove a prima facie case
in order to survive a motion to dismiss.

Bonus points if students realize that this rule is not really a rule of
pleading but a form of advanced initial disclosure that must be made even
if the defendant does not appear in the lawsuit.  This is not required,
however, as we did not study initial disclosure.  To get full credit, an
answer must refer to the fact that the rule imposes a form of discovery on
the plaintiff at the pleading stage, and ask whether this is appropriate at
the pleading stage.  References to the requirement of a document production
will receive full credit.



There is virtually no argument that a right to counsel applies in this type
of lawsuit.  Even under Lassiter, the case for mandatory counsel would
stink, and it definitely stinks under Turner.  There is no apparent danger
of incarceration at all, and thus under Lassiter no presumptive case for
counsel.  But there is an argument that Rule 126 might be constitutionally
required under Turner (not a strong argument, but an argument).  One can
recite the Matthews factors formulaically, but the basic idea here is that
the Delaware Supreme Court has attempted to find a way short of counsel to
provide access to justice for credit card debt collection defendants.
Thus, Rule 126 is analogous to the "form" to which SCOTUS attributed nearly
mythical properties in Turner.  At present, however, without a recognized
constitutional right of access to justice, this argument is fairly weak.

One argument here, again invoking the Matthews factors (the third one this
time), is that it is hard to see how much of a harm collection agencies
suffer here, unless they are depending on default judgments, which (as the
Delaware Supreme Court stated) are disfavored by public policy.  The first
thing any competent lawyer would do in this kind of lawsuit is to issue a
request for production of documents that focuses precisely on the documents
required by Rule 126.

On the other hand, rules of procedure specific to a particular type of
lawsuit are always messy.  Line-drawing problems are always present.  Would
Rule 126 apply if, for example, a debt collection lawsuit is filed for a
debt that includes both credit card and other kinds of debt? Contracts
suits in which we'd expect the defendant to have greater access to the
contact documents (as in employment and benefits contracts)?  What about
oral contracts?  Lawsuits not based on contracts that do not involve
documents?

The rule could affect the market for credit card debt.  If full
documentation is required on each and every debt, the market for debt might
become less liquid.  But perhaps not by all that much.  Again, these would
be the first documents a debtor's lawyer would request.  Thus, the effect
on liquidity might depend on how often debtors are forced to self-represent
in these cases.
Question 2(a):

There are six potential options for locations to file lawsuits:  Delaware
state and federal courts, Massachusetts state and federal courts, and New
Hampshire state and federal courts.

Rule 126 will definitely apply in DE state court.  That's bad, because we
apparently don't have the required documents.  Would it apply in either MA
or NH state courts?  No.  The issue of whether a plaintiff in a credit card
debt collection lawsuit must attach certain specified documents to its
complaint is procedural for horizontal choice of law purposes (since the
scope of "procedural" in horizontal choice of law is broad, and almost
anything that is arguably procedural is considered so).  So MA and NH state
courts would use their own law on whether we would have to attach the
documents to a complaint against Pansy and/or Draco, and they don't have
anything close to Rule 126.

Would Rule 126 apply in any of the three federal courts?  Because NH and MA
state courts would not apply Rule 126, neither would NH or MA federal
courts.  Even if the issue of documents needed for the complaint were
considered "substantive" for vertical choice of law, a NH (MA) federal
court would use NH (MA) choice of law rules, Klaxon, and as described
above, a NH (MA) state court would use its own law under a horizontal
procedural/substantive distinction, so no Rule 126.

What about a Delaware federal court?  Erie, four tests.  Under York, Rule
126 would determine the outcome of the case if we sue without attaching
documents, so Rule 126 would apply.  Under the Hannah majority, Fed. Rs.
Civ. P. 8(a)(2), 12(b)(6) require that a complaint include only a short,
plain statement showing that the plaintiff is entitled to relief, not
documents/evidence.[1]  And it is hard to see how these rules expand,
modify, or alter substantive rights, so they would appear to be valid.
Thus, under Hannah majority step one, it would appear that Rule 126 would
not apply.  Could we do a Walker on these Rules, by narrowly construing
them?  Perhaps.  This would actually look a lot like an application of
Justice Ginsburg's opinion in Shady Grove.  Perhaps the idea here would be
to say that Fed. Rs. Civ. P. 8(a)(2), 12(b)(6) speak to the issue of what
must be alleged in the complaint, not what must be attached to it.[2]
Under this theory, we would say that there is no federal law on the
subject, no contradiction, and state law can apply.  We might reach the
same answer if we try to balance the state and federal interests involved.
The federal interest is in maintaining a transsubstantive pleading rules
(the Twiqbal standard, although that doesn't matter).  Delaware's interests
are specific to this type of litigation and well grounded in an
investigation of what is actually happening in its courts, as disclosed by
the detailed comments accompanying the rule.  There is at least an argument
that DE's interests in this specific type of lawsuit, interests based in a
serious investigation, might trump the federal interests here.

      Under Harlan's Hannah concurrence, the issue is difficult.  On the
one hand, it would be a stretch to argue that Rule 126 would affect how
consumers use credit cards and whether they become delinquent.  But the
Rule might affect the collection market for credit card debt; purchasers of
debt would definitely need to make sure they procured documents underlying
each and every debt purchased.  That could get cumbersome.  See answer to
Question 1.  The question here might turn on what kind of primary conduct
Harlan was concerned about; the initial credit card user's primary conduct
(and that of the credit card issuer), or the collection market for credit
card debt.  I'd go with the former, so I'd say no effect in primary
conduct, and given Fed. Rs. Civ. P. 8(a)(2), 12(b)(6), Rule 126 does NOT
apply.

      To summarize:  Rule 126 would apply in DE state court, might apply in
a DE federal court, and won't apply anywhere else.

      Next question:  could we sue both Pansy and Draco in a single lawsuit
anywhere?  We could probably do so in DE state court.  Under Rule 20
(recall same rules of civ pro everywhere), there are common questions
everywhere, such as whether Rule 126 applies, whether the chain of
transfers from the original credit card issuer to G-Credit was valid, etc.
The harder issue is whether Pansy & Draco's lawsuit comes from the same
T/O.  Per below, it would appear as though the two debts are separate T/O
for preclusion purposes, because by analogy to coupons and bonds, we want
it to be possible to sell the debts separately on a collection market (see
Cromwell v. County of Sac).  But the policies underlying Rule 20 are
different; here, the two debts have a great deal in common, so we'd want
them to be litigated together.  So we're OK under the rules.  What about
personal jurisdiction?  Probably OK here as well, on either a general
jurisdiction theory (if Pansy and Draco are still domiciled in Delaware,
see below) or a specific jurisdiction theory (the original credit card
debts arose from charges they made in Delaware, so plenty of International
Shoe minimum contacts, with the long-arm no problem because it goes to the
limits of the constitution).

      Delaware federal court:  The previous paragraph suggests that we'd be
OK under FRCP 20 to sue Pansy and Draco jointly, and that under FRCP
4(k)(1)(A), the Delaware federal court could piggyback onto the Delaware
state court's IPJ so as to exercise IPJ over both Pansy and Draco.  Could
we lay venue?  Yes; again the debts arose in Delaware, and that's "a
substantial part of the events . . . giving rise to the claim occurred"
under section 1291(a)(2).

      What about subject matter jurisdiction?  There are two issues:
First, where are Pansy and Draco domiciled, second, can any federal court
exercise supplemental jurisdiction over a suit by us against Draco, given
that it is AIC-deficient on its own?  On the first issue, the problem is
that we are domiciled in NH and MA, so if Draco (Pansy) is domiciled in MA
(NH), we lack diversity.  Pansy and Draco were obviously domiciled
initially in DE, and if they are still domiciled there, we have complete
diversity.  Did their domiciles shift, Draco's to MA and Pansy's to NH,
when they lost their jobs, their house, and had to move in with their
parents?  Not 100% clear.  Certainly their permanent ties to DE were
severed, but they continue to have something of a marriage (communicating
somewhat regularly and applying for many of the same jobs, suggesting a
desire to live in the same location).  Perhaps more importantly, Draco
(Pansy) does not appear to have laid down serious roots in MA (NH).  Draco
(Pansy) has been in MA (NH) for a couple of years now, but time is not
dispositive (students attending 4-year colleges typically don't change
their domiciles).  Job applications appear to be focusing as much on DE as
anywhere else.  It's hard to know whether Draco's (Pansy's) housing
situation with his (her) parents is permanent.  Overall this is a close
call.

      Now, suppose that Pansy and Draco are domiciled in DE, so we have
complete diversity, but that leaves the Exxon/AIC/supplemental jurisdiction
issue.  There is no arising under jurisdiction in the picture.  Our lawsuit
against Pansy has $90K in controversy, so it's the anchoring claim.  The
question is whether we can tack on Draco's $40K lawsuit, which is AIC
deficient.  The arguments are familiar from Exxon.  On the one hand, under
1367(b), Draco's lawsuit is a claim by a plaintiff (us) against a person
(Draco) made a party under Rule 20, and the exercise of jurisdiction over
Draco's lawsuit would arguably "would be inconsistent with the
jurisdictional requirements of section 1332" since Draco's lawsuit is AIC
deficient.  On the other hand, in Exxon, SCOTUS appeared to conclude that
"the jurisdictional requirements of section 1332" refer only to complete
diversity, not the AIC, in which case we can proceed against both Pansy and
Draco simultaneously in DE federal court.

      What about MA (NH) state courts?  See above on the Rule 20 issues.
One problem whether either court can exercise personal jurisdiction over
either defendant.  General jurisdiction theories for (MA for Draco, NH for
Pansy) depend on the domicile issue, see above.  We could pretty easily tag
Draco (Pansy) in MA (NH), so that would be a big factor in our favor.  The
far bigger issue is whether we could sue both Draco AND Pansy together in a
single lawsuit in either a MA or NH state court.  The problem is that Draco
appears to have absolutely no contacts to NH other than the fact that he
occasionally visits Pansy, and vice versa for Pansy.  There is the
additional factor for Pansy that has applied for jobs in MA, but none of
these jobs has come through yet.  None of Draco's (Pansy's) contacts to NH
(MA) related to the subject matter of the lawsuit.  Absent a tag of both in
either NH or MA, it's hard to see how a MA (NH) court could exercise IPJ
over Pansy (Draco).  Of course, as neither Pansy nor Draco appears to have
counsel or be able to afford counsel, we could sue them and see if they
fail to raise IPJ (seems likely), in which case they would have waived the
issue.

      What about MA (NH) federal court?  The SMJ issues are the same as
those in DE federal court.  Under Rule 4(k)(1)(A), the personal
jurisdiction issues are the same as those in MA and NH state courts.  The
problem is venue.  Because we can lay venue in DE federal court under
1391(a)(2), that means 1391(a)(3) does not apply, and there does not appear
to be a basis to lay venue against both Pansy and Draco simultaneously in
either a MA or NH federal court.

Question 2(b)

      Pansy and Draco ("PD") can move under Rule 50 for JAMOL and under
Rule 59 for a new trial.  On Rule 50, we can argue that PD did not move for
JAMOL prior to the jury's verdict, so they did/could not "renew" their
motion under 50(b).  Whether we would win on this depends on how
technically the judge interprets the rule requiring the pre-verdict motion.
 On the one hand, PD clearly put the judge on notice that they were raising
preclusion.  On the other hand, they never explicitly raised the issue of
whether the evidence was such that a rational jury could reach only one
verdict.  We can argue that since we did not understand PD's pre-verdict
statement to constitute a motion, we did not get a chance to request that
the judge allow us to investigate additional evidence we might have put on
(such as the documents from the F-Credit lawsuit).  This feels pretty lame.
 If the judge finds that PD did raise the issue sufficiently pre-verdict,
the issue will be whether, construing all facts/testimony in the light most
favorable to us, a rational jury could have found in our favor.  On the one
hand, PD bear the burden of persuasion on these facts, and they could have
bolstered their case with official records; on the other hand, we have no
evidence contradicting PD's version of events.  If the judge holds that PD
preserved the Rule 50 issue, she might well grant the Rule 50 motion.  If
the judge holds that PD did not preserve the Rule 50 issue, she will refuse
to consider the JAMOL motion.

      What about Rule 59?  There were no objections to evidence or to jury
instructions, so PD's best argument would be that a new trial is warranted
because the verdict on the third special question is against the great
weight of the evidence.  For the reasons discussed in the previous
paragraph, this seems like a serious possibility.  The only evidence
regarding preclusion was Pansy's testimony.  The new trial might be limited
only to the issues raised by the third question, meaning we would not have
to retry the issues raised by the first two questions.

      Now, assuming that the facts are as Pansy represented them to be in
her testimony, would preclusion constitute a defense for either P or D?
Both claim and issue preclusion are potentially at issue.

      Will claim preclusion work for Pansy?  Yes.  On same claim, the F-
Credit debt was exactly the same as this one.  The judgment of dismissal
was final and was with prejudice.  F-Credit and G-Credit are not the same
party, but G-Credit bought the rights from F-Credit to Pansy's debt and
would thus be in privity with F-Credit.  G-Credit could not buy more than F-
Credit had to sell.

      Will claim preclusion work for Draco?  Probably not.  For the reasons
in the previous paragraph, we have the same party and the finality.  The
problem is whether Draco's debt is the same claim as Pansy's.  Yes, the
word "claim" in claim preclusion is to be construed broadly in a T/O sense,
and there are a lot of common facts:  the card numbers are sequential,
suggesting they were issued close in time; they were issued to a married
couple; payments were made from a joint checking account.  But none of this
is likely to be enough.  Debts are different because we want for there to
be a liquid market for things like debts, bonds, coupons, etc.   Cromwell,
Jones.  Although it turned out that Pansy and Draco's debts were sold
together, they could have been sold to different collection agencies.  If
Pansy and Draco's debts were considered the same claim for claim preclusion
purposes, they could not have been sold separately.  And whatever we think
of collection agencies, we want the debts to be separable to allow this
sort of sale debts to happen.

      Will issue preclusion work for Draco?  In NH, we do NOT need same
parties for issue preclusion.  Thus, MA law regarding mutuality makes no
difference.  Under section 1738, a MA courts must give at least as much
preclusive effect to the NH judgment in the F-Credit lawsuit as would a NH
court, and a NH court would not require mutuality, the MA court cannot
require it, either.  Do we have the same issue?  Not 100% clear, but
probably so.  The facts surrounding the transfer of Draco's debt from E-
Credit to F-Credit are identical to those surrounding the transfer of
Pansy's debt from E-Credit to F-Credit.  The issue might be phrased as
whether any debts in the batch from E-Credit to F-Credit were in fact
actually transferred.  Was the issue necessary to the judgment in the F-
Credit lawsuit?  To the extent there was a sufficiently final judgment,
yes.  Was the issue actually litigated?  To the extent we have a
sufficiently final ruling, the issue was actually litigated.  Do we have a
sufficiently final judgment that depended on the issue at hand?  Unclear.
The jury reached a verdict, but F-Credit sought JAMOL, and that motion was
never adjudicated.  Judgment on the verdict was never issued.  On the one
hand, the jury verdict alone lacks some of the safeguards that we would
like to see for finality.  On the other hand, a jury did issue a verdict
after a trial, something that hardly ever happens, and it would be
inefficient to try the same issue again (something Pansy was unable to do
in any event, probably because she had no lawyer).  Finally, what about due
process?  This may tip the balance.  G-Credit has never had an opportunity
to litigate whether E-Credit actually transferred the rights to PD's debts
to F-Credit.  Technically, this should not matter, because F-Credit and G-
Credit are in privity, see above, but this might tip the balance in a real
litigation setting.

      Will issue preclusion work for Pansy?  Same discussion as in previous
paragraph.

Question 2(c):

      At first, this would seem to be a classic instance of a 23(b)(1)(A)
or 23(b)(2) class action.  The common issue is whether G-Credit must comply
with Rule 126, and whether Rule 126 is constitutional.  The class would
appear to be numerous in that G-Credit buys thousands of debts and,
apparently, does substantial business in Delaware courts.  Only injunctive
relief is requested, and the relief requested would appear to be common to
the class, so 23(b)(2) would appear to be met.  The problems are with Pansy
as a named plaintiff and the typicality and representativeness
requirements.  Pansy has no debts outstanding that are owed by G-Credit or
that might be bought by G-Credit in the future.  Pansy was sued in
Massachusetts, not Delaware.  Pansy has no current beef at all with G-
Credit, she is not a member of the to-be-certified class, and she has never
had a problem with G-Credit that possibly implicated Rule 126.  If we
oppose, the court will deny class certification.
