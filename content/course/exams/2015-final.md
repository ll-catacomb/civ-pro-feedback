## Background Law (for Questions 2–4)

First, a statement of some tort law concepts that may or may not exist outside of this exam, but that you should assume to be true for the purposes of this exam.

- If the directors and officers of a corporation make an unreasonable decision that subjects a corporation to the potential for substantial legal liability, such as buying worthless bonds or operating a business without a needed license, a shareholder of that corporation can file what is called a "shareholder's derivative" suit. The theory of a shareholder's derivative suit is that an unreasonable decision potentially subjecting the corporation to liability hurts the corporation and thus the value of the shares the shareholders own. Thus, directors and officers should have to pay the corporation (and, indirectly, the shareholders) damages. The result: plaintiff shareholders can sue in a lawsuit against the directors and officers (meaning, for the purposes of this exam, the chief executive officer and the members of the board) for the decreased value of their shares due to the defendants' unreasonable decision. Every state relevant to this exam has this law.

- If a corporation (through any of its employees) causes harm to another person by failing to act with ordinary or due care under the circumstances, it can be sued for a tort called "negligence." To prevail on a negligence claim the plaintiff must show: (1) The Defendant had a duty of care as to the Plaintiff, (2) that duty was breached, (3) that breach caused the plaintiff's injuries; and (4) damage resulted. Every state relevant to this exam has this law. Some states allow for punitive damages in negligence claims. In other states, a plaintiff is entitled to punitive damages only if it shows that the defendant acted recklessly, meaning with unreasonable disregard of a known risk. More on this below.

From 2007 to 2010, the United States saw an euxplosion in the number of business entities using mechanical flying drones for their business, which ranged from information provision to mail delivery. In addition, private drone owners filled the skies with recreational machines. In 2010, responding to this national emergency, Congress passed what became known as the Mail-Only Act of 2010. In relevant part, Section 101 of the Mail-Only provides as follows:

> a) Any corporation or other business entity engaged in the business of delivering mail may use mechanical flying drones to effectuate such mail delivery business without a license from the Federal Aviation Administration (the "FAA"), provided that
>
> > 1) such mechanical flying drones maintain a safe distance from airports, skyscrapers, and other locations that the FAA shall designate by rule, and
> >
> > 2) such mechanical flying drones at all times (except when taking off and landing) operate between 500 and 1500 feet altitude.
>
> b) Except as provided in subsection (a), above, no individual or corporation or other business entity may fly a mechanical flying drone above 30 feet of altitude without a license from the FAA.
>
> c) The FAA may, in its discretion, sue any individual, corporation or other business entity, or state government agency for any violation of subsection (b), above. If the FAA should prevail in such lawsuit, it may collect a statutory penalty of up to $5000 for each unlawful mechanical flying drone flight.

In 2012, two years after the passage of this statute, a leading commentator on aviation matters wrote in a blog: "Congress, unfortunately, left the critical term 'mail delivery' undefined in the Mail-Only statute. Congress also failed to create a federal cause of action for anyone injured by a company flying a drone without a license. The FAA so far has not used its power under subsection c) to sue anyone. And the FAA has been charging hundreds of thousands, or in some cases (depending on the size of the business) tens of millions, of dollars for licenses to fly mechanical drones, so only a few businesses are buying the licenses. Thus, it still remains unclear who is allowed to fly drones, and our national problem of too many unlicensed drones in

the skies continues. Because businesses use drones in so many different ways, the question of what counts as 'mail delivery' is a mixed question of law and fact. And in any particular lawsuit, it could be a hard one, costing lots of money to litigate."

Chudley Cannons, Inc. is incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Manhattan, New York, which is located within the geographic jurisdiction of the Southern District of New York. All major Chudley corporate decisions are made in Chudley's Manhattan offices. Chudley is a large "big box" general purpose retailer. It maintains multiple stores in every state and sells around $280 billion in merchandise in the United States each year. All major Chudley business decisions are made by three individuals, its two corporate board members, Rufus Scrimgeour and Cornelius Fudge, and its corporate chief executive officer, Delores Umbridge. Scrimgeour, Fudge, and Umbridge are lifelong friends. All three have lived for years in a luxurious, gated, 60-acre compound with three residences (one for each) in Newark, New Jersey and commute together to downtown Manhattan in a corporate bus.

A major piece of the Chudley business model is its "instant store pickup delivery." Under this delivery system, a customer can purchase an item weighing up to 25 pounds online and, within five hours guaranteed (or the item is free), pick that item up at a Chudley store. To effectuate this delivery system, Chudley maintains a fleet of 5000 flying mechanical drones, which it uses to move merchandise from warehouses to stores and between stores. Chudley has never applied for or held an FAA flying mechanical drone license. Its corporate documents indicate that it takes the position that its transfers of merchandise among its business locations via flying mechanical drone constitute "operating a 'mail delivery' business for purposes of the 'Mail-Only' Act because we are delivering merchandise that would otherwise have been delivered by mail."

In January of 2013, the FAA publicly announced that it was initiating an investigation of Chudley's instant store pickup delivery on the grounds that it might not be operating a "mail delivery" business within the meaning of the Mail-Only Act. The FAA announcement indicated that in 2011 it had received reports of over 500 in-air collisions involving Chudley mechanical drones, with the collisions involving objects as diverse as other drones, hot air balloons, and church steeples. The FAA further revealed that its information suggested that over the last four years, Chudley had operated around 100,000 flights, potentially exposing it to up to $500,000,000 in liability under the Mail-Only Act.

Septima Slughorn, a New Jersey domiciliary, is an investor. She is the chief executive officer and sole shareholder of Fillius, Inc., a Maine corporation with its principal place of business in Portland, Maine. Slughorn invests only through Fillius, Inc. She makes all of Fillius, Inc.'s investment, business, and legal decisions, and she employees a skeleton staff of three analysts, an IT professional, and a clerical assistant. In December of 2012, Fillius, Inc. owned about 20,000 shares of Chudley stock, which was worth at the time about $2,000,000. After the FAA's announcement, the value of Chudley stock plummeted by approximately 15%, and as of February, 2013, Fillius, Inc.'s Chudley stock was valued at about $1,700,000.

In March of 2013, Fillius, Inc. filed a shareholder's derivative action in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York against Scrimgeour, Fudge, and Umbridge. The

District Judge Bertha Jorkins set the case on an accelerated litigation track. Judge Jorkins denied Scrimgeour, Fudge, and Umbridge's summary judgment motion and granted Fillius Inc.'s summary judgment motion, holding that no genuine issue of material fact existed on the matter and that as a matter of law, Chudley was not engaged in the business of "mail delivery" within the meaning of the Mail-Only Act. In April of 2014, the case proceeded to trial on the issues of whether Scrimgeour, Fudge, and Umbridge's conduct was unreasonable and/or outrageous. The case went to trial in April of 2014; both parties had filed jury trial demands, and so Judge Jorkins sat a jury. A key issue in Fillius, Inc.'s request for punitive damages was the time at which Scrimgeour, Fudge, and Umbridge first learned of the fact that there were approximately 500 accidents in 2011 involving Chudley flying mechanical drones. It was clear that if the defendants knew of this problem in early 2012, then the plaintiff was entitled to punitive damages. On this point, there were only two pieces of evidence.

- The testimony of the three defendants themselves, Scrimgeour, Fudge, and Umbridge, that they first became aware of the problem when the FAA announced it in January of 2013. All three testified that none of their lower level employees had informed them of the problem, nor did any remember seeing any documents or reports, internal or external.
- Three emails recovered from the corporate email accounts of all three defendants and admitted into evidence. The first email was from Scrimgeour to Fudge, and Umbridge, dated March 13 of 2012, and it attached a report from a non-profit organization dedicated to drone safety documenting 488 accidents involving Chudley flying mechanical drones had occurred from January to late November, 2011. The text of this first email from Scrimgeour stated as follows, "To: Fudge, Umbridge. The attached is for your eyes only. I've asked our employees not to circulate this. We need to get out in front of this problem, or it could spell big trouble for us down the road." The second email was from Fudge to the other two. It said, "Agreed. That accident rate is way too high." The third was from Umbridge to Fudge and Scrimgeour, saying "Let's discuss in our next meeting and hope that the FAA doesn't find out any time soon."

- Chudley has 15 big box store locations in Virginia and does about $1 billion in sales revenue in the state.
- Chudley has 8 big box stores in West Virginia and does about $400 million in sales revenue there.

- Regarding Virginia law:
  - Virginia requires full mutuality for issue preclusion.
  - Virginia uses the Second Restatement for horizontal choice of law.
  - Virginia's substantive law of tort allows punitive damages for negligent conduct; a plaintiff need not prove recklessness to be entitled to receive punitive damages. Further, Virginia's substantive law of tort makes clear that a failure to investigate facts that might have prevented an accident can constitute negligence, when a company exercising ordinary care under the circumstances would have conducted such an investigation.
  - Virginia's rules of civil procedure as written are substantially similar to the Federal Rules, but Virginia does NOT follow the "plausibility pleading" standard of *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal*. Instead, the Virginia Supreme Court has held that the state follows the "notice pleading" standard of *Dioguardi* and *Conley v. Gibson*.
  - Virginia's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the constitution.
- Regarding West Virginia Law:
  - West Virginia does not require mutuality for issue preclusion.
  - West Virginia uses the First Restatement for horizontal choice of law.
  - West Virginia's substantive law of tort does not allow punitive damages for negligent conduct. To receive punitive damages under West Virginia substantive tort law, a plaintiff must show that the defendant engaged in reckless conduct, which West Virginia describes as conscious knowledge of a risk and a deliberate decision to disregard it. Further, West Virginia's substantive law of tort makes clear that a failure to investigate facts that might have prevented an accident can constitute negligence, when a company exercising ordinary care under the circumstances would have conducted such an investigation. But failure to conduct an investigation does not ordinarily support the finding of recklessness needed to support punitive damages under West Virginia law.
  - The West Virginia rules of civil procedure are identical to the federal rules, and the West Virginia Supreme Court has held that the state follows *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal* pleading standards.
  - West Virginia's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the constitution.
