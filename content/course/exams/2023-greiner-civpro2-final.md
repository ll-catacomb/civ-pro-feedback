## The Law

All states relevant to this exam had a common law cause of action for negligence. To prevail on a common law negligence cause of action the Plaintiff must show: (1) The Defendant had a duty of care as to the Plaintiff, (2) that duty was breached, (3) that breach caused injuries to the Plaintiff's person or property, and (4) damage resulted to the Plaintiff. The Plaintiff must prevail on all elements to win. With respect to duty, every person owed every other person a duty to exercise ordinary care in any interaction.

All states relevant to this exam had long-arm statutes going to constitutional limits.

California law provided an affirmative defense to a negligence cause of action called "contributory negligence." As discussed in class, under this affirmative defense, if a defendant in a negligence case proved that the plaintiff's conduct was in any way negligent, and that the plaintiff's negligence caused in any part (no matter how small the contribution) the plaintiff's injury, the plaintiff received no damages. Thus, if a jury found that a defendant 99% percent at fault and a plaintiff 1% at fault for an accident in which the plaintiff suffered a $1000 injury, the plaintiff received no damages.

<!-- page: 3 -->

New York and Texas law provided a different affirmative defense to a negligence cause of action called "comparative negligence." As discussed in class, under this affirmative defense, if a defendant in a negligence case proved that the plaintiff's conduct was in some way negligent, and that the plaintiff's negligence caused in part the plaintiff's injury, the trier of fact apportioned fault between the plaintiff and the defendant, and the court awarded damages to the plaintiff in accordance with the parties' respective fault. If a jury found that a defendant was 99% percent at fault and a plaintiff 1% at fault for an accident in which the plaintiff suffered a $1000 injury, then the court would order the defendant to pay the plaintiff $990, not $0 nor the full $1000.

California and Texas used the First Restatement for horizontal choice of law.

California did not require any form of mutuality for the application of issue preclusion. Texas required that for issue preclusion to run, the parties in the second lawsuit must have been the same, and have been in the same procedural posture, as in the first lawsuit.

Remember what you already know about New York law.

In the 2015 case of *Nynaeve v. Mat*, the New York Court of Appeals (New York's highest court) held that in considering a motion for a preliminary injunction under New York state law, "a court should consider only the public interest. In our court system, we recognize that judges cannot predict the future and thus are unlikely to know at an early stage of litigation who is likely to prevail on the merits. Similarly, the harms litigants may suffer are of less importance than the interests of the public. Therefore, courts in our state in determining whether to grant a preliminary injunction motion will consider only the public interest and no other factors." In 2017, in *Egwene v. Rand*, the Supreme Court of California (California's highest court) stated, "In our state, we find the federal doctrine regarding preliminary injunctions persuasive, and we use it our state." Preliminary injunction law is procedural for horizontal choice of law purposes.

New York had four federal district courts. The Southern District of New York had jurisdiction over Manhattan and Queens in New York City. The Eastern District of New York had jurisdiction over Brooklyn in New York City. The Northern District of New York had jurisdiction over Albany, New York state's capital city. The Western District is not relevant to this exam.

Texas had four federal district courts. Austin was in the Western District of Texas. Fort Worth was in the Northern District of Texas. The other districts have no relevance to this exam.

California had four federal district courts. Sacramento was in the Eastern District of California. The other districts have no relevance to this exam.

Nevada had only one federal district court, the District of Nevada.

Ishamael's business involved constructing and training his buyers to deploy movable (with a sufficiently sized truck), some-assembly-required pop-up bumper boat facilities, called BustYourYouKnowWhatBumpers. In a nutshell, potential buyers of one of Ishamael's BustYourYouKnowWhatBumpers paid a flat fee of $300,000 for the full set of equipment and constructs as well as a three-week course in Ishamael's large back yard on how to set up and operate it all. ## The Events

In September of 2023, Tuon came across Ishamael's business online and decided to give it a try. She borrowed the $300,000-plus she needed to get started from her parents. She paid Ishamael his fee and underwent his three-week training course in Lake Tahoe in October of 2023. The course did not include any information on how to adjust the water levels in the BustYourYouKnowWhatBumpers construct to accommodate the number of boats that were floating in it. The course did, however, include several hours on how to set up the boats in the construct properly, including two hours solely on the placement of the propellers that powered the boats. In late October, Tuon took possession of the pieces of her BustYourYouKnowWhatBumpers construct in Ishamael's back yard in Lake Tahoe, Nevada. She hired a

Upon arrival in Sacramento, Tuon constructed the BustYourYouKnowWhatBumpers facility herself. She looked at some online job-seeking websites and found a notice there from Damodred saying that the latter was looking for temporary work. After a video interview, which Tuon conducted from Lake Tahoe and Damodred conducted on her cell phone from the Manhattan bar where she was working, Tuon offered Damodred a job. Damodred accepted on the spot. The two set up a second, 30-minute video-call, which Damodred took from a computer at her Manhattan bar, during which Tuon (in Nevada at the time) provided Damodred with information on how to operate the BustYourYouKnowWhatBumpers apparatus. This was the only training Damodred received on how to operate the equipment, and it included no information on how to attach the propellers to the boats. Tuon then bought a plane ticket for Damodred to fly out of La Guardia (in Queens), NY to Sacramento. Damodred landed on the morning of November 1, 2023, and reported immediately for work.

In the afternoon of November 1, 2023, Perrin was in Sacramento visiting Zarine. While out walking around, they encountered Tuon's BustYourYouKnowWhatBumpers construct. As it happened, Damodred was working, and Tuon was there keeping an eye on things. Perrin decided to give it a try. Damodred sold him a ticket and set up his boat, including the propeller. But when it came time to get in the mini-pool to drive the boat around, there was no one else present against whom to bump his boat (Zarine did not like anything energetic or exciting). At Tuon's instruction, Damodred set up another boat and got in herself.

Perrin thought the boat was great. He immediately pressed the accelerator to its maximum speed and began spinning the steering wheel from right to left, and left to right, as fast as he could. What happened next was a matter of dispute. According to Perrin, as he was trying to steer around Damodred, he felt something in the bottom of his boat catch, with the result that his boat turned sharply at top speed, throwing him clear; Damodred believed that Perrin's dangerous driving threw him clear. What was undisputed was that Perrin flew out of his boat across a short stretch of water and smashed into Damodred, pitching them both out of her boat and into the side of the BustYourYouKnowWhatBumpers construct. It was also undisputed that Perrin smashed his wrist and fingers on his right hand badly, suffering multiple severe breaks that put him in the hospital for weeks. It was also undisputed that Damodred suffered a concussion and was unable to work for a month.

Immediately after the accident, while still in the hospital, Perrin contacted you, a litigator living in (wherever you want to have been living, you pick). You agreed to be his attorney. About an hour later, you received a letter attached to an email from Damodred. She found out from an angry telephone call she received from Perrin (informing her that he intended to sue her) that you were representing Perrin. Damodred's letter informed you that she intended to sue Perrin for the injuries she suffered in the accident. She alleged that Perrin drove the boat too fast and tried to turn too sharply, causing the accident. She also said that she should not be blamed for the accident because she received only minimal training on how to put the boats together and in the BustYourYouKnowWhatBumpers apparatus in the second 30-minute video call with Tuon. If the accident was not Perrin's own fault, then the fault was with Tuon, who did not train Damodred properly.

The Advisory Committee on the FRCP could propose an amendment to Rule 50 (for eventual SCOTUS adoption and congressional veto consideration) such that a litigant need not move for JAMOL pre-verdict for insufficiency of the evidence to preserve the ability to do so post-verdict. The 50(a) requirement of a pre-verdict motion serves to put the non-moving party and the court on notice of a potential deficiency in the evidence to allow for (at the judge's discretion) correction, and it furthers a pre-FRCP practice of requiring a directed verdict motion prior to a JNOV motion so as to mask the fact that the entire practice of JAMOL is in tension with the 7th Amendment.
