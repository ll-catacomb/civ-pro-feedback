## Question 1 (28 points out of 70)

Assume the following (which might be completely fictional):

In 2009, the Delaware Supreme Court, pursuant to the appropriate procedures in Delaware law, proposed to amend the Delaware Rules of Civil Procedure by adding Rule 126, which would read as follows:

> **Del. R. Civ. P. 126:**
>
> In all cases in which a plaintiff sues a defendant to collect on a debt arising out of a credit card agreement, the plaintiff shall attach true and correct copies of the following documents to the complaint:
>
> (a) the original contract by which the defendant agreed to obtain the credit card, which contract shall indicate all applicable terms, including the interest rate, fees and penalties and circumstances in which these would apply, and credit limit(s);
>
> (b) if the original contract does not so specify, documents sufficient to identify the type of the credit card (*e.g.*, American Express, Visa) and the account number;
>
> (c) all modifications to the credit card contract made after the card's issuance; and
>
> (d) if the plaintiff is not the original credit card issuer, all documents/contracts by which the rights to the defendant's obligation on the credit card was transferred, as well as any payments made to or charges imposed by previous owners of the debt.

The Delaware Supreme Court accompanied its proposal with the following comment:

> *Comment to Rule 126:*[^1] *The new proposed Rule 126 responds to a growing problem of an explosion of credit card debt collection lawsuits filed in our courts that are decided by default judgments. Our initial investigation into case files of lawsuits filed in our trial courts reveals that the following scenario recurs: a credit card company issues a credit card to a consumer. The consumer charges purchases to the card. Under the credit card company's version of events, the consumer falls behind on monthly payments, and the company begins to charge "appropriate" fees. Eventually, the consumer stops making payments on the card, or becomes very far behind in the payments, and the credit card company freezes the card (meaning it refuses to allow the consumer to make further charges to it). But the consumer still owes an outstanding amount of money because of the charges and fees already existing on the card.*
>
> *The credit card company expends some initial effort to collect on the outstanding debt, and it may obtain some but not all of the money allegedly "due." The company then sells the right to collect on the remaining debt to a first collection agency (say, Collection Agency A). For a while, Collection Agency A contacts the consumer, by telephone or regular mail or email or an in-person visit, attempting to obtain money on the debt. Perhaps the consumer pays some of the debt, perhaps not. Collection Agency A then sells the right to collect on the remaining debt to Collection Agency B. Collection Agency B contact the consumer for a while, perhaps obtaining*

<!-- page: 2 -->

> *some money, then sells the right to the remaining debt to Collection Agency C. And so on. Eventually, say, Collection Agency G ends up with the debt and sues the consumer in our state courts. The lawsuit typically contains only the barest minimum of allegations, such as that the defendant owed money from on an American Express credit card, that the plaintiff (say, Collection Agency G) purchased the right to collect on the debt from someone else (say, Collection Agency F), and that the amount outstanding is $X.*
>
> *In about half of such cases, the defendant does not appear, and thus judgment is entered after a default. In 45% of the cases, the defendant appears to contest the lawsuit, but she/he is not represented by counsel. In 5% (or fewer) of cases, the defendant appears and is represented by counsel. When the defendant is represented by counsel, the most likely outcome of the case is that the plaintiff (Collection Agency G, in the above example) voluntarily dismisses the case as soon as it finds out that the defendant has a lawyer.*
>
> *This system is rife with the opportunity for error. In the transfer of the rights to a debt from Collection Agency A to Collection Agency B to Collection Agency C, etc., to Collection Agency G, critical "details" may get lost, such as the existence and amount of payments the defendant has previously made; the terms of the original contact, which would obviously be relevant to whether all of the charges (imposed by the original credit card company that issued the card or the subsequent collection agencies) were proper; and even whether the rights to the particular account were actually transferred from, say, Collection Agency D to Collection Agency E. But when so many defendants default in the eventual lawsuit, errors like these, and other possible errors, will not be caught. Public policy disfavors default judgments.*

You are a lawyer for the Delaware Chamber of Commerce. The membership of your organization includes credit card issuers and debt collection agencies. The Delaware Chamber has decided that it will write to the Delaware Supreme Court to urge the Court NOT to adopt the proposed new rule. But to prepare its opposition, the Chamber first wants a legal assessment of the proposed new rule. You are asked to provide this legal assessment. Your client asks you to make sure to address the following questions:

- Is the proposed rule consistent with the idea of notice pleading? With code pleading? Is it consistent with the reasoning and ideas articulated in *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal*?[^2] Is the proposed rule a rule of pleading at all?

- What are the advantages and disadvantages of having pleading rules that apply only to specific types of lawsuits? Would it make sense to apply the basic idea of Proposed Rule 126 to all breach of contract lawsuits? To all lawsuits?

- What alternatives are there to this rule?

- Is there an argument that the appointment of counsel (at state expense, for debtors who cannot afford to hire one) in this kind of case is constitutionally required? If the argument

<!-- page: 3 -->

for appointed counsel is weak, is there an argument that the proposed rule is a constitutionally required alternative to appointed counsel?

- What would the proposed rule do to the current market for debts? For credit cards?

## Question 2

Now suppose that the Delaware Supreme Court in fact promulgates proposed Rule 126. To date, however, no other state has followed suit. Note that at various points in Question 2, there may be arguments made regarding the constitutionality of Rule 126. You should not address in your answer to Question 2 the merits of whether Rule 126 is constitutional.

On other matters, both Massachusetts and New Hampshire have abandoned the mutuality requirement for collateral estoppel. Delaware, in contrast, strictly requires mutuality for issue preclusion. In Massachusetts, statutes of limitations dismissals are considered on the merits, while in New Hampshire and Delaware they are not. Assume that, except for Del. R. Civ. P. 126, the Delaware Rules of Civil Procedure, the Massachusetts Rules of Civil Procedure, and the New Hampshire Rules of Civil Procedure are all identical to the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure; that Delaware, Massachusetts, and New Hampshire case law interpreting these rules is in all ways identical to federal case law interpreting the Federal Rules; and that Delaware, Massachusetts, and New Hampshire appellate jurisdiction statutes and case law are identical to 28 U.S.C. §§ 1291, 1292, and 1651, as well as accompanying case law. Massachusetts and New Hampshire use the First Restatement approach to choice of law, while Delaware uses the Second Restatement. All three states' long-arm statutes allow their state courts to exercise jurisdiction to the limits of the federal due process clause.

G-Credit, Inc., is a collection agency incorporated in New Hampshire and with its principal place of business in Massachusetts. G-Credit has a large office in Wilmington, Delaware. It specializes in buying thousands of delinquent credit card accounts and attempting to collect on them. G-Credit often buys debts "in bulk," meaning that it purchases the rights to hundreds of accounts in a single transaction.

On May 2, 2011, G-Credit bought a batch of 200 debt accounts from F-Credit, Inc. F-Credit was incorporated in Delaware and had its principal place of business in Delaware. According to the terms of the sale of the debt accounts from F-Credit to G-Credit, F-Credit was to provide all of the documents in its possession regarding the 200 debt accounts by the end of June, 2011. In early June, F-Credit provided a set of notes (in the form of computerized files) compiled by one of its loan officers regarding each of the 200 accounts. In late June, however, F-Credit filed for bankruptcy and simultaneously came under investigation by the Delaware Attorney General's office. As a result, F-Credit's operations froze up, so it never provided any actual documentation regarding the debts.

<!-- page: 4 -->

According to the computerized notes, two of the accounts G-Credit purchased from F-Credit in the May, 2011 transaction were from Visa cards issued by Delaware Bank and Trust, Inc., a bank incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Delaware. One of the two cards, with (according to the notes) account number BX13AG6000387, was issued to Ms. Pansy Parkinson, and she was the sole account holder on that card. The other of the two cards, with account number BX13AG6000388, was issued to her husband, Mr. Draco Malfoy, and he was the sole account holder on that card. According to the computerized notes that F-Credit provided to G-Credit, the amount due on Pansy's account was $90,000, while the amount due on Draco's account was $40,000. According to these notes, Delaware Bank and Trust had issued the cards to Pansy and Draco on the same day, October 21, 2005, and payments had been made on the two accounts from a joint checking account that Pansy and Draco co-owned. Although separate checks had always been written to make monthly payments on each card, sometimes Pansy had signed checks written to pay on Draco's credit card, and sometimes Draco had signed checks written to pay on Pansy's credit card.

After purchasing the accounts, G-Credit set about finding Draco and Pansy. G-Credit's staff investigators, former *News of the World* reporters, hacked into Draco and Pansy's gmail and other computer accounts and discovered the following facts: Draco and Pansy had lived and worked for 20 years in Wilmington, Delaware until in March of 2007, when both had lost their jobs as store clerks in the Wilmington branch of Madame Malkins' Robes for All Occasions. They were unable to make payments on their credit cards or their Wilmington house, but continued to live at the house until the bank holding the mortgage foreclosed in September of 2007. Since September of 2007, Draco has lived in the basement of his parents' house in Worcester, Massachusetts, while Pansy has lived in the basement of her parents' house in Concord, New Hampshire. During the time from September, 2007, Draco uploaded online job applications to 120 clothing stores, 63 of which were located in Delaware and 57 of which were located in Massachusetts. Pansy applied for the same 120 positions that Draco applied for plus an additional 15 positions located in New Hampshire. The couple communicates regularly via email and Skype, and they are still married, but they lack the funds to visit one another very often.

From May to August of 2011, G-Credit employees called Draco and Pansy separately over 100 times on the telephone attempting to induce them to pay on their cards. G-Credit also sent 20 letters to Pansy at her parents' address demanding payment, and 20 separate letters to Draco at his parents' address demanding payment. All of these letters were sent, and all of these telephone calls were initiated, from G-Credit's Wilmington office. No payments were forthcoming.

### Question 2(a) — 20 points

**You are a lawyer for G-Credit. Since telephone calls, letters, and personal visits haven't resulted in any money, your client wants to try a lawsuit or lawsuits against both Pansy and Draco. The only cause of action the lawsuit would assert is breach of contract. Your client insists on filing one lawsuit against both Pansy and Draco simultaneously, and it would prefer to do as little as possible in the**

<!-- page: 5 -->

**way of litigating the lawsuit. What options does your client have in terms of the courts and locations in which it might sue? What are the advantages and disadvantages of each potential forum (to the extent that there are any differences among the various fora)?** *Hint: your answer should begin by identifying the six fora/courts where the lawsuit could occur.*

**Note 1: We did not study the interstate (horizontal) choice of law in breach of contract disputes. For the purposes of this question, assume that as a matter of horizontal choice of law, Delaware law will govern all substantive (in the horizontal choice of law sense) questions in G-Credit's contemplated lawsuit(s).**

After consultations, your client, G-Credit, decides to sue Pansy and Draco in a single lawsuit in Massachusetts state court. You represented G-Credit in the lawsuit. Your complaint alleges that the two accounts were opened on October 21, 2005; that they became past due sometime after that date; that your client had purchased the rights to collect on these debts from F-Credit on May 2, 2011; that no payments had been made; and that the failure to make payments constituted a breach of the original credit card contract. Your complaint demands payment in the amount of $90,000 from Pansy and $40,000 from Draco.

Pansy and Draco answer. In her answer, as her sole affirmative defense, Pansy pleads preclusion, based on a March, 2010 lawsuit by F-Credit against Pansy (more on this F-Credit lawsuit below). Pansy also denies all liability on the credit card debt. In his answer, Draco also pleads preclusion as an affirmative defense, arguing that the 2010 litigation of the *F-Credit v. Pansy* suit serves as both claim and issue preclusion preventing suit against him on debts associated with credit card BX13AG6000388. Draco also denies all liability on the debt. Both answers include a jury trial demand.

The lawsuit proceeds. Neither Draco nor Pansy takes any discovery. You suggest to your client that some discovery would be a good idea, but your client tells you that the amount of money involved will not justify the necessary expense. No one moves for summary judgment. The judge does not ask for a pre-trial order.

The case goes to trial before a Massachusetts jury. Pansy and Draco appear without a lawyer, but from your counsel table, you can see that they have a set of notes that were probably prepared by a legal aid attorney. At the trial, you call as your only witness a loan officer from G-Credit. The loan officer testifies without objection that G-Credit bought the rights to Pansy and Draco's debts associated with Visa Cards BX13AG600037 and BX13AG600038 from F-Credit on May 2, 2011; that according to the notes that F-Credit supplied as part of the transaction, the amounts due on the accounts were $90,000 (for Pansy on BX13AG600037) and $40,000 (for Draco on BX13AG600038); and that no payments had been made to G-Credit. Neither Pansy nor Draco cross-examines your witnesses.

<!-- page: 6 -->

Pansy takes the witness stand. Lacking a lawyer, she narrates in her testimony. Relying heavily on a set of notes prepared (probably with someone's help) before trial, she testifies as follows:

- In March of 2010, F-Credit sued Pansy in New Hampshire State Court, alleging that it had purchased the rights to Pansy's debt on Visa Card BX13AG6000387 from another collections agency, E-Credit, Inc. F-Credit alleged that it had purchased the rights to several hundred credit card accounts, including Pansy's in January of 2010. Specifically, the accounts allegedly transferred from E-Credit to F-Credit included both Pansy's Visa Card BX13AG6000387 as well as Draco's Visa Card BX13AG6000388.

- Pansy, who at that time had had a legal aid attorney representing her, had argued that F-Credit never properly purchased the right to her debt from E-Credit. Essentially, Pansy's argument was that at the time, E-Credit was tied up in bankruptcy, so that E-Credit could not sell any of its assets, including the right to payments on credit card accounts. As a factual matter, Pansy's defense was that no sale from E-Credit to F-Credit was actually completed, in the sense that no contract transferring rights on the debts from E-Credit to F-Credit was actually consummated.[^3]

- The *F-Credit v. Pansy* lawsuit in New Hampshire State Court went to a jury trial on the issue of whether E-Credit had in fact consummated a sale of the rights to Pansy's debt to F-Credit, and the jury issued a verdict for Pansy. F-Credit moved the court for judgment as a matter of law. Before the court had ruled on the judgment as a matter of law motion, F-Credit and Pansy settled in a separate contract not filed with the New Hampshire Court. The contract provided that Pansy would pay F-Credit $1500, and that F-Credit would move the New Hampshire State Court to dismiss its lawsuit with prejudice.

- Pansy paid the $1500. F-Credit moved the New Hampshire Court to dismiss its lawsuit against Pansy. The New Hampshire Court did so, entering an order of dismissal "with prejudice."

You cross-examine Pansy. With Ralph Waldo Emerson's admonition that "a foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds" firmly in your head, the primary point of your cross is that all of the substance of Pansy's testimony could have been proved by documents[^4] (such as the New Hampshire Court's judgment, the contract between F-Credit and Pansy for the $1500 payment, and a canceled check from Pansy to F-Credit for $1500 or a receipt from F-Credit acknowledging a $1500 payment). But Pansy has introduced no documents.

There is no other evidence, and no party makes an objection at any point during the trial.

<!-- page: 7 -->

After Pansy testifies, Draco and Pansy ask the judge to send the jury out of the courtroom so that they can "ask a question." Reading from a set of notes obviously prepared for them by a legal aid lawyer, the two defendants tell the judge that they want "to raise the defenses of claim preclusion and issue preclusion for both Draco and Pansy." They ask the judge when they should raise those defenses. The judge responds that she cannot give them legal advice, and that they should do what they think best. Again reading from prepared notes, Draco and Pansy respond that they want the judge to understand that they are "raising the defenses of claim preclusion and issue preclusion." The judge says that she understands.

The judge issues some standard jury instructions, to which neither party objects (neither party requests any particular jury instructions). The judge then submits special interrogatories to the jury, asking the following questions. The jury's answers appear in bold after each question.

(i) Did G-Credit prove by a preponderance of the evidence that Pansy owes a debt of $90,000 corresponding to Visa credit card BX13AG6000387 upon which G-Credit is entitled to collect? **Jury's answer: Yes.**

(ii) Did G-Credit prove by a preponderance of the evidence that Draco owes a debt of $40,000 corresponding to Visa credit card BX13AG6000388 upon which G-Credit is entitled to collect? **Jury's answer: Yes.**

(iii) Did Pansy and Draco prove by a preponderance of the evidence that Pansy had been sued previously by F-Credit in litigation that also focused on Pansy's debt corresponding to Visa credit card BX13AG6000387? **Jury's answer: No.**

After the jury returns its verdict, the judge asks the parties whether they have any post-verdict motions. You answer, "No." Again reading from prepared notes, Pansy and Draco tell the judge, "We will be moving at a later date for a judgment non obstante verdictum and for a new trial." The judge tells them that they had the time allotted under the rules to file their motions.

Seven days after the jury verdict, you receive a letter from Cedric Diggory, an attorney working for Delaware Legal Aid. Mr. Diggory informs you that he will be representing Pansy and Draco in post-trial litigation before the Massachusetts judge and, if necessary, on an appeal to the Massachusetts appellate courts.

### Question 2(b) — 18 points

**Mr. Diggory's letter has spooked your client. The prospect of a lawyer on the scene makes them wonder if they should settle. They want an assessment from you immediately as to the likelihood that the judge will grant either a motion under Rule 50(b) or a motion under Rule 59, if Mr. Diggory were to file either on Draco and Pansy's behalf. They also want an assessment from you as to the viability of**

<!-- page: 8 -->

**Draco and Pansy's preclusion defenses, if it turns out that the facts are as Pansy represented them to be in her testimony. What do you say?**

Eventually, you settle Pansy and Draco's lawsuit for $5000. After receiving a $5000 check from Draco, and in accordance with the settlement, you request the judge to dismiss your lawsuit against both Pansy and Draco with prejudice. The judge complies. Frustrated by the whole endeavor, you check your files and are relieved to verify that neither Pansy nor Draco has any outstanding debt issues with G-Credit.

Using your lawsuit against Pansy and Draco as a poster child (and alleging that you filed in Massachusetts so as to evade Rule 126), however, Delaware Legal Aid goes on the warpath. It mounts a publicity campaign attempting to pressure your client into complying with Rule 126 in all of its credit card lawsuits that are filed in Delaware.

In response, on G-Credit's behalf, you adopt the following strategy. When you file credit card collection lawsuits in Delaware state courts on the basis of credit card accounts G-Credit has purchased from credit card companies and other collection agencies, you attach no documents and wait to see if the defendant defaults or, if he/she does not default, then what defenses he/she says. If a defendant raises Rule 126 in a motion to dismiss or in an answer as an affirmative defense, you argue that the law is unconstitutional under the Takings Clause of the Delaware Constitution.[^5] It turns out that Delaware trial judges split on the constitutionality of Rule 126; some hold that the Rule is unconstitutional, other judges reject your contention. For those cases in which a judge rejects your contention regarding Rule 126, you request (and are invariably granted) a dismissal without prejudice of the lawsuit. For those cases in which the judge agrees with you that Rule 126 is unconstitutional, you proceed.

Delaware Legal Aid gets incredibly annoyed at your strategy. It writes you several letters and calls you several times, demanding that you stop the strategy and start complying with Rule 126 in all of your lawsuits in Delaware state court. You write a letter to Delaware Legal Aid telling it to go play in the traffic.

Delaware Legal Aid files a lawsuit in Delaware State Court. The complaint recites the facts described immediately above. The complaint requests certification of a class of persons who presently owe money on credit card accounts, the rights to which are owned by G-Credit, or who will in the future owe money on credit card accounts, the rights to which will be in the future owned by G-Credit. The sole named plaintiff is Pansy. The sole form of relief requested

<!-- page: 9 -->

is an injunction against your client requiring that it comply in full with Delaware Rule 126 in all of its credit card collection lawsuits filed in Delaware State Courts.

### Question 2(c) — 4 points

**Your client is now in a panic. It demands an evaluation of whether you can defeat a forthcoming motion for class certification. How do you respond?**

[^5]: The Delaware Constitution prohibits governmental taking of property without just compensation. Your argument is that Rule 126 was not in force at the time that Pansy and Draco put the charges on their credit cards.
