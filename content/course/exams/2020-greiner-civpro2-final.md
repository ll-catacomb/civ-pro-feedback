Law regarding self-driving trucks and front proximity detectors

                   Federal Law

In 2016, Congress passed the Willow Rosenberg Self-Driving Trucks Protection Act (the "Willow Act").
Named after a woman hit by a self-driving truck as she was standing on a sidewalk casting a spell, the
Willow Act included the following provisions:

         Section 201: Findings And Purpose
         The Congress finds that self-driving trucks pose hazards to pedestrians almost as great
         as human-driven trucks. In particular, the Congress finds that front proximity detectors1
         have proven to be a particular technological challenge for manufacturers of self-driving
         trucks. One purpose of this Act is therefore to empower the Secretary of Transportation
         (the "Secretary") to issue regulations promoting the safety and well-being of
         pedestrians through appropriate regulation, with particular attention to front proximity
         detectors.

         Section 202: Department of Transportation ("DoT") Duties
    (a) To promote the safety and well-being of pedestrians, after appropriate notice and

         comment as required by the Administrative Procedure Act, the Secretary shall issue
         regulations concerning the design, manufacture, sale, purchase, and maintenance of
         self-driving trucks, with special attention to the issue of front proximity detection.
    (b) The Secretary shall require that any business involved in the design, manufacture, sale,
         purchase, or maintenance of self-driving trucks obtain a license from the Department of
         Transportation before engaging in such business.
    (c) The Secretary shall promptly revoke the license of any such business that fails to comply
         in any material way with the Secretary's regulations governing self-driving trucks,
         including those governing front proximity detectors.

1 Front proximity detectors are devices that detect objects or obstacles in front of a vehicle. Should they detect an
object or obstacle of sufficient size too close to the front of the vehicle while it is moving forward, the vehicle's
control system should engage the brakes to stop the vehicle from hitting the object or obstacle.
In 2017, acting pursuant to the Willow Act, DoT Secretary Riley Finn promulgated the following
regulations.

         49 C.F.R. § 5X5: Front Proximity Detectors
         (a) Any corporation or other business that manufactures or sells self-driving trucks

              shall, before selling any self-driving truck to any other person or entity, conduct an
              appropriate test of the front proximity detector and corresponding braking
              mechanism (such as causing the truck to drive slowly toward an obstacle or barrier
              to see if it stops on its own) in the presence of the buyer to discover whether the
              front proximity detector on the vehicle is in good working order.
         (b) No corporation or other business shall purchase a self-driving truck without
              demanding that the test of the front proximity detector described in subsection (a)
              be conducted.
         (c) The Secretary shall promptly revoke the license of any business that fails to comply
              with this regulation.

No other provision of the Willow Act nor of Secretary Finn's regulations has any language relevant to
this exam.

                   Pennsylvania Law

In 2018, hoping to stimulate the institution and growth of a self-driving vehicle R&D and manufacturing
industry, the Pennsylvania General Assembly passed the Rupert Giles Self-Driving Vehicle Protection Act
(the "Giles" Act). The Act includes the following provision.

         Section 6.7: Heightened Pleading Standard
    (a) The General Assembly finds that lawsuits, or the threat of lawsuits, may deter investors

         from starting or investing in self-driving vehicle businesses in the Commonwealth of
         Pennsylvania.
    (b) Pennsylvania Rule of Civil Procedure 8 is hereby amended to add a new subsection (f),
         as follows: "In any action alleging negligence against a designer, manufacturer, seller,
         buyer, or distributor of a self-driving vehicle, the plaintiff shall specify with particularity
         the nature of the negligent act or omissions, including by identifying the date and
         location of the allegedly negligent act or omission. Upon a motion to dismiss by a
         defendant for failure to comply with this subsection, the court shall forthwith dismiss
         the complaint without leave to amend."

In all other ways relevant to this exam, the Pennsylvania Rules of Civil Procedure are identical to
the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

Law relating to personal jurisdiction, choice of law, respondeat superior, and civil procedure

All states relevant to this exam have long-arm statutes allowing the exercise of personal jurisdiction
whenever the Constitution so allows.

Pennsylvania, Virginia, and Delaware use the Second Restatement for horizontal choice of law. North
Carolina and South Carolina use the First Restatement.
Except as otherwise stated in this exam, all state rules of civil procedure relevant to this exam are
identical to the federal rules.

All states relevant to this exam use the doctrine of respondeat superior, meaning that a company is
legally responsible (and thus can be sued) for any action taken, or any failure to take an action, within
the scope of an employee's employment duties. For example, if a restaurant delivery-person drives too
fast on their way to a drop off a pizza at a customer's house and, as a result, hits and injuries a
pedestrian, then the pedestrian may sue the restaurant for damages as vicariously liable under the
doctrine of respondeat superior.

Law relating to the negligence cause of action

In all states relevant to this exam (the federal law of negligence is not relevant to this exam), a plaintiff
may recover for the tort of negligence by proving that (i) the defendant owed a duty of care to the
plaintiff, (ii) the defendant breached that duty by failing to act with due care, (iii) the defendant's breach
caused (iv) harm to the plaintiff.

    · For the purposes of this exam, you may assume that all persons (including incorporeal entities)
         owe a duty to act with due care to all other persons (or corporations or incorporeal entities)
         with whom they interact or anyone affected by their actions; in other words, you can assume
         that a plaintiff need not allege element (i) of negligence in a complaint, regardless of whether
         the case is brought in state or federal court.

    · With respect to element (ii):
              o A plaintiff ordinarily pleads or proves this element by alleging or showing that the
                   defendant failed to exercise the ordinary care that a reasonable person would exercise
                   under the circumstances.
              o The substantive (meaning substantive for horizontal choice of law purposes) law of
                   Virginia, Delaware, and North Carolina, but not of Pennsylvania nor of South Carolina,
                   also allows the plaintiff an alternative option with respect to element (ii). In these
                   states, to meet element (ii), a plaintiff may allege or show that the defendant violated a
                   state or federal statute established to protect a set of persons that includes the plaintiff,
                   such as, for example, Secretary Finn's regulation appearing at 49 C.F.R. § 5X5. This is
                   called a "per se" theory of negligence. In states making this option available (Virginia,
                   Delaware, and North Carolina), a plaintiff may attempt to allege or prove either the
                   standard breach of ordinary care theory or the per se theory or both, either
                   simultaneously or in the alternative. In states that do not make this option available
                   (Pennsylvania, South Carolina), a plaintiff can allege or prove element (ii) of negligence
                   not with this "per se" theory but rather by alleging or proving failure to exercise
                   ordinary care, as explained in the prior bullet point.
              o A plaintiff pursuing a negligence cause of action may seek either damages or an
                   injunction. If a plaintiff prevails in a negligence lawsuit and seeks an injunction, the
                   court will enter an order2 requiring the defendant to desist from future negligent acts
                   omissions of the same type as that which damaged the plaintiff.

2 Such an order would be an injunction after full litigation of the case (meaning as part of the final judgment). It
would not be a preliminary injunction, and so the four preliminary injunction prerequisites/factors would not be
relevant.
Law related to preclusion

Pennsylvania, Delaware, and North Carolina follow federal common law on issue preclusion. South
Carolina and Virginia require strict mutuality before issue preclusion can apply.

States and districts

Pennsylvania has three federal district courts; Philadelphia is located in the Eastern District of
Pennsylvania. Virginia has two federal district courts; Alexandria, VA is located in the Eastern District of
Virginia. South Carolina and Delaware each have a single federal district court.

Facts

Xander Harris, Inc. ("XH") is a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in Baltimore,
Maryland. It manufactures self-driving trucks of all sizes, from light pickups all the way up to 18
wheelers. All of its manufacturing occurs in a massive plant outside of Baltimore, MD. Each vehicle that
XH produces bears a distinctive script "XH" symbol on its front fender. XH conducts no sales activity to
consumers or business, nor does it advertise. Instead, it sells its products exclusively in an enormous
purchase and sale clearinghouse market facility located in Alexandria, VA called "The Spike." At The
Spike, sellers and buyers of self-driving vehicles meet, conduct inspections, negotiate sales terms,
transfer money, and take delivery of vehicles. XH sells to various distributors at The Spike. XH insists
that any company to whom it sells one of its vehicles must subsequently sell the vehicle directly to a
consumer.

Faith, Inc. is a North Carolina corporation with its principal place of business in Columbia, South Carolina.
Faith is a large distributor (to consumers) of self-driving vehicles. It advertises and sells self-driving
vehicles in a great many states in the eastern half of the United States, including Pennsylvania,
Delaware, Maryland, Virginia, North Carolina, and South Carolina. Faith purchases self-driving trucks
from, among other sources, manufacturers who patronize the clearinghouse market at The Spike in
Alexandria, VA. In early 2020, Faith began advertising a set of XH self-driving trucks throughout its
multi-state service area, with television ads running on local TV stations in different cities in
Pennsylvania as well as all of the states mentioned immediately above. Faith also ran ads in local
newspapers in Pennsylvania cities, including Philadelphia; similar ads ran in local newspapers of cities in
other states. The ads all directed those interested in self-driving pickups to Faith's website, where
potential buyers could see complete information, including photographs, of the trucks for sale. People
interested were instructed to contact a Faith employee named Cordelia Chase. The ads that ran in
