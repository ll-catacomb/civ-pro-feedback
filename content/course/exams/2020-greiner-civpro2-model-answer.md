Question 1

The court has diversity jurisdiction because plaintiff Buffy is domiciled in and thus a citizen of
Pennsylvania, while defendant XH is a citizen of Delaware (incorporated) and Maryland (PPoB), and Faith
is a citizen of NC (incorporated) and SC (PPoB), with a sufficient AIC (per the footnote). There may also
be AUJ (see below), but we don't need that yet.

The two defendants can be joined in a single lawsuit under FRCP 20 because they are both sued out of
the transaction of Buffy's accident and because a common issue is whether a proximity test was
conducted.

The E.D.Pa. can exercise IPJ over the defendants if a PA state court could (FRCP 4(k)(1)(A)), and PA has a
long-arm that goes to constitutional limits. For Faith, its advertising in PA, and its efforts to
consummate multiple sales in PA, constitute purposeful availment and therefore minimal contacts
(McGee). PA exercise of IPJ would be fair and reasonable because PA law will likely apply to at least
some substantive issues (maybe not all, see below), PA has an interest in protecting its residents from
dangerous products sent into its borders, Buffy has an interest in a convenient court location, key
evidence (Buffy & Dawn's testimony, hospital records, the truck) is in PA, and Faith's business in the area
suggests little inconvenience to it. With respect to XH, there are likely insufficient minimal contacts
relating Buffy's negligence theory and injury, XH, and Pennsylvania. Nicastro is almost directly on point
in that XH sold to a distributor over whom it exercised little control, and with the distributor selling to
the consumer. Stepping back, XH only sells to distributors in one location, and there is no suggestion
that it has any subsequent relationship to the trucks it sells. Buffy's best arguments to distinguish
Nicastro are (i) XH is domestic, so there is no prospect of international friction, and (ii) it appears that
several (10 from this set, according to Faith's advertising) of XH trucks, as opposed to a single scrap
metal cutter, were offered in PA (there may be more XH products in PA from Faith and from other
distributors, we don't know). But XH does no business in PA, does not advertise there, and maintains no
offices there. Faith's unilateral activity in moving a truck to PA does not create minimal contacts to XH
(WWW, Hanson). The fair and reasonable analysis for XH is largely the same as for Faith, except that the
absence of any XH business activity in PA suggests substantial inconvenience.

Venue is likely proper under 1391(b)(2) because sending the car to Buffy's house and the resulting
accident, a substantial part of the events giving rise to the claim, occurred in the E.D.Pa. (b)(1) does not
apply because there is no IPJ over XH, whose thus does not "reside" in the E.D.Pa. (b)(3) is not available
because venue is proper in E.D.Pa.

Regarding 12(b)(6), per the exam, the plaintiff need not allege negligence element (i), duty. Regarding
element (ii), we need to know first, whether the per se theory is available as a matter of HCOL (PA does
not recognize negligence per se, other states do), and (ii) whether the complaint alleges negligence per
se plausibly (Twiqbal). Causation and damages are alleged with specificity in paragraphs 6, 9, 10, 14,
and 15.

Regarding HCOL, the availability of negligence per se theory is analogous to the sections in the first
restatement handout as governed by the law of the place of the wrong, and is therefore substantive for
HCOL purposes. The E.D.Pa., using the Second Restatement rules applicable in PA (Klaxon), will begin
with a presumption that PA law applies because the injury occurred there. Perhaps remembering that
under the Second Restatement, we can apply the law of a state other than PA to the separate issue of
whether the per se theory is available, the presumption might well be overcome through the section 6
factors in favor of Virginia because (a) the alleged misconduct, the failure to test, occurred there, (b)
Virginia has an interested in regulating (mis)conduct within its borders (no misconduct occurred in PA),
(c) the availability of negligence per se has less to do with the injury (as to which PA law will govern)
than it does the XH-Faith relationship and sales transaction, which were centered in Virginia, where they
met and made their sales contract, (d) the VA per se negligence theory was design to prevent precisely
what happened here, so the policies of the area of law point to VA law. Under VA law, per se negligence
is available, and the complaint likely survives. Under PA law, with no per se negligence theory, the case
will be dismissed because the complaint does not otherwise allege a breach of the duty of care.

Regarding Twiqbal, the only conclusory, formulaic, or law-stating paragraphs are 11 and 13, so ignore
those. Paragraph 14, made coherent by the facts alleged in paragraphs 6-10, provides a plausible
allegation of breach of duty under the per se theory (element (ii)) and causation (element (iii)).
Paragraph 15 provides a plausible allegation of harm (element (iv)). The complaint likely survives a
Twiqbal attack.

The complaint does not comply with PA's Giles Act (procedural for HCOL purposes, as it governs
complaint content) because it does not specify the date of the failure to test (at least), something FRCP
8(a) does not require. Under the York test, non-compliance with this provision results in dismissal,
which is outcome-determinative, so the we use the Giles Act. Under the Harlan concurrence, Giles Act
Section 6.7(a) states PA's belief that this provision affects investment strategies, which is primary
conduct; if we believe that, we would use state law. For both the Scalia and Ginsburg applications of the
Hanna majority test, Rule 8(a) is valid because "really regulates procedure" (Sibbach) and thus does not
conflict with 2072(b). It concerns what must be alleged in a compliant, which is the manner and means
of administering a lawsuit. Regarding applicability and a conflict with the Giles Act, FRCP 8(a) specifies
that a plaintiff need file only a short, plain statement (bonus for noting that 8(d)(1), which we did not
cover in class, says that each allegation must be "simple and concise"), while the Giles Act requires more
(section 6.7 is even labeled "Heightened Pleading Standard"). This is likely a conflict, so Scalia would use
federal law, meaning no dismissal. Ginsburg might argue that a federal requirement of a short plain
statement does not speak to the facts that must be pled shortly and plainly, and that the Giles Act
addresses the latter issue, so there is no conflict, and we can apply the Giles Act and dismissed (see
Walker, and perhaps Scalia's dissent in Stewart, for analogous reasoning).

Because there the E.D.Pa. likely cannot exercise IPJ over XH, the case will likely be transferred or XH will
be dismissed. Whether it can be transferred depends on whether is another district where venue is
proper that could exercise IPJ over XH.

Question 2

The events that Maclay and Chase describe constitute negligence that does not depend on the per se
doctrine. A trier of fact would likely infer from the scratches on the front left fender and headlight that
truck that hit Buffy and Dawn was one of those that struck a lighting pole at The Spike, or perhaps the
truck that rear-ended the one in front of it at Faith's SC facility. If so, then Maclay or Chase or both had
reason to know that the proximity detector on that truck did not work. At a minimum, they had reason
to know that something was likely wrong with many of the trucks in the set. Both perceived this risk,
and took no action. That supports negligence without need for a per se theory. Buffy should allege in
detail this theory, adding as much detail as possible for both this theory and the per se negligence
theory.
NC, and SC likely would lack IPJ over XH (no contacts). DE and MD might lack IPJ over Faith (although
Faith sells vehicles there, none of those sales related to this lawsuit, and it would not be fair &
reasonable to adjudicate there given no witnesses, plaintiff interest, or state interest). E.D.Va is best.
Venue will be proper under (b)(1)/(c) (see below) and 1391(b)(2) because the failure to test (the per se
theory), the knowing disregard of the perceived risk (Maclay and Chase, simple negligence theory), and
the waiving off of a test (Chase, simple negligence) occurred there. Under 4(k)(1)(A) and VA's to-the-
limits long-arm, both XH and Faith purposefully availed themselves of the right to transact business in
E.D.Va. (selling and buying the trucks), with the way that business was conducted (see previous
sentence) giving rise to this lawsuit. That's minimal contacts. It would be fair and reasonable for the
E.D.Va. to exercise IPJ because VA has an interest in regulating wrongful conduct that occurs within its
borders, VA law may apply to some issues (see Q1 response), and key evidence (anything relating to the
collisions, copies of the sales contacts, witness testimony about the trucks) is located there.
Alternatively, for XH only, there may be Daimler "at home" general IPJ because it sells all of its vehicles
at The Spike, but this theory is unnecessary.

Question 3

Per above, in the E.D.Va., the jurisdictional and venue theories from Question 2 would apply equally to
the claims of all class members (Bristo-Meyers). E.D.Va. is also best because as an R2 state, we might
well get VA law on the HCOL issue of the availability of per se negligence, which would help
commonality (under Shutts, it would be permissible to apply VA law to all class claims because in each
case the failure to test occurred in VA). Because Buffy, the likely named plaintiff, would have complete
diversity and would be AIC-sufficient, supplemental SMJ would if necessary be available for the class
despite the presence of non-diverse (Supreme Tribe) or AIC-deficient (Exxon) unnamed class members.
In this (b)(3)/damages class we look for

    · numerosity (present because 94 >> 40);
    · superiority (likely met because the plaintiffs are spread out and a common course of conduct

         applied to all, although if Buffy's claim is representative, the amounts at issue might be enough
         to attract lawyers to individual litigation);
    · predominance/commonality (likely met if as a matter of HCOL the negligence per se theory for is
         available for all claims, see above, because then all were injured as a result of non-testing, but
         might not work otherwise because negligence would be specific to circumstances, such as what
         MacClay and Chase saw for each set of trucks, plus there might be different damages
         facts/evidence for injuries to body versus property);
    · typicality (likely met if the negligence per se theory works because Buffy's harm occurred in the
         same way as the unnamed class members, with the contrary argument being that Buffy's
         damages were extensive and therefore may be atypical);
    · and representativeness (same as commonality plus typicality, there being no indication of
         conflicts of interest).

Question 4
There is appellate jurisdiction under either 1291 (the judgment constitutes a "final decision") or
1292(a)(1) (the appeal from an issuance of an injunction). Regarding the appellants' argument, the
district judge's FoF are so vague as to constitute a failure to make them, violating FRCP 52. The
appellate court cannot understand the nature of the district court's reasoning, and per Q5, we cannot
apply preclusion law well because we cannot know what the judge decided.
Question 5
Claim preclusion is irrelevant; Dawn does not want to preclude herself.

The rendering court was a federal court in PA, so federal common law ("FCL") tells us to use either the
uniform FCL non-mutual offensive collateral estoppel (NMOCE) framework from Parklane and Russell (if
the basis of SMJ was AUJ) or PA NMOCE law (diversity). But PA NMOCE law is the same as FCL, so this
doesn't matter. XH and Faith had sufficient incentive (fear of an injunction) and opportunity (trial) to
litigate well, and there's no suggestion of access to proof issues. The pending appeal is irrelevant. We
have the same factual issue (whether XH and Faith were negligent) and actually litigated (trial).
Necessary to the decision is lacking (Russell/FCL). The courts FoF do not distinguish between negligence
per se and failure to act after seeing collisions, so Dawn cannot prove this element. Finally, under
Parklane, NMOCE likely depends on whether there was Smith-Grable SMJ in Buffy's lawsuit. If Buffy's
case was diversity only, then Dawn could not have joined/intervened because (a) as a SC domiciliary, she
is non-diverse from Faith, and (b) under 1367(b), her AIC-deficient suit is a claim by a plaintiff (Dawn)
against a person made a party (pick either defendant) under Rule 20 (listed). But if Smith-Grable AUJ
was available to Buffy, FRCP 20 (same accident, common issue in failure to exercise due care) would
have allowed joinder, with 1367 providing SSMJ. (Note AUJ unavailable to Dawn because she is not
asserting a negligence per se claim.)

Smith-Grable was likely unavailable, even if VA law were used to allow the per se negligence theory.
Violation of the Giles regulations is embedded within the state law/negligence COA, but non-compliance
with the regulation was not contested (XH and Faith contested causation, not breach). Further, this is a
fact-bound question, not interpretation of federal law but rather compliance with it. Finally, allowing
negligence per se based on a violation of federal regulations to support AUJ would sweep too many
cases into federal court.

The VA right to a jury trial does not prevent preclusion (Parklane & Beacon).

Question 6:
The Twiqbal standard relies on the premise that judges can tell, before discovery of facts and based on
allegations only (no evidence), whether a claim will succeed. Judges have not just truth sense but crystal
balls. But judges have neither. Judgments about how to apply 12(b)(6)/8(a)(2) should be based on
empirical examination of complaints, not judicial guesswork about the quality of trial judge guesswork.
In contrast, the abuse of discretion standard of review, which is justified in part on the idea that trial
judges' practice at making certain kinds of decisions renders them better than appellate courts at
making those decisions, seems justified. Actively managing lots of cases will provide trial judges with
some idea of how litigants react to ruling and pretrial structures. Here, experience seems likely to
matter.

Question 7:
One solution is court-based self-help materials. Such materials are hard to write well because they
require casting complex legal issues into simple language. They are hard to scale because U.S. civil
procedures (especially the unwritten ones) are so localized, so this idea requires as many sets of
materials in a state as one has counties or municipalities. Finally, parties may not be able to obtain self-
help materials when they need them and in a format they can access, given search costs, the digital
divide, and/or photocopying costs.
