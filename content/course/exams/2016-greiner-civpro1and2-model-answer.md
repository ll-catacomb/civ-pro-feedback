Question2

OR is the location of the purchase of the car and the accident as well as where HG goes to school, and because so much happened in OR (see below), the exercise of IPJ would be fair and reasonable.  Because an OR state court would have IPJ, (4(k)(1)(A) a OR federal court has specific IPJ, Shoe. Venue is proper because the substantial events (purchase, accident) occurred in OR, (1391(b)(2).  Under Hoffman, transfer to OR FedCt is permissible.  Atlantic Marine makes clear that FSC does not render venue in OR improper, and since the proposal is to transfer to a place other than that selected by the FSC, the FSC has no relevance yet.  This case is like Piper:  parties are litigating about which in which domestic forum they will litigate about whether to send the case abroad.

If HG is domiciled in NJ (likely), then the NJ state court has general IPJ over her, so under FRCP 4(k)(1)(A), the federal court would have IPJ, in which case the NJ federal court would not have to transfer.  If HG is domiciled in OR, then the NJ federal court must transfer somewhere, but in that case, the court must remand (no SMJ).

The NJ federal court should transfer.  Regarding private interest factors, key evidence (the car, the medical records, the defendants/witnesses) are in OR.  Other key evidence (regarding design and manufacture) appears to be in Mexico, which doesn’t weigh either way.  Re public interest, a NJ jury should not deal with an OR incident. Question3:

Technically, FCL may demands forum-non analysis (per dictum in Atlantic Marine), and the public interest factors probably favor OR (site of the accident and sale/warranty of the car give OR regulatory interest), but this interest is not strong enough to overcome the FSC and the fact of the manufacture of car in Mexico (given allegation of improper manufacture).  OR law would enforce the FSC.  NJ law would not.  Whether NJ law follows the transfer depends on two issues:  (i) whether HG was domiciled in NJ (if so, then NJ law might follow the transfer because NJ is a happy court, if not, then we lose SMJ); and (ii) whether enforcement of an FSC is procedural or substantive for HCOL (if the issue is procedural, then NJ HCOL rules would choose NJ law, if the issue is substantive, then because the sales contract was made in OR, OR law would apply, and the FSC should be enforced).  Per above, it is likely that HG is domiciled in NJ.  And the scope of what is procedural for HCOL purposes is broad, likely encompassing FSC enforcement because it is arguably procedural.  If we use OR law, there is no conflict worth exploring.  But likely NJ law applies.

Erie.  Under York, the FSC’s enforceability determines dismiss/not dismiss, so apply NJ law, don’t dismiss.  Under Harlan in Hanna, it is possible but not definite that if we allow large companies to select favorable fora, they will engage in more risky behavior, so likely NJ law.  Both Scalia’s and Ginsburg’s interpretation of the Hanna majority would look to the source of the federal law, FCL, and apply the twin aims of Erie.  The FSC’s enforceability would likely lead to forum shopping, so apply state law.

Question5:

We have the same issue (breach of warranty theory) and actually litigated (a jury trial).

Regarding necessary to the decision, we consider inter-system preclusion plus the Happy Court rule.  Because the federal courts at issue were sitting in diversity, state preclusion law governs.  Semtek.  But which state?  NJ law would not allow preclusion because of the two independent bases for the decision with an ambiguous jury verdict, Russell v. Place.  OR law would allow preclusion because all independent bases for the decision are presumed decided in favor of the prevailing party.  If there was IPJ and venue re Hermione in NJ (depends on her domicile, likely per above), then there is an argument that that the transfer was simply a change of courtroom, the OR federal court should act as NJ federal court by applying NJ preclusion law.  A counterargument is that it would be simpler to make the relevant preclusion law be evident from the face of the judgment and not require an inquiry into the procedural posture of the lawsuit.  Supporting the counterargument is the idea that it is the OR federal district court’s power that we are adjudicating (this argument is not so strong given the transfer).  Under that argument, OR law would apply, and we get preclusion.

Regarding mutuality, because Farley is attempting to use the prior judgment defensively, and Hermione was in a defense posture as well (at least initially), the question is NMDCE.  Under NJ law (Semtek again), which is the same as federal law, NMDCE usually allowed.  Parklane.  If OR law applies, mutuality is not required.

Finally, following Hart, the OR state court might allow issue preclusion even if not compelled to do so.

Question7

The operation of the federal court system rules (apart from SMJ, and perhaps with a state-court version of venue law) in this new state court system renders the prohibition on J.D. appearances is troubling.  First, the prohibition against JD appearances, while allowing J.D.’s to draft complaints, motions to dismiss, motions and response to motions for summary judgment, discovery requests, discovery responses, and while allowing J.D.’s to advise clients on what clients should say when they appear in court, may magnify power imbalances rather than vitiate them.  Certainly, there would be a heightened advantage for repeat player parties in this court system, who can familiarize themselves with how it works, so it this system might be even more advantageous then our current one for debt collection, insurance, and other corporate entities with a steady diet of litigation.  This new system could also benefit for parties whose education, social capital, and experience prepared them to speak on their own behalf in court (Lassiter is a painful object lesson here).  Some allegedly access-to-justice-promoting procedural devices, such as the class action, are administratively impracticable without a J.D.  Others are incomprehensible absent rigorous training (e.g., R50 JAMOL double motions).

Lawyers can be themselves sometimes be access-to-justice-promoting devices, as the litigants in the right-to-counsel cases (Lassiter, Turner) recognized.

True, as Powell in Vitek recognized, J.D.s can be part of the problem (when they complicate proceedings and render them adversarial).  But our current system is already complex and adversarial, and the current proposal does nothing to change that.

In defense of the proposal, if one is trying to equalize power, one can level up (with civil Gideon, ridiculously too expensive, Barton & Bibas) or level down, which is what this proposal tries to do.  And perhaps leveling down, by forcing the one-percenters in many cases to experience the justice system that the other 99% currently experience, would create a coalition for improvement of the justice system.  With that in mind, the current proposal’s problem may be that it does not level far enough down:  perhaps the answer is to ban all lawyer involvement in cases with less than $200K AIC.  A criminal sanction for violation might be required to make that enforceable.

What reforms would make the proposal more palatable?  First, per above, extend the ban on lawyer court appearances to preventing lawyers from engaging with the system at all.

Second, allow non-lawyers to represent clients in this system, perhaps subject to a non-lawyer licensing scheme based on an apprenticeship model of experience within this system.  Hopefully, these non-lawyers would have cheaper prices, making their services more accessible to a wider portion of the population.

Third, scrap any kind of party joinder rule, including class actions and intervention.  Scrap compulsory counterclaims.  These devices are useful to serve the policy of resolution of multiple disputes efficiently so long as we have lawyers who, in theory at least, can handle the complexity that they bring. But with no lawyers in the picture, the confusion they will engender is too great a threat to truth-seeking.

For similar reasons, alter document production rules and witness identification rules so as to expand mandatory disclosures, and allow depositions to be taken and recorded via video link.  Dispense with interrogatories and requests for admission.  Again, the reasoning here is procedural simplification, and attempt to build a system that a lay person might be able to use.

Fifth, provide court forms that allow simple statements of claims and dispense with summary judgment, again on procedural simplification grounds.

Sixth, shift to inquisitorial judging.  If parties have no clue what they’re doing, someone in the courtroom will have to guide them.  Inquisitorial judging is not anti-American; we use it every day in small claims and housing courts.

The resulting scheme would comply with procedural due process.  My system, as described above, is similar to the Small Claims Court system as it currently operates in most states.  In part because my scheme allows non-lawyer representation (although not at state expense), it comports with the procedural scheme approved in Vitek and certainly with Section 83 of the Restatement of Judgments, even though in Vitek, rights more critical than money (money cases would presumably dominate this new court system under contemplation) were at issue.  All of the basic rights guaranteed there (notice, neutral decision maker, decisions based on facts and law, final decision) are respected.

Question11

G. Under §1367, claims arising out of the same case or controversy (as defined in Gibbs) as an anchoring claim properly before the federal court may also be heard by the court as long as the plaintiff is not bringing a claim that violates complete diversity. Here, there is an anchoring diversity claim (Hopkirk = NM, Dunkin = DE and MA, $200K in controversy).  Claims 2 and 3 are not claims brought by plaintiffs.  Claim 2 fits within FRCP 14(a), shifting liability.  Claim 3 fits within FRCP 14(a)(2)(D), the same transaction or occurrence (given broad construction under Heyward Robinson).  Claim 4 is a claim by a plaintiff (Hopkirk, NM) against a person made a party under Rule 14 (Sticky’s, DE and NM) that would violate complete diversity.
