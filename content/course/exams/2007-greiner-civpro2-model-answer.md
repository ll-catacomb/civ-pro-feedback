                     Final Exam Question 1 Model Answer
Question 1(a)
      (i)   The order remanding the Second Case is not appealable.  Period.
 The end.  28 U.S.C. § 1447((d).  Not even mandamus will lie (section 1447
says remand orders are "not reviewable on appeal or otherwise . . .").
[Note to students:  This answer may have been accurate when written, but is
questionable now, after Carlsbad; the remand here could not have been for
lack of SMJ, as there is diversity jurisdiction, bur rather for the defect
in the removal proceeding, see immediately below.]
      (ii)  If we could get the Second Circuit to hear us, we would likely
succeed in getting the remand order reversed.  Our removal was not itself
proper.  Dong Ah has its principal place of business in New York and is
thus a New York citizen for diversity purposes.  Thus, under section
1441(b), our removal was improper because we were sued in our home state
court.  However, under section 1447(c), Parker had only 30 days to move to
remand on the basis of a defect other than lack of subject matter
jurisdiction.  Since Parker moved to remand more than 30 days after our
removal, the only relevant question is whether Parker could have sued us in
New York federal court originally.  He could have.  Parker is an Alabama
citizen, and Dong Ah is New York/PRC.  The amount in controversy obviously
exceeds $75K.  Diversity jurisdiction exists under section 1332.
Question 1(b)
      (i)   Judge Watson's order is likely appealable under the collateral
order doctrine.  The stay here is essentially the same as the stay in Clark
v. Lacy, that is, a stay that awaits the result of another piece of
litigation before the court dismisses.  Judge Watson's order (1)
conclusively resolves the question of whether the FSC is enforceable, and
accordingly, whether the case will go forward in the PRC (unless we screw
up somehow by not complying with her conditions), (2) resolves an important
question (whether the case can go forward in the U.S. at all) that is
independent of the merits of whether we tortiously interfered with Parker's
employment contract, and (3) is effectively otherwise unreviewable on
appeal, since Judge Watson intends to dismiss once a PRC court goes to
final judgment.  Under Cohen, then, the order is probably appealable.
      Other grounds for appeal, if they were needed, probably wouldn't
work.  Judge Watson's stay order is not a final order under 1291; it is
stay, not a dismissal.  There is only one claim, so Rule 54(b) is out, plus
even though Judge Watson said some of Rule 54(b)'s magic words ("no just
reason for delay"), she didn't direct the entry of judgment.  There is no
injunction at issue, 1292(a)(1) is out.  Regarding 1292(b), Judge Watson
did find that her FSC ruling involved a controlling question of law and
that an ultimate appeal might materially advance the termination of the
litigation, but she expressly found that there was NOT substantial ground
for a difference of opinion ("no-brainer").  A petition for a writ of
mandamus might work if Parker could convince the Second Circuit that
something really horrible is going on, but there is no suggestion that
district courts are improperly enforcing FSC clauses right and left.  Plus,
Justices Kennedy and O'Conner think they are fantastic, so a supervisory
writ (La Buy) is unlikely.
      (ii)  If Parker succeeded in getting there, the Second Circuit needs
to decide several items.  First, it needs to decide what the question is:
the enforceability of the FSC, or (less likely) forum non conveniens
(remember Stewart v. Ricoh:  sometimes the choice of question is more
important than the answer).
      Assume for the moment that the issue is whether the FSC is valid and
enforceable, which I think is by far the most likely.  To decide that, it
needs to decide what law governs the issue:  federal common law or state
law.  (Note that it's federal common law that is at issue, not federal
statutory law or a fed civ pro rule; 1404(a) and 1406(a) don't speak to
transfer/dismissal-in-favor-of a court in another country).  That's an Erie
question.  Initially, part of the problem here is that we don't exactly
know what federal law is with respect to the enforceability of FSCs.
Justices Kennedy and O'Conner in the Stewart concurrence said FSCs should
generally be enforced, so I'll assume their view represents federal law.
That means there could be a conflict between federal common law and Alabama
law, since Alabama wouldn't enforce the FSC.  Note that even if federal law
would not enforce the FSC in this case, it seems as though New York law
would, so there could still be a conflict between federal law and New York
law.  (See below re which state's law would apply here.)
      So, getting back to Erie, perhaps the most important thing is that
there is no statute (or rule of civil procedure) that governs the issue of
the enforceability of FSCs (indeed, the FNC doctrine is itself judge-made).
 That means we're NOT in a Hannah situation; rather, we're contemplating
using judge-made law, which we generally don't do (Erie overruled Tyson),
but sometimes we do (e.g., Semtek).  (Note that neither 1404 or 1406 speaks
to situations in which the would-be transferor court is abroad.)  With this
in mind, the Supreme Court has analyzed Erie questions in a lot of
different ways.  If we follow York, we can say that the FSC's
enforceability probably is dispositive of the case, but it may not be
despositive in the sense that York meant.  York involved a statute of
limitations dismissal, which is a legal end to the case.  Here, the FSC
issue may be outcome determinative because (putting aside the justice
available in the PRC court system) the economic incentives are probably not
high enough for Parker to pursue a claim abroad.  That's a lot like arguing
that an FSC ruling sending the case to the PRC is the economic "death
knell" to the litigation, though, and the courts rejected the death knell
doctrine in the context of appealability of class action orders (prior to
the adoption of Rule 23(f)).
      We could also discuss this issue in terms of what several cases
(e.g., Stewart) called the twin aims of Erie.  Plaintiffs will forum shop
to avoid FSCs, and Alabama defendants sued by Alabama plaintiffs on Alabama
causes of action will be treated differently (unequally?) from Alabama
defendants sued by non-Alabama plaintiffs or from non-Alabama defendants
sued by Alabama plaintiffs (either sued on Alabama causes of action).  I
also think that the FSCs like these are likely to affect employers'
"primary conduct" (see Justice Harlan's concurrence in Hannah) in that if
employers know that they will be sued only in their home courts, they might
be more likely to fire U.S. employees, particularly if their home courts
are abroad.  For the same reason, foreign investors might be more willing
to buy stock.  On the whole, then, I think it likely that the Second
Circuit would hold that a state law should govern this issue, although
there's certainly room for debate.  By the way, if I'm wrong about that,
i.e., if federal law should govern, then the Second Circuit might well
affirm.  Kennedy and O'Connoer's concurrence in Stewart lauds FSCs, and the
federal judiciary is generally pro-contract.  The feds would probably say
that Parker knew (or should have known) when he signed his employment
contract that his choice of forum would be limited.
      Now, supposing state law governs, which state?  The case was
originally filed in Alabama and removed there.  It was then transferred to
New York.  If it was transferred under 1404(a), the New York federal court
has to apply Alabama law, including Alabama choice of law rules.  If the
case was transferred under 1406(a), the New York federal court has to apply
New York choice of law rules.  The Judge Goblin didn't specify, so the
Second Circuit will have to decide for itself which statute applies (or
remand directing the district court to decide the issue).  That issue turns
on whether the Alabama court had in personam jurisdiction over Dong Ah.  If
it did, we do 1404(a).  If it didn't, we do 1406(a).  (Note that the
federal venue statute is not applicable because the case was originally
filed in state court and removed.)
      Can an Alabama (with its to-the-maximum-DPC-extent long-arm statute)
constitutionally exercise IPJ over Dong Ah?  Minimum contacts,
International Shoe.  Here, Dong Ah owned stock in what became an Alabama
company, but ESI became an Alabama company over its objection, so this is
sort of like Hanson v. Deckla plus.  Another contact more directly related
to the litigation is Dong Ah's merger talks with ESI, an Alabama company.
There's no information about whether the merger ever went through, however.
 We might argue that it's hard to see how Dong Ah could reasonably have
anticipated getting sued in Alabama, since there's no evidence that it does
any business there.  But, Parker might argue that Dong Ah committed a wrong
that it knew would have effects in Alabama (the lost job), see Kulko,
although the damages (lost wages) are arguably Alabama/Mississippi.
Finally, Parker tagged Dong Ah by serving process on a Dong Ah employee
when that employee was in Alabama on activities related to the proposed
merger (remember that the proposed merger was the impetus for the allegedly
tortious activity).  That's important for minimum contacts, and it might
constitute an entirely independent reason to support IPJ.  See Burnam
plurality.  Yes, the tagged employee was a low-level, but that didn't seem
to bother the court in International Shoe.
      Assume for the moment that the Second Circuit would find that an
Alabama court had IPJ over Dong Ah on whatever theory, then it should
require that Judge Watson use Alabama choice of law rules for the case.
Here, we have another substance versus procedure problem (note that the
substance/procedure line for vertical (Erie) choice of law may be in a
different place form the substance/procedure line in the between-state
context).  Every state "chooses" to use its own procedural law, so if
Alabama would consider the enforceability of the FSC to be a matter of
procedure, it would employ its own law voiding the clause.  We'd need to
research Alabama law here; if there isn't anything directly on point, we
might research how Alabama treats other quasi-procedural issues, such as
statutes of limitations.  Absent any other information, my rank speculation
is that Alabama would consider this a matter of procedure, since the issue
can be decided early in the litigation and it's separate from the "merits"
of whether a tort was committed.  If my rank speculation is correct, the
Second Circuit will reverse and order Judge Watson to proceed with the
case.
      If Alabama would consider the FCS's enforceability a matter of
"substance," then it would conduct a choice of law analysis under the First
Restatement.  The law of the state in which the last event necessary to
"complete" the tort would govern essentially all issues, including the
enforceability of the FSC.  That would probably be New York, since the
firing took place in New York.  One might argue that damages are necessary
to "complete" the tort, and that the damages were suffered in Alabama, but
this is less likely.  So, if as a matter of between-state choice of law,
the FSC's enforceability is considered "substantive," then New York law
would probably apply, and under New York law, the FSC might well be
enforceable.  In that event, the Second Circuit would affirm.
      Now, assume that the Second Circuit finds that an Alabama court could
NOT exercise IPJ over Dong Ah.  In that event, New York state choice of law
rules would apply.  Initially, we'd face the same substance/procedure
question we faced when speaking of Alabama choice of law rules, and we have
no more information here than we did there.  If New York would consider FSC
enforceability procedural, it will apply its own law, and accordingly, the
Second Circuit should affirm.  If New York would considers the FSC
substantive, the Second Circuit will conduct a New York choice of law
analysis.  Under Babcock and the most significant relationship test, the
question is which state has the most significant relationship to the issue
of the FSC's enforceability (as opposed to the whole case).  That might
well be Alabama, or even Mississippi.  The contract was presumably signed
in Mississippi, and at the time it concerned a Mississippi corporation.
Now, the corporation is in Alabama, and Parker works there.  It's hard to
see how New York has much relationship to or interest in the issue of the
enforceability of a contract clause between a person who works in Alabama
and his Alabama employer as relates to an internationally-based
stockholder.  This is like Babcock in that although the tort occurred in
New York, the issue here is one of relationships among the parties.  So New
York choice of law rules might well mandate the application of Alabama
substantive law, in which case the Second Circuit would reverse and order
Judge Watson to proceed to litigate.
      What about if the question is FNC, not the FSC's enforceability?  In
that case, the FSC should be just one among several factors.  We'd have to
go through the same federal versus state, New York versus Alabama choice of
law question to figure out which FNC law applies.  We don't know anything
about the FNC law of New York or Alabama, but their FNC doctrines are
anything like the federal doctrine as outlined in Piper, it's REALLY hard
to see how this case should end up in the PRC.  The ONLY factor suggesting
it should go there is contract provision (along with perhaps Dong Ah's
place of incorporation, but Dong Ah's principal place of business is in the
United States).  But if the contract provision along is enough, then we've
just turned the FNC analysis back into the enforceability of the FSC.
Meanwhile, almost all of the witnesses, all of the relevant events, the
plaintiff's choice of forum, and rumors about the quality of PRC courts
(see Piper re availability of some remedy) all point to stateside
litigation.
Question 1(c)
      We might try to argue that Judge Watson's order in the First Case has
issue preclusive effect in the subsequent state court litigation on the
issue of the enforceability of the contractual forum selection clause.
Since the "rendering" court here was federal, federal common law probably
governs (Semtek), which would incorporate either AL or NY preclusion law,
depending on the 1404(a)/1406(a) transfer issue discussed above; note we
could ask the New York state court to give issue preclusive effect to Judge
Watson's stay order even if the federal court would not (Hart).  Thus, back
in Albany state court, we can move to dismiss or stay the Second Case on
the grounds that the enforceability of the forum selection clause (the
"FSC") was already decided in our favor.  We would argue that the FSC issue
is the same in state court as it is in federal court, Parker argued it
fully and lost it, and the issue was necessary to (indeed, was the sole
basis for) Judge Watson's stay order.
      There are a couple of problems.  First, Judge Watson did not issue a
final judgment.  The order might still be appealable (see above), but Judge
Watson explicitly contemplated revising the order under certain
circumstances, so the order appears to lack the requisite finality.  We can
try to argue that Judge Watson contemplates no further action on the case,
especially since we intend to comply with all of her conditions, and thus
that the order is essentially final.
      Second, it's not clear that the same law would govern a motion to
dismiss or stay in the Second Case as governed in the First Case.  It's
also not clear whether that matters, i.e., whether "issue" in "issue
preclusion" means that the rendering court had to be litigating the "issue"
under the same law as would apply in the subsequent court.  If the issue is
the enforceability of the FSC, as opposed to FNC (as seems more likely, per
above), it feels like that issue was already litigated (although, per
above, perhaps not incorporated as part of a judgment), and it shouldn't
matter which law applies.  That seems most likely.
      If, however, "issue" in "issue preclusion" does mean that the issue
had to be litigated in the rendering court under the same (or similar) law
as would apply in the subsequent court, then we have to figure out whether
the law that would Second Case is substantially the same as the law that
did to the stay order in the First Case.  If there's a conflict, no issue
preclusion; if the two legal rules are pretty similar then, perhaps it's
the same "issue."  Regarding what law did apply (or should have applied) in
the First Case, the NY Supreme Court would apply NY  procedural law and NY
choice of law principles to substantive issues; if FSC enforceability is
substantive, the NY court might ell apply AL law on FSC enforceability!
