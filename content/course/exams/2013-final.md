## Background Law

Oregon Statutes Section 5541 provides as follows:

> *In any action sounding in tort and involving a plea for damages for emotional distress, a successful plaintiff shall be entitled to damages according to the following schedule:*
>
> *(a) emotional harm caused by the death of the plaintiff or of a member of the plaintiff's immediate family: $150,000.*
>
> *(b) emotional harm caused by severe bodily injury to the plaintiff or a member of the plaintiff's immediate family: $130,000*
>
> *(c) emotional harm not caused by bodily harm to any person: $80,000.*

Under federal common law, there is no limit to the amount an individual may recover for emotional distress; the issue is submitted to the jury, which may award an amount it deems proper (subject to ordinary supervision by the trial judge).

Arizona's common law regarding damages for emotional distress is identical to federal common law.

In 2002, the California Legislature passed the "Emotional Distress Lawsuits Are Out Of Control Act of 2002," known the "EDLAOOCA." In relevant part, the EDLAOOCA provides as follows.

> *1) Findings: The California Legislature notes a proliferation of lawsuits against California companies in California courts (and in courts outside of California) in which individuals sought damages for emotional distress. The proliferation of these lawsuits has caused a detrimental effect upon the California economy. Companies employing citizens of California have begun to move their operations elsewhere to avoid the reach of the law, causing job losses to other states. Companies that have remained in California have begun to engage in excessively cautious behavior to forestall lawsuits claiming emotional harm.*
>
> *2) The laws of the State of California providing a right to sue for emotional harm or distress are hereby amended so as to make them subject to the following limits:*
>
> > *a) In any action sounding in tort and including a plea for damages for emotional distress, plaintiffs may proceed only as individuals. There shall be no joinder by one plaintiff in a lawsuit with another plaintiff, and no aggregation devices shall be maintained.*
> >
> > *b) In any action sounding in tort, involving a plea for damages for emotional distress, and not involving the death of the plaintiff or any member of the plaintiff's family, a plaintiff who proves that she has suffered emotional*

<!-- page: 4 -->

> > *harm shall be entitled to an award of exactly $20,000, no more and no less.*

The law described above applies in full to the common law tort of "Outrage" or "Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress." California and Oregon both recognize this tort, but the two states define the tort slightly differently.

- Under the common law of California, "Outrage" is defined as follows: "One who by extreme or outrageous conduct intentionally *or recklessly* causes severe emotional distress to another is subject to liability for such emotional distress" (emphasis added). The California common law of tort defines "recklessness" as the "failure to exercise due care to eliminate a known risk of harm."

- Under the common law of Oregon, in contrast, "Outrage" is defined as follows: "One who by extreme or outrageous conduct intentionally causes severe emotional distress to another is subject to liability for such emotional distress."

- Thus, under the law of Oregon, a plaintiff must prove that the defendant *intentionally* committed the extreme or outrageous conduct, whereas in California a plaintiff may succeed by proving that a defendant's acts were *reckless* and need not (but may) prove intent.

- Under the law of both Oregon and California, the tort of "Outrage" is not complete until the plaintiff suffers the emotional distress that gives rise to the claim.

Arizona law of Outrage is identical to that of California.

California follows the Second Restatement on Choice of Law. Oregon and Arizona follow the First Restatement on choice of law.

All states in this exam have long-arm statutes authorizing exercise of jurisdiction to the limits of the U.S. Constitution.

California, Arizona, and Oregon all have state civil procedure provisions identical to Fed. R. Civ. P. 23, and they follow federal law interpretations of Rule 23 with respect to their respective counterpart provisions.

The law of all relevant states, as well as federal common law, includes the doctrine of respondeat superior. Oregon's issue preclusion doctrine requires full mutuality. California's preclusion law is the same as federal common law. Arizona has completely abandoned the mutuality requirement.

## Fact Pattern

Weasley's Wizard Wheezes, Inc. ("WWW") is incorporated in Delaware. It manufactures its nearly magical internet products in its enormous manufacturing facility just outside of Portland, Oregon, where it employs about 2000 people. WWW's plant is considered one of the best places to work in the state, featuring a fully complete gym with a swimming pool, a first class dining

<!-- page: 5 -->

facility, and live musical performances every Friday at 5:30 pm. The Mayor of the City of Portland regularly visits the facility to make sure that all is well at one of the City's biggest employers.

WWW's owners are Fred and George Weasley; between them, they own 65% of the company's voting stock. Fred is the Chief Executive Officer, the Chief Operating Officer, and the Chief Financial Officer. George serves as both General Counsel (he obtained his law degree from Hogwarts in 2003) and the Chair of the Board. Both are domiciled in Sacramento, California, where they enjoy life in their large two-house compound just outside the city limits. They have a small building on the compound with five rooms in it that they use as their offices, where they run the company. Their two executive assistants, Angelina Johnson and Oliver Wood, came to work in this small building, which also had a fully wired room for board meetings. The compound also has a single-building work area, where Fred and George create their latest prototypes.

The Weasleys were excited. They had just created a new product, the Weasley's Amazing Machine (the "WAM"). The WAM represented the next generation of WWW's line of Internet-based gaming products. It consisted of a full body suit that the user could wear while standing or sitting on a specially designed platform that could simulate the movements of anything from an airplane to an earthquake. The result was a total immersion in the simulation. The WAM came with a menu of over 1500 simulation-based games that a user could experience, all of them interactive and based on simulated worlds that existed on the Internet.

The Weasleys wanted to make a big splash with the WAM, and they thought the best way to do so would be to provide a group of WWW's 30 best customers with a free copy of the device for their exclusive use for a period of two months. They hoped that the users would create Youtube videos about the WAM that would go viral and attract more viewers than Gangnam Style. Accordingly, the Weasleys contacted 30 customers to invite them to a party at their Sacramento compound. The invitations were sent via gmail (which does not reveal the email recipients' physical locations).

The party, which occurred on February 23, 2013, was a roaring affair; Psy (a Korean pop star) came to perform for the 30 customers, the Weasleys, and their two assistants, Johnson and Wood. No alcohol was served, however, as each customer had been instructed to bring a vehicle with adequate trunk space for a special "end of party gift." During the party, the guests were treated to a tour of the Weasley's Sacramento compound, including a 30-minute look inside of Fred & George's workroom. Fred and George gleefully described how they had created the 30 WAM prototypes from scratch entirely within this workroom. At the end of the party, the Weasleys presented each of the 30 customers with his or her own WAM. The customers were overjoyed. Each loaded up his or her WAM and headed home.

One of these customers was Nymphadora Tonks, who preferred to go by her surname, "Tonks." Tonks was in a particularly good mood at the party because about four weeks earlier, she had discovered that she was pregnant; she and her husband (named Reus Lupin) had decided, however, that they would not tell anyone about the pregnancy for another three months, just in case something happened.

<!-- page: 6 -->

Tonks drove her WAM back to her house in Hogsmeade, Oregon, just across the California/Oregon border. She installed the unit in her bedroom, fired it up, called the office where she worked to say that she would be taking a three-week vacation. Too embarrassed to say how she really planned to spend the time, she told her office that she would be on a romantic getaway with Remus drinking wine in the Napa valley. She and Remus spent the next 20 days more or less continuously in the WAM bodysuit, taking turns playing the games. They were both delighted. At the end of the three-week WAM binge, both returned to work, but they continued to enjoy the WAM for a few hours in the evening and a little more on the weekend.

About two months after the WAM party, however, they suffered a severe setback. Tonks suffered a miscarriage. Remus and she were both horribly sad, and they cut down their WAM time some to spend more time together, although they both played about two or three hours a week.

In late June of 2013, while surfing the Internet from her home in Hogsmeade, Tonks stumbled across a new website and blog called "AllAboutWWWFans.com." When she went to the site, she was horrified to see, on the first page, pictures of Remus and herself in various states of undress in their home bedroom. After she clicked on a panel that said, "Click here if you want to learn more about these losers!", she found text that described accurately her height and weight, that stated that she exercised regularly and had good reflexes, that she had told her officemates that she would be away on a 3-week vacation in the Napa valley but that she had really spent 8 hours a day in "a new WWW product not on the market yet," and that "we think she might have even miscarried her baby." Tonks was devastated. She explored the website further and found private information, including photos, of each of the attendees of the WAM party.

That night, Tonks and Remus threw their WAM in the trash. The next day Tonks sought psychiatric counseling for grief and shock from a counselor in Sacramento. The counselor diagnosed her with post-traumatic stress disorder. The day after, Tonks consulted a lawyer. A week later, Tonks' attorney filed a lawsuit in United States District Court for the District of Oregon. The Complaint was as follows:
