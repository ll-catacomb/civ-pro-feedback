Please review Fed. R. Civ. P. 11(a).

A dismissal for failure to comply with FRCP 11 is not on the merits for claim preclusion purposes.

Texas state rules of civil procedure include the following rule. TXRCP 11:

> **(a) Signature.** Every complaint or answer must be signed on each and every page by at least one attorney of record – or by a party personally if the party is unrepresented. Each such page must state the signer's address, e-mail address, and telephone number below the signature. The court shall immediately grant a motion to dismiss responding to a complaint that fails to comply with this rule, and must immediately grant a default judgment against a defendant whose answer fails to comply with this rule. The court

> may issue an order of dismissal or default sua sponte without notice to the party affected.

In the 2010 case of *Weasley v. Malfoy*, the Texas Supreme Court (the highest court in Texas) held that the purpose of TXRCP 11(a) was to force the attorney of record to review each page of the complaint for accuracy, something it doubted attorneys would do without the signature requirement. Given the importance of this purpose, the Texas Supreme Court held that dismissals under Texas Rule 11 are on the merits for claim preclusion purposes.

Texas, and Louisiana, otherwise follow federal common law regarding claim and issue preclusion. Except as otherwise stated in this exam, Texas and Louisiana have rules of procedure and procedural law that are the same as their federal counterparts.

In all jurisdictions relevant to this exam, a tort exists called "fraud in the inducement to contract" (usually shortened to "fraud in the inducement"). The elements of a claim for fraud in the inducement are as follows:

1. a contracting party intentionally makes a statement (oral or written), or fails to make a statement, during contract negotiation with an opposite party;
2. such statement, or omission, is false or misleading;
3. such statement or omission is material;
4. the opposing party reasonably relies on the false or misleading statement or omissions by entering into a contract; and
5. the opposing party suffers damages as a result of entering into the contract.

The last event needed to make the tort of fraud in the inducement complete is that the plaintiff/opposite party suffers damages (which may be earlier than when she discovers the harm).

Case law in both Texas and Louisiana has established that, if a state or federal statute specifies that a contracting party must make a particular disclosure to the opposite party during contract negotiations/formation, then an omission of that disclosure, or a false statement made during that disclosure, is conclusively deemed to be "material" for the purposes of a fraud in the inducement tort.

In Texas, punitive damages are available for the tort of fraud in the inducement, if the plaintiff can prove that the defendant's commission of the tort was malicious, wanton, and beyond the bounds of common decency. Louisiana, in contrast, has by statute banned punitive damages for the tort of fraud in the inducement. You may assume during this exam that the availability of punitive damages is substantive for both horizontal and vertical choice of law purposes.

TX uses the Second Restatement for HCOL purposes. LA uses the First Restatement. Both jurisdictions' long-arm statutes go to the limits of the Constitution.

> **Section 9.75**
