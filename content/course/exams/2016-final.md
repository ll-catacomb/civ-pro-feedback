## Background Law

New Jersey follows the rule of *Russell v. Place*, *i.e.*, that if a finding on any of several independent theories could support a jury verdict, and the jury verdict does not disclose the theory upon which the jury relied, then issue preclusion will not be available on any of those theories. New Jersey follows federal common law (as articulated in *Parklane Hosiery*) regarding mutuality in issue preclusion. A New Jersey statute prohibits the implementation of forum selection clauses in contracts. New Jersey has a declaratory judgment statute identical to 28 U.S.C. § 2201. New Jersey rules of civil procedure allow joinder of any claim against any party, so long as that claim arises out of the same transaction or occurrence as any other claim already in a case. New Jersey's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the federal Constitution. New Jersey follows the First Restatement for horizontal choice of law. For purposes of this exam, under the First Restatement, the law of the forum governs procedural issues (as we discussed in class in the torts context), but the law of the place of a sale of a product (meaning, for the purposes of this exam, the state in which the sales contract was signed) governs the enforceability of any substantive terms in the contract for that sale.

In Oregon, if a finding on any of several independent theories could support a jury verdict, and the jury verdict does not disclose the theory upon which the jury relied, then the jury is presumed to have found for the prevailing party on all theories, and issue preclusion will be available on all of those theories. Oregon allows issue preclusion without regard to mutuality. An Oregon statute requires implementation of forum selection clauses in contracts under all circumstances. Oregon has a declaratory judgment statute identical to 28 U.S.C. § 2201. Oregon rules of civil procedure allow joinder of any claim against any party, so long as that claim arises out of the same transaction or occurrence as any other claim already in a case. Oregon's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the federal Constitution. Oregon follows the First Restatement for horizontal choice of law. For purposes of this exam, under the First Restatement, the law of the forum governs procedural issues (as we discussed in class in the torts context), but the law of the place of a sale of a product (meaning, for the purposes of this exam, the state in which the sales contract was signed) governs the enforceability of any substantive terms in the contract for that sale.

Federal common law, revised since the U.S. Supreme Court's ruling in *Russell v. Place*, provides that if a finding on any of several independent theories could support a jury verdict, and the jury verdict does not disclose theory issue upon which the jury relied, then a district judge in a subsequent case has discretion regarding whether to allow issue preclusion, depending on how the district judge believes that the jury decided the case (based on the judge's review of the first case's record). Federal common law regarding mutuality is available from cases from *Parklane*. Under federal common law, forum selection clauses are enforceable.

All states relevant to this exam have two identical causes of action.

1. The first is standard breach of contract. Among other things, breach of contract provides a cause of action for damages to a purchaser of a product if the seller promises that something is true about the product, the promise turns out to be untrue, and the purchaser suffers harm as a result of reliance on the false promise.
2. The second, called products liability, is a type of tort. If a manufacturer of a product creates a defective good, such as might happen if there is a mistake in the manufacturing process, and as a result of the defect the consumer of the product suffers harm, then the consumer may sue the manufacturer for damages.

On December 21 of 2014, after taking their last law school exams for the semester, Hermione and Farley left their condo in Eugene, Oregon in their Comet 260 to drive to Sacramento to spend the holidays with their parents. A freak snowstorm in Eugene had left snow on the ground, but Hermione and Farley felt safe in their Comet 260, given the warranty in their purchase contract. Alas, 15 minutes after leaving their Eugene condo, with Hermione driving, the car spun out on a street with approximately five inches of snow on it. The car struck a tree, and both Hermione and Farley were injured in the crash. Hermione spent a week in an Oregon hospital, while Farley spent two nights in the same hospital. Both had painful physical therapy for two months after the accident.

In May of 2015, Malfoy's general counsel received a letter from the law firm of Scamander, Sprout, & Diggory, LLP. The letter stated in relevant part:

After discovery, the case went to trial before an Oregon jury. Malfoy, as the plaintiff, went first. It introduced the testimony of several of its design engineers, who opined that the tires' treads were properly adapted for cold-weather (snow and ice) conditions. It further introduced evidence from its manufacturing engineers who, after examining the car wreckage, opined that there was nothing amiss with the car's rear tire alignment. Upon the end of Malfoy's evidence, Hermione called two witnesses expert in car manufacturing process, who testified (after examining the vehicle and the crash site) that the particular car she purchased had tires that were badly misaligned, rendering the car dangerous to drive under any conditions. In addition, Hermione called two car design experts, who testified that the design of the Comet 260 was not well-suited for a vehicle to be driven in any amount of snow because the tires, particularly the treads, were not designed for snow. Neither party moved for judgment as a matter of law.

The case went to the jury, which after instruction was asked a single question on the jury verdict form: "Has Malfoy Industries, Inc., proved that is more likely than not that it is not liable to Ms. Hermione Granger for her injuries; or has Ms. Granger proved that is more likely than not that Malfoy is liable to her for $135,000 in damages; or has neither party met the burden of proving liability/non-liability?".

In response, the jury returned a verdict form stating, "We find that Ms. Granger has proved that it is more likely than not that Malfoy Industries, Inc. is liable for her injuries and order it to pay her the sum of $135,000." The Oregon judge entered judgment in accordance with the jury's verdict on July 12, 2016.

In response, a meeting was held in the general counsel's office of Malfoy, Inc.
