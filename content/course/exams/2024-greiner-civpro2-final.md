## The Law

**Forum non conveniens and forum selection**: A Colorado state statute prohibits the enforcement of forum selection clauses and bans the use of forum non conveniens. Arizona common law and federal common law both provide for enforcement of forum selection clauses, and Arizona law is the same as federal common law with respect to forum non conveniens.

**Respondeat superior**: All states relevant to this exam follow the doctrine of respondeat superior, meaning that an employer/company can be held liable for the acts or omissions of its employees performed during the discharge of their duties.

**HCOL Restatements**: Colorado follows the First Restatement for Horizontal Choice of Law, Arizona the Second Restatement.

**Long-arms**: All states relevant to this exam have long-arm statutes going to constitutional limits.

**Limitations**: In both Colorado and Arizona, the statute of limitations for personal injury lawsuits is 1 year from the events that gave rise to the lawsuit. In both states, the statute of limitations for breach of contract is six years from the events constituting the breach.

**Preclusion**: Under federal common law, statute of limitations dismissals are not on the merits for claim preclusion purposes. Under the law of both Colorado and Arizona, statutes of limitations dismissals are on the merits for claim preclusion purposes. Both Colorado and Arizona require strict mutuality for issue preclusion purposes. You already know federal issue preclusion law.

**Districts**: Colorado and Arizona each have only one federal district court.

**Congressional statute**: In early 2021, Congress passed the Intoxicated Pilots Act. Section T-800 of the act provided as follows:

> The Congress finds that intoxicated airplane pilots pose a danger to passengers, to people on the ground in airports and elsewhere, and to property. The Congress hereby directs the Secretary of Transportation and the Secretary of Commerce to promulgate rules to protect the public against threats that drunk pilots pose. Such rules shall include a prohibition on the possession and consumption of any amount of alcohol in the cockpit of an airplane capable of flight. The United States Department of Justice, but not any private individual, may sue to effectuate rules that either Secretary promulgates.

Neither Secretary has as of yet promulgated regulations pursuant to section T-800.

**Prejudgment seizure scheme**: A Colorado statute called the "Prejudgment Seizure Act ("PSA") of 2017," still in force, provides as follows:

> A plaintiff may initiate a civil action in this state by filing a motion with the court requesting the seizure of any thing of value located in this state at the time of the seizure and owned by the defendant in the lawsuit only if the purpose of the seizure is to assure payment of liability in that lawsuit. Such seizure shall be effectuated only by a sheriff of the court, and only upon an order by a judge of that court. To request a seizure, the plaintiff shall file all the following documents with the court:
>
> - An affidavit, sworn under penalty of perjury, from the plaintiff/petitioner detailing the facts, and identifying the cause of action, that plaintiff/petitioner believes will result in the imposition of liability on the defendant;
> - True and correct copies of any and all documents that the plaintiff/petitioner contends will support its contentions;
> - A description of the item to be seized, along with a statement of its anticipated value;
> - A list of the names and contact information, if known, of any witnesses to the events leading to the civil action, which list must identify the witnesses that the petitioner/plaintiff might call to testify in support of their contentions;
> - A statement from the petitioner/plaintiff summarizing the anticipated testimony that the witnesses identified as possibly supporting the petitioner/plaintiff;
> - A bond from a properly financed issuer for double the value of the property to be seized, guaranteeing the payment of damages to the defendant if the plaintiff/petitioner loses the litigation and is unable to pay; and
> - A statement of reasons as to why the seizure is required to protect the plaintiff/petitioner's interests, including a brief discussion of why the lawsuit cannot effectively proceed in personam. Note: The fact that the defendant is a corporation located outside the United States, holding no assets in the United States, shall be deemed a reason sufficient to justify a seizure under this statute (provided that all of the other requirements of this statute are met).[^1]
>
> A judge receiving a petition for a writ of seizure, upon being satisfied that the foregoing are met, that (based on the information thus far available) the plaintiff/petitioner is likely to succeed on the merits and likely to face substantially uncertainty or difficulty collecting on any judgment, shall issue a writ of seizure. The sheriff will execute the writ of seizure forthwith. Thereafter, the judge shall
>
> - Require that the petitioner/plaintiff immediately provide notice of the action, including a copy of all papers described above, to the defendant;
> - Within two weeks of the seizure, or as soon as possibly practicable thereafter, hold a hearing in which the defendant may contest the seizure with any

<!-- page: 5 -->

> evidence or legal arguments, and in which the court shall consider the plaintiff/petitioner's likelihood of success in the lawsuit, the harm suffered to both parties if the seizure does or does not occur, and the public interest;
> - Allow the defendant to post a counter-bond from a properly financed issuer for an amount up to the damages the plaintiff/petitioner alleged to have been owed, and upon the posting of such a counterbond, return the seized item to the defendant immediately; and
> - If the order of seizure is not vacated at the above-described hearing, expedite adjudication of the merits of the plaintiff/petitioner's claim.

**Negligence cause of action**: All states relevant to this exam have a cause of action called negligence. The elements of the negligence cause of action are

- the existence of a duty from the defendant to the plaintiff (all persons have a duty to exercise due care in their interactions with other persons),
- the breach of that duty through the defendant's failure to exercise ordinary care under the circumstances,
- the breach caused the plaintiff an injury, and
- the injury to the plaintiff resulted in quantifiable damages.

A plaintiff may prove a negligence cause of action in two ways, first by alleging and proving that the defendant's actions or inactions fell below the common understanding of ordinary care, and second that the defendant violated a statute or agency rule (federal or state) enacted to protect persons like the plaintiff (if you've taken torts, this latter theory is called "negligence 'per se.'")

**Breach of contract cause of action**: All states relevant to this exam have a cause of action called breach of contract. The elements of this cause of action are the existence of a valid contract, the defendant's breach of that contract, and damages to the plaintiff caused by the breach.

## The Events

**The Grand Canyon Tour**: In April of 2022, dialing from her college apartment in Boulder, CO, Sarah Connor called Skynet's booking number, hoping to find a trip she could take in June. She reserved a seat on Skynet's two-seater airplane for the "Grand Canyon Tour" in early June. The Grand Canyon tour's flight plan consisted of taking off from Nogales, Mexico; traveling northwest for a two-hour low-altitude flyover of several of the Grand Canyon's most beautiful locations;[^2] following the Colorado River north into Colorado for a 20-minute flight "so low that you'll feel like you're swimming" (in the words of the Skynet telephone salesperson); and then following a straight line back to Nogales. In part because of the amount of fuel required, the price of the "Grand Canyon Tour" was expensive, but Connor's relatives and friends chipped in to make the trip an early graduation present.

In late May, after taking her last exam, Connor took a bus from Boulder, CO to her childhood home in Phoenix. She spent a couple of weeks relaxing with her family.
