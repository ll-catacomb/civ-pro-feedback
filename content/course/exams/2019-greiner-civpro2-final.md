Third, the law in all fifty-one United States includes the tort of "Business Disparagement." Under this
tort, a defendant is liable to a plaintiff if the defendant

    (i) utters (orally or in writing), or produces, or participates in the production of,
    (ii) a false or misleading statement or omission, or false image, or false video,
    (iii) which harms the reputation, public perception, or standing of a business, and
    (iv) which harm to reputation, perception, or standing causes a loss of value to a business,

              corporation, or partnership.
Under the law in all fifty-one states, a harmed business may sue for the tort of Business Disparagement,
asserting as damages the amount of the lost value to the business or corporation caused by the false
statement or video. Also, any shareholder or partial owner of a harmed business may sue for the tort of
Business Disparagement, asserting as damages the fraction of that individual's ownership in the
business multiplied by the amount of the lost value to the business or corporation caused by the false
statement or video. The last event necessary to complete the Business Disparagement tort is the
infliction of the harm, in the form of the loss of value by the business.

Finally, Massachusetts, unlike any other state in the United States, provides an affirmative defense to
the Business Disparagement tort called the "Public Access" defense. Under Massachusetts law, the
Public Access defense means that if a defendant proves that the affected business has constant and
ready access to the public or to media outlets, then the plaintiff cannot recover.2 This defense applies
regardless of whether the plaintiff is the affected business itself or one of its shareholders, so long as the
affected business has constant and ready access to the public or to media outlets.
