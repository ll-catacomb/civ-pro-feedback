Assume the following, all of which may be (and probably is) entirely fictional.

Under federal common law, judgments against a plaintiff based on a failure of the plaintiff to file a lawsuit within the applicable limitations period are not entitled to claim preclusion effect. Under the laws of New Hampshire and Massachusetts, judgments based on a failure of a plaintiff to file within the applicable limitations period are entitled to claim preclusion effect.

In 2005, Congress passed the "Professional Malpractice Limitations Act," or the PMLA. The PMLA provided that in any action in federal court in the nature of lawyer, medical, engineering, or other "professional" malpractice (meaning negligence by one of these professionals) or by an organization providing professional (medical, legal, engineering) services, the applicable limitations period is one year.

In New Hampshire and Massachusetts, the applicable limitations period for actions in the nature of professional (medical, legal, engineering services) malpractice is six years.

An issue that sometimes arises in the context of limitations periods is when the limitations clock begins to "accrue" or tick. In other words, suppose that a statute of limitations for a certain kind of tort is X years. At what point in time does the X-year period begin to run? Broadly speaking, there are two general approaches to this question: the "misconduct rule" and the "discovery rule." If the "misconduct" rule applies, the applicable statute of limitations begins to run on the date that the act or omission giving rise to the claim occurs, assuming of course that some harm eventually results (or else there would be no tort). In other words, under the "misconduct rule," the limitations period begins to run on the date that the defendant commits the relevant misconduct (the act or omission); the clock begins to run when the act or omission occurs, not when the injury/harm occurs. If the "discovery rule" applies, in contrast, the limitations period begins to run instead on the date that the claimant first knows about, or first should have known about, the act or omission giving rise to the claim. In other words, under the "discovery rule," the limitations period begins to run on the date that the plaintiff first discovers, or should have discovered, that the defendant committed some kind of misconduct. This might be when some hidden injury or disease manifests itself in an unmistakable way.

To illustrate the difference between the "misconduct rule" and the "discovery rule": Suppose a patient visits the doctor on January 2, 1996. The doctor tells the patient that she is healthy. In fact, a non-negligent doctor would have diagnosed the patient with a serious illness. The patient does not find out that she in fact has been suffering all along from a serious illness until she visits a second doctor on May 4, 1999. Under the "misconduct rule," the applicable limitations period began to run on January 2, 1996. Under the "discovery rule," the applicable limitations period might not begin to run until May 4, 1999.

Federal courts have long used the discovery rule. New Hampshire and Massachusetts use the "misconduct" rule.

## Selected Provisions of the National Medical Malpractice Reform Act (the "NMMRA")

*Reminder: this is the federal statute, the one Congress passed.*

> *Section 601: **Findings:** The Congress finds that the process of detecting cancerous masses suffers from the lack of a national depository of radiological images that would allow radiologists to compare images for a particular patient to images in the depository that are known to be cancerous or non-cancerous. The lack of a depository causes thousands of mistaken or missed diagnoses of cancer each year, costing the Medicare and Medicaid programs millions of dollars and having a deleterious effect upon interstate commerce.*

> *Section 602: **Establishment of a National Registry:** The Secretary of Health and Human Services is hereby directed to establish a National Registry of radiological images. The Secretary shall establish a classification system of such images, indexing them in a manner that he shall determine, but such indexing shall include classification by area of the body imaged, whether cancer was ultimately found, the type of cancer (if any), as well as the socioeconomic and demographic characteristics of the patient (age, race, gender, height, weight, and medical history).*

> *Section 603: **Deposit of Electronic Images:** Any provider of medical services who receives any funds under the Medicare or Medicaid programs, and who produces, receives, examines, or obtains any radiological image of any patient, shall forward an electronic copy of that image to the National Registry, along with all information about the patient required to enable the Secretary to index the image pursuant to Section 602 of this statute. The information forwarded to the Secretary about each patient shall be updated in a timely manner to include the diagnosis ultimately made for each patient.*

> *Section 604: **Secretarial Power To Implement:** The Secretary shall have the power promulgate rules to implement Sections 601-603 of this statute, including rules that define any terms in these Sections.*

. . .

> *Section 901: **Bloc Grant Program:** The Congress finds that the system of adjudicating medical malpractice suits in courts has caused providers of medical services and their insurers to suffer from crushing attorneys' fees, insurance premiums, and other costs.*

<!-- page: 12 -->

> *Accordingly, the Secretary of Health and Human Services shall establish and administer a system of bloc grants, which shall be funded by yearly appropriations this Congress shall make. States who by law establish administrative agencies to adjudicate medical malpractice claims may apply for and, pursuant to the program the Secretary establishes, receive funding to defray the costs of such agencies.*

> *Section 902: **Priority in the Bloc Grant Program:** In allocating funding, the Secretary shall give priority to states whose administrative schemes include any or all of the following:*
>
> *(a) cadres of neutral medical experts who can opine on whether conduct subject to a medical malpractice claim is in fact negligent,*
>
> *(b) expedition of the adjudication of medical malpractice claims to the maximum extent possible, and*
>
> *(c) exclusive jurisdiction in the state agencies (that is, a prohibition on bringing medical malpractice lawsuits in state courts).*

> *Section 903: **Drafting of a Model Statute:** To assist states seeking to take advantage of the funding opportunity under Section 902, the Secretary of Health and Human Services shall draft and make public a Model State Medical Malpractice Reform Act (the "MODEL STATE ACT") for states to use in deliberating about schemes they may choose to effectuate.*

> *Section 904: **Prohibition on Removal:** No medical malpractice proceeding initiated in a state administrative agency funded in whole or in part by funds allocated pursuant to this statute may be removed to the federal courts.*
