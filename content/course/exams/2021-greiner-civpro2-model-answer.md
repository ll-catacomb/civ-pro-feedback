Question 1
The initial attempt to provide notice to Montoya was likely reasonable (Mullane) in that Montoya
regularly communicates, and did communicate with Buttercup and Westley ("BW"), via
youkilledmyfather.net, and because mailing to a P.O. Box Montoya listed there is logical. When Valerie
found the website down and received the certified slip unclaimed, however, she had a duty try other
reasonable means (Flowers). The easiest and highly reliable means available would be to serve Montoya
personally at the RV plot he'd rented; Valerie had taken note of that location. Insufficient notice likely
renders the seizure invalid.
The seizure scheme likely does not provide sufficient opportunity to be heard. On the positive side, a
judge (not a clerk) issues the order, the plaintiff must file a FRCP8-like statement, the statement must be
sworn true (all lowering the risk of erroneous determination), and a hearing on the seizure must occur
somewhat promptly (20 days) of the order (reducing the threat to the defendant's interests). But torts,
including this one, are fact-specific and often (as here) require live testimony (Doehr) to adjudicate well;
the plaintiff need not file a bond (these two show high risk of error); the plaintiff need not, and this
plaintiff did not, have a property interest in the item seized (lower plaintiff interest); there is no exigency
(Humperdinck did not hurry his seizure order, plaintiffs filed 3 year after the explosion) because an
ordinary IPJ suit can protect BW's interests (lower plaintiff interest); and Montoya needs the bank
account to live and work (high interest). Montoya's wanderlust might make SoP difficult, but he is
domestic and makes his whereabouts known, and there is no concern of asset waste (lower plaintiff
interest)
The assertion of quasi-in-rem jurisdiction is likely invalid because the bank account is likely not
"located" in Texas. Location options for the incorporeal bank account include:

    · Dallas: Perhaps HvB suggests that the account follows the account-holder, and the account
         holder's current location is somewhat ascertainable, but that means the location will be
         transient and uncertain, plus a bank account for Montoya is an asset not a debt.

    · Pensacola: Montoya and Florin might anticipate this location as the place where their
         relationship vis-ŕ-vis this account began, and is the likely state of Montoya's domicile (see
         below), but no third party or to a court contemplated a pre-notice seizure would know, making
         it hard to administer. In addition, account holders sometimes have little current relationship
         with the place where they opened the account, making this location seem arbitrary (although
         the same is often true of a human being's domicile).

    · Reno or Delaware: These are certain and easily ascertainable. HvB suggests one of these two (a
         bank account is a debt the bank owes the account holder). Reno is likely stronger because a
         bank's PPoB is a reasonable proxy for its system of records (all a modern bank is). Either choice
         might be fair to Montoya because he can be deemed to have had knowledge of these locations
         when he chose BoF as his bank; if he wanted a different location, he could have chosen a
         different bank.

Even if the account is in Texas, QIR might itself be unconstitutional. One interpretation of Schaeffer,
focusing on the strangeness of the DE sequestration statute there, leaves QIR undisturbed; if so, QIR
might be OK here if the account is in Texas. But taking Schaeffer at its word (despite the Scalia Burnham
opinion), we'd apply Shoe. Texas' statutes authorize the seizure, and its long-arm goes to constitutional
limits, but non-resident Montoya lacks minimal contacts connected to this litigation and Texas. None of
the (i) work on or shipping of the computer, (ii) negotiations with BW, or (iii) receipt of the checks,
occurred in Texas. Montoya's check cashing and mostly temporary physical presence in Texas is likely
not enough. His website is visible everywhere in the world, and nothing in it is Texas-specific. Nor
would the exercise of jurisdiction be F&R: Texas has little interest in this transaction (no conduct
occurred in Texas nor Texas resident is involved), Texas substantive law will not apply, no evidence is
located in Texas, and Buttercup has little interest in litigating so far from home.
Question 2
Valerie's argument will fail under Matthews. The requirement that one seeking to seize property file a
litigation bond is less threatening to their interests than is a refusal to appoint counsel when a
fundamental interest is at stake. Buttercup's interest, assuring that assets will be available if she
prevails, is monetary and of lesser magnitude than either incarceration (Turner) or parental rights
(Lassiter), for which there was no A2J remedy. Buttercup can still sue for BoWC (or products liability)
without the seizure, admittedly with more risk that Montoya will hide his assets (no indication that he
will do so, despite is wanderlust). Courts believe that the market signal the bond provides reduces the
risk of erroneous determination; they ignore the fact that inability to pay even a small amount distorts
that market. A bond protects Montoya's interests (considered under Doehr) by assuring compensation
for costs and damages if the seizure is unlawful. Texas has little separate interest.

Question 3

Rule 20 allows Montoya to sue BW together in a single lawsuit for a declaratory judgment of non-
liability (i.e. for a declaration of the $ he must pay to BW, which he contends is $0 because the computer
wiring was not defective) because there is only one transaction (the computer fire) and a common issue
(computer (non)defectiveness).

There might not be SMJ in a suit that includes Westley, a Michigan domiciliary. There is no federal issue,
embedded or COA, so no AUJ. Re DJ, if Montoya's domicile is Michigan, there is no complete diversity
(Strawbridge). Perhaps a one-year RV plot rental, registering to vote, and obtaining a PO box constitutes
living plus an intention to stay indefinitely (Mas), in which case Montoya's domicile is now Florida. But
the declaratory judgment against Westley is AIC-deficient (at most $55,000), so 1332 is unavailable. And
while the claim against Westley is from the same case or controversy (see R20 discussion) as that against
Buttercup (OH domicile, $500,000 AIC, so diversity, and anchoring under 1367(a)), that claim is by a
plaintiff (Montoya) against a person (Westley) made a party under Rule 20 where the claim is
inconsistent with 1332 (AIC deficient), so 1367(b) prohibits SSMJ.

Regarding venue, 1391(b)(1) is unavailable because BW are domiciled in different states (see above),
1391(c)(1). But under 1391(b)(2), substantial events likely occurred in N.D. Ohio (Buttercup's
negotiations), perhaps the E.D. MI (W picked up computer there, not clear if that's enough), and
definitely D. VT (accident), making 1391(b)(3) irrelevant.

Regarding IPJ, under Rule 4(k)(1)(A), we'd ask if any of Ohio, Michigan, and VT state courts could
exercise over IPJ over both BW. For any specific IPJ, all states have long-arms to constitutional limits.

    · Ohio: Ohio has general IPJ over Buttercup. Regarding minimal contacts relating Westley, the
         litigation, and Ohio, Westley made Buttercup, in Ohio (as she always told Montoya), his agent
         for the computer purchase negotiations; that said, the negotiations were online. Ohio might
         fairly and reasonably exercise IPJ over Westley, given the parties' expectations that Ohio law
         might be involved (from the negotiations), the convenience for Westley (he's in Ohio all the
         time), and his relationship with Buttercup. Some evidence, definitely the computer (key for
         deciding who is at fault) and perhaps any notes Buttercup took during the negotiations, are in
         Ohio. Montoya has no special interest in litigating in Ohio. Ohio has an interest in protecting
         Buttercup, but none vis-ŕ-vis Westley.
    · Michigan: Michigan has general IPJ over Westley. Regarding Buttercup and minimal contacts,
         she instructed Montoya to ship the computer to Ann Arbor and instructed her "agent" (Westley)
         to pick it up there. Michigan might fairly and reasonably exercise IPJ over Buttercup given her
         relationship with Westley and the resulting lack of inconvenience, but Michigan has no interest
         vis-ŕ-vis Buttercup, no Michigan law will apply, no evidence relevant to the case is in Michigan.
         Montoya may be familiar with Michigan, but has no interest in litigating there.

    · Vermont: BW drove the computer to the state and used it there, where the accident occurred,
         likely constituting minimal contacts. Vermont could fairly and reasonably exercise IPJ over BW
         because it has an interest in regulating dangerous products brought inside its borders, its law
         may apply, it's somewhat convenient for BW (they vacation there), and key evidence (wiring) is
         there.

    · Likely, no other state has minimal contacts. Regarding Oklahoma, in particular, nothing
         whatsoever connects Westley to OK.

Regarding HCOL, E.D. Mich. and D. Vt. would use R1 (Klaxon) and thus apply Vermont (place of injury)
law to the products liability tort, making punitive damages available. The N.D. Ohio is the best forum for
Montoya because it would apply R2, raising at least a chance that the court would apply Ohio's
prohibition on punitive damages. The parties might have expected Ohio law to apply because the
centers of their negotiation relationship, to the extent it was anywhere (negotiations were online), were
Oklahoma/Ohio. Oklahoma was just where Montoya happened to be at the time, while Ohio was where
Buttercup lived. Per above, some evidence might be in Ohio (especially the computer), so the needs of
the interstate judicial system would be served by Ohio law, and Ohio has some interest in the case
(although applying its law disadvantages Buttercup). These factors might be enough to overcome the R2
presumption that the law of Vermont (injury location) law would apply.

A disadvantage to Montoya is that the N.D. Ohio might apply Ohio's 10% deposit requirement to the
federal diversity declaratory judgment statute, raising a VCOL issue. A Montoya inability/refusal to
make the deposit would kill the lawsuit, while 28 USC 2201 has no such requirement, so this is outcome
determinative under York, use state law. Harlan might analogize the 10% deposit law to the bond
requirement in Cohen (discussed in his Hanna concurrence), particularly given the Ohio legislature's
findings regarding frivolous lawsuits and risky pre-litigation (primary) conduct, and thus apply state law.
Scalia would say that 2201 is valid, citing Congress' constitutional power to provide remedies available in
the lower federal courts it creates (Art. III), and applicable because it speaks to what federal district
courts may do vis-ŕ-vis declaratory judgments; Scalia would not require the 10%. Ginsburg would agree
with Scalia on validity; on applicability, she might say that the Ohio law speaks to an issue (a deposit
necessity) about which 2201 is silent, allowing the apparently important state law policy to flourish, but
might also be unwilling to interpret a federal statute (as opposed to a federal rule) that narrowly.

they will lose both with the contemplated federal case went to judgment. Because that might raise the

A jury trial would be available because the BW counterclaim would seek damages in a traditional BoWC
law claim (Beacon). (If necessary: issues, such whether hotel or computer wiring caused fire, are
obviously factual on law/fact divide.)

Question 5
The Texas state court will apply its own 3-year limitations period because limitations are procedural for
HCOL purposes; it will dismiss because the lawsuit was filed 38 (>36) months after Buttercup's injury.
Under Texas law, limitations dismissals are on the merits for CP. Because any subsequent lawsuit by
Buttercup would be by the same party (her) over the same transaction (the computer fire), dismissed on
the merits (see above) in a final judgment, Texas would apply CP to dismiss a subsequent lawsuit under
tort or contract or both theories. Under section 1738, that means any U.S. court (state or federal) must
similarly preclude any suit such suit by Buttercup.
Issue preclusion would not run in a lawsuit in another state because the only issue actually litigated was
compliance with Texas limitations law, which is different from any other state's limitations law.
Neither claim nor issue preclusion would apply to any suit by Westley. He was not a party to Texas
litigation, he is not in privity with B because he exercised no control there nor is he B's successor in
interest.

Question 6
SCOTUS treats a class as a new entity in diversity-only class actions, deciding diversity only by reference
to the named plaintiff (Supreme Tribe). SCOTUS treats a class as massive joinder in Exxon's
supplemental jurisdiction analysis (it considered a simple joinder case and class case simultaneously
under the same reasoning). We do not know which SCOTUS will to IPJ; a new entity theory would mean
that BMS might not prevent exercise of jurisdiction over a defendant with respect to unnamed out-of-
state class members' claims, while a massive joinder theory would suggest that BMS might disallow IPJ
exercise. I prefer the new entity theory. Sometimes differences in scale become a difference in kind.
The massive joinder theory appeals to a formalistic ideal that unnamed class members could still litigate
their claims (Scalia in Shade Grove). Like many formalisms, this ideal has relevance only in a world ruled
by fantasy. Unnamed class members have little control, and frequently little practical interest in, their
claims. They trust plaintiffs' attorneys with those claims. So let's treat the class's claims, which must
meet newly-stringent commonality (Walmart) requirements, thus binding them together, as a single
new entity, akin to a trust. Doing so minimizes litigation shenanigans, such as multi-national
corporations pretending as though they care about venue, or about IPJ and separate state sovereignty
when they are in fact forum shopping (supposedly a type of evildoing in which only plaintiffs' attorneys
engage).
