Question 1(a)

There is clearly DJ (PP = NH, DD = MA, $2M AIC), IPJ over DD and venue OK (4k1A, MA = DD’s home state).  The prompt says that the complaint had great specificity, so no Bell Atlantic or Iqbal issue.  So the only possible basis for a dismissal is Ma. Rule 26(a)(1)(F), with which PP did not comply and which, if applicable, would require dismissal of the case (while FRCP 26 would not require dismissal).  HCOL:  because the scope of what is procedural is broad, very likely MA would apply its own rule, thus so would federal court, Klaxon.  VCOL:

Applying the four tests we learned in class:

York: the difference in law is outcome determinative of this lawsuit, so we would apply state law and dismiss.

Scalia/Hanna:  FRCP 26(a)(2)(D)(i) can be viewed as regulating the manner and means of administering rights and remedies, and is thus is valid under the REA.  It is also probably applicable in that it specifies when an expert report must be disclosed to the other side or filed, and nothing is required until 90 days before trial.  This is just a time limit.  Adopting a narrow interpretation of FRCP 26(a)(2)(D)(i) to avoid a conflict with Ma. Rule 26(a)(1)(F) would essentially recreate Justice Ginsburg’s test.

Harlan: the problem with Ma. Rule 26(a)(1)(F), as detailed in Subparts 1(b) and 1(c), is that it might further substantive or procedural purposes.  To the extent that one views Rule 26(a)(1)(F) as a timing requirement, or a timing requirement designed to facilitate settlement, a difference in law would not affect pre-litigation primary conduct, and we would use federal law.  But if Rule 26(a)(1)(F) lessens defensive medicine, then it unquestionably affects pre-litigation conduct, and we would use state law.  The relevant rationales are contained in the Question 1(b) commentary (supporting the use-federal-law interpretation, and refuse to dismiss) and in the Question 1(c) Ma. Court ruling (supporting the use-state-law interpretation, and dismiss).   That Harlan’s “test” might get at the heart of the substance/procedure divide does not make it easy to apply in all cases.

Ginsburg/SG:   we should look for a way to avoid a conflict.  Here, for once, that seems easy.  FRCP(a)(2)(D)(i) requires disclosure of the expert report “at least 90 days before the date set for trial” and does not speak explicitly to whether the report must be filed with the court  Ma. Rule 26(a)(1)(F) says that the report must be filed with the complaint.  If a report is filed with the complaint (and thereby disclosed to the other side), then under a literal reading of FRCP(a)(2)(D)(i), then it is disclosed “at least” 90 days before trial.  There is no conflict, so the state law can apply, and we dismiss. Note that this result allows effectuation of either the articulated (settlement or defensive medicine) policies, although the latter seems decidedly more powerful.  And without more on the reasons behind FRCP 26(a)(2)(D) (right now it seems like a time limit), it’s hard to see how there’s a lot of interference with a federal policy.

My most analogous case is Cohen.  There, a state rule of civil procedure imposed a requirement upon a plaintiff to do something at the time of filing (bond).  The FRCP had no such requirement.  One could interpret the “silence” in the FRCP as a statement that the state law requirement (bond) was not a requirement in federal court (and thus a clear state/federal law conflict is created), or as a statement of no particular preference on the issue (in which case there is no conflict and state law can apply).  Cohen chose the latter interpretation and applied state law.  Like the timing requirement here, the bonding requirement in Cohen could serve substantive or procedural purposes, as Justice Harlan’s Hanna concurrence discussed.

[Note:  No single right answer here.  I am interested more in students’ justifications than in the case they pick.  From Sergio Campos:  Couldn’t Hanna be analogous too? Massachusetts state law (like in this example) said that personal service “in hand” was required for executors when Federal Rules only required leaving it at defendant’s home with someone of suitable age? In Hanna, as in your exam question, state law adds an extra requirement not present in the federal law, and the Rule can be read as indifferent to “in hand” service – its sufficient, but not necessary. I also think that Hanna can serve substantive ends – to help with the winding down of an estate.

Shady Grove also a contender, for reasons similar to those above.]

Question 1(b)

Per above, the commentary supports an interpretation that the rule is a timing requirement and nothing more, and therefore it is procedural.  This matters not a bit to York (outcome conflict still the same) or to Scalia/Hanna (Scalia doesn’t want to look at the state law at all to determine a federal law’s validity or applicability).  But, per the answer to Question 1(a), the commentary suggests that Harlan would say that the Ma. Rule is procedural (so use federal law) because its purpose appears to be directed toward litigation (settlement) not primary (medial practice) conduct.  And the commentary should dampen Justice Ginsburg’s enthusiasm for an accommodation, because now the state law policies the rule furthers don’t seem as powerful.

Question 1(c)

Now we get the polar opposite from 1(b).  Again, no different to York test or Scalia/Hanna because outcome conflict is still there and Scalia will not look at state law purposes.  But now, we have a definitive (Ma. high court) statement of 26(a)(1)(F)’s purpose, which trumps the commentary (Erie:  a state law means what the state highest court says it means, end of story).  Per the answer to Question 1(a), Harlan in Hanna points to state law because its purpose is to affect pre-litigation conduct (medical practice), and Ginsburg should be rabid for an accommodation that would allow the state law to govern because of the importance of the relevant state policy.

Question 1(d)

Justice Stevens, per Shady Grove, would follow Justice Ginsburg but be less willing to bend FRCP 26(a)(2)(D)(i).  He might say that a conflict is unavoidable.  But then he might be willing to say that the application of FRCP 26(a)(2)(D)(i) in this case “abridges, enlarges, or modifies” DD’s substantive right under MA law to have the medical profession essentially “screen” PP’s complaint, in which case he would hold FRCP 26(a)(2)(D)(i) invalid as applied to this case.

Question 1(e)

[Lots of ways to go here]

The back-and-forth interpretations from 1(b) and 1(c) show the risks of Justice Ginsburg’s and Harlan’s approaches.  Neither gives us much certainty of result.  It’s even possible that a federal court might first hold characterize 26(a)(1)(F) as procedural (and thus refuse to apply it); then the MA high court intervenes with a substantive-sounding interpretation of the Rule (as per Question 1(c)); and then the federal court would have to hold that the MA rule is substantive, and apply it.  Such uncertainty sows confusion, and among other things, creates traps for pro se litigants.

But certainty is not everything.  By choosing an Erie test that depends on state law, once must be willing to live with uncertainty inherent in state law (where research is harder to conduct, and variation is unavoidable).   If we extol states for other purposes, we should be willing to live with their quirks, and one quirk is changing law.  After all, federal law changes, too.

Subpart 1(f)(1) and (2)

We have same party/plaintiff (PP), the same claim (same transaction = set of events), and a judgment in the MA federal lawsuit that is final.  Under Semtek (rendering court federal/diversity, subsequent court state), we need to know whether MA law would consider a dismissal pursuant to Rule 26(a)(1)(F) to be “on the merits.”  (Semtek clarifies that Fed. R. Civ. P. 41 does not govern this issue.)  Is this dismissal more like a dismissal for failure to prosecute (usually considered on the merits) or a lack of IPJ dismissal (not on the merits)?  We need MA law to tell us.  This is just like the limitations-based dismissal in Semtek, and SCOTUS remanded for an inquiry into state law.

In my opinion, the answer to this question should depend on the policies the law was designed to further.  This is the whole induce-settlement versus prevent-defensive-medicine debate all over again.  Once the complaint is filed, the parties can start negotiating, and without some expert analysis, a plaintiff’s medical malpractice claim will not go far, anyway.  So if settlement is the issue, we don’t need the potentially harsh incentive of claim preclusion to further the relevant policy.  Timing issues can be taken care of via the limitations period.  But we might want the serious incentive that claim preclusion provides if something as important as reducing medical care costs is at issue.

Subpart 1(g)

Whether PP attaches a report to her NH lawsuit means nothing under MA preclusion law, which we still don’t know, and need to.  So there is no change to the answer to 1(f)(1).

On the “should” question, everything depends on how we interpret PP’s failure to produce the expert report previously and her ability to do so now  On the one hand, the application of claim preclusion seems unduly harsh and silly; PP has her expert report.  What policy is further by precluding her?  The idea of using the medical profession as a screen against frivolous lawsuits is sufficiently served by having a case without an expert report dismissed without prejudice.  If the plaintiff is able to come up with such a report, then the medical profession has “screened in” this complaint.  Again, issues of timing can be left to limitations law.  On the other hand, what gives here?  It seems like PP could have gotten an expert report before, she just didn’t.  Do we want to “punish” this failure?  And there might be a fairness problem to DD, who must defend another lawsuit.  Further, maybe we want to use PP’s suit as an example to make plaintiffs (more accurately, their attorneys) think before they file, to forum shop well.

Question 2

(a)  We file for a declaratory judgment action under federal, NY, or NJ declaratory judgment statutes asking for a declaration that our LB’s conduct was not negligent.

Possible courts:  NY state and federal, NJ state and federal.

Could we sue in any federal court?

SMJ:  DJ:  LB = DE and NY (PPoB = nerve center, Hertz), PC = NH (home address), AIC = $80K only if NY law applies; if NJ law applies, the punitive damages prohibition reduces AIC to $20K < $75K.  What law applies?  If we sue in NY federal court, we get NY HCOL rules, 2d Restatement stuff, and a tough call.  The harm was felt in NY.  The allegedly harmful conduct occurred in Brooklyn, NY, and PC was in NY when she got her credit card.  NY would have an interest in regulating the conduct of banks with PPoB there.  But the application of NY law hurts the NY party.  And NJ has an interest in regulating the conduct of a bank with a major office within it.  But NJ law hurts the NJ domiciliary.  Hard to say.  If we sue in NJ federal court, we get NJ HCOL rules, first restatement, look to where the harm occurs, which is almost certainly NY, so no damages limit.

Arising under:  No federal cause of action, but Smith-Grable?  The negligence per se theory depends on federal law and is embedded in the cause of action.  The CFPB regulation seems pretty important, given the financial crisis.  The historical facts do not appear to be in dispute, and the whole idea of negligence per se is to remove factual disputes.  But think about the implications of saying that any time a defendant violates any federal regulation or statute, it commits negligence per so allowing the plaintiff to sue in federal court.  Way, way too many cases would be swept into federal district court.  (If students cite Merrell Dow, great, but it was not assigned.)  FQJ unlikely.

IPJ in federal court:  No problem.  4(k)(1)(A), use state law, both long-arms go to the limits of the constitution.  If we sue PC in NJ federal court, NJ is PC’s home state, done.  If we sue PC in NY federal court, International Shoe, there are massive NY contacts (she got the credit card while there, the allegedly negligent conduct occurred there, her bank account was affected there).

Venue in federal court:  See IPJ analysis.  If we sue PC in NJ fed ct, venue lies because she, the only defendant, resides there.  If we sue PC in NY fed ct, venue lies on substantial events.

State courts?  IPJ is no problem, see federal court discussion.

We should definitely sue in federal court.  We think federal courts are pro-bank, great.  But if we get the case dismissed on us for lack of SMJ, good things happen, see Question 2(b).  We’d love to get the NJ law applied, and the only hope we have of doing that is to sue in NY, with its looser HCOL rules; see above re HCOL.  So best place for us is NY federal court.

Question 2(b)

We would LOVE to have the federal court dismiss our own federal court declaratory judgment lawsuit for lack of SMJ.  To dismiss for lack of SMJ, the court would have to hold that there is no DJ, and the only way to do that is an AIC deficiency.  That means that the court had to hold that, as a matter of HCOL, NJ substantive law applies, which gives us the punitive damages disallowance.  Great!

Now think about a subsequent lawsuit in which PC claims punitive damages.  The HCOL issue, which is at least as fact-bound as the holder in due course in Cromwell (and thus subject to issue preclusion), had to have been actually litigated, necessary (indeed, the basis for) the decision, and is the same issue.  If applicable law requires mutuality, we have it.  Issue preclusion would prevent PC from suing for punitive damages.

Question 3

Lot of different ways to go, but to get full credit, the student should state the basic theory that at the MTD, MSJ, and JAMOL are all about weeding out non-meritorious lawsuits.  If a high bar is placed early, one might justify lowering a bar later in litigation.  That said, only in the Rule 56 context (not in the Rule 50 context, unless we’re talking directed verdicts) is there a serious hope of preventing the expenditure of party and social resources associated with a trial.  In the JNOV context, the expenditure has already occurred.
