Question 1

Because the lawsuit would end under state law (filed in year 2.5, Massachusetts SoL is two years) but
continued under federal law (3-year SoL), the difference is outcome determinative, so use state law and
dismiss. Under Harlan in Hanna, limitations period differences drive the investment strategies of large
(especially insurance) companies, so use state law. Under Hanna majority, Scalia version, Rule 87 is valid
because it really regulates procedure (Sibbach) in that limitations can be reasonably characterized as
time limits only (as they are in HCOL). Rule 87 is applicable because by its plain language ("any lawsuit
filed in a federal district court") it provides an SoL, so use federal law, and deny the motion. Ginsburg
would find Rule 87 valid for the same reason, but might interpret Rule 87 as silent on whether a lawsuit
must be filed in a time period shorter than three years, so long as it could be not be filed after three
years. She might thus say that state law can apply, and dismiss.

Stevens would take seriously the validity inquiry and ask whether the seemingly procedural
Massachusetts limitations period was part of the state's scheme of rights, as its placement in the MGL in
the same section that defines the rights, and his use of York/SoL as an example of such laws in Shady
Grove, might suggest. If so, Stevens might hold that Rule 87 is invalid under the REA as applied here. He
might also hold, given his presumption that "there must be little doubt" that a state rule is bound up in a
state's substantive scheme, that SoLs are simply time limits, and thus do not implicated 2072(b).
Despite his preference for Ginsburgian sensitivity to state interests in interpretation, he is less willing to
twist plain language and would likely agree with Scalia that the Rule is applicable.

Erie is best understood as a rule of prudence informed by federalism, separation of powers and
questions about the nature of law. Separation of powers, together with congressional power to create
the lower federal courts (augmented as necessary by the necessary and proper clause), provides
Congress latitude to prescribe mechanisms for the creation of doctrines rationally characterizable as
procedural for use therein. Congress did so in the REA, followed to create Rule 87, a limitations period,
something that in many contexts (e.g., HCOL) is procedural. The goal of horizontal uniformity across the
lower federal courts (e.g., national market for lawyers) suggest, prudentially, honoring the congressional
choice. The difference in SoLs might induce forum shopping, but likely not much more so than, say,
class action availability (Shady Grove), or SoP methods (Hanna). As Harlan points out, Erie was about
more than forum shopping and inequitable administration of the laws; Congress has a say in how
federalism operates. This is not a situation in which the federal courts have purported to "find"
something called the general law; the court system acknowledged that it made the law, doing so
through a congressionally created process open to public comment.

Question 2

Class actions can promote access to justice ("A2J") by assuring that members of the class can borrow
and pool resources to achieve at least some legal representation (Rule 23(g)), although not an attorney
who will become familiar with the specific aspects of each member's facts. Thus, there will be counsel
even though Lassiter/Turner would not so provide. Class actions reduce class members' effort costs
(Shutts, "sitting back"). Class actions are useful in promoting A2J when the unnamed plaintiffs have low-
value claims (per our Fells Wargo class hypo), allowing litigation of those claims when it would otherwise
make no economic sense to do so. Class actions are ordinarily of little value for parties who are in a
defensive posture, or for parties whose claims are fact-specific (such as tenants in summary eviction
cases) and thus cannot meet Walmart's stringent commonality requirements. Put another way, class
actions provide at best mass, not individualized, justice. The "loss" of individualized justice, a fictional
concept in modern society, is not much of a loss; the Tooth Fair is not real. All that said, class actions
can promote A2J only if courts are willing to certify them and push their administration; Shutts and
Walmart give little reason for hope here.

Question 3

There is no federal issue at all, so no form of 1331 SMJ. 1332 provides SMJ on Groucho's (Texas) Zeppo
(Delaware, $250,000 AIC) claim. The Sylvania (Delaware, Oklahoma, $50,000 AIC) claim features
diversity, but it is AIC deficient. 1367(b) prohibits SSMJ for this claim by a plaintiff (Groucho) against a
person made a party (Sylvania) under Rule 20 (OK because the two claims concern the "Duck" theft and
have a common issue of the work of art's value) (Situation 3). Exxon dictum. Only the Sylvania claim
must be remanded.

Assuming denial of the remand motion, the defendants might move to dismiss for lack of IPJ. Neither is
from TX, and neither does > 50% of its "business" in TX. Given 4(k)(1)(A) and the Texas long-arm to
constitutional limits, we look for minimal contacts. Zeppo traveled to Dallas where he first encountered
"Duck," a key event. For Sylvania, it does not solicit business in TX, and it has few TX customers other
than Groucho. But it used a TX-based server to store its part of its customers' data, including "Duck,"
and it accepted TX-based customers like Groucho (McGee), which is likely sufficient. True, Groucho
opened the Sylvania account elsewhere and then returned to his TX home, Hanson, but the server is an
additional, sizeable contact. Regarding fair & reasonable (for both), TX has an interest in regulating
allegedly wrongful conduct connected to its borders; TX is convenient for Groucho; TX is not
inconvenient for Sylvania (server) or Zeppo (got there once); evidence (Chico's testimony) is in TX; TX
law could apply in this R2 state (Klaxon) as overwhelmingly the center of gravity of the parties'
relationship (despite the R1 presumption in favor of OK law, see below).

Question 4

1332 now provides SMJ for both claims because the Sylvania claim now has sufficient AIC. See above re
IPJ. Venue is improper. 1391(b)(1) is unavailable because Zeppo is domiciled in DE, not W.D. Tex
(1391(c)). 1391(b)(2) is unavailable because the Chico meeting occurred in the N.D. (not W.D.) Tex. No
event (aside from Groucho's pre-lawsuit-facts viewings) occurred in W.D. Tex. As discussed below,
"Duck" itself is not best located with Grouch in W.D.Tex. The case must be transferred or dismissed.

Question 5

D. Del. can exercise GIPJ over both defendants, as both are citizens/domiciliaries there. For the same
reason venue would be proper there under 1391(b)(1). Per Question 3, SIPJ is likely available in a TX
federal court, and 1391(b)(2) venue is proper in the N.D. Tex. because the Chico meeting is a key event.
Under Hoffman, no other district is available. IPJ over Sylvania is unavailable in a Cal. court and a S.D.
court, and over Zeppo in D. Massachusetts and W.D. Ok. and D.S.C., because there are no minimal
contacts relating those defendants to those districts, respectively.

Channeling the Gulf Oil factors, of the two, N.D. Tex. is more likely because a key event (the Chico-house
meeting) and thus key evidence (Chico's testimony, Chico's computer if required) is there, and a Texas
court (R2) might well apply Texas law, even though D. Del. has the advantage of eliminating any
questions about IPJ and venue. Texas may not be convenient for Zeppo, but Delaware is equally
inconvenient for Groucho.

Question 6

There is no appellate jurisdiction. The transfer is not a final order under 1291, there was no judgment
under 54(b), there were none of the 1292(b) findings/recitations, the transfer order is effectively
reviewable later so no collateral order (Lauro Lines), and the order does not involve an injunction. A
mandamus petition might get the circuit court's attention (this is the fact pattern of Hoffman), but there
is no urgency/exigency here. If the circuit court did review the propriety of the transfer, it would review
issues regarding interpretation of the venue statute de novo; it would review any findings of fact (e.g.,
where Zeppo is domiciled) for clear error; and in the unlikely event that it found venue in W.D. Tex.
proper, it would review the transfer order for abuse of discretion because that decision involves the
application of the law to facts.

Question 7

Sylvania bears the trial burden of persuasion on the affirmative defense of either contributory or
comparative negligence. It discharged its burden by producing evidence that, if uncontroverted, would
compel a rational trier of fact (despite all reasonable inferences in Groucho's favor) to conclude that it
met its persuasion burden that Groucho was negligent in failing to enable 2FA (engineer affidavit) and,
independently, his failure to keep his password secure (obvious password and waived it around) (Chico
depo) (Celotex, Brennan). Groucho did not meet his now-shifted production burden by testifying that
he could not remember 2FA or his treatment of his password. The absence of evidence is not
contradictory evidence. Judge Marcal was likely to grant Sylvania's motion, finding that Groucho was
negligent on either or both the 2FA or password theories.

Whether Groucho's negligence constitutes a complete defense depends on HCOL. Under Ferrens,
because venue in the W.D. Tex. was unquestionably improper, D. Del. should employ Delaware's R!, so
we look for the last event. Per the prompt, the negligence tort was complete at the location of "Duck"
when it was stolen. Where was that? The artwork is incorporeal, and it does not consist solely of its
code; the code is worthless without an interface to play it, and as this case demonstrates, code can exist
in an large number of places. To locate incorporeal things, we consider policy arguments.

    À Pierre: this is where the account-holder opened the Sylvania account (and here, the place
         where the account-holder relinquished total control of the item), but as this fact pattern
         demonstrates, this location could be gratuitous, depending on the vagaries of travel plans, and
         thus is both indefinite and accidental.

    À San Antonio: This is where the account holder is domiciled, which is definite and ascertainable,
         and might comply with the holder's expectations. But it might not comply with company
         (Sylvania) expectations, and it would mean that property provided to Sylvania is located all over
         the world, which SCOTUS might find objectionable.

    À Delaware or Oklahoma are ascertainable and definite, and they have connections to the events.
         Either is reasonable in a party-expectation sense because Sylvania is strongly connected to both,
         and a customer can be charged with knowledge of the location of companies with whom they
         choose to deal. Of the two, OK is more connected to events because decisions relevant to the
         security policy of digital items were likely made there, and thus key evidence would be there.
    À Dallas, Columbia, or Boston: the data constituting "Duck" were physically in all three, so part of
         what constituted "Duck" was there when stolen. But the fact that there are three locations,
         each with an equal claim to reasonableness, demonstrates that each is an unwise choice.

If the artwork was in MA, SC, DE, or TX, then the SJ ruling that Groucho was negligent would reduce but
not eliminate Groucho's recovery. If it was in OK or SD, recovery would be barred. OK, and thus the
complete bar, seems most likely.

Question 8

The Rule 54(b) certification was likely improper. Litigation against Zeppo will continue, and the facts of
the Zeppo lawsuit are intertwined with those of the Sylvania claim. Groucho's theory is that Zeppo was
able to steal "Duck" only because Sylvania was negligent. It would be more efficient for the appellate
court to treat Groucho's lawsuit as a single unit.

Because venue was unquestionably improper in W.D. Tex., the transfer presents no special preclusion
problems (i.e., no possible Happy Court rule application to preclusion). Under Semtex, the OK state
court must provide as least as much preclusion as a DE state court would. Because the OK litigation
concerned events that occurred well after the "Duck" facts (likely after the first lawsuit was filed),
concerning a different artist's work viewed in a different location and stolen by a different alleged
assailant, it relies on different evidence. In addition, each artwork (like each bond) should be considered
unique to preserve market structures. This a different "claim." There is no claim preclusion despite the
same plaintiff (Grouch) and a final judgment (the appeal does not matter) on the merits (summary
judgment).

There are common issues in the DE and OK litigation, namely, whether Groucho's failure to enable 2FA
was negligent (contributory or comparative). The password change and the cessation of the use of a
paper record meant that negligence on this basis was not present in the OK litigation. DE follows FCL for
preclusion. We have actually litigated (summary judgment grant) and full mutuality. But under Russell,
we do not have necessary to the decision because Judge Marcal did not specify which of the two
independent grounds (password carelessness, 2FA) supported her decision. There will be no issue
preclusion unless the OK state court, following Hart, chooses to apply more preclusion than the
rendering court would.
