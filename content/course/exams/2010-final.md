## Question 1 (25 total points)

Assume that the law of all 50 states (there being no federal law of slander) defines the tort of "slander" as an oral utterance of a false statement, heard by a third party, that is harmful to the reputation of the plaintiff. It is enough to commit the tort of slander that the statement is heard; the third party who hears the statement need have no connection to either the plaintiff (who is the subject of the statement) or the defendant (who uttered it). The tort is complete when any listener hears the false statement, and a plaintiff is entitled to at least nominal damages even if the listener does not know the plaintiff. A false statement that a person committed a non-trivial crime is generally grounds for slander. Libel is the same thing as slander except that the statement is printed, not uttered orally. A newspaper reporter may be found liable for libel if she writes a story (that is subsequently printed) that is false, and does so with reckless disregard for the truth. Thus, a critical issue in a libel case against a reporter is how much the reporter knew about the truthfulness of a statement the reporter printed, including whether the reporter had any reason to doubt that truthfulness before printing.

Assume that Maryland and Massachusetts both have long-arm statutes that allow their state courts to exercise personal jurisdiction in any case in which the exercise of such jurisdiction would be constitutional. Both follow the First Restatement regarding choice of law.

In January of 2010, Dean Thomas, a student at Hogwarts University (located in New Hampshire) was riding on an Amtrak train from Boston to Washington, DC. Dean was annoyed. Dean had been in love with Mary Catherine Blanton, a stunningly intelligent and accomplished young woman who spoke five languages fluently, who was a starting midfielder for the U.S. Women's National Soccer team at the age of 14, and who wrote movie reviews linking art films and great books in her spare time. Dean and Mary Catherine had dated for several years (during which time Mary Catherine published several solely-authored papers in the Journal of the American Statistical Association while modeling for Vogue) until Harry Potter stole her away from him (Dean). Both Harry and Mary Catherine were also students at Hogwarts University. Harry was born in Boston and had lived his whole life there before attending Hogwarts, although Harry was reasonably well-known around the Boston area (but not much beyond it) for his community activism campaigns against the evil and corrupt regime of long-time Boston Mayor Voldemort. Mary Catherine was originally born in Texas, but she moved to New Hampshire with her family at age six (just after she recorded her first album as a jazz pianist) and had lived there until she enrolled in Hogwarts. Dean, meanwhile, had lived his whole life in Connecticut before going off to school.

On that fateful train ride, Dean happened to be sitting next to Rita Skeeter, a reporter for the Boston Daily Post. Rita was born, grew up, and lived in Maine; she commuted to Boston for her reporting job. The Boston Daily Post is a limited-circulated weekly newspaper; it has no website or mail-order business. Its distribution is limited to the Boston area.

Back to the train ride: Rita thought Dean looked shady and said nothing to him during the whole trip. Dean got madder and madder about his loss of Mary Catherine as he rode the train. He imbibed a large quantity of firewhiskey from his hip flask. His anger reached its peak as the train stopped in Maryland and took on a large touring party of Maryland residents, who got on the train to go to DC for the day. As Dean's anger boiled over, he stood up on the top of his train seat and shouted, "Harry Potter is a criminal who supports Mayor Voldemort. Harry Potter has murdered two people. I have proof."

Rita, thinking she had the story of her life, immediately returned to Boston and began composing an article about Harry and murder for the Boston Daily Post. After a few days, however, she thought she should have at least one conversation with Dean before publishing. At first, she could not figure out how to get in touch with him. About five months later, though, she happened to see him on a Boston street corner, and invited him to lunch. At the lunch, Dean told Rita that he couldn't remember anything about the train ride from Boston to DC. But, he said to Rita, "I do know, and have proof, that Harry stole thousands and thousands of gold galleons from the Hogwarts University vault." After the meeting, Rita abandoned her story about Harry being a murderer, and instead wrote a story stating that Harry stole valuable items from Hogwarts. The story ran in the Boston Daily Post in September of 2010.

Harry, knowing that he could prove that he never murdered anyone or stole anything from Hogwarts, sued Dean and Rita in the United States District Court for the District of Maryland. Harry's complaint had three counts. Count 1, which was against Dean only, was for slander for

## Question 2 (45 total points)

<!-- page: 5 -->

Assume the following: Texas follows the First Restatement with respect to choice of law while Alabama and Delaware follow the Second Restatement approach. The Supreme Court of Alabama held, in *Johnson v. Bell*, "Our legal system does not recognize the doctrine of forum non conveniens in any form." In *Wood v. Spinnet*, the Supreme Court of Texas held, "Texas recognizes the doctrine of forum non conveniens. In our state, we consider the same factors as are applicable under federal law regarding forum non conveniens; however, we are much more reluctant to grant a forum non conveniens dismissal than are the federal courts. Only if the facts are such that a trial in Texas would be oppressive against the defendant should a forum non dismissal be granted." Texas and Delaware both have two-year statutes of limitations for actions in products liability and breach of a warranty clause in a contract. Alabama and Mexico have a three-year limitations period for both kinds of actions. Alabama, Texas, and Delaware rules of civil procedure regarding discovery and class actions are the same as the federal rules of civil procedure. Under Texas law, statutes of limitations dismissals are considered on the merits for claim preclusion purposes. Under Alabama law, statutes of limitations dismissals are not considered on the merits. Both Texas and Alabama use a transaction or occurrence test to decide whether two lawsuits stem from the same "claim" for claim preclusion purposes.

CRASH!, Inc. is a Delaware corporation with its sole manufacturing facility in San Antonio, Texas and its headquarters in Birmingham, Alabama. CRASH!, Inc. makes bicycles, particularly models that appeal to riders who enjoy the more extreme version of the sport. Unlike many bicycle makers, it manufactures most of its components itself in its San Antonio facility. CRASH!, Inc.'s latest model is the Deathtrap IV, a (supposedly) sturdy model designed for off-road, rough-terrain riding. The company sells its products in a storefront in San Antonio located adjacent to its manufacturing plant; part of the attraction of the store is that would-be purchasers can view the plant as they shop.

In June of 2007, Piers Polkiss, who was born and raised in the Oaxaca region of Mexico and who was at that time a student at Universidad Autónoma Benito Juárez de Oaxaca (in Mexico), came to visit his mother and father, who had recently moved to San Antonio. An avid biker, Piers stopped by the CRASH!, Inc. store to check out the scene. After touring the manufacturing plant, Piers had an extended discussion with the CRASH!, Inc. salesperson to the effect that Piers wanted a bike that could handle the famous Paseo de la Muerte trail in the Oaxaca region, one of the most rugged and difficult bike trails in the world. The salesperson responded by showing Piers the warranty language in CRASH!, Inc.'s bicycle sales contract stating, "CRASH!, Inc. warrants that for a period of two years after a sale, its Deathtrap models are fit for biking over any reasonable terrain." Piers, entranced by the sleek paintjob of the Deathtrap IV, took a store model for a test ride along a San Antonio bike trail, then made the purchase.

One month later, in July of 2007, Piers took his new bike on the most rugged portion of the Paseo de la Muerte trail, in Mexico. There, a tire blew out during his ride, causing him injury. He stayed for a week in a hospital in Oaxaca before making his recovery.

<!-- page: 6 -->

In September of 2009, Piers sued CRASH!, Inc. in Texas state court; the sole count of the complaint was a products liability tort claim. Specifically, Piers alleged that the tire blow-out was a result of a manufacturing defect in that the tire on his particular Deathtrap IV had been made with rotted rubber. Piers requested $200,000 in compensatory damages (including some $100,000 in medical bills) and $2,000,000 in punitive damages; the basis for the punitive damages request was that CRASH!, Inc. knew of previous problems with bad rubber in the tires in its Deathtrap models but failed to correct them. CRASH!, Inc. removed the case to the United States District Court for the Southern District of Texas and filed a "Motion To Dismiss, or in the Alternative, to Transfer." CRASH!, Inc.'s motion urged the court to do the following:

(i) to dismiss the action on limitations grounds; or, in the alternative,

(ii) to dismiss the action based on the doctrine of forum non conveniens; the primary argument here was that CRASH!, Inc. intended to defend on the basis that the terrain over which Piers was riding at the time of the incident was rough to the extreme, that no reasonable tire could have stood up to such punishment, and that crucial evidence regarding the roughness and extremity of the terrain in the Paseo de la Muerte trail was in Mexico, as was crucial evidence regarding Piers' claims of medical-related damages (the veracity of which CRASH!, Inc. intended to dispute), so that the case should be litigated in Mexico; or, in the alternative,

(iii) to transfer the case to the United States District Court for the Northern District of Alabama, because crucial evidence regarding the agreements for the purchase of the (allegedly defective) rubber for the Deathtrap IV's tires was in CRASH!, Inc.'s headquarters in Birmingham, Alabama.

In early October of 2009, Piers filed a second lawsuit against CRASH!, Inc., this one in Alabama state court located in Birmingham, AL. The complaint had only one count, breach of warranty/contract, on the theory that the Deathtrap IV bicycle that CRASH!, Inc. had sold to Piers did not meet its warranty that the bike would stand up to a ride on any "reasonable terrain." In this complaint, Piers requested damages in the amount of $200,000 (including some $100,000 in medical bills) in compensatory damages for breach of warranty/contract. CRASH!, Inc. answered, denying liability.

In late October of 2009, the United States District Court for the Southern District of Texas issued a brief order stating, "In the interests of justice, this case is hereby transferred to the United States District Court for the Northern District of Alabama. The court makes no ruling on dismissal on any of the urged grounds; it leaves that to the Alabama federal court." When the record of the Texas federal court case arrived in Alabama federal court, which is located in Birmingham, AL, CRASH!, Inc. immediately renewed its motion to dismiss, arguing again that

(i) the case should be dismissed on limitations grounds; or, in the alternative,

(ii) the case should be dismissed on forum non conveniens grounds, in favor of a lawsuit in Mexico.

Piers opposed both motions and responded by filing two of his own motions, specifically,

(a) a motion to transfer the case back to the Southern District of Texas, Piers' theory being that the case was improperly transferred to the Northern District of Alabama due to lack of venue in AL federal court; or, in the alternative,

<!-- page: 7 -->

(b) a motion that the Alabama federal court stay the whole case in favor of his new lawsuit in Alabama state court.

CRASH!, Inc. opposed both motions.

In April of 2010, Piers propounded the following interrogatory upon CRASH!, Inc. in both the case in Alabama state court and the case in the Alabama federal court: "Identify with particularity (providing dates, names of persons involved, contact information, and any other known details) any incident known to you involving the failure, or alleged failure, of a tire on a model Deathtrap IV bicycle, including if available a description of the terrain upon which the bike was being used at the time of the failure or alleged failure." CRASH!, Inc. objected to the interrogatory as requesting irrelevant information and moved in both the state and federal courts for protective orders against the disclosure.

It is early November of 2010. In the past year, discovery in both the Alabama state court and the Alabama federal court has progressed, but the Alabama federal court has not ruled on either CRASH!, Inc.'s motion to dismiss, on Piers' motion to stay, or on CRASH!, Inc.'s motion for a protective order.
