2012 Civil Procedure 2, Greiner, Exam Model Answer



Question 1(a)

      Regarding the MTD, do we have venue, IPJ over Pince, and SMJ?  We
laid venue properly because a substantial portion of the events occurred in
MA (e.g., Pince's office visit with the SM doc, and the failure to request
information from the National Registry), 1392(c)(2).  For similar reasons,
the MA federal district court, under FRCP 4(k)(1)(A) and using MA's limits-
of-the-constitution long-arm, can constitutionally exercise IPJ over Pince:
 the fact that Pince went to SM's is purposeful availment for IPJ.  SMJ
could rest in DJ or FQJ.  For DJ, AIC is well over 75K, but do we have
complete diversity (Strabridge)?  SM is DE and MA, but IP could be
domiciled in either NH or MA.  Start with the fact that she was born in NH;
did her domicile ever change to MA?  Not clear.  IN 2009, she lived mostly
in Boston, but she was homeless, not exactly an "intent to settle"
anywhere.  In 2011, she became a student, which ordinarily does not change
domicile, and in fact she has to go to NH periodically anyway.  I'd say NH
domiciliary, meaning complete diversity OK.  If Pince = MA, no DJ.  Re FQJ,
we apply the coercive action rule, and since we have only a state law cause
of action (malpractice), we need Smith/Grable.  Perhaps the issue of the
NMMRA's constitutionality is embedded in Pince's cause of action, because
the allege malpractice negligence per se in the failure to comply with the
NMMRA's registry consultation provision, which SM says is unconstitutional,
or we might argue that the per se negligence claim itself encompasses a
federal law issue (negligence = failure to comply with federal law).   This
seems possible but unlikely.  If the court buys it, it will look to the
other Smith/Grable factors to assess whether exercising federal
jurisdiction any time a plaintiff would prove a state-law cause of action
by citing a def's failure to comply with a federal law of questionable
constitutionality would sweep lots of cases (or too few) into the federal
system (seems like not too many), and whether this issue is primarily legal
or factual (on violation of the standard of care it seems like both, an
application of undisputed facts to unclear law, but medmal causation is
always intensely factual and messy).  This feels like no Smith/Grable.

      There is no hope for Colorado River abstention because there at
present no parallel proceeding, only a threat of one, and we did not study
the other abstention bases.



Question 1(b)

      [Policy question, lots of ways to go.  Here is a more specific-type
answer.]

      Did Congress want this kind of case in federal court?  On the one
hand, Congress could have prohibited (but didn't) the federal courts from
exercise SMJ over individual medmal disputes.  But the fact that Congress
did not withdraw SMJ of this type of action can't alone prevent us from
concluding that Congress didn't want this kind of case in federal court, or
else we shouldn't have the domestic & probate exceptions to DV, or
abstention, or federal court FNC.  And there are powerful indicators that
Congress didn't want individual medmal cases in federal court, or indeed in
any court.  NMMRA 904 prohibits removal; sections 901-03 provide enormous
federal help to get medmal cases out of the state court system ($ and
statute-drafting expertise).  Congress appears to have wanted these
disputes decided by state administrative agencies, not federal courts (or
state courts).

      We can look to the policies underlying the non-CR abstention
doctrines, and supplemental SMJ, for guidance.  Almost medmal law is state
tort law; there are few federal issues.  But the NMMRA does provide some
federal law, obviously, such as the national registry at issue in Pince's
case.  The administrative scheme Congress's duly empowered agent (the
Secretary) suggested for the states, as set up in the MSMMRA, is complex,
and federal declaratory judgment or garden variety DJ actions could
interfere with it.  Medmal and regulating (licensing) the medical
profession is traditional state business, but it is becoming increasingly
federalized (like so much else).  There are no tax or criminal law issues.
 And then there are the factors above discussed in Smith/Grable; more
likely that no FQJ, again suggestion Congressional ambivalence to these
suits.

      True, if we say that this kind of case does not belong in federal
courts, we might be undercutting the rationales behind DJ.  But again, so
do the probate and divorce exceptions, and so does CR abstention.  It
happens.



Question 2

      There is no change to the venue, IPJ, or SMJ discussion.  There is
now a much stronger CR abstention argument because there is a parallel
state proceeding; nothing in CR turned on the distinction of a state court
versus administrative agency, so we do a regular CR analysis.  For the
reasons discussed in the previous response, the NMMRA here as analogous to
the McCarren amendment in CR in that it is a statute that appears to
manifest Congressional intent for a certain case type (here, individual
medmal) to be adjudicated by a state body.  On the other hand, it does not
appear as though there's a lot of difference in convenience between a
Boston federal court and a Concord NH state agency.  Concord & Boston are
about an hour apart, and there shouldn't be document production or witness
issues.  As discussed above, there's a mix of federal and state law issues.
  The two proceedings began within days of each other . Against all this,
we should weigh the federal court's unflagging obligation to exercise the
jurisdiction given it (if indeed it has it, see question 1(a)).  Tough
call.



Question 3

      There are three broad classes of issues:  IPJ over SM; the lien order
(a procedural due process =  PDP issue); the PDP status of the whole NH
Agency ("NHA") administrative scheme.

      IPJ over SM:  First, we have to see whether there is IPJ over SM,
even though the proceeding is before an administrative agency.  Otherwise,
a state could evade IPJ limits entirely, and thus project its coercive
power anywhere around the globe, simply by creating administrative agencies
to adjudicate disputes.   So we analyze the IPJ issues as though the NHA
were a court.   SM does not have overwhelming contacts to NH for general
IPJ, so specific IPJ:  minimum contacts, International Shoe.  Here, SM
solicited, but did not actually provide, business/services in NH.  And it
used newspapers of limited circulation, not a broad medium such as the
Internet, to do so.  This seems like purposeful availment.  Further, NH
(through the MSMMRA, section 302(b)) has a long-arm specific to this kind
of solicitation, putting medical providers (like SM) on notice, see
Shaeffer & McGee, as opposed to Hanson.  Against all this, we weight the
fact that SM does not provide services or ship products into NH.  I'd say
the exercise of IPJ is OK here, but if not, SM should file an appropriate
motion raising this issue (even if there is no explicit rule in the NHA
allowing for such a motion.  The NHA can't prevent SM from raising a
constitutional defense by refusing to provide a procedural mechanism for
raising it.

      The lien order:  One way to analyze is to follow Schnaidach and
simply assess the PDP status of the scheme leading to the lien order.  If
we do that:  The order was imposed without notice to SM or an opportunity
to allow SM to dispute the merits and on a blisteringly fast time schedule
(within hours of filing of the complaint).  Pince need not post a bond to
protect SM against consequences of erroneous imposition, and indeed, it is
not clear that SM could even recover for such consequences at all.  Yes,
Pince had to produce sworn affidavits from competent witnesses (not just
allegations from lawyers), but there are two problems with this
requirement:  First, the Deciding Officer ("DO") does not adjudicate
whether the affidavits provide sufficient factual support for the
conclusion that malpractice occurred (i.e., their persuasiveness), only
that the affiant had the right kind of credentials and concluded that
malpractice occurred.  Second, there is no requirement of any evidentiary
support on the amount of the lien; and here, it appears that the amount may
have been abusive, with a lot of punitive damages in the mix.  But the
scheme does have elements that can save it.  Most important of these is
that it is REALLY easy to get this lien order dissolved; all that SM need
to is file a copy of an insurance policy with a sufficiently high liability
limit, something SM surely has.  In effect, then, this whole scheme has the
same legal consequence as a NH law requiring an entity like SM to maintain
adequate insurance, which is pretty innocuous.  Second, the adjudication on
the merits will occur VERY fast, which will resolve the lien order anyway.
A close call so far.  For me, the key point is that there is no reason for
this scheme.  As in Doehr, the defendant (here, SM) isn't going anywhere,
and there is no evidence that it will hide its assets.

      Another way to analyze the lien order  is to start with Matthews test
(Doehr says apply this to old property):  What is the procedural
improvement SM would say should have been adopted?   Probably a pre-
imposition hearing with opportunity to submit evidence.  Pince's interest
in the lien order?  Low; see previous discussion re fact that SM is not
about to hide assets for flee.  SM's interest?  Medium.  The lien may put
it in technical default on its mortgage and other legal obligations, but it
is really easy to get the lien order dissolved.  Risk of erroneous
deprivation?  Very high, particularly as to the amount (see above); no
adversarial process in place, SM doesn't get to submit evidence.  Added
value of the additional procedure?  High; allowing both parties to submit
evidence is a classic way to get good information on the merits.  Expense
of the additional process?  Not all that high; the procedure on the merits
is highly accelerated anyway, so a request for a lien could simply be
folded into adjudication on the merits.

      The overall scheme:  We can get some guidance from the PDP "mini-
codes" in Vitek and in the Restatement of Judgments (2d) Sec 83.  Neither
authority is directly binding, but both can help us figure out what should
be required from a new adjudicatory scheme.  Both begin by requiring
adequate notice, which we have (service as per FRCP 4), see SMMRSA 504.
Vitek requires a "hearing . . . in person" and an opportunity to present
witnesses, whereas Sec 83 requires only an opportunity to submit evidence
and legal argument.  The SMMRSA provides the latter, not the former, but my
sense is that's probably enough; adjudication on the papers is now the norm
in federal courts (via MTD or SumJudg motions), and there is no requirement
of oral argument in that context.  Sec 83 requires a formulation of issues
by reference to specific rules; Vitek does not require this; again, I think
Sec 83 has this right, and the SMMRSA probably provides enough here.  Each
party can submit evidence by affidavit and argument by written brief, and
medmal law is highly developed.  Sec 83 requires a rule of finality, and we
have that.  Vitek requires an independent decisionmaker, and we have that.
 Nothing requires that the decisionmaker be a lawyer (see Elliot).  Vitek
requires a written statement of reasons by the deciionmaker, Sec 83 does
not.  The SMMRSA does not provide that.  From our study of FRCP 52, we know
that such statements are helpful because they induce higher quality
decision making, allowing better appellate review, and help us figure out
preclusion.  But there is no appellate review here (SMMRSA 517), and the
other two policies are probably not of constitutional importance, despite
Vitek.  Vitek requires effective and timely notice of the foregoing rights,
and we have that.  Note that to the extent that we are providing less
protection than was required in Vitek:  there was a big liberty interest at
stake there (stigma), while here, it's just money.  Forget about counsel at
state expense; this is garden variety tort stuff, with no loss of physical
liberty in the picture.

      But there is a lot here that makes me nervous in a PDP sense.  The 7th
A's right to a jury trial has never been applied to the states, so there's
no constitutional issue, but still, there is a long tradition of jury
adjudication of this kind of issue, particularly punitive damages, which
often involve a sense of the community (juries are better on that) as to
how much the def should be punished.  The public rights exception would not
apply, were there a jury right at issue, because no sovereign is a party
acting as a regulator.  There is no appeal.  There is no serious discovery
(just of the medical file).  The pace of the adjudication is blistering,
even though medmal issues are complex.  There is no live hearing, even if
one is not constitutionally required.  Per above, there is no written
explanation of the result.  The amounts involved can be high.  All this
adds up to making me very nervous in a PDP sense, even if I cannot point to
a particular aspect of the scheme that violates the PDP principles.




      Question 4(a)

      There is no information on numerosity, but there are probably enough
folks who had consulted SM for radiological matters.  There is probably
commonality on the issue of whether SM acts illegally by refusing to
participate in the national registry.  Pince has the same claim as the
class (she says she was hurt by the failure to comply with the national
registry), so she's probably typical, and she seems as adequate a
representative as any.  There are no inherent structural conflicts in the
class to the extent that the whole class seeks forward-looking, injunction
relief.  There may be some danger that Pince would sell the class down the
river for a larger damages award on her malpractice claim, but that could
only happen in a settlement, and FRCP 23(e) should take care of that.  The
class seeks an injunction, so seems like a good 23(b)(2) class.

      Question 4(b)

      Erie:  federal discovery rule versus state misconduct rule.  Apply
four Erie test.  Under Harlan's test, when the limitations period starts is
tied strongly how long a plaintiff may wait between the events giving rise
to the suit and filing; whether this affects primary conduct is exactly the
same question as whether the limitations period itself does.  The argument
re affecting primary conduct is stronger for large entities that have a lot
of litigation, such as insurance companies; my sense is that a medical
provider, even a large one, doesn't fit, so I'd say no affect, apply
federal law, do not dismiss lawsuit.  For Hanna majority, the key issue is
whether the discovery rule is considered part of the federal statute
specifying the limitations period or a common law rule.  If the former,
apply it in step one (clearly applicable, probably valid under Congress'
power to regulate federal courts), and don't dismiss lawsuit.  If the
latter, go to step two, twin aims, predictably different results leads to
forum shopping, apply state law, and dismiss.  But this makes no sense
because in state court the suit would not be dismissed because the longer
state limitations period would apply.  Same thing with York:  it's outcome
determinative only if we narrowly look at the discovery rule versus the
misconduct rule; but that's silly because we're not looking at what would
happen in state court (again, the case would survey due to the longer
limitations period).  For Ginsburg, we look to the interests and attempt to
preserve federal and state polices, but nothing makes any sense except the
idea that the same law should govern BOTH the length of the limitations
period and when the clock starts ticking.

      All this points to a flaw in this whole analysis:  once we've decided
that federal law determines the length of the limitations period, it would
not make sense to apply state law as to when that period starts.  The two
decisions are tied too closely together.  A legislature would consider when
the clock starts ticking together with how long it should run.  That's what
happened here.  The state starts the clock ticking immediately but lets it
run longer.  The feds don't start the clock ticking immediately, but when
it does start ticking, it goes for a shorter time.  This is analogous to a
criticism of the Second Restatement's issue-by-issue approach in the HCOL
context; pull issues apart and nothing may make sense.  The federal
discovery rule should apply, and the suit should live.

      Question 4(c)(i)

      No.  It's not a final order because the remaining claims are still

      Question 4(c)(ii)

      The pendency of an appeal does not affect the finality (for
preclusion purposes) of the MA federal court judgment, so it's irrelevant.

      Issue preclusion:  No.  The only possible common issue is whether
Pince can recover damages.  But the federal court decided the damages issue
on the grounds of a limitations period that the NHA will not apply (because
it will apply the longer NH period under a HCOL limitations-usually-
procedural analysis), so there is no "same issue."  Limitations was
actually litigated and necessary to the decision, and we have same parties,
but that doesn't matter without the same issue.

      Claim preclusion:  We have a final judgment, and same party we are
trying to preclude (Pince).  But it's not clear as to whether the
limitations dismissal will be considered on the merits in the intersystem
preclusion setting.  This probably turns on whether the MA federal court
was sitting in DJ or FQJ, see question 1(a) response.  If the former, per
Semtek, MA law (state where the MA federal court sits), we are told to
assume MA law says a limitations dismissal is on the merits, and the NHA
proceeding dies.  If FQJ, then federal law, we are told federal law says a
limitations dismissal is not on the merits, NHA proceeding goes on.

      Question 4(d)

      Whether the agency ruling can have any preclusive effect may depend on
the PDP issues discussed above, per Restatement section 83.  Assume those
are OK and we can just apply preclusion law.

      Issue preclusion:  No.  Pince won in the NHA!  SM can't preclude Pince
on an issue when Pince won on it.  SM can't even say that the $2M award
means no punitive damages, because we don't know what the DO actually
decided; the DO might have awarded 1.5M in compensatory and $1.5M in
punitives.

      Claim preclusion:  probably , but only as to the federal court damages
claim, not the injunction or the class action.  By analogy to the rule in
Marresse, the NHA had no jurisdiction to certify a class or award an
injunction, so its ruling cannot preclude an effort to seek those remedies.
 Re claim preclusion on the federal court damages claim:  we have the exact
same claim and a final decision, and it is unquestionably on the merits.
Pince can't recover more money in a different court on the exact same
lawsuit.
