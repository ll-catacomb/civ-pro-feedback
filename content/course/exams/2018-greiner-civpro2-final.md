In 2007, Congress passed the Disposition of Property Deriving from Federal Lands Act (the "DPDFLA").
The statute created an agency to deal with litigation relating in any way to property deriving from
federal land. You may assume that Congress had the constitutional power to enact this statute. The
statute in relevant part provides as follows.

Section 29: Creation of the Federal Land Dispute Resolution Agency ("FLDRA"), Jurisdiction, Procedures

    (a) The FLDRA shall be composed of administrative law judges (ALJs) appointed by the Director of
         the Bureau of Land Management. It shall adjudicate cases relating in any way to property
         derived from federal government public lands.

    (b) There shall be one division of the FLDRA in each state, and each division shall exercise
         jurisdiction over persons or property as though the division were subject to and empowered by
         Fed. R. Civ. P. 4(k)(1)(A); thus, each FLDRA division shall, for the purposes of personal
         jurisdiction, act as if it were a federal district court in located in the state in which that division
         sits. Each FLDRA division shall consist of a panel of three ALJs that, unless otherwise specified in
         this statute, shall adjudicate cases as a three-judge panel.
    (c) The FLDRA divisions shall conduct adjudications with an eye toward minimum formality of
         adjudication. The rules of evidence do not apply; the FLDRA divisions shall consider all
         submissions that the parties might make. Any concerns about the admissibility of the evidence
         shall instead be directed toward the weight that such evidence should receive.

    (d) A party seeking relief from an FLDRA division shall initiate a proceeding therein by filing a short
         and plain statement of the grounds for the FLDRA division's jurisdiction, of the claim showing
         that the party is entitled to relief, and of a demand for the relief sought, which may include
         relief in the alternative or different types of relief.

    (e) Notice to the opposing party of the FDLRA division proceeding may be made in any manner
         reasonably likely to apprise the opposing party of the proceeding, but shall include a copy
         (electronic or otherwise) of all papers filed at the proceeding's initiation.

    (f) In general, an FLDRA division will allow an initial submission by both parties of evidence to
         support their contentions, followed by a second submission of legal arguments and rebuttal
         evidence. The FLDRA divisions shall make their findings of fact and conclusions of law based
         only on written submissions from the parties. The record may include affidavits, as well as
         authenticated documents and other exhibits. There shall be no live hearings or oral
         presentations, nor shall there be any discovery. The FLDRA divisions shall make maximum
         reasonable use of Internet communications devices to facilitate its adjudication.

    (g) The FLDRA divisions shall have jurisdiction only with respect to claims by a single plaintiff against
         a single defendant, although they may hear joined claims and counterclaims. In particular, the
         FLDRA divisions may not allow joinder of parties, intervention, or class actions.

    (h) In all other matters, and subject to subsection (c) of this section, the FLDRA divisions may use
         such procedures as they deems appropriate, including any principles or rules used in federal
         courts, such as the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

Section 30: Creation of a Cause of Action that Can Be Asserted before the FLDRA, Law Applicable, Non-
Preemption of State Court Litigation, Waiver of Sovereign Immunity

    (a) A party who has suffered a tort, breach of contract, or other wrong relating in any way to
         property derived from federal public land may initiate a proceeding within the relevant FLDRA
         division for appropriate relief. The FLDRA division may award any remedy in law or equity that it
         finds just and appropriate, including damages, declaratory relief, and/or an injunction. Within
         the confines of these limits, the FLDRA shall otherwise have the capacity to hear any case or
         controversy within the judicial power of U.S. Const. Art. III.

    (b) Notwithstanding any other provision of law, the federal courts will have no jurisdiction over any
         dispute subject to the FLDRA jurisdiction. Nothing in this statute shall be deemed to prevent
         any litigation otherwise available in state court, and there shall be no removal of such actions to
         federal court or to the FLDRA.

    (c) The laws of the several states, except where the Constitution or treaties of the United States or
         Acts of Congress otherwise require or provide, shall be regarded as rules of decision in the
         FLDRA divisions, in cases where they apply.

    (d) All decisions of the FLDRA shall be final and unappealable to any other adjudicatory body,
         including any federal or state court.

Section 31: Pre-adjudication Seizure of Property
A party may request an emergency order from the relevant FLDRA division for immediate seizure and
appropriate disposition of property deriving from federal land. The FLDRA division shall deploy the
following procedures with respect to such a request.

    (a) Initial filing: A party requesting an emergency order shall file
              1) a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the FLDRA division has jurisdiction
                   over the claim, that the party is entitled to relief, and that the ultimate relief sought in
                   the case is appropriate;
              2) a copy of any documents relevant to either the claim and to the emergency order
                   request in the claimant's possession;
              3) affidavits or sworn statements, if any, relating to the claim for relief or to the request for
                   the seizure order; and
              4) an emergency request for immediate adjudication of whether an order of seizure should
                   issue.

    (b) Order of seizure: A single FLDRA judge shall immediately examine the papers referenced in
         subsection (a) of this section. If the ALJ finds that good cause exists, she shall immediately issue
         an order authorizing an appropriate law enforcement officer to seize the specified goods and to
         dispose of them in an appropriate manner, including but not limited to holding the goods until
         the conclusion of the FLDRA division proceeding. In the event that the goods are sold, the
         proceeds of the sale shall be deposited in an account the FLDRA division controls, and the FLDRA
         division shall thereafter make appropriate disposition of such proceeds.

    (c) Post-seizure proceedings: After issuing its order of seizure, the FLDRA division shall proceed
         according to its ordinary procedures to adjudicate the case.

Section 32: Promulgation of Regulations

A randomly selected panel of seven FLDRA division administrative law judges shall have the power to
promulgate regulations, binding on all FLDRA divisions, for the purpose of creating procedures for the
divisions to use. The procedures promulgated pursuant to this section shall preserve the informal and
flexible character of FLDRA proceedings, but will not violate Section 29, above. No rule created pursuant
to this section may abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right.

Under Washington and Oregon state law, a plaintiff may sue for tort called negligent interference with
contractual relations. To prevail in this cause of action, a plaintiff must prove that the defendant (i)
committed an act or omission that is a departure from ordinary care, that (ii) the plaintiff knew, or
should have known, would cause the plaintiff to suffer damages in the form of an inability to receive the
benefit of a valid contract not involving the defendant, (iii) that the plaintiff did in fact suffer such
damages.

Under California, Delaware, and Idaho state law, there is no tort for negligent interference with
contractual relations. There is, however, a cause of action called intentional interference with
contractual relations. To prevail in this cause of action, a plaintiff must prove that the defendant (i)
committed an act or omission with the intention of causing the plaintiff to suffer damages in the form of
an inability to receive the benefit of a contract not involving the defendant, and (ii) that the plaintiff did
in fact suffer such damages.
For both the negligent and the intentional version of the interference with contractual relations tort, the
last event necessary to complete the tort is the breach of the contract with which the defendant
interfered.

All states relevant to this exam have a standard cause of action for breach of contract. To prevail on a
common law breach of contract claim the plaintiff must show: (1) The plaintiff and defendant made a
valid enforceable contract, (2) the Plaintiff performed his/her part of the contract or offered a
justification for nonperformance, (3) the Defendant failed to perform his/her part of the contract, and
(4) damage resulted to the plaintiff. The Plaintiff must prevail on all elements to win.

Oregon and Washington follow the First Restatement for horizontal choice of law. Delaware, California,
and Idaho follow the Second Restatement.

Oregon and California preclusion law matches the federal common law of preclusion. Delaware,
Washington, and Idaho preclusion law provides that a limitations dismissal is considered on the merits;
there is no mutuality requirement for issue preclusion; and multiple independent grounds for a jury
verdict are considered resolved in favor of the prevailing party.

Oregon, Idaho, and Delaware have only a single federal district. Washington has two federal districts,
the Eastern and Western. The city of Potomac is located in the Eastern District of Washington.
California has four federal districts. The city of Sacramento is in the Eastern District of California.

Oregon, Idaho, Delaware, Washington, and California all have long-arm statutes that go to the limits of
the Constitution.

Florean Fortescue was a farmer. He leased federal land in Oregon, where he was born and lived his
entire life, to grow crops. His primary farmland was located in an isolated area of northwestern Oregon.
Due to recent fires, washouts, and other natural disasters, Florean's farmland was accessible only by a
logging road that, in the sleepy town of Hogsmeade, Oregon, passed over the Hogsmeade Bridge. The
town of Hogsmeade raised revenue by charging any vehicle larger than a passenger car a toll to cross
the bridge.

In early 2017, the Commissioners of the town of Hogsmeade decided that the Hogsmeade Bridge, which
was also used by the town's few residents, was in need of repairs, although the repairs were not urgent.
They contracted with Cho Chang Contracting Corp., incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of
business in Potomac, Washington, to do the repairs. Chang Corp does business exclusively in the states
of Washington and Oregon. Chang Corp. advised the Commissioners that the repairs would require that
the Bridge be shut down for approximately two weeks. The contract for the repairs stated that Chang
Corp. would take care to schedule the shutdown "keeping in mind the needs of farmers in northwestern
Oregon to access the bridge for the purpose of delivering their harvests to markets in the central and
eastern portions of the state, and to give due notice to such farmers of the dates of any shutdown."
Otherwise, the contract allowed Chang Corp. to choose the time of the shutdown, so long as all repair
work was complete by January of 2021.
