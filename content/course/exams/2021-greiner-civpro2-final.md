                                                    Relevant state laws

All states relevant to this exam have long-arm statutes extending to the limits of the United States
constitution. All states relevant to this exam, except Ohio, have declaratory judgment statutes
equivalent to 28 U.S.C. § 2201. Ohio's declaratory judgment statute is the same as § 2201 but also has
the following provision:

Ohio L. Ann § 2201(b)(26.9): Deposit Required in Declaratory Judgment Statute Actions
The Legislature of Ohio finds that too many frivolous "strike suits" seeking declaratory judgments of
non-liability have been filed in Ohio courts, frequently by large corporations seeking declarations of non-
liability. These strike suit plaintiffs are engaging in risky conduct that they then attempt to immunize
from liability by using declaratory judgment lawsuits against injured parties who cannot adequately
respond to their suits. We the Legislature of Ohio seek to deter such risky conduct. Therefore, when
initiating any declaratory judgment lawsuit filed in any court in Ohio, the plaintiff shall deposit with the
court 10% of the amount in controversy in the lawsuit, to be held by the court until final judgment in the
lawsuit to pay the defendant's litigation costs and attorneys' fees if the court ultimately decides not to
issue the declaratory judgment. Any lawsuit in which the plaintiff does not comply with this requirement
shall be immediately dismissed.

Ohio, Delaware, and Nevada use the Second Restatement for horizontal choice of law. Texas, Vermont,
Michigan use the First Restatement for horizontal choice of law.
All states relevant to this exam have a standard breach of written contract cause of action. Breach of
written contract requires a plaintiff to prove that there was a written contract, that the defendant
breached it, and that the breached caused the plaintiff harm. Punitive damages are never available in a
breach of contract lawsuit.

All states relevant to this exam have a standard products liability cause of action. Products liability
requires a plaintiff to prove that the defendant sold the plaintiff a product, that the product was
defectively designed or manufactured, and that the defect caused the plaintiff's injuries. Products
liability is a tort. Ohio does not allow punitive damages in products liability cases. All other states
relevant to this exam do allow punitive damages in products liability cases, but in these states punitive
damages are only available for a plaintiff who has suffered physical injury. You may assume that the
availability of punitive damages is substantive for horizontal choice of law purposes.

Texas' limitations period for products liability lawsuits is three years. Ohio, Nevada, and Delaware all
have five-year limitations periods for products liability cases. Michigan and Vermont have six-year
limitations periods for products liability cases. All states relevant to this exam have ten-year limitations
periods for breach of a written contract lawsuits. All limitations periods relevant to this exam begin to
run on the date that the plaintiff suffered the harm/injury forming the basis of the lawsuit.

In Texas, Delaware, and Nevada, limitations-based dismissals are on the merits for claim preclusion
purposes. In Ohio, Michigan, and Vermont, limitations-based dismissals are not on the merits for claim
preclusion purposes. Under federal common law, limitations-based dismissals are not on the merits for
claim preclusion purposes.

Texas law includes the following provisions:

Tex. L. Ann. § 4.10: Pre-judgment seizure of property in support of suit for breach of a written
contract
A party to a contract who alleges that they have suffered injury caused by another's breach of a written
contract, which contract includes a right to pursue a pre-judgment seizure of property, may petition a
court of this state for an order seizing any property pursuant to the contract, provided that

    (a) the petitioner/plaintiff files with the court
              (1) a complaint containing a short plain statement of the facts constituting the alleged
                   breach of the written contract, of the amount of damages the breach caused, and of the
                   property to be seized;
              (2) a copy of the written contract; and
              (3) a statement swearing under oath (i.e., under penalty of perjury) that the facts alleged in
                   the short plain statement are true to the best of the petitioner/plaintiff's knowledge,
                   and that the copy of the contract filed is true and correct.

    (b) In addition, the plaintiff/petitioner shall file a litigation bond equal to the value of the property
         seized assuring payment of the respondent/defendant's damages in the event that the seizure is
         adjudicated to be wrongful and the petitioner/plaintiff is unable to pay such damages.

Tex. L. Ann. § 4.20: Seizure to establish traditional quasi-in-rem jurisdiction in tort lawsuit
A party who alleges that they have been damaged by the commission of a tort by a defendant may
establish jurisdiction over an action in a court of this state using a traditional quasi-in-rem theory, as
follows. The plaintiff shall file with the court

    (a) a complaint including a short plain statement
         (1) identifying the property to be seized,
         (2) describing the facts constituting the alleged tort, and
         (3) stating the resulting damages; and

    (b) a statement swearing under oath (i.e., under penalty of perjury) that the facts alleged in the
         short plain statement are true to the best of the plaintiff's knowledge.

Tex. L. Ann § 4.50: Procedure to be followed in actions initiate under §§ 4.10 and/or 4.20
A judge of a court of this state, having reviewed the petitioner/plaintiff's filings in an action pursued
under sections 4.10 or 4.20 (or both simultaneously) and being satisfied of a petitioner/plaintiff's
compliance with those sections, shall order seizure of the property named in the complaint(s). The
property shall remain in the court's control until further order of the court. The judge shall schedule a
hearing to take place within 20 days of its seizure order, at which time the judge shall permit the
respondent/defendant to introduce any evidence and to make any argument to the effect that the
seizure order should be rescinded. In addition, at the time of the seizure order, the judge shall require
the plaintiff/petitioner to use means reasonably likely of success to provide the respondent/defendant
with notice of the pending proceeding and the seizure, including paper or electronic copies of the filings
in the lawsuit.

[Note: Throughout this exam pattern, remember that a state court can constitutionally seize property
only if that property is located within that state's geographic limits.]

Inigo Montoya was born in Detroit, MI, and grew up without family, living largely in foster homes. He
loved vintage computers and computer games, particularly games with revenge themes. After
graduating from high school in Detroit, and wanting eventually to pursue a career in the revenge
business, he began to support himself by obtaining computers from the 1970s and early 1980s, restoring
them so that they could play games from that era, and selling them to customers with whom he
connected on the Internet. His business was a modest success, and having always wanted to travel, he
bought himself a full-service recreational vehicle ("RV"), which he christened "Roberts," to use as his
home. Hating the cold, in 2015 at the age of 23, he drove Roberts to Pensacola, FL, and rented an RV
plot for one year, registering to vote there, obtaining a FL driver's license, and receiving mail at a local
post office box. Financially, he opened a business checking account with the Bank of Florin at a
Pensacola Branch, giving the bank his RV plot address and his post office box. The Bank of Florin was
incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Reno, NV. The Bank of Florin operated a
massive computer complex in warehouses outside Reno, where it kept all of its business records
(including backups). The bank had 2000 branch locations, and approximately 15,000 ATM machines,
across the United States. Although Montoya did almost all of his banking online (he received all of his
statements electronically), occasionally he visited Bank of Florin branches wherever he happened to be,
sometimes to deposit old-style personal checks that his customers sent him as payment. Perhaps
illegally, Montoya never paid taxes.

In 2016, when the lease on his Pensacola RV plot expired, Montoya secretly closed his Pensacola post
office box (telling no one) and began to drive around the country in Roberts, parking for as long as he
felt like staying, then moving on. He never stayed in one location for more than four months. He
received any necessary mail at a FedEx facility near wherever he happened to be (he always told people
with mail he wanted to receive, which FedEx facility to use, and did not worry about any other mail). As
he began to travel, he bought an old Polaroid instant camera and a scanner, and he began to document
his travels on his personal website, youkilledmyfather.net. He also ran his business from this website.
He listed his (now-closed) Pensacola P.O. Box on the website, but he never used it. His business model
was as follows. Montoya perused various traditional and non-traditional outlets, mostly online, across
the world for no-longer-functioning vintage computer hardware as well as vintage software packages,
particularly pieces that no longer worked. He bought these items cheaply and had them shipped to a
FedEx wherever he happened to be at the time. He then refurbished and repaired the vintage items,
and marketed them on youkilledmyfather.net, which could be viewed anywhere in the world. He sold
the products to customers from whom he received orders online, shipping them to addresses and
receiving payments via Paypal. He transferred the payments to his Bank of Florin account electronically
whenever he needed funds. Montoya paid money in and out of this Florin account as needed to support
his business, the upkeep on Roberts, and his few personal needs.

In early 2017, Montoya finished work on a multi-month project, the restoration of a rare first edition of
a Xerox Soprano personal computer, capable of running Guilder, one of the first multi-game personal
computer software packages. Montoya listed the device on youkilledmyfather.net, with accompanying
software, at $110,000.

Buttercup and Westley, an unmarried couple in their 40s whose relationship had lasted over 15 years
but who lived in separate apartments, contacted Montoya about the device via youkilledmyfather.net's
chat function. Buttercup lived all her life in Toledo, Ohio, but had met Westley on one of her trips to
Ann Arbor. Westley grew up and lived all his life Ann Arbor, Michigan, about an hour north of Toledo.
Neither made a lot of money, but both were fanatic about vintage computer systems, and they decided
to spend much of their life savings on the Soprano computer, thinking that they could play on it for a
while and then resell it at a profit. Buttercup conducted all of the negotiations via the
youkilledmyfather.net chat function from her Toledo apartment, keeping Westley informed about the
status of the negotiations when the two met in Ann Arbor. Buttercup began all of her posts to
youkilledmyfather.net's chat function, "Hello from not-so-sunny northern Ohio! Almost as bleak as a
fire swamp!". At the time of his posting to the website's chat functions to negotiate with Buttercup,
Montoya happened to be in Oklahoma, parked outside Oklahoma City.

The negotiations between Montoya and Buttercup resulted in the following arrangement: Westley
agreed to pay Montoya $30,000 via a personal check drawn on a Michigan bank. Buttercup agreed to
pay $80,000 via a personal check drawn on an Ohio bank. Each would separately mail their checks to a
Fed Ex facility in Oklahoma City. When he received the checks, Montoya would ship the computer and
accompanying disks to a FedEx facility in Ann Arbor, Michigan, with instructions to allow Westley to pick
it up. Buttercup told Montoya that Westley and she were looking forward to using their new machine
for the first time on a vacation they were planning just outside Burlington, Vermont, where they
intended to stay in their favorite bed and breakfast (in a historic building, constructed in the 1800s) and
visit nearby lakes. All three parties electronically signed1 a written contract that, in addition to
describing the computer to be sold and specifying the price, included the following provisions:

    · Warranty of fitness: The seller warrants that the product sold pursuant to this contract is safe
         and suitable for its intended use.

    · Right to prejudgment seizure: The parties agree that in the event of a breach of this contract by
         either the seller or the buyers, the damaged party may initiate a lawsuit to seize any property
         owned by the breaching party in a court with jurisdiction over that property to assure payment
         of any damages that the same court finds, after an appropriate adjudication, to be owed. The
         parties agree, however, that nothing in this provision waives any constitutional rights of any
         party, and that any seizure pursuant to this paragraph must be accomplished consistently with
         the United States Constitution.

The transaction went off without a hitch. Westley and Buttercup mailed their separate checks to the
FedEx location that Montoya specified outside of Oklahoma City. Montoya received the checks, but did
not immediately cash them. After mailing the computer from Oklahoma City to the specified FedEx
location in Ann Arbor, MI, he hopped into Roberts and started driving south, intending to spend some
time in Dallas, Texas. When he arrived, he rented an RV plot for Roberts in Dallas. The "lease" on the
plot was for four months. When he arrived in the Dallas area, he visited a local bank outlet of Florin bank

other banking there related to his ongoing business.

On April 14, 2017, Westley retrieved the vintage computer from the FedEx in Ann Arbor, Michigan. He
then drove his car to pick up Buttercup in Toledo, and the couple drove through the night to Burlington,
Vermont, arriving on April 17, 2017. Too excited to go to bed, they checked into their bed and
breakfast, plugged it in, and started to play the vintage games. Buttercup was playing, Westley
watching, when the computer caught on fire and exploded. Buttercup suffered moderate burns. She
was transported via helicopter to a hospital in Boston, MA, where she spent three days before being
released to recover back at her home in Ohio. Her medical bills were approximately $100,000. Westley
suffered no physical injury; he stayed at a friend's house in the Boston area to be with Buttercup while
she recovered, responding to her every request with, "As you wish." Back in Burlington, the fire had
been contained in the couple's bed and breakfast room before spreading very far. The fire chief on the
scene determined that the fire started at or near the outlet into which Buttercup and Westley had
plugged their new computer, but did not attempt to determine further whether the wiring on the outlet
was old or faulty, or whether there was something wrong with the computer's power cable. Buttercup
took the damaged computer back with her to her Toledo, Ohio apartment.

Near the end of May, 2017, Westley and Buttercup received their bank statements and saw that
Montoya had cashed their checks at the Bank of Florin branch in Dallas, TX. They wrote to Montoya
using the chat function of youkilledmyfather.net. They told Montoya about the fire and Buttercup's
injuries, and informed him that they thought that faulty wiring on the computer's power cable caused
the fire. They demanded that Montoya pay Buttercup $100,000 in medical bills plus $50,000 in pain and
suffering, and that he pay Westley $25,000 for emotional distress. Alternatively, they demanded that
Montoya refund exactly what they each paid for the computer. Montoya refused, writing, "There was
nothing wrong with the computer's power system. I wired that part of the computer myself, and I know
what I'm doing. If you want to sue someone, sue the bed and breakfast where you stayed. Its wiring
caused the accident."
