2009 Greiner Civ Pro Exam, Model answer
Question 1(a)
      Federal court SMJ generally seems OK here on diversity (Flamel = NC,
Dursley = GA/SC, $100K AIC).  There may also be FQJ, but we don't need it
here (yet; see below).
      The most promising basis for appellate jurisdiction is the 1292(b)
double discretion doctrine.  Judge Jigger found something pretty close to a
reasonably grounds for a difference in opinion ("tough question," and "I
could use some help") and that immediate review would material advance the
end of the litigation (if she's wrong, Dursley wins), and Dursley began the
appellate proceedings within ten days.  If the Fourth Circuit agrees that
these findings are close enough to the 1292(b)-mandated findings, the
Circuit would have discretion regarding whether to hear Dursley's appeal.
The problem is, why should it?  There's nothing special about the case, the
choice of law issue is unlikely to recur, and the proceedings are not
particularly high-cost.[1]
      Other bases of appellate jurisdiction are unlikely to work.  A denial
of a summary judgment motion is not a final order.  Re the collateral order
doctrine, the issue of whether Georgia law provides the definition of the
word "business" can be effectively reviewed later (Lauro Lines is analogous
here), plus this issue is not collateral to the merits (it is the merits).
There is only one claim, and Judge Jigger said none of the no-just-reason-
for-delay magic words (she didn't even purport to enter judgment on
anything), so R54(b) is out.  There is no suggestion of recurring district
court misbehavior, so a supervisory writ of mandamus is out.  Nor should a
"regular" (non-supervisory) writ be issued; Dursley cannot point to any
irreparable harm it would suffer absent the writ.  There's no injunction in
the picture, so 1292(a) is out.
Question 1(b)
      Snape should move for summary judgment on the basis of issue
preclusion; whether he prevails probably turns on whether there was arising
under jurisdiction over the Flamel-Dursley litigation, as follows.
      First, the basic issue preclusion requirements:  the issue of whether
Dursley could properly charge a 15% interest rate in this transaction was
actually litigated (summary judgment motion), and Judge Jigger's rejection
of Dursley's theory here was necessary to the decision.  The fact that the
decision is on appeal does not deprive it of the requisite finality for
issue preclusion purposes.  And it seems as though the issues Dursley's
answer in Snape-Dursley are the same as those in Flamel-Dursley, in that
Dursley's defenses would depend on using the Georgia definition of
"business" (the wording in the two contracts was identical) and on
asserting that the FGBRA is unconstitutional, which is exactly what was
litigated before.  So the only element left is the same parties.
      State law varies on the mutuality requirement, but that's a red
herring here; the rendering court is federal, suggesting that federal
common law would govern preclusion issues (see Semtek, involving the
rendering federal court sitting in diversity; here, the rendering federal
court might have FQJ, which presents a stronger case for FCL), but whether
FCL borrows SC state law or uses the its own preclusion doctrine, it's all
the same, because the question specifies that the preclusion law of SC = Ga
= NC = federal.
      In the declaratory judgment proceeding, Snape is "really" the
plaintiff (and is the plaintiff on the counterclaim), so his summary
judgment motion would be an attempt at non-mutual offensive collateral
estoppel.  Because all of the relevant states follow federal preclusion
law, we go under Parklane Hosiery, which says that NMOCE is generally not
allowed (reason:  if it were allowed, the party invoking NMOCE could/would
wait on the sidelines in the first lawsuit to get a free ruling, and we
want to incentivize this party to join the first lawsuit).  But NMOCE might
be allowed if the party invoking NMOCE could not easily have joined in the
first lawsuit, as was true in Parklane itself.  (Note that other Parklane
factors, such as the availability of additional procedural tools and the
sufficiency of incentives to litigate in the first case, don't appear very
informative here.)
      Could Snape have joined as a plaintiff in Flamel's suit against
Dursley?  Suppose the sole basis of SMJ in Flamel-Dursley was diversity
(note Flamel = NC due to long residence, no mention of an intent to leave,
and Dusley = SC (principal place of business) and GA (incorporation), $100K
in controversy), centering on the fact that the explicit basis for Flamel's
lawsuit was state law breach of contract.  Snape's identical claim is also
state law breach of contract, and then Snape could not have joined the
lawsuit because Snape = SC (long residence, no mention of an intent to
leave), same as Dursley, destroying complete diversity.  (1367(b) and Exxon
would prevent Snape from asserting that his claim was supplemental to
Flamel's claim.)  So in that event, Snape could not have joined Flamel-
Dursley, and under Parklane Hosiery, Snape might be able to use NMOCE.
      If, however, Flamel-Dursley was an FQJ case, then Snape-Dursley is as
well, in which case the lack of diversity between Snape and Dursley would
make no difference to SMJ, and Snape could have joined in Flamel-Dursley.
Note that given the tight factual relationship between Snape's and Flamel's
claims against Dursley (same contract wording, same venture, same loan
amount, same interest rate dispute), Rule 20 would have allowed Snape to
have joined in the Flamel lawsuit; these are same T&O.  Heyward-Robinson.
If so, then under Parklane, Snape can't use NMOCE.
      So, was Flamel-Dursley an arising under case?  Not explicitly, as
Flamel's cause of action was state law breach of contract.  But his breach
of contract theory depended on a construction of the FGBRA, making the
Smith/Grable arising under theory potentially available.  The issues to be
decided (the applicability of a choice of law clause to the FGBRA,
constitutionality of the FBGRA) are purely legal, which points in favor of
FQJ.  Nor does it seem like allowing Flamel-Dursley to be litigated in
federal court would deprive the state courts of a huge number of cases, nor
would it upset the federal/state balance.  There aren't likely to be that
many FGBRA-centered lawsuits.  Finally, there appears to be a strong
federal interest at stake because Dursley challenged the constitutionality
of the Secretary of Interior's actions under the FGBRA, making this case a
lot like Grable.  Still, Empire Health Choice said that the Smith/Grable
exception was to be construed very narrowly.  On the whole, probably FQJ,
although this is close, so no NMOCE.


Question 2
      The Jorkins case is (currently) properly in federal court.  There is
diversity jurisdiction (Jorkins = NJ (see Mas), Foolim = Delaware and New
York, $85K in controversy).  Jorkins sued in NY state court (Foolim's back
yard), so Foolim's removal was improper, plus Foolim didn't remove within
30 days.  But now that 30 days have passed from the removal, the only
relevant issue is whether there is any basis of SMJ over the Jorkins-Foolim
lawsuit, and diversity (per above) provides that basis.
      Jorkins' motion to transfer the case back to NY federal court should
probably be granted, although without more facts, we can't be 100% sure.
There was general IPJ (NY is Foolim's principal place of business) over
Foolim in NY; venue doesn't apply because the case was removed.  1404(a)
allows a transfer of a properly brought suit only if the suit could have
been brought in the transferee district.  Under Hoffman, the question is
whether Jorkins could have laid venue and gotten IPJ over Foolim' in SDWV.
These two questions are the same thing.  In a diversity case involving a
corporate defendant, 1391(a) says venue lies in a district where a
substantial part of the events occurred (in the Foolim' lawsuit, this would
be NY or NJ, contract language was even drafted in NY, but not WV, so this
is out), or a district where the defendant "resides," with "resides" for a
corporation meaning subject to IPJ (1391(c)).  Because venue would have
been proper in NY, 1391(a)(3) is out.[2]
      So, could a SDWV federal court have exercised IPJ over Foolim in the
Jorkins lawsuit.  The SDWV court would piggyback on the state long-arm
under Rule 4(k)(1)(A).  Because WV's long-arm goes to the limits of the
constitution, so, minimum contacts, International Shoe.  General or
specific IPJ?  We don't have enough facts to adjudicate general IPJ (maybe
more discovery here?), but this is rarely available outside a corporation's
home state (either one).  Foolim's business in WV would have to be so
pervasive that it would have to be fair for a suit arising from facts in
Alaska or Hawaii to be brought in WV; hard to picture.
      Specific IPJ in WV over Foolim in the Jorkins lawsuit?  Unlikely.
Yes, Foolim does some business in WV, but these contacts are unrelated to
the Jorkins litigation (the only relationship is that Foolim is allegedly
fooling WV borrowers in the same way as it is fooling NY and NJ borrowers)
and thus count for less.  Meanwhile, the Jorkins-Foolim lawsuit had no
other contact to WV.  Jorkins doesn't want to litigate in WV, so it's not
like the plaintiff's interest are being protected here.  True, there is an
interest in the part of the interstate judicial system in having Jorkins
and Vance's claims adjudicated together, but this alone is rarely enough.
Meanwhile, it's hard to see much of an argument for fair play, substantial
justice, etc.  In the Jorkins lawsuit, the plaintiff wants to litigated in
the defendant's back yard.
      Should either the Vance or the Jorkins classes be certified?  For
Vance, a preliminary question is whether FRCP 23 or WVCP 23 applies; for
Jorkins, the issue is whether NY's prohibition on class actions applies
(which it would if state law applies because in Jorkins' case the putative
class definition seeks recovery of civil penalties; Jorkins would not have
the $75K AIC met without the statutory penalty).  Both are Erie questions.
Note that if state law applies, WV law would apply to the Vance case
(parties whole relationship was there), and NY law would apply to Jorkins
(Klaxon says transferor choice of law rules, Babcock would say state with
most significant relationship is NY).
      What Erie approach?  We know better than to apply the York outcome-
determinative test literally; but it actually doesn't seem as though the
class certification decision would be outcome-determinative anyway, except
perhaps on a death knell theory, which was rejected in Coopers & Lybrand;
so York might actually point to federal law here (which it usually
doesn't).  What about a focus on primary conduct, per Harlan in Hanna?
Particularly in the NY case, this is very powerful.  NY's stated purpose
for its class action prohibition (avoiding the stifling of business
innovation) was intensely substantive, related explicitly to primary
conduct, and appears to be part and parcel of NY's decision to create
statutory penalties in the first place.  NY was explicitly worried about
over-enforcement of penalties that it created, and the use of the class
action device would thwart that strong state policy.  Under Harlan's
approach, it doesn't matter whether FRCP 23 is explicitly on point; it
should yield to state law here.  As far as the question tells us, WV hasn't
said why it wants class actions to go forward there, but the reasons are
likely to be bound up with the enforcement of WV law, and thus the same
reasoning might apply.  (In the alternative, one might argue that WV's
failure to specify a reason for its class action rule means that we
shouldn't take those interests very seriously, see Byrd and the Gasperini
balancing test below.)
      Under the approach of the majorities in Hanna and Stewart, it seems
as though FRCP 23 is directly on point, and that the clash between state
law (either one) and federal law is hard to avoid.  Because FRCP is
arguably procedural and thus validly enacted under the Rules Enabling Act,
it seems as though it would apply, regardless of state law.  We never get
to twin aims, and it's hard to see how we could adopt a narrowing
construction of FRCP 23 to preserve the state law.  True, the spins on FRCP
23 versus WVCP 23 are judge-made, not in the identical texts of the rules
themselves, but RCPs mean what judges say they mean (not what their texts
"say" in some metaphysical sense), so this should not matter.
      For similar reasons, it's hard to see how we could accommodate both
state and federal interest through some construction of FRCP 23 and either
NY or WV, as Ginsburg tried to do in Gasperini.  So, if per Gasperini we
tried to balance the interests here, which comes out stronger?  It doesn't
seem as though FRCP 23 is designed to further substantive policies; it's
just convenience and efficiency in adjudication.  Nothing like the 7th
Amendment (Byrd, Gasperni) is in the picture here, and class actions don't
feel like a distinctive feature of the federal courts.  Meanwhile, NY's
interest feels strong; not so sure about WV's, but might be something
similar.  If we're to balance, seems like state law would win, at least in
the NY case.
      Now, I'll suppose that FRCP 23 applies.  If so, note that under
Amchem, the fact of the Vance settlement matters little to the 23(a) and
23(b)(3) prerequisites.  Under 23(a), we need numerosity, commonality,
typicality, and representativeness; for 23(b), this has to be a (b)(3)
(Foolim's large cash reserves mean no limited fund argument, which wouldn't
work anyway, and there's no injunction in the picture), so we need
predominance (which supplants commonality) and superiority.  On numerosity,
it's not 100% clear that the Vance case would make it; 35 plaintiffs isn't
all that many, although the plaintiffs are dispersed throughout the
country.  Jorkins probably has a large enough number. On typicality and
representativeness, Vance is a member of the class she seeks to represent,
at least with respect to the WV "version" of the Mortgage Fraud statute.
True, there is also the NY version of this statute, and Vance isn't a
member of the class of persons who can collect a civil penalty, and under
Shutts, we'll probably have to use the law of the state in which the real
estate is located for each transaction.  But there are only two versions of
state law here (the WV and NY versions), and it's not as though the
availability of the civil penalty creates a conflict of interests among the
class members (cf Amchem).  Note that that under all versions of the
statute, the statutorily created tort provides the exclusive remedy for
mortgage fraud.  The availability of the additional civil penalty relief
simply means that those who can collect civil penalties would be able to do
so, and the civil penalty calculation appears pretty mechanical (one simply
needs a valuation of the house).
      The key issues are predominance and superiority.  Here, the problem
is that the Mortgage Fraud statute by its own terms ("reasonable borrower
standard") contemplates an examination of the individual borrower's
objective circumstances to define the tort.  It is not enough to show a
fraudulent statement; we have to examine each borrower's income stream and
assets, the price and condition of the house, etc.   And we know nothing
about these individual borrowers circumstances because neither Vance nor
Jorkins took discovery on that (see Falcon).  The putative class does have
the language of the Foolim mortgages in common, as well as Foolim's
practice of calculating interest rate adjustments monthly.  But is
certification on this issue alone (which neither party requested) a
superior way to adjudicate, when one will have to go back and look at each
borrower's individual circumstances anyway?  Do those two common issues
"predominate"?  A tough call.
      What if state law applies?  Under NY law, no class action for
Jorkins.  Under WVCP 23, with the presumption in favor of the class, seems
like the tough calls should be construed in favor of certification, so we
may certify here.

Question III (no full answer written)
Part (a):  Major issue in the Jones v. Clinton complaint is (3), the link
between her refusal to engage in a sexual relationship and the adverse
circumstances she suffered.  The allegations regarding the use of Trooper
Ferguson to procure women, the hotel room at the convention, and Clinton's
mentioning of the ultimate head of Jones' department are sufficient to
allege under color of state law.  Jones refusal to engage in sex is clear
from the hotel encounter allegations.  And the complaint does state adverse
employment consequences (failure to achieve promotions, failure to receive
pay raises, etc.); this part isn't that specific as to which jobs, which
promotions, but it probably doesn't have to be, even under Bell
Atlantic/Iqbal.  (Note that Jones was ultimately held not to be able to
prove the adverse employment consequences, but that's not the issue on an a
12(b)(6) motion).
      The problem is (3).  Under Iqbal, we separate the conclusory
statements from the underling factual statements, disregard the former, and
test the latter under a plausibility standard, keeping in mind other
potential explanations.  What facts are alleged in the complaint to make
the causal link between Jones' refusal to engage in a sexual relationship
and the adverse employment actions she alleges she suffered?  There's not
much in the complaint on this.  Clinton did mention "Dave," her ultimate
supervisor.  But there's no allegation of a communication from Clinton to
anyone.  In other words, if Jones proved just the facts alleged in her
complaint, could a reasonably trier of fact find in her favor?  Not clear,
without some nonconclusory allegations specifically going to causation.
Part (b):  Policy, must mention efficiency, trouble plaintiffs have in
alleging prior to discovery; good if mention argument concerning amendment
of FRCP without REA process.
