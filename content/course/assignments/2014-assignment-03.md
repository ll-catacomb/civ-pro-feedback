## Assignment 3, Question

In 1998, the Massachusetts legislature passed (and the governor signed) the "Limitations System Act of 1998." The law provided that in Massachusetts, a two-year limitations period would apply in products liability (tort) actions, but that limitations-based dismissals are not on the merits for claim preclusion purposes. The next year, in 1999, the New Hampshire legislature passed (and the governor signed) the "Litigation Fairness Act of 1999." Two of the provisions of this Act provided that in New Hampshire, a six-year limitations period applies to products liability (tort) actions, but that limitations-based dismissals are on the merits for claim preclusion purposes. The cause of action of "products liability" is a state law claim, not a federal law claim.

New Hampshire's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the Constitution.

Olivanders is a Massachusetts corporation with its principal place of business in Massachusetts. Olivanders makes wands. Ordinarily, the wands are of the finest quality, and magicians everywhere prize them. Olivanders sells wands only in two ways: (i) from its wand shop in Boston, where it does all of its manufacturing; and (ii) through Independent Sellers. Olivanders' relationship with its Independent Sellers is unusual. A person who wants to be an Olivanders Independent Seller must demonstrate, to the satisfaction of Mr. Olivander (the corporation's founder, chief operating officer, and largest shareholder), that she knows how to use and care for wands properly. Then, the would-be Independent Seller signs a contract with Olivanders specifying that she is an independent contractor making her own decisions as to where to sell, how to advertise, and to whom to sell. Olivanders then hires a private investigator to conduct a background check. If the Independent Seller clears the background check, then on any particular day, at 7 am, she may pick up no more than five wands from Olivanders' Boston location. Each wand has with it a removable tag specifying that it is an Olivanders product along with the minimum price that Olivanders will accept for the wand. An Independent Seller must return any wands still in her possession by 7 pm the same day to the Olivanders'

<!-- page: 3 -->

Boston shop. For any wands not returned, the Seller must pay Olivanders the price on the removable tag.

Luna Lovegood was an Olivanders Independent Seller. On March 3, 2012, she picked up five wands at 7 am. As she left, she told Mr. Olivander, "I've been having great luck selling wands in New Hampshire. I'm going there today, and I hope to make lots of sales!" Mr. Olivanders replied, "Yes, I encourage you to head to New Hampshire. I've always thought that I'd sell more wands if my Independent Sellers were just willing to cross the border north." Luna drove from Boston to Concord, New Hampshire, where she sold one of the wands to Pansy Parkinson, a domiciliary of New Hampshire. That very afternoon, Pansy attempted to use her new wand at her job for the New Hampshire Ministry of Magic. But during the first spell she tried, the wand blew up on her, causing her severe injury that required a four-week stay in St. Mungo's Hospital for Magical Maladies and Injuries. Her hospital bill was approximately $800,000.00.

On April 29, 2014, Pansy filed a products liability action against Olivanders in Massachusetts State Court. She served Olivanders with process the same day. On May 14, 2014, Olivanders moved to dismiss, claiming that the lawsuit was barred by the Massachusetts statute of limitations. The Massachusetts State Court granted the motion to dismiss on July 7, 2014 and dismissed the case.

On August 5, 2014, Pansy sued Olivanders (the company, not Mr. Olivander) in a products liability action in the United States District Court for the District of New Hampshire. She served Olivanders with process the same day by delivering a copy of the lawsuit to Olivanders' Boston headquarters (hand-delivered to Mr. Olivander himself) and by mailing a copy to Olivanders' Boston address. Filed simultaneously with the federal court complaint was a motion by Pansy to transfer the case to the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts. On August 13, 2014, before Olivanders had filed anything in the federal court suit, the federal court issued a short order as follows: "In the interests of justice, and given that the wand was manufactured in Boston, this case is transferred to the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts." The file was transferred to the U.S. federal court in Boston the same day.

On August 22, Olivanders filed a motion to dismiss or, in the alternative, for summary judgment. In the motion, Olivanders made three arguments: (1) that this lawsuit, too, should be dismissed as barred by limitations; (2) that the doctrine of claim preclusion should compel dismissal of this lawsuit; and (3) that the doctrine of issue preclusion applied, under which the case has to be dismissed because Pansy had already litigated the issue of whether the lawsuit was filed too late, and she could not litigate it again.

You are a law clerk for the Boston federal judge. The judge asks you for a memo addressing two things:

First, are any of Olivanders' three arguments well-founded?

Second, regardless of what the law actually is, *should* the law be such that this case be dismissed on any of the three grounds Olivanders argues? Note: this is a "should" question. I am interested in your views about what the law should be given the policies and principles we have discussed in class.
