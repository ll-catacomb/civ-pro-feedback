Summary judgment is appropriate when the evidence shows "there is no genuine issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law." Fed. R. Civ. P. 56(c). An appeal of Judge Wright's ruling may succeed, particularly since summary judgment is reviewed de novo.

## Quid Pro Quo Sexual Harassment Claim

Jones' strongest argument is that Judge Wright failed to acknowledge the severity of the diminution of her job duties, and that the diminution constitutes a "tangible job detriment." Judge Wright emphasizes that "an alteration of job responsibilities" is insufficient; yet Jones made clear at her deposition that her responsibilities were not altered, they were obliterated: "And I didn't have any work to do. My work had been gone. I was sitting there doing nothing." (Pl.'s Depo. at 14.) The maintenance of Jones' pay cannot hide her job's substantive transformation, and Judge Wright's emphasis that Jones performed data entry before and after the incident (Opinion, p. 159-60) demonstrates that the substantive nature of work is legally significant.

An appeal on these grounds is bolstered by Jones' claim that she was officially demoted. Jones contends that upon returning from maternity leave her job title had changed: "I was not the purchasing agent anymore." (Pl.'s Depo. at 14.) Jones argued that she "might have kept the salary, but I was demoted." Id. at 25. A dispute between two pieces of evidence – in this case Jones' testimony and the AIDC's employment records – renders summary judgment inappropriate. Likewise, Jones' claim that she went down "a grade" in salary (Id. at 7) conflicts with employment records. Although Jones later came close to contradicting herself by saying she "might have kept the [same] salary" after returning from maternity leave (Id. at 14), an appeal could still emphasize this discrepancy between deposition and records. Although an appellate judge may deem that no rational trier of fact would believe uncertain testimony over objective documents, an appeal could stress that facts should be viewed in a light most favorable to the non-moving party in summary judgment proceedings.

That job applications were discouraged is less persuasive. Although Jones claims she applied for jobs and was rebuffed, that testimony was imprecise: she could remember neither the specific jobs applied for nor the specific instances that she discussed her applications with Ms. Pennington. (Id. at 5.) Nonetheless, viewed together with the diminution of work responsibility, there seems to be a decent case that there remain genuine issues of material fact.

## Hostile Workplace Environment

Jones' case on this issue is strong. First, granting summary judgment is questionable considering the use of a "totality of circumstances" test. In contrast to the "tangible job detriment" test for the sexual harassment claim, the totality of the circumstances test carries more leeway. Since there is room in the joints, Judge Wright's decision that no rational trier of fact could find for the plaintiff seems troubling.

Although Judge Wright declares "[n]o single factor is determinative," her opinion concentrates on Jones' job upgrade and salary increase. (Opinion, p. 161.) While these elements should be considered, a wise appeal would argue that this evidence must not overshadow Jones' perceptions of hostility. Nor should Jones' continuation at work or lack of psychological consultation (Id.) be used against her. Doing so would place victims in the impossible position of choosing between risking their jobs by making their frailty apparent, or sacrificing their hostile workplace claim in order to ensure job stability.

Ultimately, the highly interpretive nature of this claim makes an appeal strong. One troubling issue for Jones, though, is proving causation. Perhaps members of the AIDC made Jones' workplace hostile, but demonstrating that those conditions were the result of unwelcome sexual harassment is difficult. The defense suggests an alternative source (Jones' incompetence) for any hostility by highlighting instances where peers criticized her work. (Pl.'s Depo. at 17-18.) In attempting to overcome this hurdle, an appeal should draw attention to Mr. Ferguson's comment that revealed his knowledge of Jones' boyfriend's name. (Id. at 45.) There is little explanation for Ferguson having this knowledge unless some form of retaliation or enduring surveillance against Jones was at play.

## Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

Like the totality of the circumstances test, the central issue in IIED cases – deciding if conduct was "utterly intolerable in a civilized community" – seems best suited for jury determination. Furthermore, the court argues that Jones' actions "do not portray someone who experienced emotional distress," but granting summary judgment because of a lack of outward symptoms or because Jones "never missed a day of work following the alleged incident" puts an unfair burden on IIED claimants. (Opinion, p. 163.) The trial court may have unfairly dismissed Dr. Carnes' testimony about Jones' condition. Considering that evidence should be viewed in a light most favorable to the nonmoving party in summary judgment proceedings, the decision to discredit Carnes should be challenged. Even if the President's conduct was not sufficient to qualify as assault (Opinion, P. 163, Para. 1.), the question still remains as to whether the conduct was outrageous.
