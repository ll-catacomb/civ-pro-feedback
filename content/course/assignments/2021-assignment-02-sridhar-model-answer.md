## Q1

**SMJ** exists under § 1332 — Canadian plaintiff vs. ND defendant, $300K > $75K. **There is PJ** — D is at home in ND (principal place of business, incorporated there, 4(k)(1)(A) empowers the federal court). **Proper venue** under § 1391(b)(1) (company is subject to PJ there). We are left with three arguments, all with *Erie* implications.

### Forum non conveniens

*York*

Definitely outcome-determinative. If we use state FNC law, dismissal (events occurred in SD and Canada); if we use federal law, have to continue analysis, with likely no better forum than ND (where the wrongdoing that the complaint focuses on occurred). **State law prevails.**

*Hanna* majority

Federal FNC law is common law. Ask if using state law would prevent forum shopping/inequitable administration of justice. Here, it would — a lawyer representing a foreign national wanting to litigate in ND, if AIC >$75K (thus diversity jurisdiction), would have to file in federal court, but couldn't in state court. The analysis is the same under Scalia and Ginsburg versions. **State law prevails.**

*Harlan concurrence*

No reason to think that FNC affects primary conduct. Drug manufacturers likely to behave the same way with or without FNC; even if they can't be sued in ND by foreign nationals, they can be sued by domestic plaintiffs, and they likely make the same drugs for all. **Federal law prevails.**

### Attaching bills

*York*

Outcome-determinative. Under state rule we dismiss, under federal rule (FRCP 8(a)) (which does not mention medical bills) we proceed. **State law prevails.**

*Hanna* majority (Scalia)

FRCP, so ask if valid and applicable. Under Rules Enabling Act, FRCP 8(a) doesn't "abridge, enlarge, or modify substantive right." It truly does govern procedure (*Sibbach*). FRCP 8(a) is applicable here — states all that is necessary for claim of relief. **Federal law prevails.**

*Ginsburg (Shady)*

Ginsburg tells us construe FRCP with an eye to protect state interests. Here, could argue that there isn't actually a conflict between FRCP 8(a) and the ND statute — FRCP 8(a) is inapplicable because it sets a floor for pleadings and ND adds onto it. Ginsburg tells us that **state law prevails** here.

*Harlan concurrence*

Doesn't affect primary conduct at all — this is a question of what gets attached to a complaint; no effect on incentives to behave a certain way. **Federal law prevails.**

### Statute of limitations

*York*

Outcome-determinative. Under state rule we dismiss (has been at least 26 months since action that caused injury), under federal law we continue (22 months since discovered injury). **Use state law.**

*Hanna* majority (Scalia)

Statute is valid under Congress' Article III power to create — and thus regulate — the lower federal courts. It is directly applicable here — defines statute of limitations tolling for all suits filed in federal district court. **Use federal law.**

*Ginsburg (Shady)*

Ginsburg would have us construe federal law narrowly to try and avoid a conflict with state law — but unlike with the FRCP conflict, basically impossible to avoid conflict here. **Use federal law.**

*Harlan concurrence*

Again, probably little effect on primary conduct. Perhaps companies will hide defects to make injury take longer? But unlikely that SOL affects either companies or consumers. **Use federal law.**

## Q2

The three issues deal with different types of federal law: common law (FNC), a FRCP (attachment) and a federal statute (commencement of the action). Under *Hanna* majority, we treat these differently (don't ask if common law is valid & applicable); also, Ginsburg's test only applies to FRCPs and statutes. Moreover, as described above, there's actually an argument (if not that persuasive) for primary conduct influence for the SOL issue, and none for the other two issues.

## Q3

Yes — in particular, with the statute of limitations issue, we run the risk of a confusing 'hybrid' if we apply the federal SOL running rule. (We'd have a SOL determined by state law but when it begins to run is determined by federal law.) Could end up in an undesired outcome — maybe ND legislature would've drafted a shorter SOL if they'd known SOL would run in federal district court cases from the time of discovering injury. Maybe the best recourse is having the legislature revise its SOL statute to be cognizant of this possibility (e.g. '2 year SOL in state court and 1 year SOL in federal court') — put state and fed court litigants effectively on par.

## Q4

*Hanna* majority is the dominant test, so if that prevails on the statute of limitations issue, we use federal law and end up with an inequitable administration of justice, which *Erie* seeks to prevent. The fortuity of diversity ends up mattering, since out-of-state plaintiffs have longer to decide whether to sue than in-state plaintiffs. Plaintiffs under these statutes will be individuals, but nonetheless, we worry about a *Black and White Taxicab* type situation in which plaintiff is incentivized to artificially change domicile to take advantage of a better ruleset — real forum-shopping concern. There's a separation-of-powers concern with judicially brokered compromise — judge, rather than either state legislature or Congress, decides to mix the federal SOL running rule with state SOL period. Also a potential federalism issue — federal government usurps state power by implementing the federal running rule. No real Rules of Decision Act issue — statute of limitation is definitely procedural (though it may signal the state's view on the substantive merits of such suits.) Finally, from a general prudential standpoint, it is unwise to create 'Frankenlaw' — the federal running rules and state statute of limitations were not designed to coexist.
