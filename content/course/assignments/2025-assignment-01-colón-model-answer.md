## SMJ

Connor could attempt to dismiss under 12(b)(1) for lack of SMJ. Courts have SMJ over a declaratory judgment if the court would have SMJ over a suit by Connor (imaginary lawsuit rule — § 2201). There is no AUJ under § 1331 because negligence is a state law claim (*Mottley*). Because there is only one claim, there is also no § 1367 S-SMJ.

There is likely no D-SMJ in a suit against Reese and Skynet. Connor is an AZ domiciliary given she was born and raised in AZ, has an AZ license, parents are still in AZ, and she only relocated temporarily for college (*Mas*). Reese is also likely an AZ domiciliary — he relocated to AZ, bought a house there, and planned on staying for the rest of his life, so there is no complete diversity (*Strawbridge*). Although Connor would have complete diversity against Skynet in a separate lawsuit (Skynet is incorporated/PPoB in Mexico — "52nd state"), AIC is insufficient ($60k < $75k) as to both defendants.

The court *may* have jurisdiction through *Smith-Grable* because Connor planned on using section T-800 of the IPA, a federal statute, to prove breach of the duty of care (embedded federal issue in state law claim). The IPA (T-800) is arguably a substantial federal issue (evidenced by Congress's findings on dangers to the public) and disputed by the parties (whether Reese violated the IPA/his drunkenness). However, the dispute here is purely factual (*Empire*) and allowing a T-800 violation serve as negligence per se to support AUJ would likely lead to too many cases being swept into federal court (*Merrill Dow*). The fact that Congress expressly barred individuals from suing under the IPA likely supports this point. *Smith-Grable* is thus likely unavailable.

## IPJ

Both CO and AZ have long-arm statutes extending to the limits of the Constitution — 4(k)(1)(a).

There is likely no GIPJ over Connor in CO. If *Daimler*'s "at home" rule applies to humans (open question), then it's possible Connor meets the substantial and continuous contacts threshold. Connor has been in CO for 3+ years and currently spends almost 100% of her time in CO. If not, then there maybe are minimal contacts for SIPJ. Connor contacted Skynet from CO, possibly entered into an oral contract from CO, and she bought a ticket for a flight that she thought would go over CO (because it regularly does go there), even though her particular flight never entered CO. For F&R factors, CO is arguably a convenient location for Connor because she lives there for most of the year (although not domiciled), the witness (Ventura) presumably lives in CO, it is not an inconvenient location for Skynet (it operates flights in CO) nor Reese (same), there's a high interest for CO to adjudicate tort-like conduct that occurs in the state (possibly future flights that do fly over CO). On the other hand, Reese/Skynet can argue that the flight never actually flew over CO and the evidence is in AZ, where the injury occurred. For HCoL, CO is a 1st restatement state so "lex loci delicti" applies (where was the blood spilled? — *Alabama*

*R.R.*) Because the injury occurred in Arizona, AZ's substantive law would apply. However, both CO and AZ have the same negligence statutes so applying either state's negligence law would lead to the same substantive outcome on the merits of the case.

On net, SIPJ is a toss-up given the tenuous minimal contacts between Connor, the forum, and the subject matter of the controversy (and F&R seem to net out).

## SoL

Connor's negligence claim is barred by SoL. The statute of limitations in Colorado is 1 year. For HCoL purposes, statute of limitations is procedural, and a Colorado court will apply the forum state's procedural law (*Klaxon*). Because the events giving rise to the suit occurred in June 2022, and this declaratory judgment suit was filed in September 2023, before any Connor lawsuit was filed, Colorado's SoL bars any negligence suit by Connor against Reese and/or Skynet.
