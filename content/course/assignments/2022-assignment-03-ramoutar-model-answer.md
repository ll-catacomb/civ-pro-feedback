## #1(a)

**IPJ:** Probably not. This is an *in rem* proceeding, the property is the subject of the dispute, and it is located within Massachusetts. In addition, the property owner (Picard) is a MA domiciliary, so there is GIPJ. So even if a literal reading *Schaeffer* applies to *in rem* proceedings, this is likely sufficient contacts to allow jurisdiction. If *Schaeffer* is not that broad, then this *in rem* proceeding is standard.

## #1(b)

There's probably a PDP violation.

Picard could argue that he received insufficient notice. *Mullane*.

a. Email/phone could be insufficient because messages are frequently missed.
b. The email bounced back to the sender. He could argue that more steps should've been taken under *Jones*. The Sheriff did email the Bank, but that is probably insufficient even applying *Dusenbery v. United States* because the Bank is the adverse party.
c. Picard could argue that all *in rem* proceedings on real property should require a notice delivered to the property. The address is readily available. *Mennonite / Tulsa*.

Additionally, he could argue that he had insufficient opportunity to be heard. *Sniadach*.

Factors in Picard's favor include:

a. Picard likely has a property interest in his home.
b. Adverse action (seizure of possession) was taken immediately. *Roller v. Holly*.
c. It likely was not urgent for the property to be seized because homeowners don't usually seek to destroy the property in which they live.
d. A clerk, not a judge approves the pre-judgment seizure.
e. There is no bond requirement and no opportunity to post a counter-bond.
f. Burden of proof: just an affidavit from one party.

Factors in Borg's/County's Favor:

a. The bank owns the title to the home, so it has its own property interest.
b. They needed to repair the home.
c. State has expressed interest in assuring real estate liquidity.
d. Reasonable to believe someone might trash property if they know they're getting evicted.
e. Burden of proof: Bank can easily demonstrate on paper that money hasn't been paid.

1. Private interests involved:

    a. Government has expressed an interest in protecting banks.
    b. Government likely would prefer quick turnaround in litigation.

## #3a

**IPJ:** Probably not an issue. FRCP 4(k)(1)(A) says use state IPJ law. Massachusetts has general jurisdiction over all parties because all parties are citizens of the state. *International Shoe*. Picard was born and raised in Massachusetts. Borg Bank has its PPoB in Boston, MA. *Hertz*. Worf County is located in Massachusetts.

**SMJ:** There is no DJ because all three parties are citizens of Massachusetts. *Strawbridge v. Curtiss*.

Because 1983 is a federal COA, Picard can invoke § 1331 for SMJ against Worf County. *Mottley*. But theft, against Borg, is a state COA.

So, for Borg, he will likely need to rely on § 1367 and FRCP 20. For FRCP 20, we need the same T or O and a common issue. The T&O is the house seizure and sale, and the common issue is whether the procedures the bank and county used are constitutional. Because we have the same T&O, we have the same case or controversy under § 1367(a). § 1367(b) does not apply because the anchoring 1983 claim is AUJ.

A district court likely would not decline to exercise jurisdiction under § 1367(c)/*Gibbs*, because there probably is not a complex issue of state law (just tort claim), and damages against Borg does not substantially dominate over the constitutional questions towards the county.

**Venue:** Likely not an issue because all parties are citizens of Massachusetts, and Massachusetts only has one district court. Additionally, all of the facts described occurred in Massachusetts.
