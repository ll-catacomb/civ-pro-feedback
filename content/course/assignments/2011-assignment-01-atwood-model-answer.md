The judge will remand the case back to state court.

The judge will grant a motion to remand the case back to South Carolina state court because there is definitely a procedural violation and probably a lack of federal jurisdiction. There is a procedural violation because not all defendants agreed to removal. Thus if a timely remand motion is filed, it will be granted. Because a remand motion must be filed within 30 days, and 3 weeks have already passed, the motion should be filed ASAP.

The case could probably also be remanded on the ground that there is no federal jurisdiction. Diversity jurisdiction likely doesn't exist. Libatius, as a corporation, is domiciled in Delaware because this is its place of incorporation. Its second domiciliary is its principle place of business. This is likely Georgia because its decision makers and directors sit there, making Georgia its decision making "nerve center". The boys could claim they are domiciled in South Carolina, and thus trigger diversity jurisdiction. Although attending school out of state does not usually trigger a change in domiciliary, in this case the boys dropped out of school and are operating a business. This question is a close call, but the judge would probably still find the boys to be citizens of Georgia because they are technically still intending to re-enter school in South Carolina, meaning diversity between the parties does not exist.

Even if the judge found the boys to be domiciliaries of South Carolina, the case should still be remanded back to state court. This is because a case cannot be removed on the basis of diversity if the forum is the home state of the party who removed the case. In this case, if the boys are domiciliaries of South Carolina they cannot remove the case to federal court in their home state. Thus, no matter where the court determines the boys are domiciled they are out of luck for getting the case into federal court on diversity grounds so long as a remand motion is filed within 30 days. Because three weeks have already passed, however, Libatius must file the remand motion within the next week.

Arising under jurisdiction is also likely absent. The *Mottley* well pleaded complaint rule dictates that a federal cause of action be present in the original complaint. Because this is a suit that asks for a declaratory judgment, however, the imaginary lawsuit rule applies and the court will look to the cause of actions present in the coercive action. In this case the coercive action claims are state claims, not federal claims, so arising under jurisdiction is not triggered. The defendants might try to claim that the *Smith*/*Grable* exception is triggered because the case pertains to FDA regulations, an important federal administration. This is likely not enough to trigger this exception, however, because the question at issue involves Libatius' actions, not whether the FDA labeling requirements are sufficient. Thus, the FDA issue does not necessarily need to be raised for the boys to win. Furthermore, FDA regulations, while important, are not a substantial and disputed national issue. Thus, a *Smith*/*Grable* exception claim would fail and the case would probably be remanded to state court.

The suit will be dismissed on statute of limitations grounds.

Libatius will be able to win the statute of limitations argument. Although a choice of law issue may present itself in deciding the merits of the case, in horizontal choice of law issues (like the present dispute) statute of limitations is considered procedural so the South Carolina court will apply its own statute of limitations. The statute of limitations for the suit in South Carolina is two years, and since more than two years has elapsed the court will dismiss the suit.

If the boys had filed the suit themselves in Georgia there would be no statute of limitations problems, so it seems patently unfair that Libatius should be able to seize the forum in a way that deprives the boys of their day in court. This demonstrates the problem with classifying statute of limitations laws as procedural. Statute of limitations is not a small procedural matter like paper size, but rather an important issue that often changes the outcome of cases so it is more aptly characterized as a substantive issue. This is especially essential in the context of declaratory judgment statutes where the defendant can choose the forum. Forum selection has always been a privilege relegated to the plaintiff, so the combination of allowing the defendant to choose the forum and classifying statute of limitations as procedural is particularly dangerous.
