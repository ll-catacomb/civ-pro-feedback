All states relevant to this assignment have a tort cause of action called "negligence." The elements of a negligence cause of action are that the defendant owes a duty of ordinary care to the plaintiff, that the defendant breached that duty by failing to act with ordinary care under the circumstances, and that the breach caused plaintiff to suffer quantifiable damages (such as pain and suffering). All persons (and corporations) owe all other persons (and corporations) a duty of ordinary care in regular day-to-day interactions; courts assume that this duty exists at all times, and parties do not need to articulate the existence of this duty in pleadings. All states relevant to this exam follow the doctrine of respondeat superior, meaning that a plaintiff may sue a company for damages caused by the acts of the company's employees within the scope of their employment.

All states relevant to this assignment have long-arms statutes that extend to constitutional limits.

Pennsylvania's state Rule of Civil Procedure 8 is identical in language to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 8, except Pennsylvania's state rule adds an additional subsection (f). The Pennsylvania legislature added subsection (f) via statute in 2015. In the preamble to the statute that added the subsection, the legislature stated, "The Legislature finds that medical malpractice lawsuits are imposing undue insurance costs in this state. Some of those costs come from plaintiffs who file frivolous claims for damages due to pain and suffering. Some of those costs come from defendants who dispute obviously meritorious claims for damages due to pain and suffering. The high malpractice costs are making it more difficult for Pennsylvania to attract qualified physicians to the state. The legislature seeks to respond to this problem with Rule 8(f)."

**Pa. R. Civ. P. 8(f)**

> (1) In any suit alleging a tort of personal injury (such as negligence) and demanding compensation for pain and suffering, the plaintiff shall attach to the complaint an affidavit from a competent licensed physician detailing under penalty of perjury that the affiant has personally examined the plaintiff, and that the amount demanded is plausible given the plaintiff's injuries. The Court shall sua sponte promptly dismiss any personal injury tort lawsuit that does not comply with this requirement.
>
> (2) In any suit covered by subsection (1) of this section, upon plaintiff's compliance with subsection (1), a defendant shall attach to the first pleading or motion (including a motion to dismiss) an affidavit of a duly licensed physician. The affiant shall swear under penalty of perjury that they are available at an appropriate time to examine the plaintiff and to offer subsequently testimony on behalf of the defendant as to the veracity of the plaintiff's pain and suffering demand. If a defendant fails to comply at the time of the filing of the answer or motion to dismiss with this subsection, the court shall upon a plaintiff's motion issue an order prohibiting

Pennsylvania has three federal districts, the Eastern, Middle, and Western. Harrisburg, PA, is in the Middle District. Philadelphia, PA, is in the Eastern District. South Carolina, Delaware, and Maryland each have a federal single district.

The complaint was served the same day by first class mail and hand delivery to Daario's corporate headquarters in Baltimore, MD. Baratheon & Martell has had extensive experience and success litigating cases in Pennsylvania state court and prefers to litigate all its cases there. It believes that it has its best chance of success in any case in Pennsylvania state court. If it cannot litigate cases in Pennsylvania state court, it would prefer Pennsylvania federal court to a court outside the state. The complaint read as follows.

1. Plaintiff Podrick Payne is a citizen of South Carolina.
2. Defendant Daario Naharis, Inc. is a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in Baltimore, MD. Its business was the procurement and sale of exotic plants.
3. Plaintiff was a connoisseur of rare flowers. He has dedicated his life to the collection, study, purchase, and sale of exotic flowering plants. He maintains a small estate with a greenhouse in his home in Columbia, SC. He traveled widely in pursuit of his passion.

**Count 1: Negligence**

14. Defendant and its employees owed a duty of ordinary care to Plaintiff. Stark or some other employee of Defendant failed to act with ordinary care when they placed, not the Frey plant that Plaintiff ordered, but an orchid, in the box for delivery to plaintiff's house. Defendant did so with knowledge of Plaintiff's severe allergy to orchids. This failure caused Plaintiff to be exposed to an orchid, which in turn caused Plaintiff to go into shock, to suffer a severe and painful reaction, and to be forced to pay extensive medical bills.
