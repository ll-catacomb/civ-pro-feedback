## Question 1

The only judgment that has been rendered is that of the District of Delaware ("DoD"). All subsequent litigation occurs in state courts. We are therefore confronted with questions of intersystem preclusion where the rendering court was federal and the subsequent courts state. *Semtek* is inapposite because DoD was not exercising its diversity jurisdiction. The subsequent state courts must afford DoD's judgment at least as much preclusive effect as would DoD. *Hart*. However, because those courts are in states the preclusion laws of which are identical to federal common law, they will afford DoD's judgment precisely as much preclusive effect as would DoD.

The parties are identical throughout (Bagman was an unnamed class member because no one opted out).

### Gringotts — Pettigrew

Gringotts will almost surely estop Pettigrew from relitigating causation. DoD's judgment addressed the very issue of causation Pettigrew's counterclaim raises. Causation for Pettigrew's individual case was actually litigated during the three-week trial, and Snape's conclusion that causation was wanting was necessary to the judgment because it was the reason Snape found against Pettigrew.

Gringotts would likely claim preclude Pettigrew, too. DoD's final judgment resolved a claim arising from the same transaction or occurrence ("T/O") from which Pettigrew's fraud-in-the-inducement ("inducement") claim arises. Furthermore, DoD litigated the factual merits of Pettigrew's claim because DIPA and inducement are almost identical.

Pettigrew's motion for summary judgment ("MSJ") is based only on claim preclusion because the issue of missed payments was not litigated in DoD. Whether Gringotts is claim precluded depends on whether its loan collection claim was compulsory under Rule 13(a) because Pettigrew was the named plaintiff in the class

<!-- page: 2 -->

action. That question turns on whether the claims arise from the same T/O, a difficult question here. Indeed, although whether Gringotts misled Pettigrew into assuming the loans involves evidence different from whether Pettigrew missed a payment, one could view the entire loan process as one transaction, in which case Gringotts' counterclaim would be compulsory.

### Gringotts — Bagman

Gringotts is unlikely to estop Bagman from relitigating causation because whether Gringotts' caused *Bagman* to assume a loan was not actually litigated in DoD.

However, Gringotts will likely claim preclude both of Bagman's counterclaims. Bagman's DIPA counterclaim is claim precluded because DoD's final judgment resolved the merits of that very claim. Bagman's inducement claim will also likely be claim precluded. Indeed, Bagman's inducement claim arises from the same T/O as his DIPA claim. Moreover, although DoD did not litigate the factual merits of Bagman's inducement claim, the claim is unquestionably the type we would want Bagman to litigate alongside his DIPA claim.

## Question 2

It seems unjust to preclude Bagman's DIPA claim with a judgment that denied relief to the class because there was no bank-wide policy of misleading prospective investors. After all, Gringotts might have misled Bagman. It also seems unjust to preclude his inducement claim if he could not have litigated it in the class action. Accordingly, Gringotts' MSJ should be denied because, for reasons I will explain, Bagman likely could not have litigated his individual claims in the class action.

Bagman likely could not have litigated his individual claims in the class action. An unnamed class member cannot litigate a claim the class doesn't assert unless the unnamed class member intervenes. But Bagman almost surely cannot intervene under Rule 24(a) because his inducement claim does not relate "the subject of the [class] action" and because Pettigrew "adequately represent[ed]" his DIPA claim (adequate representation is a prerequisite to class certification under Rule 23(a)(4)). Nor can I imagine a court granting intervention under Rule 24(b). Furthermore, Bagman's

<!-- page: 3 -->

inducement claim would likely violate Rules 23(a)(2) (commonality) and 23(a)(3) (typicality) because his inducement claim would not be representative of and would involve a question of law different from the class. Bagman's inducement claim would also likely violate Rule 23(b)(3) (predominance).

If Bagman's plight is unconvincing, consider this example. Suppose an unnamed class member participates in a class action under Title VII against an employer for race discrimination in its *hiring* practices. The court finds otherwise and denies relief to the class. An unnamed class member files suit against the employer under 42 U.S.C. § 1981, alleging she was not *promoted* because of her race. If claim preclusion bars Bagman's inducement claim, then it would almost surely bar the § 1981 lawsuit. *But see Cooper*, 467 U.S. 867 (1984). I find that result untenable.

I therefore propose the following rule, which limits claim preclusion's breadth in the class action context:

**IF** claim preclusion is
1. based on a judgment *rendered in a class action*;
2. asserted against the claim of a party *all the members of whom were unnamed class members* in the class action; and
3. *the named plaintiffs* in the class action *did not litigate the claim* against which claim preclusion is asserted;

**THEN** the claim against which claim preclusion is asserted is the "same" as the claim that was litigated in the class action only if the members of the party whose against which claim preclusion is asserted could have asserted their claim as intervenors in the class action.

Neither Bagman nor my unnamed class member would be precluded under this rule.

The unnamed class members should consider suing Antioch for malpractice because proving causation for this class is extremely difficult, and failure to do so has extinguished their inducement and DIPA claims (the latter of which would be more likely to succeed if litigated by individual plaintiffs).
