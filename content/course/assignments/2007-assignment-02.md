You are an associate in a large law firm. Your (meaning your supervising partner's) client is Charming Lovely Art Posters, Inc. ("CLAP"), incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Boston. CLAP is a startup company attempting to establish itself in the business of manufacturing and distributing (via its Boston store) fine quality, poster-sized reproductions of famous paintings. On July 15, 2007, your client signed a contract with Pablo Picasso's grandson, Harry Picasso, who is a citizen of (and is domiciled in) Massachusetts. The contract provided that your client would pay Mr. Picasso the sum of $20,000.00, and in return, Mr. Picasso would grant CLAP the exclusive right to make and sell poster-sized reproductions of his recent painting, "Eric Cartman in Guernica, Reclining." The relevant portion of the contract provision read: "I, Harry Picasso hereby grant a license of the copyright to my work, 'Eric Cartman in Guernica, Reclining,' to CLAP. In so doing, I intend to grant CLAP (and no others) the exclusive right to produce posters of the image in this painting." Your client informs you that this work of art has become well-known in the New England area, so much so that Yale Law School has devoted a course to analysis of it.

CLAP proceeded to obtain several high-quality photographs of the painting and, in August-September, produced approximately 200 poster-pictures of the work (the cost of this production was approximately $5,000.00). In addition, at the cost of about $10,000.00, CLAP opened a temporary location in Connecticut to sell its posters, including the Picasso work (it has already shipped its products to the Connecticut location), and it expects to generate substantial revenue (it cannot tell how much) from sales of its other posters from persons coming into its store to buy "Eric Cartman.." CLAP plans to sell the "Eric Cartman" posters for $300.00 each. In late September, however, just before starting to sell the poster-pictures, CLAP learned that Mr. Picasso was about to sign a contract with another company called Strange Products And Tastes, Inc. ("SPAT"), incorporated in Maine with its principal place of business in Connecticut. SPAT has only ever sold products or maintained offices in Connecticut. The SPAT deal, if it went through, would allow SPAT to produce posters of above-named painting for sale in SPAT's Connecticut stores.

Your supervising partner wrote a letter to Mr. Picasso protesting this planned second deal. The reply came in the form of a letter from Mr. Picasso's attorney, stating that the July 15 contract granted CLAP the right to sell "Eric Cartman" posters only in Massachusetts, leaving Mr. Picasso free to grant a license for Connecticut sales to SPAT. The attorney further stated that if CLAP attempted to sell "Eric Cartman" posters in Connecticut, Mr. Picasso would sue CLAP for copyright infringement under 17 U.S.C. §§ 502, 504, requesting damages and an injunction under those provisions.

Your supervising partner is contemplating a suit against Mr. Picasso and SPAT for a declaratory judgment allowing CLAP to proceed with sales of the "Eric Cartman" posters in Connecticut. The partner would also like to sue SPAT for tortuous interference with contractual relations under Massachusetts law. Finally, the partner is contemplating a request for a preliminary injunction preventing the consummation of the contemplated Picasso-SPAT deal. Because Mr. Picasso recently gave a large donation to the Massachusetts State Judges Retirement Fund, and because the partner believes that a Connecticut court would be hostile to what it might see as an attempt to limit competition in that state, the partner is only interested in a lawsuit in Massachusetts federal court.

Your client requests that you provide the following:

1. A brief memo (or an outline) of your client's chances of obtaining a preliminary injunction against Mr. Picasso and/or SPAT

2. A draft complaint (a SHORT plain statement, along the lines of the Forms) against Mr. Picasso and SPAT

---

**Word limits:**
- Memo or outline: 750 words
- Complaint: 450 words

**Time limit:** 210 minutes (from the time of downloading the question)

NOTE: You may assume that if your client can prevail against Mr. Picasso, it would also prevail on the substantive merits of its claim against SPAT. In other words, do not spend space in your memo or outline on the substantive, tort-law elements of a tortuous interference claim. Further, all you need to know about 17 U.S.C. §§ 502, 504 is that they provide a cause of action for damages and an injunction for violation of copyright law. You will not receive any credit for researching or knowing anything additional about copyright law.
