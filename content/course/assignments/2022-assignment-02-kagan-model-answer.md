### Subject-Matter Jurisdiction

Buddy can move to dismiss for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction.

**AUJ:** There is no arising under jurisdiction. There is no federal COA in the well-pleaded complaint (*Mottley*). The *Smith-Grable* exception looks more plausible: there is likely an embedded, substantial, and disputed issue in the complaint (the issue of how to minimize harms from drunk drone operators, of federal interest according to the DDDA). However, the federal issue at stake is not very significant, sending every issue of drunk drone driving to federal court could sweep too many cases into the federal courts, and this is an issue of fact and not law.

**Diversity:** There is also likely no diversity jurisdiction (1332). The AIC is $100k (> $75k). However, Buddy lived all his life in RI, so he is domiciled there. Though Bob left RI at 18 to go to college in MA, he does not have plans to stay (he and his wife plan to move to FL). Temporarily relocating for school without intent to stay doesn't change domicile (*Mas*) so likely domiciled in RI. True, he has an MA drivers' license, votes in MA, and inherited a condo there, but still likely not sufficient to change domicile. So Buddy and Bob are likely both domiciled in RI, therefore there is no complete diversity.

### Personal Jurisdiction

Buddy is unlikely to prevail on a motion to dismiss for lack of personal jurisdiction. Though there is no general PJ (Bob is not at home, since he is domiciled in RI, and there is no consent), there is specific personal jurisdiction. MA long-arm goes to limits of the constitution, (4k1a, federal court use's state's long-arm statute). Next, there are minimal contacts between the defendant, the subject-matter of the litigation, and the forum because Buddy purposefully availed himself of MA laws by entering the state to visit a friend, and the accident occurred in MA. Fair and reasonableness factors support PJ: though it's unclear how often Buddy comes into MA, witnesses and hospital bills are in MA, the state has interest in protecting its students and minimizing its hospital's expenses, and MA law will likely apply.

**Venue:** Venue is proper under 1391b2 (substantial parts of events occurred in MA).

### Complaint

Buddy can move to dismiss under FRCP 12b6, failure to state a claim. There are three elements of negligence, and line 3 of the complaint shows ii and iii (breach caused harm and that it was quantifiable). However, under plausibility pleading (*Twiqbal*), lines 4 and 5 are conclusory and should be struck. Without them, Bob has not alleged the first element of negligence moves from possible to plausible, either under simple negligence or negligence per se.

To survive a 12b6 motion, the complaint should have mentioned that the CPSC, operating under authority from DDDA, prohibits operating a drone with blood-alcohol content above .08, specifically to decrease threats to health and safety from drunk drone operators. A police report found that Buddy had a blood-alcohol level of .15, which means both that he likely operated the drone erroneously and that he violated a law designed to protect people like Bob.

MARCP 8 and 12 are identical to FRCP, but don't follow *Iqbal*; this complaint would be dismissed under federal law but not state. VCOL / Erie question. Four tests:

1. Under *York* outcome-determinative test, this is outcome determinative (the case would be dismissed if using federal law), so use **state** law.
2. Under *Hanna* Majority Scalia, validity of a federal rule is enabled by Rules Enabling Act, which says Federal Rule cannot "abridge, enlarge, or modify any substantive right"; *Sibbach* says the test is if the FRCP "really regulates procedure." This rule regulates procedure because it has to do with filing the complaint, so it is *valid*. The rule is straightforwardly *applicable* in this case because they govern motions to dismiss for failure to state a claim, so use **federal**.
3. *Hanna* Majority Ginsburg: validity is the same as with Scalia; for applicability, state interest is apparent (deliberately construed the rules differently to allow for more cases to survive motion to dismiss); however difficult to construe narrowly (*Walker*, *Shady Grove*) because normally the FRCP is floor and state RCP is ceiling — here state and federal are incompatible. Likely **federal**.
4. Harlan concurrence: state likely was worried about pre-litigation conduct (would suits be brought at all if likely to be dismissed), however unclear how much this actually impacts pre-litigation conduct in reality. Unclear, might lean **state**.
