### Background Facts and Law

*(Note: all of the following is fictional.)*

An issue that sometimes arises in the context of limitations periods is when the limitations clock begins to "accrue" or tick. In other words, suppose that a statute of limitations for a certain kind of tort is __ years. At what point in time does the __-year period begin to run? Broadly speaking, there are two general approaches to this question: the "misconduct rule" and the "discovery rule."

- If the **"misconduct" rule** applies, the applicable statute of limitations begins to run on the date that the act or omission giving rise to the claim occurs, assuming of course that some harm eventually results (or else there would be no tort). In other words, under the "misconduct rule," the limitations period begins to run on the date that the defendant commits the relevant misconduct — the date upon which the act or omission occurs — not when the plaintiff discovers the fact that an injury/harm has occurred.

- If the **"discovery rule"** applies, in contrast, the limitations period begins to run instead on the date that the claimant first knows about, or first should have known about, the act or omission giving rise to the claim. In other words, under the "discovery rule," the limitations period begins to run on the date that the plaintiff first discovers, or should have discovered, that the defendant committed some kind of misconduct. This might be when some hidden injury or disease first manifests itself.

To illustrate the difference between the "misconduct rule" and the "discovery rule": Suppose a patient visits the doctor on January 2, 1996. The doctor tells the patient that she is healthy. In fact, a non-negligent doctor would have diagnosed the patient with a serious illness. The patient does not find out that she in fact has been suffering all along from a serious illness until she visits a second doctor on May 4, 1999. Under the "misconduct rule," the applicable limitations period began to run on January 2, 1996. Under the "discovery rule," the applicable limitations period might not begin to run until May 4, 1999.

Federal courts, as a matter of federal common law, have long used the discovery rule. The states of Maine and Nebraska use the misconduct rule.

The Nebraska limitations period for suits alleging negligent driving of a motor vehicle is two years. The Maine limitations period for suits alleging negligent driving of a motor vehicle is three years. Both states have long-arm statutes that go to the limits of the U.S. Constitution.

Both Nebraska and Maine have rules of civil procedure identical to the federal rules, and their state high courts have handed down opinions interpreting their state rules as requiring plaintiffs' complaints to meet the plausibility pleading test identified in recent United States Supreme Court case law. Under both states' laws, the elements of the tort of negligence are that the defendant owed a duty of due care to the plaintiff, that the defendant breached that duty by failing to act with due care, and that the breach caused injury suffered by the plaintiff.

In 2011, Congress passed the "Federal Limitations Period Act of 2011" (the "FLPA"). In relevant part, the FLPA provides as follows:

> In any action in a federal court in which the plaintiff alleges damages resulting from the negligent operation of a motor vehicle, the applicable limitations period shall be one year.
