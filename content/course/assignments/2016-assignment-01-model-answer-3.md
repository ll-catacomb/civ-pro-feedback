## Question 1

We need to determine whether the SC federal court has SMJ over the claims.

Because this is a declaratory judgment action, we analyze SMJ for the Imaginary Lawsuit that would likely have been brought by Helga but for Gringotts' declaratory judgment action.

#### Does the SC fed court have diversity SMJ?

If Gringotts and Helga are diverse parties and the AIC (amount in controversy) is met, then the SC fed court has diversity SMJ over Gringotts' declaratory judgment motion. The AIC is met because, though neither of Helga's claims exceeds $75,000 (and thus neither could be asserted by itself as a diversity claim), they may be aggregated for the purposes of a SMJ AIC calculation. Aggregated, the claims equal $85,000, which is greater than the required $75,000.

Gringotts is a citizen of NC by the *Hertz* nerve center test (because board meetings are held there). Gringotts is also a citizen of Delaware because that is the state in which it is incorporated. Therefore, the SC federal court has diversity jurisdiction if Helga is not a citizen of NC or Delaware.

Helga's domiciliary status is ambiguous. Helga was born in NC, where her family lived, so is a citizen of NC for the purposes of diversity SMJ unless her citizenship has since changed. Helga lives in a mobile home now and seems to have few personal and sentimental connections to NC remaining. Further, she attends school in SC, and though university attendance typically does not alter one's citizenship, perhaps here it evinces a decision or intention to live in SC permanently. Against this is the fact that, for the majority of her studies, she lived in NC.

Eventually, however, Helga did explicitly intend to change residence to SC, and was searching for a house to buy. She was living out of her car, which she began parking in SC. Is this enough to change her place of residence? It is unclear — she intends to stay if she successfully closes on the SC house, but if the house doesn't close she might go back to parking the car in NC, indicating that she was only temporarily residing in SC.

#### Does the SC fed court have AUJ?

Assuming the SC federal court does not have diversity jurisdiction over the claims, can it still hear them on the basis of AUJ?

The slip-and-fall claim is a classic state law tort and thus it can only be heard in federal court if it is heard supplemental to an anchoring claim. The title-passage claim is ostensibly another classic state law property claim. However, just like in *Grable*, the title passes

pursuant to a federal statute, and so it is likely the *Smith-Grable* exception kicks in because the state claim has an embedded federal question, resulting in AUJ.

Note that *Smith-Grable* requires that granting AUJ won't upset the balance of powers between state and federal courts, and it's hard to tell how this housing statute will affect that balance. Further, this is a very situation-specific issue that will likely draw on the circumstances surrounding Helga's finances and her aunt, though ruling on the constitutionality of the czar's order is a more general issue of law. Finally, this is likely a circumstance where federal courts would worry about quality of state adjudication — the federal courts likely value interstate uniformity in EHMA interpretation. Therefore, the *Smith-Grable* factors, on balance, point to AUJ.

Finally, for § 1367 supplemental jurisdiction, the slip-and-fall claim must arise out of the same case or controversy as the title-passage claim. These claims are causally linked, and the same parties are involved, however the connection between the two claims is tenuous. If they are part of the same case or controversy, and none of the discretionary 1367(c) factors are met, the fed court has supplemental jurisdiction over the tort claim.

### Question 2

We must perform a choice-of-law analysis in both forums to determine likelihood of success on the merits. Because a slip-and-fall claim is a substantive issue, we must look to the restatement each forum abides by.

*If the litigation is brought in NC:* NC is a 2nd restatement state, so we must look to state interests and the "center of gravity" of the case. Because the tort occurred in SC, the house Helga was looking at was in SC, and Helga might even be domiciled in SC, SC likely has the most significant relationship with the case. Further, SC has a strong interest in protecting its citizens from dangerous puddles and spills within its borders. Therefore, SC law likely applies. Therefore, "recklessness" would be the legal standard and the declaratory judgment action would likely succeed. If for some reason, perhaps because the court finds Helga an NC resident, the court chooses to apply NC law, "negligence" would be the legal standard. In that case the action is more likely to fail.

*If the litigation is brought in SC:* SC is a 1st restatement state and so because the harm occurred in SC, SC law will apply. This means "recklessness" is the legal standard, which is certainly not met. Hence the declaratory judgment action is likely to succeed.
