## Question 1:

### In Rem Jurisdiction (IRJ)

Pugsley can argue that there is improper exercise of IRJ, though the ME statute authorizes it.

Under the narrow interpretation of *Shaffer*, the court can exercise IRJ if the property is located in Maine. NFTs are incorporeal objects like debt (*Harris*), and there are multiple states where it could be located. Under a functional approach, the court may locate it at its owner's domicile. But here, it is unclear if the owner is Lurch (DE, ME) or Pugsley (MA). There is also an argument to locate it in ND, where the code necessary for it to exist is stored. In the interest of stability, the court could choose to locate it in DE, where Lurch is incorporated, since Pugsley could have easily ascertained the state of Lurch's incorporation.

Under the broad interpretation of *Shaffer*, *International Shoe* requires IPJ with Pugsley. Pugsley is domiciled in MA, so ME cannot exercise GIPJ over him unless there is consent, waiver, or in-state tag (which didn't occur here). ME has a long-arm, and SIPJ requires minimal contacts between Pugsley, the subject of the litigation, and ME. There is no evidence that Pugsley knew Lurch operated the business from ME, and likely did not expect ME law to apply. But the facts are similar to *McGee* (contract with a ME citizen, suit about that contract), it likely controls, and the court may therefore find minimal contacts met. Nevertheless, IPJ over Pugsley is likely not fair and reasonable under *McGee* because he has no relationship with ME and did not expect to be hauled into court there, although ME does have an interest in protecting its citizens from unpaid debts.

### PDP

Pugsley is likely to succeed in raising PDP concerns on this pre-hearing seizure under USPL compared to a post-hearing seizure (*Mathews*).

The private interests for both parties are relatively high. Lurch has an ownership interest in the NFT, but subsistence is not at issue (like in *Goldberg*). Pugsley also has an ownership (equity) interest in the property since he has already completed four payments.

The risk of erroneous deprivation is high because this is a pre-hearing seizure, which requires special circumstances (*Doehr*), and there appears to be no reason for the rush to a seizure (Lurch could simply sue Pugsley in personam and use a seizure post-judgment). No judge (or any court) is required to authorize the seizure, so there is no live testimony and no litigation bonds.

Finally, the government has an interest in accuracy and low costs, both of which would be better served by requiring a pre-seizure hearing.

## Question 2:

The Due Process Clauses of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments to the Constitution and FRCP 4 both address the issue of proper notice. The Due Process Clause, as interpreted in *Mullane*, mandates that notice must be "reasonably calculated" to inform the defendant of the action and give them an opportunity to respond (*Mullane*). FRCP 4 responds to this requirement of notice by outlining specific procedures for service of process to properly notify defendants of lawsuits. FRCP 4 goes above the constitutional requirement of notice because it requires the defendant be served with a copy of the complaint alongside the summons via certain methods, none of which include electronic communication.

To properly serve a defendant (individual or corporation), FRCP 4(e) and 4(h) requirements are more stringent than the broad constitutional requirement for reasonably calculated notice. For example, they do not allow electronic service of process via e-mail, even though e-mail has become a primary method of communication and may even be faster and more reliable than mail. The plaintiff might have to expend additional resources to discover the defendant's physical address even if their e-mail address is more readily available, creating tension with the constitutional principle that no "heroic efforts" are required by the plaintiff to serve proper notice.

Additionally, FRCP 4 can be read as not fully encompassing the constitutional requirements for notice, because it contains no language about the plaintiff's continuing duty as outlined in *Jones v. Flowers*. In that case, the Supreme Court held that the plaintiff owes a continuing duty to notify the defendant if they know that their first attempt at notice failed, but FRCP 4 does not address this issue.

FRCP 4 can be amended to better align with the constitutional requirement for notice, such as by including language about the plaintiff's continuing duty to notify the defendant if they know that their initial attempt failed. To better respond to evolving methods of communication, FRCP 4 can also be amended to allow electronic service of process, although this might be problematic for certain defendants (e.g., older). These changes require balancing the interest of not unduly burdening the plaintiff with the due process interests of the defendant to be properly informed and given an opportunity to respond to lawsuits.
