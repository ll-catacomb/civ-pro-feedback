## Q1

**H1:** If state law applies, the case should be dismissed. If federal law applies, the motion to dismiss should fail.
**H2:** If state law applies, a preliminary injunction should issue. If federal law applies, it shouldn't.
**H3:** If state law applies, the case is dismissible for being filed outside the statute of limitations. If federal law applies, the case isn't time-barred.

Under *York*, state law should govern in H1 and H3 because the choice of law is outcome-determinative. H2's preliminary injunction, formally, isn't outcome-determinative — instead, affecting the litigants' rights before final adjudication on the merits. However, because the court found significant risk of irreparable harm to the movant and *York* sought to avoid "substantially different result[s]" in state and federal court, the preliminary injunction decision does seem outcome-determinative of harm to the movant. Thus, state law should arguably apply.

Under the *Hanna* majority test, federal law applies if valid and applicable. Federal courts have never found the FRCP invalid under the REA or Constitution. And because FRCP 11 and 65 could be considered to "really regulate procedure" under *Sibbach*, they are both valid. (This includes H2's judicial interpretation of FRCP 65.) The federal statute in H3 is also valid because it was passed via bicameralism and presentment and is constitutional under Congress' power to create the lower federal courts and the Necessary and Proper Clause. Here, Scalia and Ginsburg's interpretations of the test diverge. If Scalia finds federal law applicable to the situation at hand (which is likely), he believes federal law should govern. Ginsburg would identify a substantive policy being advanced by state law and, if possible, construe the federal law to avoid a conflict.

Harlan's *Hanna* concurrence considers whether the choice of law would affect parties' primary (pre-litigation) conduct. If so, state law is substantive and should apply. If not, the law is procedural and federal law applies.

**H1**

*Scalia:* FRCP 11 is applicable because it addresses complaint signature requirements, which this dispute hinges on. Federal law controls.

*Harlan:* SC's signature requirement governs a litigation document so doesn't affect parties' primary conduct (except possibly in choosing a meticulous lawyer). Federal law applies.

*Ginsburg:* Ginsburg may have difficulty finding a substantive policy goal in SCRCP 11. Two possibilities could be an inducement to prepare complaints with care or a desire to prevent fraudulent legal filings. Both are fairly weak. That said, with such a finding, Ginsburg could easily construe the FRCP 11 mandate as a floor (not a ceiling), thus avoiding a conflict with SCRCP 11 and allowing SC's stiffer requirement to govern.

**H2**

*Scalia:* FRCP 65 (as judicially interpreted) directly applies to the issuance of preliminary injunctions. Federal law controls.

*Harlan:* Nothing about preliminary injunctions affects parties' pre-litigation behavior. Federal law applies.

*Ginsburg:* A clear state policy goal is being permissive/flexible in granting preliminary injunctions. However, it would be hard for Ginsburg to narrowly interpret FRCP 65 as inapplicable. FRCP 65 will probably apply.

**H3**

*Scalia:* The statute is applicable because it speaks to all diversity civil actions. The federal statute of limitations will govern.

*Harlan:* The applicable statute of limitations generally won't affect primary conduct except for sophisticated parties like insurers. If that's enough, the state statute of limitations controls. Otherwise, the federal statute of limitations controls.

*Ginsburg:* There are clear state policies implicated by a statute of limitations: desire to put conflict to rest, limit the specter of liability, and compel timely discovery. Though a stretch, Ginsburg — prompted by federalism concerns — may read the federal statute of limitations as applicable only absent a pre-established limitations period for a given cause of action, interpreting away conflict with federal law. Accordingly, the state statute of limitations would govern.

## Q2

The four *Erie* tests are insufficiently flexible to address variations in the magnitude of state/federal interests implicated. In H1, a court might find (contrary, apparently, to the judgment of state lawmakers) that requiring multiple signatures provides minimal benefit compared to one signature. However, H2's differing standards for the issuance of preliminary injunctions could lead to stark differences in substantive outcomes for litigants. Despite this, H1 and H2 are subject to the same valid-applicable analysis for FRCP in Scalia and Ginsburg's versions of the *Hanna* majority test. Ginsburg's framework is more flexible because it wrestles with state policy interests and federalism, yet in H2, it seems especially difficult for her to interpret away conflict between state and federal law, despite a far more state-interest-encumbering result when federal law applies in H2 compared with H1. Given that *Erie* aimed to avoid forum-shopping and "inequitable administration of the laws," it seems problematic that choice of federal or state forum of adjudication would so drastically affect the rights of litigants.

The *York* outcome-determinative test is inflexible in its own way, almost always favoring state law. It thus needlessly prevents uniform procedure in diversity cases, even when the ultimate results are negligible.

Harlan's test comes closest to taking into account the gravity of state concerns, worrying about effects on primary conduct, an area generally left for the states to regulate. However, it still precludes federal overreach outside the realm of primary conduct. In H3, for example, when the federal government broadly preempts all state statutes of limitations in diversity cases, it gives rise to serious federalism concerns.
