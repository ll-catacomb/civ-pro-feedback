## Question

On July 1, 2008, due to an inability to raise operating capital on faltering credit markets, the multilayered entities that previously ran the "Walmart" national chain of superstores in the United States sold all of their assets (including the stores and the "Walmart" brand name) to a single corporation, MegaShop, Inc. MegaShop was incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in New York. MegaShop executives decided to keep the Walmart brand name and all of its stores, the operations of which remained essentially unchanged. Indeed, industry observers commented that consumers "never knew the difference."

On July 15, Ms. Padma Patil, a resident and citizen of South Carolina, was traveling in her car on the way to see her sister, Parvati, who lived in Great Falls, Virginia, right next to the Maryland/Virginia border. On her way through North Carolina, Padma stopped at a Walmart store and bought a tacky lamp as a present for her sister. The shade of the lamp pictured beach scenes of Georgia, both Carolinas, Virginia, and Maryland. When Padma arrived at her sister's house and plugged in the gift, the lamp exploded, causing serious injuries to both Patils. Both were hospitalized in a Maryland hospital, and each ended up incurring over $90,000.00 in medical expenses.

You are an associate in the law firm who represents the Patils. The partner in charge of the case did some research on jury pools in various states. The partner decided that the most sympathetic jury pool was likely to be in South Carolina, and so on September 2, 2008, suit was filed (naming both Patils as plaintiffs and MegaShop, Inc. as the sole defendant) in a United States District Court for the District of South Carolina (South Carolina has only one federal district). The complaint alleged that MegaShop, Inc. negligently designed and manufactured the lamp, and that it also was strictly liable in tort for the Patils' injuries.

On September 17, MegaShop moved to dismiss the action for lack of venue. The partner has asked you for a memo analyzing two related issues:

- The legal standards applicable to MegaShop's motion, as well as how the facts currently known relate to those standards; and
- The additional facts needed to defend against the motion. On this latter point, the partner asks you to discuss BRIEFLY (two paragraphs at absolute most) what a discovery plan to find out these additional facts might look like, identifying what discovery tools might be most useful in this regard.

Note: Assume that South Carolina's long-arm statute provides that its trial courts may exercise personal jurisdiction in any case when such exercise is consistent with the United States Constitution.
