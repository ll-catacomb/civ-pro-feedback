## The Law

***Respondeat superior***: All states relevant to this assignment follow the doctrine of respondeat superior, meaning that an employer/company can be held liable for the acts or omissions of its employees performed during the discharge of their duties.

***Long-arms***: All states relevant to this assignment have long-arm statutes going to constitutional limits.

***Limitations***: In Colorado the statute of limitations for negligence lawsuits is one year from the events that gave rise to the lawsuit. In Arizona, the negligence limitations period is two years.

***HCOL Restatements***: Colorado follows the First Restatement for Horizontal Choice of Law, Arizona the Second Restatement.

***Negligence cause of action***: All states relevant to this assignment have a cause of action called

<!-- page: 3 -->

negligence. The elements of the negligence cause of action are

- the existence of a duty from the defendant to the plaintiff (all persons have a duty to exercise due care in their interactions with other persons),
- the breach of that duty through the defendant's failure to exercise ordinary care under the circumstances,
- the breach caused the plaintiff an injury, and
- the injury to the plaintiff resulted in quantifiable damages.

A plaintiff may prove a negligence cause of action in two ways, first by alleging and proving that the defendant's actions or inactions fell below the common understanding of ordinary care, and second that the defendant violated a statute or agency rule (federal or state) enacted to protect persons like the plaintiff (if you've taken torts, this latter theory is called "negligence 'per se.'")

***Congressional statute***: In early 2021, Congress passed the Intoxicated Pilots Act. Section T-800 of the act provided as follows:

> The Congress finds that intoxicated airplane pilots pose a danger to passengers, to people on the ground in airports and elsewhere, and to property. It shall be unlawful for any airline engaged in the business of transporting passengers to allow pilots to fly while intoxicated. The United States Department of Justice, but not any private individual, may sue any airline that fails to comply with this prohibition, and in such a suit, the court shall impose a civil penalty in an amount necessary to deter future violations.

## The Events

***The Grand Canyon Tour***: In April of 2022, dialing from her college apartment in Boulder, CO, Sarah Connor called Skynet's booking number, hoping to find a trip she could take in June. She reserved a seat on Skynet's two-seater airplane for the "Grand Canyon Tour" in early June. The Grand Canyon tour's flight plan consisted of taking off from Nogales, Mexico; traveling northwest for a two-hour low-altitude flyover of several of the Grand Canyon's most beautiful locations;[^1] following the Colorado River north into Colorado for a 20-minute flight "so low that you'll feel like you're swimming" (in the words of the Skynet telephone salesperson); and then following a straight line back to Nogales. In part because of the amount of fuel required, the price of the "Grand Canyon Tour" was expensive, but Connor's relatives and friends chipped in to make the trip an early graduation present.

In late May of 2022, after taking her last exam, Connor took a bus from Boulder, CO to her childhood home in Phoenix. She spent a couple of weeks relaxing with her family. On the appointed morning, in early June of 2022, she drove her parents' car across the border to Nogales and checked in at Skynet's office.

Connor boarded the Skynet two-seater for her tour. The other passenger was a woman named Ginger Ventura, a student at the University of Denver, in Colorado. Connor and Ventura struck up a conversation, and before takeoff exchanged contact information so as to stay in touch after their adventure. Both passengers were in their seats when pilot Kyle Reese arrived. Connor noticed that he carried a 750 ml bottle of Woodford Reserve Bourbon Whiskey in his left hand as he entered the cockpit. The bottle was full. Connor also noticed that Reese did not lock the door to the closed cockpit.

At first, the flight went off as planned. The ride was smooth, and both Connor and Ventura enjoyed themselves. Toward the end of the time over the Grand Canyon, while the plane was still in Arizona, the plane's path became erratic. The plane's flight went from bad to worse. At that point, the plane's cockpit door banged open, and the Woodford bottle rolled out in the passenger compartment. It was empty.

While still over Arizona, the plane turned around and began climbing. It eventually landed back in Nogales without further incident. Ventura and Reese emerged from the flight with only a few bumps and bruises. Connor was less lucky. She fractured her shoulder and eye socket, requiring painful reconstruction of her face. Her medical bills were about $40,000.
