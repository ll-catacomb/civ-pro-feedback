## Question 1

You could file the complaint in either state or federal court in both Maine and NH. For SMJ, state courts are courts of general jurisdiction and there is DJ for FedCts: There's diversity — AD is domiciled in Maine, therefore he is a citizen of that state, and GW is domiciled in NH since she was born there and never lived anywhere else (*Mas*), therefore she is a citizen of NH; and AIC is good, since we have $150,000 in damages. Venue is properly lain in either district: For D.N.H., § 1391(b)(1) because GW resides in district; for D. Me., § 1391(b)(2) because substantial events occurred there. GW is a citizen of NH, so NH has general IPJ, so a NH state (general jurisdiction) or a NH federal court (FRCP 4(k)(1)(A)) can exercise general IPJ. Maine doesn't have general IPJ but certainly has specific IPJ under minimum contacts test (*International Shoe*): Maine has a long-arm statute that goes to the constitution; for minimal contacts, accident happened in Maine, AD is a citizen of Maine, and the state has an interest in regulating drivers on its road; for traditional notions of fairness, more convenient for AD to litigate, all the evidence is there, NH is close to Maine so not too inconvenient for HP and GW. So a Me. state or federal (FRCP 4(k)(1)(A) court) could exercise IPJ.

AD should sue GW in NH state court because it uses notice pleading, so it's easier to withstand MTD. Even if GW removes to FedCT under § 1446, AD can have it remanded, since GW is a citizen of NH and it is a DJ case. There is at least a strong probability that any federal court would use a plausibility pleading standard under a *Hanna* majority *Erie* analysis, so AD should avoid federal court. And a Me. state court would definitely use plausibility pleading.

## Question 2

**Jurisdiction And Venue**

1. Court has SMJ pursuant to § 1332 since there is diversity of citizenship and AIC is greater than $75,000.
2. Venue is proper under § 1391(b)(2) because substantial portion of events transpired in this district.

**Parties**

3. Plaintiff AD is a citizen of the State of Maine.
4. Defendant GW is a citizen of the State of NH.

**Facts**

5. On March 15, 2018, Plaintiff was crossing the street with his dog while wearing a bright orange reflector shirt so that he would be visible to cars on the road.

<!-- page: 2 -->

6. While crossing, Plaintiff was hit by a car.
7. After colliding with Plaintiff, Defendant got out of the car on the driver's side.
8. Defendant's companion, HP, got out of the passenger side and went to check on Plaintiff, who smelled alcohol on HP's breath, leading Plaintiff to believe they had been drinking.
9. Because she was under the influence, Defendant was unable to detect Plaintiff crossing the street, despite his bright orange reflector vest.
10. Defendant's alcohol-inhibited attention and driving skills led her to hit Plaintiff with the car.
11. In late April 2018, Plaintiff visited his doctor, who detected a life-threatening degeneration of Plaintiff's hip vasculature.
12. According to Plaintiff's doctor, the degeneration was caused by trauma to the area, such as that caused by a car accident.
13. Plaintiff needed surgery to correct the degeneration, incurring $150,000 in expenses.

**Count I: Negligence**

14. Plaintiff incorporates by reference paragraphs 1 through 11.
15. Under the circumstances, Defendant's drinking and driving was inconsistent with ordinary care.
16. Defendant's failure to act with ordinary care led to them hitting Plaintiff, thus causing the accident.
17. The injury that Plaintiff suffered was a result of the accident.

## Question 3

For appellate jurisdiction, go through *Liberty Mutual*. There's no final judgment, so no jurisdiction under § 1291. There's no injunction, so no jurisdiction under § 1292(a)(1). There's no order about which there's substantial ground for difference of opinion on the question, so no double-discretion jurisdiction under § 1292(b). There's only one claim, so no jurisdiction under Rule 54(b). Also, the SJ order is not collateral to the case, so no jurisdiction under *Cohen*. Finally, nothing extraordinary happened, so no mandamus.

The First Circuit should grant AD's motion to dismiss GW's appeal.
