# Assignment 1 – Model Answer 02

The first thing to do is move to remand back to state court, where Alabama law ensures that the forum non conveniens motion will fail. Although the parties are diverse, Diggle is still a resident of Alabama, since there is no evidence that he intends to make Texas his permanent residence. Since the state suit was in Diggle's home state, he had no right to remove to federal court and Pettigrew can move to remand, as long as he does so before June 12, staying within § 1447(c)'s 30-day time limit for non-subject-matter-related defects. Since the parties are diverse and the amount in controversy requirement is met, the federal court has diversity jurisdiction.

Failing that, Pettigrew can argue that the federal court should use the Alabama law on the motion to dismiss. He can use *Walker* to argue that there is no "unavoidable" clash between any federal procedural law and state law, since forum non conveniens is a matter of common law. Since there is no such conflict, *Hanna*'s arguably procedural test does not apply, and the "twin aims of *Erie*" are considered instead: discouraging forum shopping and avoiding inequitable administration of laws. Allowing plaintiffs to dismiss on forum non conveniens grounds in federal courts but not state courts will encourage plaintiffs to ensure their cases are brought into state court, creating inequitable administration of laws and giving defendants who get their cases into federal court an inequitable advantage over defendants who must remain in Alabama state court. Additionally, Pettigrew can use the test of *York* and *Ragan*, which mandates that state law be used if changing to federal law would "substantially affect" any right granted by the state. Dismissing the case from the Alabama courts would affect the right given by Alabama to sue for negligence, since moving the case to Mexico, with its different system of justice, will certainly substantially affect the trial.

Diggle could counter that moving the case to Mexico is not really outcome determinative: it would not make the case impossible, in the way that using a shorter statute of limitations would do, since the lawsuit could still continue in Mexico. He could also analogize from *Stewart* and argue that forum non conveniens is a clearly procedural matter that is legitimate federal law under *Erie* because it does not impinge on substantive rights.

Pettigrew can argue in the alternative that a forum non conveniens dismissal is not justified. The one witness and the car are located in the United States. Evidence of medical costs as well as the accuracy of the whiplash diagnosis is in the United States, where Pettigrew was treated. The burden on Pettigrew of going to Mexico is high; although he is a citizen of Mexico, he is currently attending school in Texas. A judgment against Diggle, an American citizen, would be far easier to enforce if it was issued by an American court.

Diggle might counter that it would be easier for Mexico to apply Mexican law than Alabama: since the accident occurred in Mexico, and since the First Restatement of Conflicts of Laws requires that the law of the locus of the injury be used, any trial in Alabama would have to use Mexican law. Any evidence regarding the conditions of the roads where the accident occurred is in Mexico; if the road has since been changed, any witnesses of the prior condition of the roads are likely to be in Mexico. Since a forum non conveniens motion should be dismissed "unless the balance is strongly in favor of the defendant," *Gilbert*, Pettigrew is likely to prevail.
