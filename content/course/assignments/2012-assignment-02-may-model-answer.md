In assessing Megashop's motion to dismiss, the Virginia federal judge must decide whether to apply federal or state law (vertical COL = Erie analysis) and if the latter, which state's law to apply (horizontal COL). The motion to dismiss will be granted if either the federal 2-year SOL, or Virginia's 3-year SOL, applies.

Let's start with the horizontal COL. The Virginia federal court decides whether to use the COL analysis of the South Carolina federal court based on whether the latter is happy or sad. (*Van Deusen*). Is South Carolina a happy court? This is a state-law tort claim that gets subject-matter jurisdiction via diversity: Ps from SC and Virginia and D from Delaware/NY; at $90,000 each the AIC is well over the amount in baseline. The court probably has general personal jurisdiction over Megashop because of its vast presence in all 50 states. (*Perkins*). If we can't get GIPJ, we'll have to go for specific IPJ, which requires both a long-arm statute and minimum contacts to satisfy constitutional due process concerns. (*International Shoe*). We have a state long-arm statute that goes to the limits of the Constitution, so per 4(k)(1)(a), the federal court piggybacks on the state statute. For minimum contacts, we can make a somewhat strained argument that putting into the stream of commerce a lamp targeting South Carolina consumers with images of their state constitutes purposeful availment under the WWV line of cases. Courts sometimes consider P's interest in a convenient forum, although the extent to which this interest weighs heavily varies tremendously (e.g., the plaintiff's convenience didn't seem to matter much in WWV). In any case, we probably have GIPJ. Venue is entirely statutory: per 1391(b)(1), venue is proper anywhere a defendant resides, and per 1391(c)(2), a corporation resides anywhere that personal jurisdiction is proper. Since we have PJ, we have venue.

Because South Carolina is happy, Virginia federal court will apply the horizontal COL rules that the SC federal court would have applied: procedural rules of the forum (SC), and substantive rules of lex loci (Virginia). Because SOLs for horizontal COL are procedural, the Virginia federal court would apply SC's SOL — IF it applies state law at all.

Here we move on to vertical COL. *Erie* tells us that a federal court in a diversity case will apply substantive state law and federal procedural law. But if there is a conflict between the state and federal procedural law, then the federal court must use one of the *Erie* tests to decide which law to apply.

Under *York*, the federal judge should apply state law if the decision to do so would be "outcome determinative." The Patils' case lives or dies based on whether SC or federal SOL applies, so *York* points to applying state law. This outcome seems to speak to the federalism interest some see in *Erie* — that federal laws should not trump state laws in diversity cases; however, it's worth remembering that *York* dealt whether a federal court should apply a state SOL in a situation where there *was* no federal SOL. In other words, for the court in *York* to apply anything other than state SOL would be common law, which is what *Erie* had aimed to stop. Thus, the facts of *York* spoke more clearly to the separation of powers rationale — judges shouldn't make up laws. But if that's the rationale, then in the Patils' case, the presence of a statute means federal law should apply. Similarly, if *Erie* was a prudential case, then the court should apply federal law because Congress' position is clear. In the end, the outcome determinative test in *York* is

dead — any difference between laws will at some level be outcome determinative, so it's useless as a test. We move on to *Hanna* majority.

This is a two-part test: is there a valid, applicable federal statute, rule, or Constitutional provision on point? If so, use it. If not, analyze in light of the twin aims of *Erie* (discouraging forum-shopping and promoting equitable administration of state law). Validity refers to whether Congress had the power to pass the law at issue, and here, Congress would seem to have the power to pass a limitations period pursuant to its power to create the lower federal courts. Re applicability, here it's hard to see any non-risible argument that a federal SOL for tort claims is not somehow applicable vis-à-vis a state SOL for torts. Even in *Walker*, which created the presumption of narrowly construing laws to avoid conflicts, the court had a difference in tolling dates to work with. No such opportunity exists here. So, *Hanna* majority points to applying federal law.

*Hanna* concurrence: the test is whether applying federal instead of state law would affect "primary behavior" of a sort generally regulated by states. It's hard to see how an SOL would affect primary behavior in the same way as *Multicolored Taxi*, where the decision of which law to apply actually promoted incorporating in one state versus another. Since it won't affect primary behavior, apply federal law.

Thus, given that *York* is dead, the motion will likely be granted.
