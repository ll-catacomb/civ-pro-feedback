## Question 1

There are two distinct vertical choice-of-law issues; applying state law in either results in granting the motion to dismiss.

### Limitations Period (SoL): DLPA 2013 (4 years) vs. TX (2 years)

The *York* test favors state law because the SoL would be outcome-determinative: TX law would bar but DLPA would permit the 3-year-old suit.

Harlan's *Hanna* concurrence test favors federal law because private individuals like Patil and Dursley are not changing pre-litigation conduct in any way based on SoL, though sophisticated business entities like insurers might act differently based on perceiving reduced litigation risk.

Scalia's *Shady Grove* plurality test favors federal law. The federal law is applicable: Congress, in plain language, intends to establish SoL covering all diversity actions, in direct conflict with TX law. The federal law is valid: Art. I § 8 of the Constitution expressly grants Congress the power to create lower courts and legislate as necessary and proper to do so.

Ginsburg's *Shady Grove* dissent test favors federal law. SoL might serve important state interests like providing finality in Texan lives and incentivizing quicker dispute resolution. Note, the DLPA is a federal statute, not an FRCP, so Ginsburg might think twice before trying to narrowly construe it. (But see *Gasperini*). However, it seems impossible to interpret out of a conflict in this case: TX SoL is 2 years, and DLPA says the SoL "shall be" 4 years.

### Signature Requirement: FRCP 11(a) (1 page) vs. TXRCP 11(a) (all pages)

The *York* test favors state law because the signature requirement would be outcome-determinative. The suit, with a complaint signed only on the last page, would be non-compliant with TXRCP 11(a) but fine under FRCP 11(a).

Harlan's *Hanna* concurrence test favors federal law because the rules deal exclusively with litigation conduct: the signing of litigation documents.

Scalia's *Shady Grove* plurality test favors federal law. The FRCP is applicable because it would be read to broadly control signature requirements. The FRCP is valid because Congress delegated authority to promulgate FRCP in the REA, and the rule doesn't abridge, enlarge, or modify any obvious substantive right — TX Supreme Court says the TXRCP is for improving complaint accuracy.

Ginsburg's *Shady Grove* dissent test favors state law. Ginsburg would credit the important state interest identified by the TX Supreme Court: more accurate complaints means better litigation and representation for Texans. To avoid finding the rule applicable, she would narrowly construe the FRCP as a minimum requirement — the "must be signed" language requires one signature — that doesn't preclude requiring more. She would thus allow the TXRCP to co-exist, layering it on

as an additional requirement by Texas, like what NJ was allowed to do with bond requirements in *Cohen*.

## Question 2

### Limitations Period

*York* is distinguishable because there was no federal statute. With the DLPA, Congress has spoken clearly on the precise issue, reducing concerns about separation of powers between branches of the federal government (i.e. the judiciary creating common law without congressional approval). Prudential concerns around judge-created common law overriding state law (e.g. with *Erie*) are absent. SoLs do not raise a serious federalism concern. The Constitution expressly grants to Congress the power to "constitute Tribunals inferior to the supreme Court" (Art. I, § 8, cl. 9) and the ability to "make all Laws which shall be necessary and proper" (Art. I, § 8, cl. 18) to execute its enumerated powers. By the Supremacy Clause (Art. VI), these laws trump individual state laws. The strongest constitutional challenge comes from equal protection concerns: a 4-year SoL for all diversity suits, if applied in Texas, favors out-of-state plaintiffs (ones with diversity of citizenship from their defendants) compared to in-state plaintiffs who have otherwise identical negligence tort claims. This encourages forum-shopping and bringing suit in federal court instead of state court. Ultimately, this seems insufficient to outweigh the underlying constitutional authority supporting Congressional authority for DLPA.

### Signature Requirement

With the REA, Congress delegated authority to promulgate the FRCP to the judiciary. The FRCP are a strange blend of statutorily-authorized common law. However, Congress's later refusal to do the same for the Federal Rules of Evidence (passed as legislation with significant changes) suggests an intention to curb the rule-making authority of the judiciary. Accordingly, when faced with conflicting state law, we should treat the FRCP as less authoritative than an actual statute and be more cautious in overriding state law. However, there is a reasonably strong federal interest in a uniform set of procedures in its courts. Uniformity of procedures allows for a common market of professional lawyers (government needs legal staff at DoJ / agencies to handle cases across the country) and makes it easier for repeat players (not only the government, but entities with a national presence e.g. big businesses, labor unions) to participate in the federal court system.

How does this compare to other conflicts between FRCP and state rules / laws? It is possible to follow what the Court did in *Cohen*, narrowly construing the FRCP as a minimum, non-exhaustive requirement and allowing TXRCP 11(a) to apply. However, it's difficult to take the TX Supreme Court's explanation of the reasoning behind TXRCP 11(a) seriously: introducing this additional procedural burden on attorneys is more likely to incentivize them to spend less time on substantively improving the document, or to pass on increased costs to their clients. Ultimately, I

<!-- page: 3 -->

weigh the value of having a uniform set of procedures in federal courts more highly and would uphold the FRCP.
