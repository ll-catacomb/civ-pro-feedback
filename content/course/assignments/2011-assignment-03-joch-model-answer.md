There are two major issues for appeal — those with due process and those with the right to a jury trial.

The first potential due process issue on appeal is the inadequate notice. It is clear that the Boston Five's actions did not send mail that was "reasonably" expected to inform the parties of the suit in a timely manner. *Mullane*. Specifically, when the Boston Five member found the phone number associated with Townsend was disconnected, he likely had a duty to follow up and make additional attempts to find Townsend, especially since the number and address came from the same Yellow Pages source. *Jones v. Flowers*. Since the information was reasonably available on the Townsend web page and would require no "heroic efforts" but rather a simple web search, it was not unreasonable to expect the Five to find the actual current address.

The second due process issue is the adequacy of the procedural weapons available to Bosley and Townsend at the hearing itself. Because Bosley was not able to challenge the Globe article in any way — through objecting to its admission, confronting Secretary Galvin, or compelling production of the letters stating that the under-printing was Townsend's fault — it was unlikely that the hearing actually ensured the protection of any of Townsend's rights. Due process is flexible but in this situation the *Mathews* factors were likely not met. The private interest affected was a sum of money — and potentially a large sum of money from future suits if the Five proved successful (a difficult nonmutual offensive collateral estoppel issue). There was a high risk of error because unsubstantiated statements from a political figure facing public outcry were admitted with no realistic way to challenge them, whereas compulsory appearance of the Secretary and production of letters would have offered Townsend adequate opportunity to rebut his statements. But compulsory appearance is apparently not within the procedural ambit of this agency; that makes things worse in due process terms, not better. The costs would have been relatively low since the hearing was quick and the government has a high stake in a correct outcome since it deals with election law.

The final due process issue concerns time to prepare. It is possible that the amount of time given to prepare was inadequate. *Roller*. Whether 20 days is "reasonable" is arguable.

The second major issue is the denial of a jury trial. The first step in the two-part *Tull*/*Terry* test is resolved squarely on Townsend's side; the "negligence" claim is a common-law tort that would have been brought in law. However, the second part of the test is generally considered the more important of the two, and is less clear. The relief sought has elements both of 18th century law and equity claims. There is a clear legal remedy in the form of compensatory damages. There is, however, equitable relief in the form of the injunction requested. The old "clean-up" rule would likely not apply here since the legal damages seem to dominate and at best the equitable relief is of only equal importance to the legal relief. THE REFERENCE TO THE CLEANUP RULE IS DESIGNED TO HELP YOU FOR EXAM PREPARATION.] Under the *Tull*/*Terry* test a jury trial seems warranted.

It might be countered that this is a new "public right" created by Congress through the USEA and therefore not subject to a jury trial, since no such right existed in 1791. *Atlas Roofing*. Here, the fact that the government is not a party is not dispositive but is certainly to be considered. Ultimately, the power to bring a claim rests in private hands — here, those of the Five — and the claim was ultimately one based on a theory of negligence which was neither statutorily created nor a public right. *Granfinanciera*. Since this is solely a new forum to adjudicate private rights, the *Tull*/*Terry* test must be applied as above (and likely comes out in Townsend's favor).

Because she objected at all stages Bosley preserved her right to appeal all these issues. Additionally, though under the USEA the court cannot review the sufficiency of the evidence to support the ALJ decision, that does not come into play here because the court is analyzing fundamental constitutional and procedural rights rather than the particular sufficiency of evidence.
