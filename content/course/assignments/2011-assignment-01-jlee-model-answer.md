The goals of Libatius (LB) are twofold: (1) remand our declaratory judgment (DJ) suit to South Carolina state court, and (2) succeed on our statute of limitations (SOL) argument. Both goals are eminently achievable.

## Remand of the Declaratory Judgment Suit

Our strongest argument in favor of remand comes in the form of a procedural challenge to the Defendants' removal action. Because Fred Weasley was unreachable during his sojourn in Africa, George Weasley was the only signatory on the removal petition. Removal of the suit was therefore improper because removal requires agreement by all defendants. 28 U.S.C. § 1446(a). Pursuing this argument requires haste, however, because motions to remand on the basis of a defect other than subject-matter jurisdiction (SMJ) must be made within 30 days. 28 U.S.C. § 1447(c).

Moreover, removal of a suit is only proper if the suit could have been brought in federal district court in the first instance. We can therefore challenge removal on the basis that the DJ suit would have lacked SMJ in South Carolina federal district court.

When considering issues of SMJ in a DJ suit, one's analysis should focus on the "imaginary" suit, also known as the coercive action, that the DJ defendant would have brought as a plaintiff (Imaginary Lawsuit Rule). Because the Weasleys threatened a tort suit premised on negligence, the "imaginary" claim would have relied on state law and could not have established "arising under" jurisdiction under Article III and 28 U.S.C. § 1331. This problem cannot be remedied simply by using LB's failure to adhere to an order by a federal regulatory agency (the FDA) as evidence of negligence because the argument does not change the nature of the Weasley's state claims.

There are rare occasions, however, when "arising under" jurisdiction lies over state-claims that implicate significant federal issues. *See* *Smith*; *Grable*. Indeed, the *Grable* court stipulated that a federal court may hear this type of action if (1) the state action implicates a contested and substantial federal issue, and (2) federal adjudication of the matter will not upset the federal-state balance of judicial responsibilities.

The Weasley's "imaginary" suit does not satisfy these requirements. First, their claim concerns a single company's failure to adhere to a specific FDA order. While the harm resulting from LB's actions may be significant (potential for death) and numerous (500 bottles mislabeled), the actual federal interest is quite narrow. Unlike *Grable*, there is no federal issue that must be resolved prior to deciding the state claim, and the suit does not involve a significant, wide-ranging federal question (compare *Grable*, which considered the type of notice required when the IRS seizes private property). The government would have little to no interest in securing a federal forum for this question.

Regarding the second prong, federal adjudication of the Weasley's claim poses a clear threat to the federal-state division of labor. A decision that "arising under" jurisdiction exists in this case could permit any state tort action premised on a private actor's failure to adhere to a federal agency order to be brought in federal court. Given the expansive scope of federal regulation, this could lead to a flood of state claims into the federal judiciary and greatly upset the existing balance.

Alternatively, the Weasleys could argue that their suit possesses diversity jurisdiction, which requires that every plaintiff is diverse from every defendant. *Strawbridge*. Corporations are considered domiciliaries of the states in which they incorporated and of their principal place of business (*Hertz*'s "nerve center" test) — for LB, this means Delaware and Georgia respectively. Determination of a citizen's domicile depends on the state in which the citizen resides plus the intention to remain. The Weasleys will argue that they are domiciliaries of South Carolina given that they reside in the state and have demonstrated an intention to remain via their ownership and operation of Weasley's Wheezes.

However, removal is not permitted when a state court action is brought in the state in which the defendant is domiciled. 28 U.S.C. § 1441(b). Thus, even if the Weasleys successfully argue that they are domiciliaries of South Carolina for the purposes of diversity jurisdiction, removal is still nonetheless precluded by § 1441(b), as the DJ action was brought in South Carolina state court. The Weasleys might respond by arguing that removal is proper because they are, in fact, the plaintiffs of the Imaginary Lawsuit/coercive action; however this argument should not succeed because the § 1441(b) rule is intended to protect the actual defendants of a state court action and not Imaginary/coercive action defendants.

Our argument that the Weasleys' removal action conflicts with § 1441(b), like the procedural argument discussed above, must comply with the 30-day limit of § 1447(c). Thus, we should move to remand as soon as possible.

## Horizontal Choice of Law on SOL

SOLs are considered procedural in nature for horizontal choice of law, and thus the SOL of the forum state should govern. South Carolina's two-year SOL for tort claims will therefore apply in this case, and any tort suit brought by the Weasleys concerning injuries resulting from consumption of Essence of Dittany will be time-barred. This might strike some as patently unfair because the Weasleys had originally threatened to bring suit against LB in Georgia federal court, which has a three-year SOL rule for tort claims, and thus would not have found their claims time-barred. In this way, LB has not only seized the forum, but seized victory as well.
