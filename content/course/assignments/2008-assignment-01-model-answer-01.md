# Assignment 1 – Model Answer 01

First, we should immediately move for remand. Diggle's removal was improper, since Alabama is his home state. § 1441(b). If we make the motion within the 30-day deadline from the removal, the case will be remanded to Alabama state court, which will apply Alabama state law prohibiting forum non conveniens dismissal. Note that under Alabama's First Restatement choice of law rules, the Alabama state court will still apply Mexican tort law.

If a motion to remand is not made before June 11, the case will remain in federal court, which has original jurisdiction over the case. § 1447(c). The plaintiff and defendant are diverse, as their status as students has not made them domiciliaries of Texas, and the amount-in-controversy requirement is met. § 1332. In federal court, the choice of state or federal forum non conveniens rules is an *Erie* question.

In moving for a forum non conveniens dismissal, Diggle is not directly invoking any federal statute (such a dismissal is governed by neither § 1404 nor § 1406), so he cannot apply the rule from *Hanna*, applying federal statutes wherever they are valid and applicable. This leaves a conflict between state law and federal judicial practice, which should be resolved by the line of reasoning from *Erie*, *York*, *Byrd*, and *Hanna* (that *York* should be limited by the "twin aims" of *Erie*). Here, the question of forum is potentially outcome-determinative, as Alabama and Mexico could have different choice of law rules that affect the outcome (Diggle will counter that both are likely to apply Mexican tort law). Moreover, we can argue that use of state law here may well encourage forum-shopping, a factor under *Hanna*. Plaintiffs can choose forums that are unlikely to allow forum non conveniens dismissal (ironically, exactly what we are trying to do in this case). Justice Harlan's concurrence in *Hanna* would disagree, as the law regarding forum non conveniens is unlikely to influence the primary behavior of negligent drivers. Diggle could also argue that, though the decision under review is not a transfer but a dismissal, *Stewart* is still better respected by applying federal law. Though about a statute, it answered fundamentally the same question, and specifically mentioned that forum selection can be considered a procedural matter.

If both remand and application of state law under *Erie* fail, we have to argue the merits of the forum non conveniens dismissal, which should be made only if the balance of equities strongly favors the defendant. *Gulf Oil*. Here, litigation in Alabama is probably not too inconvenient for Diggle, as he's domiciled there. Diggle will argue that litigation in Mexico is not a large burden on Pettigrew, for the same reason, but the symmetry of the situation makes it unlikely that the deference granted to plaintiff's choice of forum would be overturned. Also, Pettigrew currently lives in the US. The evidence is split between the two locales (the car, witnesses, and defendant made it back to the US after the accident, but the allegedly dangerous road is in Mexico). Since Pettigrew's choice of forum appears motivated by legally valid reason and not by forum-shopping (since Alabama will likely apply Mexican tort law anyway), the deference granted to his choice is very high. *Iragorri v. United Technologies*. Diggle may be able to more easily obtain a § 1404 transfer to Texas (venue is good as defendant resides there, § 1391(a)(1), and that's where parties and evidence are currently located), but that would effect no change of law. *Van Dusen*.
