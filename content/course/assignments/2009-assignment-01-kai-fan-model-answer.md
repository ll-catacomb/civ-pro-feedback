# Assignment 1 Model Answer

The best strategy is to have Diggle dismiss the suit for lack of in personam jurisdiction (IPJ). Defendants cannot remove a case to federal court unless the federal court has original jurisdiction (§ 1441). Since Pettigrew filed state law tort claims, the basis of federal jurisdiction has to be diversity. Both Diggle and Pettigrew grew up in SC and attended college in VA, so the court might treat them as citizens of the same state. To ensure complete diversity, Diggle should try to dismiss the suit and leave Weasley as the only defendant. The exercise of IPJ requires minimum contacts (*International Shoe*). One party's unilateral act cannot create minimum contacts between another party and the forum state (*Hanson*; *World-Wide Volkswagen*). While Diggle's car was involved in an accident in Maryland, it was Pettigrew and Weasley who unilaterally drove the car there. Diggle grew up in SC, attended school in VA, and worked in Barbados. He lacks minimum contacts with Maryland, and he will probably succeed in dismissing the suit. Once Diggle is out of the case, Weasley can remove it to Maryland federal court. Federal court has diversity jurisdiction because Weasley is a DC citizen, and Pettigrew is clearly not a DC citizen. In addition, Pettigrew's medical bills were over $100,000, which satisfies the amount in controversy requirement under § 1332.

If Diggle is unable to get out of the lawsuit, it is still possible to remove the case to federal court. To prove complete diversity, Diggle may argue that Pettigrew is a citizen of VA. A person is a citizen of the state that she intends to reside indefinitely (*Mas v. Perry*). Pettigrew attended college and law school in VA, registered to vote there, and obtained a VA driver's license. It is true that Pettigrew planned to move to DC and work for the DOJ. However, this plan is contingent on DOJ's acceptance of Pettigrew's job application. As long as Pettigrew lacks a definite intent to leave VA, he remains a citizen there. Diggle, on the other hand, is not a citizen of VA. He stayed in VA only for college, and moved to Barbados immediately after graduating college. Since his assignment to Barbados will last until further notice, Diggle is probably a citizen of Barbados. Weasley, the other defendant, is a citizen of DC. Thus, the requirement of complete diversity can be satisfied, even if Diggle remains in the case.

Once the case is removed to MD federal court, Weasley and Diggle can try to transfer it to VA federal court. The transferee court has to be a proper venue, and it must have IPJ over defendants (*Hoffman v. Blaski*). The IPJ of VA federal court is determined by VA's long-arm statute (Rule 4(k)(1)(A)). Assuming that VA's long-arm statute reaches constitutional limits, VA federal court probably has IPJ over Diggle. Diggle attended college in VA, so he has substantial contacts with VA. On the other hand, it is unclear whether VA has IPJ over Weasley. Weasley had a relationship with Diggle, a former VA resident. In addition, she drove to VA to pick up Pettigrew. However, there is no analogous case suggesting that such contacts are sufficient for IPJ. Therefore, the court may go either way on this issue. In addition, VA court is probably not the proper venue. Weasley and Diggle do not reside in VA, so § 1391(a)(1) does not apply. They can try to argue that a substantial part of the events occurred in VA, because the trip started in VA (§ 1391(a)(2)). However, the vacation was in MD, and the accident also occurred in MD. Given the lack of proper venue, the attempt to transfer will probably not succeed.

If the case remains in MD state court, Weasley can try to move it to DC by arguing forum non conveniens. The relevant factors for forum non conveniens include, among others, access to evidence, the cost of obtaining attendance of witnesses, local interest in the controversy, and choice of law (*Gilbert*). Based on the First Restatement, the location of injury determines choice of law. Since the accident occurred in MD, MD law applies. However, other factors suggest that DC is the better forum. Most of the evidence is available in DC: the car is in DC, and so are the hospital records. It is also less costly to call witnesses in DC. Ernie, who first discovered the leak, works in DC. The doctors who attended Pettigrew's injuries are also in DC. In addition, MD has no substantial interest in adjudicating the case. Weasley's negligence occurred in VA, and Diggle's occurred in DC. Pettigrew, the victim, is not a MD citizen, and he visited MD as a tourist. Therefore, the lawsuit would constitute an unreasonable burden on MD jurors. The factors weigh in favor of a conclusion of forum non conveniens.
