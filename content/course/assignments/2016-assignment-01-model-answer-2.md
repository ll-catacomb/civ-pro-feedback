## Assignment 1
Civil Procedure, Fall 2016
Professor Jim Greiner

### Question 1(a)

Owing to lack of SMJ, Gringotts cannot sue Helga Hufflepuff (HH) in SC federal court for a federal declaratory judgment (FDJ) stating only that Gringotts owes nothing to HH on the slip-and-fall suit. This suit is purely a state-law claim, and it doesn't present an embedded federal question that is substantial, disputed, and legal (as necessary for AUJ under *Smith/Grable* and *Empire*). Thus, there's no AUJ. Moreover, this suit fails to satisfy the requirements of diversity jurisdiction because the AIC is $20,000. Although this defect is sufficient to bar diversity jurisdiction, the parties' lack of complete diversity (*Strawbridge*) would also probably keep the suit out of federal court. HH is likely a domiciliary of NC, her home state, since college doesn't alter domicile, residence is distinct from domicile (*Mas v. Perry*), and HH hasn't expressed the intention to remain in SC following graduation. (However, it's possible that HH's domicile has changed to SC because she has apparently severed her ties to NC and begun living in SC, where she intends to own a house.) As a corporation, Gringotts is domiciled in its state of incorporation (DE) and — per *Hertz* — its "nerve center," or where decisionmaking and overall control are based (NC).

### Question 1(b)

Gringotts almost certainly can sue HH in SC federal court for a FDJ on the quiet title action alone. This particular action falls under the *Smith/Grable* exception for AUJ. There is a federal question embedded in this state-law claim (whether EHMA violates the Constitution, thus rendering the mortgage czar's orders invalid). The issue is substantial (per Section 1's assertion that EHMA's "provisions . . . are perhaps the most important . . . [ever] passed by Congress") and disputed (per scholarly debate over EHMA's constitutionality). In accordance with *Empire*, this dispute is largely legal, not factual, since the statute's constitutionality is a question of law.

Exercising AUJ here wouldn't impermissibly infringe on state sovereignty because cases involving EHMA — a congressional statute — are of particular interest to federal courts (which are well suited to adjudicate them), and ordinary quiet title actions remain the province of state courts. (NB: There is no diversity jurisdiction for this claim since the AIC is $65,000.)

### Question 1(c)

Per § 1367(c) and *Gibbs*, Gringotts can probably sue HH for FDJs on both claims in one lawsuit. We're dealing with claim joinder, and the anchoring claim (quiet title) has AUJ as its SMJ basis, which simplifies the assessment of supplemental jurisdiction for the slip-and-fall claim. Broadly speaking, the tort claim forms part of the "same case or controversy" as the quiet title action because it occurred in the midst of the parties' disagreement over ownership of #4 Privet Drive. But there isn't much fact-relatedness between these claims, since the quiet title action is largely legal and thus doesn't really share a "common nucleus of operative fact" with the tort claim (*Gibbs*). However, the federal issue predominates (§ 1367(c)(2)), and the tort claim doesn't appear to raise complex or novel issues of state law (§ 1367(c)(1)); furthermore, judicial economy would militate in favor of exercising supplemental jurisdiction because the parties to the dispute are the same (*Gibbs*). Because supplemental jurisdiction isn't a plaintiff's right but rather a doctrine of discretion (*Gibbs*), it's difficult to predict with certainty whether SC federal court would exercise supplemental jurisdiction. Nonetheless, because there don't appear to be "compelling reasons for declining jurisdiction" (§ 1367(c)(4)), SC federal court would probably exercise supplemental jurisdiction. (Alternatively, if HH is a SC domiciliary, then the claims can

### Question 2

Gringotts might well prevail in litigation over the tort claim, although this probability hinges on where the suit is filed. As a threshold matter, the slip-and-fall standard is a substantive issue, meaning that we look to the Restatements for the HCOL analysis. SC uses the 1st Restatement, and its inquiry thus turns on where the wrong blossomed into a tort. NC uses the 2nd Restatement, and therefore its inquiry rests on fuzzier "interest" and "center of gravity" (COG) analyses. SC state law requires recklessness, while NC state law is satisfied by negligence. The facts establish that Gringotts was negligent, but not reckless, regarding the puddle (no one at Gringotts knew about it, but the manager *should* have known).

If the suit were filed in SC, Gringotts would prevail because the "blood" is in SC, meaning that the recklessness standard applies. Since HH cannot show that Gringotts was reckless, Gringotts avoids liability under SC law. However, if HH were to file in NC, then Gringotts might not prevail, since the interest/COG analyses under the 2nd Restatement could result in the application of NC law, which only requires negligence (and which HH can establish). In representing Gringotts, we would argue that SC law should apply because the tort occurred at an office within SC (interest) and evidence/witnesses are likely located in SC (COG). HH would likely argue for an extension of the doctrine in *Babcock* and *Neumeier*, contending that because HH and Gringotts are (arguably) both domiciliaries of NC, state interest and convenience tend toward the application of NC law. It's hard to say how a NC court would rule on this issue, and thus the HCOL outcome is less predictable if the claim is filed in NC.
