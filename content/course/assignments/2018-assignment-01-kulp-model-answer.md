## Question 1

**SMJ:** DJ is possibly not satisfied. AIC = $150,000 (>$75,000) is sufficient. Gringotts is a corporate citizen of both DE (place of incorporation) and NY (PPoB — *Hertz*). However, Spinnet's domicile is unclear. Although entering college normally doesn't trigger a domicile change (*Mas*), in which case Spinnet = DE, Spinnet clearly intended to stay in Texas, even going so far as to throw away her cold-weather clothes. That being said, she hasn't yet acquired the permanent TA job and hasn't purchased the intended condo so her change is not yet definitively permanent. The court could reasonably go either way (Spinnet = DE or TX; if DE, no diversity).

There's no federal COA so likely no AUJ (*Mottley*). Is there a *Smith*-*Grable* exception? TBCIAA embeds a federal question into a state COA. Given the Great Recession, the effective promulgation of TBCIAA is likely of great federal interest. However, fraud in the inducement cases are fact-bound, and accepting *Smith*-*Grable* could sweep many state claims into federal courts and upset the federalism balance for state COA's. Nor does this have anything to do with TBCIAA's constitutionality (*Smith*). It seems that states can handle this effectively so no *Smith*-*Grable* exception.

**IPJ:** There's no general IPJ of Gringotts since only a small portion (one branch) of business in Texas (*Daimler*), but specific IPJ? Federal court "piggy-backs" on Texas long-arm statute that goes to the limits of the constitution (FRCP 4(k)(1)(A)), so we must perform a minimum contacts test (*International Shoe*). The fraudulent Lestrange conversation and contract signing in Texas should be enough to satisfy the minimal contacts portion of the constitutional test for specific IPJ. With regards to fairness and reasonableness, although Spinnet deposited the funds in Louisiana and Greyback's email came from NY, Texas has a strong interest in redressing conduct against its citizens (*McGee*). Besides, the email encouraged fraudulent

conduct and was directed to Lestrange in Texas, and funds entered the account which was opened there. Specific IPJ and thus IPJ is not a problem.

**Venue:** Houston branch (the only Texas branch) is in SDTX but the case was filed in NDTX. There's only "district-by-district" IPJ over Gringotts in SDTX (§ 1391(d)) because there's no residency in NDTX and no substantial part of events in NDTX. Because venue is proper elsewhere (*e.g.*, EDLA, see below), § 1391(b)(3) is unavailable. Therefore, venue is improper (§ 1391(b)) and the case should be transferred.

**Transfer:** A case can only be transferred to a court where it could have been brought (*Hoffman*). The case should be transferable to EDLA. Specific IPJ is likely, for: (i) Voldemort runs oil rigs near New Orleans which Gringotts knowingly invested in, resulting in damages, (ii) the money was deposited at a New Orleans branch, and (iii) Lestrange knew it'd be deposited there. It's reasonable that conducting business as such means that Gringotts purposefully availed itself of Louisiana's laws. Venue's probably proper in EDLA, as the aforementioned "substantial part of the events" occurred there (§ 1391(b)(2)).

Transfer will also be proper to NDNY and DDE, as there exists general IPJ (§ 1332(c)(1)) for Gringotts due to its incorporation in DE and its PPoB being in NY (*Hertz*). Furthermore, because the PPoB is in Albany (which is in NDNY), venue under § 1391(b)(1)/§ 1391(d) is proper in NDNY. Delaware has only one judicial district and since IPJ exists in DE, then so too is venue proper.

## Question 2

<!-- page: 3 -->

If transferred to EDLA, Texas COL rules won't follow since Texas court wasn't a "happy court" (lacked venue, see Q1). Use Louisiana COL. Louisiana is a 1st Restatement state, and so the actual realization by Spinnet of the sufferance of damages would determine which law to apply because it completes the tort under Louisiana law. Thus, if damages (loss of funds) were realized by Spinnet while in Louisiana, punitive damages are allowed. If while in Texas, punitive damages are not allowed.

Spinnet received the email in New Orleans on the same day it was sent and thus realized the damages in Louisiana. Therefore, if Spinnet's successful in proving her claim of fraud in the inducement, she will indeed be able to recover punitive damages.
