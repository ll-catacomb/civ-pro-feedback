## 1a.

No. This in-rem suit fine under any jurisdictional interpretation. A literal reading of *Schaffer* requires minimum contacts between defendant, property, and subject matter of the litigation even when suit in-rem. Have that here — Picard domiciled + owns property in MA and the litigation is about failure to make mortgage payments on that property. *Shoe*. Under a narrower reading of *Schaffer* — that the court was offended by DE trying to subject objecting, out-of-state defendant to a limitless judgment even if it exceeded property value in state — leaves traditional IR jurisdiction undisturbed, and MA can exercise jurisdiction because the property in MA.

## 1b.

Likely yes. Picard can challenge prejudgment seizure for lack of opportunity to be heard. To evaluate scheme, consider the *Matthews* factors, which apply to old property. *Doehr*.

### Private Interest

Picard's interest is enormous — this is Picard's only place to live, has life savings in house, and has no income to sure housing until end of September. This may be worst case scenario, and others being foreclosed may have other options (family, friends, savings, steady income to get cheaper housing). It's not exactly like welfare (*Matthews*), but housing still core to survival. Moreover, when the property is seized, Picard can't sell, so the effective market value goes to zero. *Doehr*. But unlike *Doehr*, the bank has pre-existing property interest. They retain legal title and Picard only has $28k in equity ($20k down + 4 x $2k monthly payment), leaving bank with $72k.

### Risk of Erroneous Decision

The alternative is to wait for judgment to seize and evict (which may or may not include an in-person hearing, MBPA § 8(b)). The bank filed a sworn affidavit describing delinquency and intent to sell property, and this is not the kind of case that requires experts and testimony (it's really just, did you pay or not?). *Doehr*. But if the affidavit is defective or not persuasive, there are few safeguards to prevent erroneous transfer of possession because a clerk makes the determination, not a judge. The bank also doesn't have to put up a bond, so there is nothing deterring banks from quickly seeking unwarranted foreclosures against vulnerable, unsophisticated mortgagors.

### Public Interest

Legislature found that inability to quickly foreclose jams up housing market liquidity, which poses a "grave threat to the public interest." A court should maybe defer to that, but not clear why waiting a small amount of time for the judgment will significantly jam up liquidity more. All bank cannot do in the interim is make repairs and prepare the home for sale. The legislature also wanted to prevent homeowner from damaging home, but that is dubious. Why would Picard (or any homeowner) damage the home? The only interest here is impairing ability to recover principal in case of foreclosure, so seems unlikely that such swift eviction necessary for that reason. On balance, OtbH challenge likely succeeds.

## 3a.

Yes. PJ fine in both actions — all defendants MA citizens. 4k1a. Venue fine because MA has only one district and all defendants MA citizens, and all conduct took place in MA. § 1391(b)(1)/(2). 1983 action federal cause of action, so AUJ. Theft action state tort claim with no embedded federal issue (no AUJ) and all parties MA citizens (so no DJ). We need SSMJ. Both suits arise under the same "case or controversy," § 1367(a), because both are related to Picard's failure to pay mortgage and subsequent loss of his property. § 1367(b) does not apply because the anchoring claim is AUJ. The § 1367(c) factors don't support discretionary denial of supplemental jurisdiction. No novel theory of state law implicated, the federal claim dominant, the federal claim has not been dismissed, and nothing else extraordinary is going on. Actions survive.

## 3b.

Yes. The 7A preserves right to jury trial in suits at law. 2 part test: is there a founding-era analog cause of action at law? Is the remedy requested one traditionally granted at law or equity? The second prong is more important. *Chauffeurs*. Not sure about founding-era analogs, so evaluate requested remedy.
