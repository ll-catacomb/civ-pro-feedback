## Q1

L1's rendering court was a Delaware federal court exercising AU jurisdiction. The L2 and L3 subsequent courts must give at least as much preclusive effect to L1's judgment as the rendering court would. It's not clear which jurisdiction's preclusion law applies with a rendering AU federal court and state subsequent courts, but *Parklane Hosiery*'s rule might apply.

### A. Gringotts's L2 MSJ

Gringotts's MSJ in L2 will be granted on CP grounds. Pettigrew's L2 counterclaim is the same claim as that of L1, because both relate to the same transaction/occurrence and the same evidence would be used to prove both. Snape's judgment was final, because no elements of the claim are pending/undecided and ongoing appeals don't influence the judgment's finality. The judgment is on the merits, because Snape decided that an element of the case wasn't satisfied. Finally, L2 meets the *Gonzalez* requirements, because they're literally the same parties.

Gringotts's MSJ will also succeed on IP grounds. The narrowly interpreted issue is whether the false statements caused Pettigrew to take out the loans. This same issue was actually litigated in L1. To decide whether to award statutory penalties in Pettigrew's individual DIPA claim, Snape needed to determine that Pettigrew didn't prove the causation element. Thus, Snape's causation finding was necessary to the decision. The parties satisfy strict mutuality — which is more than Delaware or FCL preclusion law requires — so IP applies.

### B. Pettigrew's L2 MSJ

Pettigrew's MSJ in L2 will be claim precluded under FRCP 13(a). He can argue that, because L2 has a "close and logical relationship" to L1, the claims should have been tried with the same evidence. *Heyward-Robinson*. Furthermore, there would have been no issue with IPJ

<!-- page: 2 -->

under FRCP 13(a)(1)(B), because Pettigrew filed L1 in Delaware federal court. Under FRCP 13(a), Gringotts was obligated to raise the loan collection claim as a compulsory counterclaim in L1, and its failure to raise the claim in the Delaware litigation claim precludes it from doing so now.

Pettigrew's L2 MSJ will fail on IP grounds. Showing that the loan contracts were based on Gringotts's false/misleading statements would be a complete defense to the collection suit, so Pettigrew wants to issue preclude that finding from L1. Although the false/misleading statements issue was raised and actually litigated in L1, it wasn't necessary to Snape's decision in Pettigrew's individual case. His decision was based on Pettigrew's failure to prove causation, so Snape's finding that Pettigrew proved Gringotts made false/misleading statements to him was gratuitous. *Rios*.

### C. Gringotts's L3 MSJ

Gringotts's MSJ will be denied on both CP and IP grounds.

Under the CP analysis, Snape's decision was final and on the merits (see above). Gringotts can argue that Bagman's claim is part of the same very broad transaction/occurrence as was litigated in the L1 class action (meaning the bank's granting of loans nationwide based on the Knut rate from 2009 to 2011). While litigants can interpret "same claim" broadly and manipulate its definition to achieve policy ends, this seems to be going too far, however. Regardless, Gringotts probably cannot show that the L1 class and Bagman are the same party. Privity typically applies to parties in contractual relationships, so under current doctrine, it's unlikely that an unnamed class member would be found to be in privity with a class. The *Marrese* rule (can't preclude someone out of a claim if that person couldn't have brought the claim in L1 for SMJ reasons) doesn't affect the analysis, because CP doesn't apply anyway.

<!-- page: 3 -->

The IP analysis is brief. In the L1 class action, the issue was whether Gringotts had a bankwide policy of misleading customers about the interest rate. Bagman needs to prove that false/misleading statements caused him to take out his loans, which is a different issue from the policy litigated in L1. Thus, no IP applies.

Snape's statement in the record would apply most clearly to Gringotts's MSJ in L3. However, because neither CP nor IP applies, it doesn't change my analysis.

## Q2

I agree with the law as it stands in Parts A and B. To promote judicial economy and efficiency, CP should apply in Part C, as well.

The goals of class actions and preclusion principles are similar: dispose of duplicative legal claims to decrease unnecessary litigation. When L1 is a 23(b)(3) class action — from which potential class members can opt out to preserve future legal claims — it makes sense to apply CP in L2, if a class member tries to sue the same defendant. By choosing to go forward with the class, class members are implicitly giving up their rights to pursue their individual claims. For the law to reflect these policies, "same claim" would have to be interpreted as broadly as possible and the privity doctrine would need to be expanded. An argument for holding class members to be in privity is that by choosing not to opt out, they're entering the equivalent of a "contractual" relationship with the class as a whole.

Judicial efficiency must be balanced against fairness to class members. Snape's statement causes concern, because it shows that class counsel's incompetency in L1 could prevent unnamed class members from ever vindicating their rights. Additionally, joining claims under multiple states' laws can destroy commonality or superiority, which disincentivizes looking out

<!-- page: 4 -->

for individual class members. *Shutts*; *Amchem*. However, FRCP 23's protections for class members outweigh these dangers. Notice and the opt-out are two important safeguards. By providing substantial checks on class counsel's qualifications, FRCP 23(g) is another significant protection. Finally, *Wal-Mart*'s high commonality bar is an additional check on potentially unfair classes.

Because strong protections exist for class members and efficiency is best preserved by dismissing duplicative lawsuits, CP should apply when L1 is a 23(b)(3) class action.
