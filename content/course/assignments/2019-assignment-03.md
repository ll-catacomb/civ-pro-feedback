## Substantive Assignment

Consider the following three short hypotheticals.

**Hypothetical one:** South Carolina Rule of Civil Procedure 11 requires that a lawyer (for a represented plaintiff) or the plaintiff herself (if unrepresented) must sign every page of the complaint. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 11, however, requires only that a "pleading" (a term that includes a complaint) "must be signed by [the] attorney of record . . . or by a party personally if the party is unrepresented," a requirement that means only a single signature on a multi-page complaint. A plaintiff files a multi-page diversity complaint in South Carolina federal court bearing an attorney signature only on the last page, and the defendant moves to dismiss for failure to comply with S.C.R.C.P. 11. By the time the South Carolina federal court adjudicates the motion, the limitations period has run, so a new lawsuit would be time-barred.

**Hypothetical two:** Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 65 addresses preliminary injunctions. It provides that to obtain a preliminary injunction, a movant must convince a court that the balance of the following four factors favors the issuance of the injunction: likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, balance of the equities, and the public interest. From our discussion of *Thane-Coat*, you know in the Fifth Circuit (Texas is in the Fifth Circuit), FRCP 65 makes the four items just listed prerequisites (meaning a movant must prevail on each one), as opposed to factors to be balanced. Assume for the purposes of this question that the rule from the Fifth Circuit (*i.e.*, that the four listed items are prerequisites, not factors) is the rule in all federal courts across the United States. A plaintiff files a diversity action in a Texas federal court and moves for a preliminary injunction. After a hearing, the judge hearing the motion informs the parties that she finds the plaintiff's evidence in support of the motion extremely strong with respect to irreparable harm, balance of the equities, and the public interest. But she thinks that the plaintiff's showing on likelihood of success on the merits, while more than tenuous, was not enough to tip this consideration to the plaintiff's favor, although she acknowledges that the plaintiff might well be able to develop persuasive evidence in the next several weeks. The movant plaintiff argues that the injunction should nevertheless issue under the standards of Texas Rule 65.

**Hypothetical three:** On October 1, 2019, Congress passes, and the President signs, the following statute: "In any civil action commenced after the passage of this statute in which the federal district court sits in diversity, the applicable period of limitation shall be three years." On October 21, a plaintiff files a diversity action in a federal district court. The otherwise applicable limitations period of the state in which the district court sits is two years. The plaintiff filed the action approximately 30 months after the events that triggered the lawsuit.
