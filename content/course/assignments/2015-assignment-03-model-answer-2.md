## Question 1

In the Idaho state court case (L2), the court will likely grant Gringotts' summary judgment motion. Pettigrew's counterclaim is claim-precluded by the 2011 lawsuit (L1): it involves the same claim (same "transaction," i.e. Pettigrew's loans from Gringotts); it involves the same parties; and Delaware federal court rendered a final judgment "on the merits." That Pettigrew is now asserting a theory under state law — not under DIPA — doesn't mean that Pettigrew is now asserting a different "claim." Following *Semtek*, Idaho state court would probably grant as much preclusive effect as Delaware fedcourt would — which likely means federal common law because Delaware fedcourt had "arising under" jurisdiction over the DIPA claim. However, none of this matters here where there are no differences in preclusion law.

The court is unlikely to grant Pettigrew's SJ motion. Pettigrew could cite claim preclusion, arguing that because Gringotts' claim in L2 relates to the same "transaction" (Pettigrew's loans) as in L1, it was a compulsory counterclaim (Rule 13) that Gringotts cannot raise now after it failed to do so in L1. However, the court might define the "transaction" narrowly, finding that Gringotts is precluded from suing for unpaid payments due *before* L1, but not payments due thereafter (as the *Jones* court might have, absent an acceleration clause). Alternatively, Pettigrew could cite issue preclusion, arguing that because the court in L1 found that Gringotts made false statements to him, this issue shouldn't be relitigated. However, this finding is not preclusive because it wasn't "necessary to the decision" in L1. The court found that Pettigrew would've taken out the loans even if "no such statements had been made." It would've reached its decision (in favor of Gringotts) regardless of whether the statements were made.

In the South Carolina state court case (L3), the court will likely grant Gringotts' SJ motion. Bagman's counterclaim is precluded by the class-wide claim in L1: it involves the same claim (same transaction, i.e. all Gringotts Knut-rate loans between 2009 and 2011, including Bagman's); it involves the same parties since Bagman was a member of the class in L1 (no members opted out); and Delaware fedcourt rendered a final judgment "on the merits." Again, SC state court would probably grant as much preclusive effect as Delaware fedcourt would under *Semtek*.

*Marrese*, however, might cut in the other direction. In *Marrese*, the Supreme Court refused to find claim preclusion where the plaintiffs were unable in their first claim to assert a federal anti-trust theory that formed the basis of their second claim — even though both claims involved the same facts. Here, similarly, Bagman was unable to assert his state-law theory in L1, where he was a member of a class asserting a class-wide DIPA theory. As such, the SC court might find that Bagman's counterclaim — or at least, his still-unasserted state-law theory — shouldn't be precluded.

If so, Gringotts might still cite issue preclusion, arguing that the court's finding in L1 — that there was no "bankwide policy" of making false statements — precludes Bagman's DIPA theory. However, this finding doesn't concern the "same issue." The issue in L2 is whether Gringotts made false statements to Bagman on an individual basis, not as a matter of bankwide policy. This specific issue wasn't "actually litigated" in L1 and is therefore not precluded.

## Question 2

The law *should* preclude Pettigrew's counterclaim in L2. Pettigrew had the opportunity to fully litigate the same claim and issues in L1. Notably, the issues that were crucial to his state-law theory — false statements and causation — were specifically litigated and ruled upon in L1. This isn't a situation like in *Marrese*. Pettigrew had his "day in court." Allowing him to relitigate would run counter to the efficiency principles at the core of preclusion law.

Whether the law should preclude Gringotts' claim in L2 is a closer issue. Allowing Gringotts to bring this claim when it could've raised it as a counterclaim in L1 invites an inefficient result, i.e. repeated litigation over the same loans. However, if we want to preserve the liquidity of debt, it makes sense to allow lending institutions to sue for each unpaid payment separately.

Meanwhile, the law should *not* preclude Bagman's counterclaims in L3. Unlike Pettigrew, Bagman didn't have a meaningful opportunity to litigate the issues that might've afforded him recovery under state law. In L1, the court ruled on whether Gringotts made false statements causing *Pettigrew* to take out loans; it didn't consider Bagman's individual circumstances. What's troubling is that, although Bagman was technically a party to L1 — as a class member — he didn't have the kind of meaningful involvement that should properly be guaranteed by the "same parties" requirement in claim preclusion. Like the plaintiffs in *Marrese*, Bagman didn't have a chance to assert his individual state-law theory in L1.

Judge Snape's comment underscores the issue. The implication is that counsel disserved the unnamed members of the class — who, like Bagman, might've had viable state-law claims as individuals — when it proceeded on its class-wide DIPA claim and lost, binding all members to an adverse judgment and precluding them from recovering under state law. This is problematic where, as here, class members are entitled to recovery under diverse theories. We might argue that such classes should never have been certified — under the rigorous commonality requirement in *Wal-Mart* — but assuming that some such classes might still squeeze past Rule 23, we should refrain from unfairly wielding preclusion against them.
