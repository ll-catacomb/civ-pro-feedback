# Assignment 2 Model Answer

## Part (a): Can Hufflepuff sue York County in federal court?

Hufflepuff can sue York County in federal court. She can allege that York County's actions were in violation of her rights to procedural due process guaranteed under the Fourteenth Amendment. 42 U.S.C. § 1983 provides the statutory basis for her cause of action.

Hufflepuff can first claim inadequate notice before deprivation of her property. The bank may claim they put her on notice that they planned to foreclose, also advising of the 10-day response period. She may have been negligent in being incommunicado for so long. However, the test is not whether she actually received notice but whether the government made reasonable efforts. *Greene* dictates that reasonability of notice is decided with reference to the other available methods. Here, the other available methods would also have been unsuccessful.

However, the government is required under *Mullane* to make an effort reasonably calculated to give notice to the defendant. It seems the government was not acting as if they actually wanted to give notice under § 5(c) when they ignored the fact that the email was undelivered. Also, *Flowers* requires that the government make follow-up attempts via other methods when it knows that its first attempt failed. Notice can be insufficient even if sufficient notice would also have failed.

Hufflepuff may also claim that she had an insufficient opportunity to be heard. *Mathews v. Eldridge* outlines a three-factor balancing test for identifying insufficient procedure: (1) the claimant's interest in the property; (2) the increased risk of erroneous deprivation; (3) the State's interest. Hufflepuff's interest is exceptionally strong because she is at risk of losing her home. However, the state's interest also seems strong, if § 1 is taken at face value. *See also Di-Chem*. It seems that there is significant risk of erroneous deprivation because there is only 10 days to respond before default. People frequently are out of touch and 10 days might not be long enough to mount a proper defense. Additionally, the bank isn't really required to make any kind of evidentiary showing before the mortgagor potentially defaults by accident — all they have to do is file a complaint.

On the other hand, evidence presented is likely to be straightforward in a breach of contract case and could easily be presented on appeal. *See Shaumyan v. O'Neill*. The government may argue that Hufflepuff can appeal and even retain possession of the property via a bond, making the risk of deprivation small. However, considering that most people similarly situated cannot pay their mortgage payments, much less a bond equivalent to five months of payments, it may be a hopelessly ineffective procedural safeguard. The fact that Hufflepuff won the lottery and could, as a matter of fact, actually afford such a bond is merely a happy coincidence.

## Part (b): How can Hufflepuff best avoid a jury trial?

Hufflepuff should avoid triggering York County's Seventh Amendment right to a jury trial. For causes of action that did not exist in 1791, *Chauffeurs v. Terry* provides a two-part standard: (1) determine if the claim is most analogous to a historical equitable or legal action; (2) observe whether equitable or legal relief is requested. It is difficult to think of an analogous action at common law in this case (challenging the judicial procedures of an administrative court). Therefore, Hufflepuff should seek a form of equitable relief like an injunction and avoid monetary damages, since the kind of relief (as usual) will be dispositive. She should not seek both forms of relief because, under *Beacon Theatres v. Westover*, the court will hear issues of law first in order to preserve the right to a jury trial.[^1]
