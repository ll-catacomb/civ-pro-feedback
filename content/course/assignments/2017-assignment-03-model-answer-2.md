## Assignment 3 — Civil Procedure 2, 2017

### Question 1: Motion to Dismiss

This case should not be dismissed.

Since the parties are domiciled in different states and the amount in controversy is over $75,000, the Maine District Court can exercise diversity jurisdiction.

**Failure to State a Claim**

Dumbledore's pleading satisfies the standards of FRCP 8, *Twombly*, and *Iqbal*. The conclusory statement that Weasley was acting "negligently" should be struck pursuant to *Twombly*. However, Dumbledore's remaining complaint still pleads the elements of negligence. He pleads that Weasley owed a duty of due care because she was driving on a public road. He further pleads that Weasley breached this duty by driving drunk. Lastly, Dumbledore pleads that defendant's breach led Weasley to strike him, causing damages. If accepted as true, these facts would nudge Dumbledore's claim across the line to plausibility. Thus, this case should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim.

**Statute of Limitations**

We must also ask if this case should be dismissed because it was filed outside the statute of limitations. Under federal law, the statute of limitations is one year and begins to run upon discovery of the misconduct. Under both Nebraska and Maine law, the statute of limitations is longer (2 and 3 years respectively), but begins to run when the misconduct occurs. Because under either state law the filing would be timely and the misconduct rule applies, a horizontal choice of law analysis is unnecessary. However, if both the federal SOL and the state misconduct rule were applied, this case would not be timely and should be dismissed. If any other combination of law applies, it would be timely and may proceed. An *Erie* analysis is therefore necessary.

Under the *York* test, the state SOL would be applied. This case is almost the inverse of *York* — under the federal SOL (for which there was no statute in *York*) the case could proceed, while under the state SOL it could not. Because the SOL would be outcome-determinative, it would be treated as substantive law and the state SOL would apply. Under Justice *Harlan*'s concurrence in *Hanna*, SOL would most likely be procedural because it is unlikely to affect the pre-litigation conduct of the parties. While it is possible that it would change the pre-litigation conduct of large actors who regularly take part in litigation (e.g., insurance companies), that is not sufficient to make SOL substantive. Therefore, the federal SOL would apply. Under the *Hanna* majority, the Federal Limitations Period Act of 2011 is a valid exercise of Congress's Article III powers over the judiciary. And it is applicable because there is no way for both the federal statute and state SOL to apply at the same time. So the federal statute would apply, the twin-aims of *Erie* notwithstanding. Based on the precedent from *York* and the *Hanna* majority, the federal SOL should be applied.

We must also apply the *Erie* tests to determine whether the misconduct or discovery rule should govern.

Under *York*, state law would be applied. Under the state-adopted misconduct rule this case would be untimely and would be dismissed. Under the federally-adopted discovery rule this case would be timely and would not be dismissed. Because this difference is outcome-determinative, the rule is substantive and state law would apply. However, under *Harlan in Hanna*, the federal discovery rule would apply. These rules would impact the pre-litigation conduct of some large parties, such as insurance or investment companies, because they would face greater liability under the discovery rule (more timely litigation) than the misconduct rule. However, because the discovery and misconduct rules are generally irrelevant to parties' actions before they engage in misconduct that could give rise to litigation, it is unlikely that they would affect pre-litigation conduct. So the rule would be considered procedural and federal law would apply. Under the *Hanna* majority test, state law would apply. Because the federal rule is based on common law, we look to the twin aims of *Erie*. Allowing Dumbledore to apply federal law would encourage forum shopping, given the outcome-determinative difference between these rules. Thus, under *Hanna*, state law would apply.

Overall, the federal law should apply. But under either law, this case should not be dismissed. We cannot blend elements of state and federal SOL, which is the only way to render this case untimely. Therefore, Dumbledore should be able to maintain his suit.

### Question 2: Issue Preclusion Against Harry

No, this issue is not precluded. While the question of Harry's negligence was at issue and was decided in the prior suit, that decision cannot bind him in the subsequent case for two reasons. First, it is not clear whether that issue was necessary to the decision. While the jury did not have to find Harry negligent to rule in Ginny's favor, Harry's negligence was the only defense Ginny presented. Therefore, arguably, it was necessary to the decision of no damages. Even so, Harry was not a party in the prior suit, and he and Ginny are not in privity. Therefore, regardless of whether federal law or some state law applies, the issue of Harry's negligence is not precluded. To hold otherwise would deny him his right to Due Process.
