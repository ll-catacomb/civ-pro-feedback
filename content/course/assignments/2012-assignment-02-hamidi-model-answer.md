## Assignment 2 Sample Answer

The motion will fail only if the four-year (South Carolina) statute of limitations applies. In order to determine if the four-year statute of limitations applies, we must first determine whether the South Carolina court's choice of law decision transfers with the case, and then whether the federal statute trumps the state one.

### SC HCOL follows the case to Virginia.

The SC court's choice of law rules will follow the case to Virginia because the SC court had proper subject matter jurisdiction (SMJ), proper personal jurisdiction (PJ), and properly laid venue — in short, because the SC court was a "happy" one.

The SC court had proper 1332 diversity jurisdiction because the parties (SC & VA vs. NY/DE) were completely diverse (*Strawbridge*) and because the amount in controversy exceeded $75,000.

The SC federal court had both specific and general personal jurisdiction over Walmart (here, Walmart = Megashop). The SC court had general personal jurisdiction because the size and market share of Walmart's activity in the state likely rose to the level of "systematic and continuous" activity (*Perkins*). Even if the SC court couldn't establish general jurisdiction over Walmart, they could likely establish specific personal jurisdiction under a minimum contacts analysis (*Shoe*). Walmart purposefully availed themselves of the SC market and its legal protections (*Hanson*) by specifically creating and marketing SC-related products. They should have possessed a reasonable anticipation of being hailed into court, exceeding mere foreseeability (*Volkswagen*). Rule 4(k)(1)(a) then allows the federal court to piggyback on state grants of jurisdiction, and SC's California-style long-arm statute (which goes to the limit of the constitution) grants such jurisdiction.

The Patils properly laid venue in the SC court under Rules 1391(c) and 1391(a). Rule 1391(c) deems that a corporation has residency in any state where it is subject to the court's personal jurisdiction. Therefore, for the purposes of venue, Walmart also has residency in SC. Rule 1391(b) then allows a court to lay venue in any jurisdiction where a defendant resides.

### VA Court will follow SC HCOL and classify statute of limitations as procedural.

The VA court will follow SC's HCOL rules, including SC's decision as to whether statutes of limitation are substantive or procedural (*Ferens*). SC uses the 1st Restatement (*lex loci delicti*), under which statutes of limitations are always procedural (*Alabama RR*). This means the SC court would have used its own (4-year) statute of limitations. As a result, the VA court will use the SC court's 4-year statute of limitations.

### The case requires Erie analyses because the SC statute appears to conflict with the federal one.

### The motion fails under the York test (VA court would use SC's 4-year limitations period and not dismiss).

Under a *York* analysis, the motion fails because the decision to use the 4- or 2-year limitations period determines the outcome of the case. Because the decision is outcome-determinative, *York* would classify the state law as a substantive one warranting federal deference. Most obviously, this satisfies *York*'s rationale of eliminating forum shopping (a practical rationale behind *Erie* as well). But when it comes to some of *Erie*'s competing rationales, the reasoning begins to break down. This case is very similar to *York* but distinct in one critical way — there was no federal limitations statute in *York*. Therefore, the separation of powers undercurrent in *Erie* (concern about federal courts "creating" laws through Federal Common Law and stepping on Congress's toes) doesn't hold up — here the federal court isn't "creating" anything, it's applying a federal statute. This points toward using the federal statute under *York*. However, the federalist rationale behind *Erie* (powers not given to Congress by the Constitution are held by the states) suggests Congress may have exceeded its powers by venturing into tort law and its remedies. Under that *Erie* rationale, the *York* court would likely defer to the state statute.

### The motion succeeds under the Hanna majority test (VA court would use federal 2-year limitations period and dismiss).

During step one of a *Hanna* majority analysis, we ask whether the relevant federal law is valid and applicable — if so, we apply the federal law (Supremacy Clause, Art 6 § 2). Here, the law will likely be considered valid because Article 3 § 2 grants congress the power to create the federal courts and because Article 1 § 8 ("Necessary & Proper" clause) grants congress the power to create laws concerning those courts. The law is applicable and is therefore in direct conflict with the state statute of limitations. You simply can't construe "notwithstanding state law to the contrary" in a way that lets you *Walker* your way out of this one. The *Hanna* majority analysis thus concludes after step one, and the court will have to apply the federal statute.

### The motion succeeds under the Harlan concurrence test (VA court would use federal 2-year limitations period and dismiss).

Harlan cares most about primary conduct, and using one limitation or another certainly wouldn't have changed either party's pre- or post-lawsuit behavior. As such, the federal law trumps the state one, the motion succeeds, and the case would be dismissed.

> **Instructor note:** Since we're not dealing with an FRCP, the Rules Enabling Act is irrelevant. This is a case like *Stuart v. Ricoh*.
