As of the time of the events described here, Texas had four federal districts. San Antonio was in the Western District. Ft. Worth was in the Northern District. Galveston was in the Southern District. Tyler was in the Eastern District.

California also had four federal districts. San Diego was in the Southern District. San Francisco was in the Northern District. Los Angeles was in the Central District. Sacramento was in the Eastern District.

California and Texas are both First Restatement States for purposes of horizontal choice of law.

All states relevant to this exam had the tort of products liability. As I explained in the class session on *World Wide Volkswagen*, a plaintiff whose injuries were caused by an improperly manufactured product could sue the retailer or the manufacturer for damages. In all states relevant to this exam, the plaintiff could request as damages their medical expenses as well as a sum for pain and suffering.

Two years ago, California amended its long-arm statute, which previously provided that its courts could exercise personal jurisdiction whenever permissible under the U.S. Constitution. It retained the authorization that California courts could go to the limits of the Constitution, but it now also included the following provision:

> **Products Liability:** The legislature finds that over the last several decades, California has been inundated with dangerous products that enter the state from out-of-state manufacturers. Therefore, any company that sells a defective product to a California consumer, via either direct sale or through a corporate intermediary, shall be subject to the jurisdiction of the California state courts.

Texas had a long-arm statute that goes to constitutional limits.

The Texas and California rules of Civil Procedure were identical to the federal rules in all ways with one exception. In Texas, but not in California, Rule 8 included a section 8(a)(4), which provided as follows:

> **Tex. R. Civ. P. 8(a)(4):** In addition to the foregoing, in a complaint alleging a products liability cause of action under the law of any state, the complaint shall state with particularity the alleged defect in the manufacture, design, or disclosure/warning. The complaint shall also identify, in a separate paragraph labeled "Testifying Expert," the name and contact information of at least one expert in the product's field who will testify that the product at issue was defective and that the defect caused the injury.

<!-- page: 3 -->

The Texas Legislature was responsible for the addition of Rule 8(a)(4) to the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure. The law that added this provision included the following language: "The legislature finds that the state has been inundated with meritless products liability lawsuits, and that as a result, companies in Texas are under-investing in production of consumer products, resulting in lost jobs for the Texas populace and reduced tax revenue for the state. Rule 8(a)(4) is one of the measures the legislature is adopting to address this problem."

Snow Industries, Inc. was incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Ft. Worth, Texas. Snow manufactured top-of-the-line bow and arrow sets (including quivers, sites, armguards, stabilizers, and targets), costing approximately $5000 per set, in its factory in San Antonio, Texas. It conducted no advertising and attended no trade shows. Its single-page website described the company as in the business of making archery equipment and listed its mailing address, email address, and snail mail address. The website included no way to order products online directly from Snow. Instead, Snow sold its archery sets exclusively through Capitol, Inc. which was incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Galveston, Texas. Capitol sold sports equipment of all kinds throughout all fifty states and the District of Columbia in its retail stores. It had 16 stores in California, four in each of the federal districts. It had 20 stores in Texas, five in each of the federal districts. Although it stocked items from various manufacturers for other sports, it stocked only Snow's line for archery. Since it began offering archery equipment three years ago, Capitol sold about ten Snow sets per year in each of California and Texas, with sales scattered throughout its various stores there.

Three months ago, Katniss and Peeta, who grew up and lived all their lives in San Diego, California, got engaged. To celebrate, Peeta visited the Capitol sports equipment store near their house to buy Katniss a new archery set. Katniss was overjoyed. The same day as the purchase, the two took the set out to some woods nearby to give it a try. Katniss asked Peeta to set up two targets about 50 meters apart from each other, both about 100 meters away from her. Peeta set up the first target, walked to the second, and began to set it up. Katniss, who had never in her life missed a shot by five meters, much less fifty, took careful aim at the first target and fired. The arrow spun wildly away from the target and hit Peeta in the arm. After rushing Peeta to the hospital, and while Peeta was in surgery, Katniss examined the archery set carefully. She found two problems. First, the archery set's site was badly misaligned. Second, the arrows were all warped in a way that would cause them to fly in unexpected ways. Peeta ended up needing three expensive reconstructive surgeries and a four-week hospital stay.

Peeta sued Snow in the United States District Court for the Southern District of California. His complaint stated a products liability cause of action. In paragraph one, he identified Snow (naming Snow's state of incorporation and state of principal place of business) as the manufacturer of the archery set and himself a California domiciliary. In paragraph two he described the facts listed in the previous two paragraphs with particularity. In paragraph three, he alleged that the defective site and the warped arrows caused his injuries. In paragraph four, he listed his medical expenses, which totaled $350,000. In paragraph five, he asked the court to award him $350,000 for his medical expenses and $50,000 of pain and suffering. There were no other paragraphs in the complaint.
