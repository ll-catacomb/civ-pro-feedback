# Civil Procedure 2 — Assignment 3 — Draft Model Answer
**2013**

**(1)** Traditional claim preclusion attaches where the second lawsuit concerns the same claim and the same party and the first judgment was "on the merits," unless the rendering court lacked subject matter jurisdiction over the second lawsuit's theory of relief (*Marrese*). Given section (b) of the proposal, Rule X should not alter this SMJ caveat. It will, however, extend the initial description of claim preclusion to: same claim, any known justiciable party, "on the merits."

As Peverell's case involves medical malpractice, it is presumably governed by state tort law. The first lawsuit was nevertheless allowable in Georgia federal court because there existed complete diversity and the AIC was greater than $75K. The Georgia federal court would also have had diversity jurisdiction over the claim against Derwent, and so Derwent's motion to dismiss on the basis of Rule X should be granted.

Complete diversity did not exist, however, between Peverell and DMC, because DMC maintains its principal place of business in South Carolina. And according to § 1367(b), the Georgia federal court could not have exercised supplemental jurisdiction over DMC either. Thus in spite of Rule X, claim preclusion should not attach, and Peverell's case against DMC should go forward.

**(2)** One reason we might like Rule X is that it prevents a plaintiff from playing jury roulette. This goal can to some extent be accomplished with defensive nonmutual collateral estoppel. But not all states allow DNMCE, and under *Semtek*, we know that state law matters in diversity cases. But even with DNMCE, some issues will undoubtedly remain after a first (failed) lawsuit that can be used to secure liability in a second. And, of course, DNMCE would not apply if the plaintiff *prevails* in the first lawsuit (and yes, there might be a subsequent lawsuit if the plaintiff prevailed in lawsuit #1 when, for example, the plaintiff is unable to recover in full from the defendant in the first lawsuit). By forcing the plaintiff to join all known parties in the first instance, we give the plaintiff precisely one fair trial on the basis of one transaction or occurrence.

Another reason to favor Rule X is that it is likely to reduce the transaction costs of litigation. While the first lawsuit will probably be more complicated and last longer than

otherwise, we can eliminate subsequent lawsuits both where the plaintiff sues other defendants and where the defendants sue each other over the allocation of losses. If the first lawsuit is *too* complicated, presumably a court can exercise its Rule 42 authority to cut it up without jeopardizing the plaintiff, who will have already satisfied her Rule X obligations.

Finally, Rule X may increase judicial administrability. A full and complete picture of the facts may be more likely with all parties involved.

One possible downside to Rule X is unfairness to defendants. It may be that some defendants would prefer to proceed quickly to a jury trial, while others thrive by extending the litigation process for years without ever reaching a jury. The less sophisticated individual defendant may thus be stuck with the lawsuit hanging over her head for ages longer than otherwise, while absent Rule X the plaintiff may have been happy to sue her separately.

Another possible downside is unfairness to plaintiffs. A plaintiff may be in need of damages now, but will be forced to choose between delaying a lawsuit while she puts together a case against every possible defendant, and potentially losing the ability to sue a defendant who was actually most at fault or had the deepest pockets.

Finally, there is some inherent uncertainty in the proposed rule that could be problematic. What does it mean to "know" you have a claim for relief against another person? Theoretically it should be enough if you have knowledge of facts which would survive a motion to dismiss. But it would require a very odd lawsuit indeed to hold you to this standard. Essentially, the defendant you were going after in lawsuit 2 would have to argue that you knew she was likely at fault when you initiated lawsuit 1 — but then if she loses at the motion to dismiss stage she will have to argue that in spite of that stuff you ought to have known, she is nonetheless not liable. Alternately, Rule X may incentivize plaintiffs to do inadequate homework, so that they do not "discover" claims for relief against other parties until they finish their first lawsuit.
