# Assignment 2: Model Answer

## SMJ

There is diversity jurisdiction. 28 U.S.C. § 1332. Slughorn is citizen of Delaware (incorporation) and NC (central nerve). Honeydukes is a citizen of SC (central nerve and incorporation). AIC is at least $90,000 > $75,000.

## IPJ

Honeydukes could argue no IPJ (and if no IPJ, then lawsuit would have to be dismissed and the FF returned). FRCP 12(b)(2). Establishing IPJ requires long-arm statute and a constitutional basis. Here, the NC long-arm statute goes to the limits of the constitution, so IPJ OK if there is general or specific jurisdiction: federal court "piggy-backs" on long-arm statute of state in which it sits. FRCP 4(k)(1)(A).

Honeydukes can argue no general jurisdiction: NC is not Honeydukes's home state, and no in-state tag.

Honeydukes can also argue no specific jurisdiction. The minimum contacts/*International Shoe* requirement applies in all exercises of specific jurisdiction. *Shaffer v. Heitner*. Honeydukes can argue that it did not purposefully avail itself of NC law, since it may not have been aware that Slughorn was an NC corporation, and therefore did not target that forum. *See Hanson v. Denckla*; *J. McIntyre*. Slughorn's website provides no business address. The fact that Honeydukes made payments to an NC bank is insufficient to establish that Honeydukes was aware Slughorn was based in NC; wiring money through a bank that happened to be in NC doesn't establish minimum contacts with NC; the money could end up anywhere. Neither the NCRCP nor NC's long-arm statute provides notice to out-of-state buyers that specific IPJ will be exercised against them if they miss installments. *Compare McGee* (specific long-arm) *with Hanson* (no specific long-arm).

## Venue

Venue is proper under § 1391(b)(2) because a substantial amount of the events giving rise to the claim occurred in the district and, if there is IPJ, then venue is also proper under § 1391(b)(1).

## PDP

Honeydukes can argue insufficient opportunity to be heard. Temporary deprivation is a property deprivation requiring PDP. *Sniadach*. Honeydukes was denied the right to attend Slughorn's ex parte hearing and had no opportunity to be heard before the property was seized. *See Vitek*. Furthermore, Slughorn presented no argument for urgency. Because Felix Felicis (FF) is not destroyed during manufacturing, this is not an emergency situation requiring immediate seizure to prevent destruction of the FF. Another indication of insufficient PDP is lack of a bond requirement. *See Doehr*. Under NCRCP 65, Slughorn was not required to post a bond before property was attached, and Honeydukes had no opportunity to post a counterbond to regain possession.

There are some elements of NCRCP that support its constitutionality: the fact that Slughorn had to file a specific complaint detailing the allegations against Honeydukes, including sworn affidavits and certified copies of email exchanges, the fact that the presiding decision maker was a judge not clerk, *see Vitek*, and the fact that Slughorn retained some property right in FF. Although these elements establish a minimal level of PDP, they are insufficient to overcome the fact that there was simply no urgency here to justify the pretrial seizure, and that Honeydukes had no opportunity to be heard prior to attachment of its property. *See Fuentes*.

## Erie

Honeydukes can argue pretrial seizure of the FF is a procedural issue, so federal law should govern, and federal law would not allow this seizure.

*York*: a rule is substantive if outcome determinative. The most analogous FRCP is Rule 65. For preliminary injunctions, movant must show likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, balance of equities, and that public interest would be served. Slughorn can show none: no likelihood of success on the merits because no IPJ, no irreparable harm because could sue for damages for later, balance of equities favors Honeydukes because Honeydukes cannot manufacture chocolate frogs without FF (whereas Slughorn shows no need to possess the FF except as a litigation tactic), and public interest would be disserved because buyers would be discouraged from doing business if property could be seized without hearing. Under *York*, use state law.

Harlan concurrence, *Hanna*: a rule is substantive if it would affect primary conduct. There is no reason that the parties' primary conduct (conduct before lawsuit filed) would be different if they could access federal law in addition to state law. The difference between federal and state law would be that state law allows access to pretrial seizure. Because state law has no requirement for Slughorn to post a bond and no opportunity for Honeydukes to post a counterbond, these companies would have no reason to take out different insurance policies or restructure their finances to plan for this contingency. Therefore, use federal law.

*Hanna* majority/*Stewart*: Honeydukes argues Rule 65 is valid (would not "abridge, enlarge, or modify any substantive right" — *see* REA) and applicable (requires similar showings as NCRCP 65, including likelihood of success on the merits). However, Slughorn argues that FRCP 65 is inapplicable because does not govern pretrial seizure. Slughorn argues therefore, to discourage forum-shopping and ensure equitable administration of the law, NCRCP governs.

## Jurisdiction Over Property

NCRCP 65 essentially grants a right of replevin, which operates analogously to a Quasi in Rem lawsuit. Therefore, one should expect that in a writ of Replevin case, as in a Quasi in Rem, courts have jurisdiction over corporeal property only when the property is physically present in the jurisdiction. Honeydukes can argue that because the FF was in SC when the writ was issued, the NC court did not have jurisdiction over the property.
