# Assignment 2 Model Answer

## Q1

### 12(b)(1) SMJ

1331 against Leto: The RRA is a federal CoA, so there is § 1331 jurisdiction for the Leto claim (*Mottley*).

There is no § 1331 AUJ against Duncan: Chani alleges a state tort, and there is no embedded federal issue (*Smith-Grable*). There is no § 1332 jurisdiction. Both Duncan and Chani have lived their entire lives in PA, so there is no complete diversity (*Strawbridge*). But using the Leto AUJ anchoring claim (making § 1367(b) N/A), Duncan's claim can be appended — it clearly arises out of CNOF and thus the same C&C. No § 1367(c) factors are present.

### 12(b)(2) PJ

Leto is incorporated in NY, so NDNY has GIPJ.

Duncan is domiciled in PA, so NDNY does not have GIPJ. Regarding SIPJ for Duncan (*Int'l Shoe*), 4k(1)(a) and NY's long-arm allows IPJ to constitutional limits.

There are minimal contacts between the state (NY), the defendant (Duncan), and the litigation subject matter. Duncan operates trains leaving from Manhattan, and he sold the ticket to Chani and omitted the inspection in Manhattan.

Regarding the fair and reasonable factors: Duncan has significant contacts with NY — he operates a train with a NY station. Chani can clearly reach SDNY — she's done so before. If there was video or testimonial evidence of failure to inspect, that would be in NY. However, the train and witnesses of actual decoupling are in Philadelphia. Duncan frequently travels to NY. For HCOL, see below but likely NY substantive law applies, which a NY court knows best. NY has an interest in providing means of redress for residents purchasing tickets (*McGee*).

It's likely that a NY federal court has SIPJ over Duncan.

### 12(b)(3) Venue

§ 1391(b)(1): EDPA. § 1391(c): Duncan (domicile) and Leto (PPOB) both "reside" in PA.
§ 1391(b)(2): Events occurred in EDPA (crash) and SDNY (omission), nowhere else.
§ 1391(b)(3): N/A because venue is proper in EDPA or SDNY.

Venue is improper in NDNY under § 1391(b)(1) — see above — and § 1391(b)(2) — no relevant events. The case must be dismissed or transferred.

### 12(b)(6)

If the substantive law chosen requires recklessness (PA law), there is a failure to state a claim (parties agree that Duncan was not reckless). If simple negligence suffices (NY law), claim likely survives (parties agree negligence).

### HCOL

Any NY federal court will employ HCOL of NY for state claims (*Klaxon*).

The difference between negligence and recklessness is substantive for HCOL/VCOL.

NY is R2nd — presume *lex loci delicti* (PA) but consider the center of gravity of the lawsuit (Section 6). Here the conduct causing injury occurred in NY, and Duncan and Chani's relationship was centered in NY (contract signed). Evidence of omission in NY, but witnesses to the decoupling in PA. PA and NY probably both have an interest in regulating railroad safety (see above).

HCOL analysis (without COL clause) says likely that NY negligence law will be chosen.

### Erie

This conflicts with COL clause, which would establish PA recklessness standard. FCL recognizes enforceability but PA/NY do not.

**York:** If COL clause survives, PA recklessness means claim is likely dismissed as opposed to claim surviving if NY negligence law applies — outcome determinative. Use state law.

**Harlan Concurrence:** COL clause's enforceability changes ex ante conduct — a higher standard for railroad negligence means that Leto/Duncan are less careful/trained. This affects more than ex post lawyer conduct. Use state law.

**Hanna Majority (either version):** FCL, so we consider twin aims. Allowing FCL (COL clause enforceable) promotes forum shopping and inequitable administration given outcome-determinative difference from state law. Use state law.

Ultimately, state law is likely chosen — all Erie tests favor state law. The COL law clause is thus unenforceable.

This means NY R2nd controls, and it's likely that Section 6 factors make NY substantive law govern (see above), and the claim survives 12(b)(6). If not, and *lex loci delicti* presumption controls, then we have dismissal based on PA substantive law.

## Q2

Venue is improper in NDNY, so we must transfer. We can only transfer to happy courts (*Hoffman*), which are SDNY and EDPA. SDNY is happy (see PJ and venue analysis above).

EDPA is happy because venue proper (see above), and we have GIPJ over both Duncan (domicile) and Leto (PPOB).

We want it transferred to EDPA, ultimately to get PA's recklessness standard on our side. When we transfer, given that NDNY was unhappy, the transferee court's HCOL controls (*Van Dusen*). If we get EDPA, then PA's R1st HCOL means we get PA substantive law of recklessness (blood spill was in Philly) and a likely dismissal under 12(b)(6). If we get SDNY, then NY's R2nd HCOL likely means we get NY substantive law of simple negligence (see above) and likely no dismissal.
