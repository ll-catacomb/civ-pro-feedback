## Part 1

**(a)** The Durmstrang Federal Court's ability to apply state procedural law will vary depending on which *Erie* test is applied. Under *York*, where state law governs so long as it is "outcome determinative," state law will almost certainly apply. Pleading standards, almost by definition, are outcome determinative, since they determine whether the merits of a case can be heard and whether relief may be granted.

Turning to *Hanna*, we must first ask whether the federal rules 12(b)(6) and 8(a)(2), and their judicial interpretation, are **directly applicable and valid**. Since the language in the federal and state codes are identical, and both would control the outcome of a motion to dismiss challenge, the federal rules are directly applicable. Note that here, the federal and state rules are in **direct conflict**; it's hard to see how to construe the federal rules narrowly (*Walker*). The court would also likely find that the federal rules are also valid under the REA. This statute grants SCOTUS the power to promulgate the FRCP, which it did by properly adopting 12(b)(6) and 8(a)(2) in accordance with its validly delegated powers. With both conditions met, the federal rule controls.

Finally, under *Hanna*'s Concurrence, we ask whether this affects the "primary conduct affecting human decisions," i.e. is this something someone will care about pre-suit. Here, the Durmstrang Supreme Court clearly believes it will, as a more permissive pleading standard will deter negligence and other behavior. As a result, the state rule probably controls.

**(b)** The Rules of Decision Act plays no direct role in the analysis. However, it is indirectly relevant. § 1652's command that *state* law provides the "rules of decision" in diversity actions provides the statutory basis for *Erie* and its progeny, such that a case interpreting *Erie* can be said to be interpreting § 1652. In addition, § 1652 (as interpreted in *Erie*) makes clear that the fact that the relevant state authority comes in the form of state judicial interpretations (i.e., case law) makes no difference to whether federal courts should use state or federal law.

**(c)** Under rules governing diversity actions, defendants cannot remove to federal court if they are sued in their home state's state court (i.e. a Durmstrang domicile is sued in Durmstrang state court). In all other circumstances, they could remove to federal court. A federal court hearing this case, therefore, would likely face a situation where the plaintiff made a deliberate choice *not* to "trap" the defendant in a jurisdiction with a more plaintiff-friendly pleading standard, perhaps because of a more favorable jury pool or other strategic issues. So what should the Court do? The three *Erie* tests detailed above are relatively clear, and are not explicitly dependent on the underlying equities. However, a federal judge looking at this system could reasonably conclude that the pleading standards were *not* the most important element to the plaintiff, and, notwithstanding the general principle that the plaintiff is "master of her suit," elect to apply the more defendant-favorable federal standards, as opposed to letting the plaintiff get all the procedural advantages of state law in a federal court lawsuit.

## Part 2

Durmstrang state judges should *not* be required to apply *Iqbal* in arising under cases.

First, the equities are different in arising under cases. Unlike a state-law action, the plaintiff can always file in federal court, and the defendant can always remove to federal court. If they are still in state court after the 30 day removal clock has expired, it is because they are both presumably comfortable with the pleading standard. While *Erie* is concerned with the unfairness of different outcomes in the same geographic jurisdiction, in this case, those differences are to some extent *voluntary*, which may be less of a concern. In addition, because litigants would know they could always go to federal court, there would be less risk of a difference in standards affecting the *primary conduct* that Justice Harlan was so concerned about.

Second, applying federal pleading standards would be difficult in cases of supplemental jurisdiction, where there are both state and federal claims. Would *Iqbal* apply only to the federal claims, but a more permissive standard for the state claims (even if they stem from the same transaction or occurrence)? Or would federal standards apply for *all* claims, thus depriving the state of the ability to set procedure for how its laws will be litigated. This raises both practical concerns from an implementation perspective and federalism concerns about the right of states to run their own judicial systems.

Third, our system very explicitly establishes a system of *concurrent jurisdiction* for federal law issues, with the expectation (realized in current practice) that most federal law issues are settled in state courts. This system necessarily presumes that states can create process to govern their own judiciaries, and while such a presumption can be overruled, it should not be done lightly.

Finally, the assumptions underlying *Erie* work poorly in a "reverse" context. Unlike for diversity cases, there is no federal law that creates a presumption that federal rules of decision govern (as discussed above, the presumption is reversed). With the ability to remove-at-will, there is less of a chance of primary conduct being dramatically affected. And finally, *Erie* was designed to promote *state* interests, which requiring state judges to apply *Iqbal* would, of course, not do.
