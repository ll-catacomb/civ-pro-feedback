## Q1

**Hypo1:** *York* test results in state law because outcome-determinative: suit is possible under federal — but not state — law. *Hanna* concurrence favors federal law because the pleadings signature requirement would not affect people's pre-litigation conduct, so it is therefore procedural. Under "Scalia" test, FRCP 11 is valid because Congress validly passed the REA and delegated authority to promulgate the Rules. This Rule probably qualifies as "arguably procedural" under *Sibbach* test because it doesn't appear to enlarge/abridge/modify any substantive right — so therefore valid. FRCP 11 also clearly applicable here since it articulates different standards for the exact same thing. Since the federal rule is valid and applicable, it controls because of supremacy. Under "Ginsburg" test, the Rule is similarly valid, but probably not applicable. Ginsburg would read narrowly and say the Rule doesn't conflict with state law because both require the same thing — one signature — and are therefore not in conflict. The state law just puts an extra signature requirement on top of the federal minimum, so state law controls because the Rule isn't applicable.

**Hypo2:** The injunction would go opposite ways under state/federal law, but unclear whether this is an "outcome" for the *York* test. In theory, whether the preliminary injunction is issued has no bearing on the later judgment, so this wouldn't be outcome-determinative. But functionally, the grant/refusal of a preliminary injunction can greatly affect the status quo and parties' rights — and often results in settlement — so this probably qualifies as outcome-determinative and state law would apply. *Hanna* concurrence: few specific situations where differing standards re the availability of injunctive relief would create opportunistic pre-litigation behavior, so federal law probably applies. Like Hypo1, the Rule would be valid for both Ginsburg/Scalia tests. Further, both Ginsburg/Scalia would probably find the Rule applicable. There's little grounds not to find a conflict; TXRCP and FRCP diverge on how the same 4 factors apply to the exact same analysis. Since FRCP is valid and applicable, both the Ginsburg and Scalia tests would yield federal law.

**Hypo3:** *York* = same as Hypo1. *Hanna* concurrence = similar to Hypo2, but statute of limitations difference would likely affect peoples' pre-litigation conduct (ex: SoL length changes how companies value litigation risks and affects the price of insurance). However, probably only changes the behavior of the relatively few parties who strategically think specifically about litigation *ex ante*; few regular people are concerned about how long they have to bring suit until *after* they are considering suing. For both Scalia/Ginsburg, federal statute is valid because passed through bicameralism/presentment and appears constitutional (Article I, § 8). Statute seems even more clearly applicable than Hypo2: applying federal SoL would entirely contravene any state SoL and vice-versa. Since federal statute is both valid and applicable, federal law applies.

## Q2

All three hypos concern the same basic problem: whether or not federal law overrides state law when the two prescribe different things on the same issue. The primary difference between hypos is the importance of the interests that the state/federal provisions attempt to govern. In Hypo1, the interest at issue is how closely the attorney/plaintiff needs to read the complaint, ostensibly for the purpose of promoting effective assistance of counsel and judicial economy. In Hypo2, the interest is the availability of pre-trial injunctive relief, which involves determinations balancing fairness and due process. In Hypo3, the interest is whether the plaintiff can sue at all, weighing fairness and access to the judicial system against economy and finality.

The *Erie* tests — as currently articulated — are incapable of distinguishing the differences in how important/serious these interests are. Promoting careful lawyering via signature stipulations for pleadings might well be a worthy state policy goal, but it bears on a right that's clearly less important than provisions controlling whether suit can be brought *at all*: it's irrelevant if 0/1/all pages of the complaint are signed if you're time-barred from suing. Ironically, under current *Erie* doctrine, Hypo3 appears to be more conclusively "procedural" than Hypo1, even though the statute of limitations requirement regulates a far more significant right.

Current *Erie* doctrine tends to promote supremacy and uniformity. The FRCP, for example, usually control over state law, helping to establish uniform rules and expectations across federal courts in different states. Few statutes/rules ever qualify as invalid, and the determination of whether state/federal law controls hinges on (contorted) decisions regarding "applicability". This isn't necessarily *bad*, as uniformity and equivalency/predictability of forum across different states is one of the primary goals of the federal courts in diversity cases.

<!-- page: 3 -->

But this current scheme isn't really true to the philosophical/constitutional *point* of *Erie* doctrine: that state law will control re important stuff, and federal law will control ticky-tacky mechanics of litigation. Bizarrely — as Q1 illustrates — our current system would likely prescribe state law for the *least important issue* (whether every page of the complaint must be signed) and federal law for the *most important* (whether you can sue at all). This appears totally opposite to the philosophical justification for *Erie*, and pays little concern to principles of federalism like deference to state lawmakers (in Hypos 2/3, for example, federal law would essentially be invalidating state laws controlling important rights).
