## WW's Challenges

Under *Chauffers*' two-prong test, WW likely should have been granted a jury trial. Under *Chauffers*' first prong, we look to an analogous 18th-century suit to determine whether the suit would have been brought in a court of law or equity. In this case, WW would likely argue that this is a suit for negligence which historically was an action at law. On the other hand, this suit also looks like a third-party breach since Margolis is a third-party beneficiary to WW's contract with the Commonwealth of MA, which was historically an action in equity. Taking these into account, the first prong does not provide a definitive answer. Under *Chauffers*' second prong, the remedy sought is damages, which is legal in nature. Although the first prong doesn't provide a definitive answer, the second prong, which is the more important factor under *Chauffers*, supports a finding of a right to a jury trial.

Further, the public rights exception is likely not applicable in this case. The government is not a party to the dispute, and even though the government is acting as a regulator, the case centers around private rights (liability for third-party breach of contract or negligence) between two private parties (an individual and a corporation). (*Atlas Roofing* / *Granfinanciera*) Additionally, although federal election law likely represents a public regulatory scheme/program, the suit is to recover damages which is also a private right. Due to the mix of both law and equity, the legal remedy sought, and the fact that the public rights exception likely does not apply here, a jury trial should have been granted.

WW could also argue that the statutory scheme violates procedural due process under the DPC. *Vitek* provides a series of factors to consider when evaluating if a new adjudicatory scheme satisfies irreducible minimums of due process. First, WW was given notice of the action and invited to respond, but depending on the exact notice procedures utilized by the USEA, WW may have grounds for a due process challenge. We would need further information to evaluate this. Second, WW may have a due process challenge based on the opportunity to be heard. Although the opportunity to respond/be heard was given before a resolution was issued by USEA, WW may be able to challenge the type of opportunity that was given. For example, maybe a hearing should have been granted with live testimony, rather than just an opportunity for a written response, given that the case likely turned in part on a factual dispute (whether the MA Secretary of State ordered more ballots). Further, WW was not granted the opportunity to present, confront, or cross-examine any witnesses for Margolis; indeed, Margolis didn't offer any other than herself, and her testimony concerned more her damages than WW's wrongdoing. Due to this, they were not given the opportunity to confront any of the claims made against them in the evidence provided by Margolis. Lastly, in addition to their decision and order, the USEA did not provide a written statement or any information regarding the evidence they relied on in reaching their decision. While *Vitek* may not require all of these factors, an absence of them supports a challenge for a violation of WW's due process rights.

## Margolis' Challenges

Margolis might challenge the USEA decision on the grounds that her right to counsel was violated. However, there is not a concern of incarceration or a significant risk to Margolis' liberties, and therefore, under *Lassiter*, Margolis likely does not have a strong case for a right to counsel in this proceeding. Nevertheless, according to *Lassiter* and *Turner*, the court conducts a *Mathews/Eldridge* test analysis, considering the private interest, risk of erroneous deprivation, and government interests, to evaluate a party's right to counsel.

First, Margolis' private interest is whether she will have access to damages for her pain and suffering. While legal counsel would likely help Margolis make a more detailed and succinct claim to the USEA, with the procedures USEA currently has in place regarding submissions of evidence for claims, Margolis' private interests are likely not as significant as the rights of a parent.

Second, it is unlikely that legal counsel would significantly reduce risk of erroneous deprivation. Neither party is required to have counsel to file the requisite documentation to form the written record in which the USEA bases its dispute decisions. Additionally, neither party is entitled to discovery or live testimony. Therefore, there is no substantial risk of erroneous deprivation without access to counsel.

Lastly, the government's interest in expeditious disputes regarding violations of federal election law likely outweighs Margolis' private interests. Lawyers extend proceedings, and increase the level of formality within disputes (*Powell*, in *Vitek*). Margolis' interests and risks probably do not supersede the government's. Therefore, Margolis likely does not have a strong claim for a right to counsel.
