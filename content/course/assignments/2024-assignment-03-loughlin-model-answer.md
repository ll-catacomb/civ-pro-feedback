**Civil Procedure Assignment 3 - Draft Model Answer**

## Question 1

### *Motion to seize the animations*

The court will likely grant the motion, because the requirements of the FRCP governing pre-judgment seizures can be met, and the court can exercise QIRJ.

#### *FRCP*

First, there is DJ over Hiccup's theft lawsuit against Fishlegs. Hiccup is incorporated in Delaware (there is no clear PPOB, but it isn't Maine), and Fishlegs is a domiciliary of Maine. (*Strawbridge*). The best AIC is the value of the inventory, $1.25M, above $75,000.

Second, policy considerations suggest that the artwork is best located within the "geographic boundaries" of Delaware. Animations, like debt, are incorporeal objects. (*Harris*). The servers for the different components of the animations are located in three separate states, and the components must converge digitally to create artwork, suggesting that locating the animations at the servers is not best. Because Hiccup owns and controls exploitation of the content, the components functionally combine within the company, incorporated in Delaware. Parties may reasonably expect to litigate content-related disputes in Delaware, and the relationship of the directors is most centered in the state, where they chose to incorporate. While there may be more evidence regarding the theft on Fishlegs' Maine servers, Delaware is the most ascertainable and definite state available to locate the artwork.

Third, Hiccup owns the animations, and attached supporting evidence to the complaint, including a copy of the contract assigning ownership of the artwork to the corporation.

#### *QIRJ*

Under the narrow view of *Shaffer*, the court can exercise QIRJ because, as discussed above, the artwork is probably located in Delaware, and there is no problematic statute at issue. Under the broad view of *Shaffer*, it is unclear if QIRJ still exists. Still, there are likely sufficient minimum contacts linking the defendant's interest in the property, the subject matter of the litigation, and the forum. (*International Shoe*). Per FRCP 4(k)(1)(A), the district court follows Delaware's long-arm statute, which goes to the limits of the constitution. Fishlegs does not have an ownership interest in the artwork. However, as co-CEO of Hiccup, Fishlegs purposefully availed herself of Delaware's law. Unlike in *Shaffer*, the property to be seized (the artwork) is directly

related to the subject matter of the litigation (the theft). Finally, theft is complete "where the object was removed from the control of those who should have it." If the artwork was located in Delaware when it was removed from Hiccup's control, the theft took place in Delaware.

The exercise of QIRJ is fair and reasonable. (*McGee*). It would not be inconvenient for Fishlegs to appear in a state where she already operates a business. Delaware has a substantial interest in ensuring its theft law is followed, particularly in the corporate context, as many companies rely on the state's laws to protect their assets. Assuming the artwork was located in Delaware, the theft was complete there, so Delaware law likely applies.

#### *Electronic service*

Plaintiffs must make "reasonable efforts" at notice. (*Mullane*). Electronic service is reasonable because Astrid and Ruffnut have been communicating with Fishlegs through electronic channels, and have not received any alert that their communications failed to get through. (*Contra Flowers*). Astrid and Ruffnut also have no physical address for Fishlegs, so electronic service is the most effective method of notice.

#### *Defenses*

Fishlegs could assert PDP defenses. Pre-judgment seizures ordinarily require notice and a hearing unless there are extraordinary circumstances, which are not present here (*Doehr*). Like the pre-judgment seizure in *Doehr*, this seizure likely violates the *Matthews* test. The private interests of Hiccup and Fishlegs are relatively balanced. Hiccup, in contrast to Fishlegs, has a property interest in the artwork, but the animations are not essential like a house or car (and Hiccup, a corporation, does not have human needs). Fishlegs' private interests are implicated because a seizure could prevent the sale of the artwork, depriving Fishlegs of income. There is a high risk of erroneous deprivation. The seizure order takes effect instantly, and requires no presentation of evidence prior to the deprivation. Like *Doehr*, the seizure would be based on a one-sided affidavit without any live testimony. Hiccup is also not required to post a bond.

## Question 2

Hiccup should seek a remedy that is traditionally equitable. Hiccup could seek an injunction barring Fishlegs from accessing or selling the content, or ordering Fishlegs to restore the access of Astrid and Ruffnut to the Maine servers.

## Question 3

<!-- page: 3 -->

The court should deny Hiccup's MSJ because the record suggests a genuine issue of material fact. (*Celotex*, Brennan J. dissenting). Hiccup, the moving party, bears the burden of persuasion on its theft claim. Although Hiccup produced evidence, including two affidavits and the forensic investigative reports, it has not met its burden of production. The affidavits and forensic reports demonstrate only that Astrid and Ruffnut's servers were hacked from somewhere in the New England states, and that Fishlegs ceased communicating with them. Drawing all reasonable inferences in favor of the non-moving party, a rational jury may find that Fishlegs was not behind the theft. Even if Hiccup had met its burden of production, Fishlegs has produced evidence showing there is a genuine issue of material fact. In her affidavit, Fishlegs stated she did not hack any servers, and provided an alternative reason for her silence. The court should thus deny the MSJ, regardless of whether the burden shift is triggered.
