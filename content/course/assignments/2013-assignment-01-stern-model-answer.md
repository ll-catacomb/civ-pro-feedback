**1.**

__Motion to Dismiss for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction (IPJ) over St. Mungo's (SM)__

SM is unlikely to succeed in a motion to dismiss Pince's claim based on lack of IPJ in NH.

NH's long-arm statute grants its court system the power to exercise jurisdiction over defendants outside NH, depending on the extent to which the defendant has formed a connection with NH.

SM is a citizen of DE (where it's incorporated), and MA (nerve center), so general jurisdiction in NH is out.

Regarding specific jurisdiction, SM requires that all employees perform their medical work only in MA. However, SM purposefully availed itself of NH through advertising (*International Shoe*). The use of unique NH characteristics in the phrase, "Has living in the granite state been hard on you?", shows SM's intent to benefit from NH. SM shouldn't be surprised by a lawsuit drawing it into NH court when its advertisements are designed to attract patients from that state.

Moreover, NH's interest in litigating the case weighs in favor of keeping it there. NH has a stake in protecting its residents against faulty medical diagnoses and debilitating healthcare costs. Pince also has an interest in litigating in NH, where she currently resides, because she is in unbearable pain and would have difficulty traveling to MA. While the tort occurred in MA, and MA state law would likely apply, it wouldn't be difficult for a NH court to apply MA law. Similarly, while some of the litigation materials will be in MA, it should be easy enough to transport documents and X-Rays across the border to NH. Therefore, I think a motion to dismiss for lack of IPJ is unlikely to succeed.

**2.**

__Motion to Dismiss for Lack of Subject Matter Jurisdiction (SMJ)__

Pince can succeed on a motion to dismiss SM's declaratory judgment (DJ) suit for lack of SMJ only if she is domiciled in MA and the court does not find an important federal question embedded in her state law claim.

A DJ suit, such as the one SM is bringing, can only be heard in federal court if the "coercive action" plaintiff could have brought the case in federal court. We need to check diversity jurisdiction and arising under. For the former, there must be complete diversity (1332) to remain in federal court. We certainly meet the required amount in controversy ($12 million), but we don't necessarily have diversity of citizenship. Per above, SM is a citizen of MA and DE, so we only have diversity if Pince is domiciled in NH. Pince was born in NH, so she starts out domiciled there. The two years she spent in a MA homeless shelter were likely out of necessity rather than a desire to stay, and she always returned to NH during the summer while going to school in MA. These factors suggest that her domicile never changed from NH. In that case, Pince's motion to dismiss for lack of SMJ likely would not succeed because the DJ suit can be heard in federal court on the basis of diversity.

If she is domiciled in MA, however, the court should dismiss for lack of SMJ unless SM can show that there is an important federal question embedded in Pince's claim (*Smith-Grable*). Here, Pince's malpractice case is a state law claim, but her claim of negligence per se hinges on compliance with the federal statute. SM might succeed if the court believes the claim raises an important federal question because it involves evaluating the constitutionality of the statute. SM also accepts reimbursements pursuant to Medicaid and Medicare, both federal health care programs, so the hospital's compliance with the statute would be an issue of strong interest to the federal government. However, Pince's case is a typical state law tort claim. The outcome of the lawsuit would likely not affect other states or have a major impact of concern to the federal government greater than would other negligence per se claims hinging on federal statutes. Exercising federal SMJ any time a state law claim depends on a negligence per se claim (based on a federal law) would probably sweep too many cases into the federal court system. On SM's side, the facts do not appear to be in dispute, so the issue is likely to be a clean issue of law. Therefore, I think state law likely would apply, and her motion to dismiss would probably succeed.

__Motion to Stay__

Pince also has a chance of succeeding on a motion to stay. In general, federal courts have an unflagging obligation to exercise the jurisdiction given them. Now that a suit between the same parties over the same issues is pending in state court, the federal court might be inclined to stay SM's claim for purposes of judicial economy (*Colorado River*). Furthermore, the state court claim was filed first, the tort claim is a traditional matter of state law, and there is little difference between the two courts with respect to convenience of the parties since Boston and Concord are geographically close to each other, and at least SM could travel easily between them. These factors all weigh in Pince's favor for succeeding in her motion to stay SM's lawsuit. On the other hand, there is nothing here like the McCarren Amendment that suggests a congressional policy in favor of adjudicating these cases in federal court, so the issue is unclear.
