## Question 1

I should advise that the case be not dismissed.

### 1. SOL

The Texas Limitation period has been exceeded, but not that of the Act. *Erie* is required to decide which applies:

**A. *York*:** Outcome determinative, so we would expect to use state law. No Act of Congress was at issue in *York*, however, so may not be applicable here. Or, because Congress is not subject to the "Rules Enabling Act," it may be that even if outcome-determinative, the Act should govern.

**B. *Hanna* Majority/Scalia:** The Act covers the point in dispute. It "represents a valid exercise of Congress' authority under the Constitution" (*Stewart*), because it falls under the power to create the lower federal courts and the "necessary and proper clause." The Act governs.

**C. *Hanna* Concurrence:** May affect pre-litigation conduct, because parties might adjust their life and investment strategies based on when lawsuits expire. On the other hand, limitations periods are also about when lawsuits must be filed, which might not affect how clients (as opposed to their lawyers) behave. The answer is unclear.

**D. *Hanna* Majority/Ginsburg:** The TXSOL seems to serve a state policy, and create a right "not to expect suits." But, the Act and TXSOL are not reconcilable, and Congress is not subject to the Rules-Enabling Act ("REA"), and does not seem to have exceeded constitutional limits. So, Act applies.

### 2. Signature

If TXR, then must dismiss. If FR, case may continue. *Erie* is required to decide which applies:

**A. *York*:** Outcome determinative, so, TXR probably applies. No FR was at issue in *York*, so a little unsure.

**B. *Hanna* Majority/Scalia:** Rule applies, because within REA and Constitutional Bounds. There is a conflict, because TXR presents an "additional hurdle." FR applies because the rule "really regulates procedure" (*Sibbach*) and does not, on its own, abridge or enlarge a right. It speaks to the issue at hand because it says that a signature is required, not a signature required on every page.

**C. *Hanna* Concurrence:** Would not have affected pre-litigation conduct (except, probably, for choosing a meticulous lawyer). FR applies.

**D. Ginsburg:** TXR serves an important state policy. FR and TXR may be reconciled, if we take the signature requirement of the FR to be the minimum required, which is supplemented by the TXR (*Cohen*). TXR would apply.

Thus, the case should not be dismissed, because plaintiff complied with the Act and Federal Rule.

## Question 2

I think the case should be dismissed for exceeding the TXSOL, but not for noncompliance with the TXR.

### 1. Limitation

There may be something to be said for bringing unity into the Federal system. Yet, such an Act presents a terrible alternative, both because it confounds *Erie*, and allows Congress unprecedented and dangerous liberty.

<!-- page: 2 -->

*Erie*, for all its "murky water," was justified as a means to prevent forum-shopping and the inequitable administration of the Law. Such a sweeping rule would close the Federal courts before plaintiffs, howsoever legitimate their claim or excusable their delay, compelling them to stand before a state court. Where a huge corporation is a defendant, for instance, we are left with something worse than the pre-*Erie*, *Swift* regime, because defendants may choose the state with the most favourable SOL, and plaintiff may not even appeal to the old "LAW" of *Swift*.

Furthermore, Congress is not bound "not to abridge or enlarge" substantive rights; and, should it be allowed to change procedure in such a fashion, is to engender a harm that nothing can remedy but a judgement of Unconstitutionality, applicable only to individual Acts. Any rule may be characterised as Procedural, and the Federal Courts are Congress's "creatures," and Congress may set their "house-keeping rules." Is it so inconceivable that, moved by no higher interest in justice but that of corporate lobbyists, Congress would make it much harder for consumers, say, to sue manufacturers by such apparently procedural "House-keeping" Acts? I think not.

### 2. The Signature

This rule presents the other extreme, and, I think, should not be allowed to govern in Federal Court.

While there is some plausibility to the reasons given by the Texas Supreme Court, applying such clearly procedural rules in Federal Courts deserves a special scrutiny. Could not imposing an "Anti-misspelling Rule" be justified on the same grounds? What about proper formatting? Paper size? This would bring us back to the time when Federal Courts had to use the procedures of State Courts, without the old advantage of knowing that this would always be the case.

By extending *Erie* so far, it seems that we also disserve its aims.

Between the Act and the Rule lies the virtuous mean; where the states may realise their substantive aims without wantonly claiming Substantive Purposes for clearly procedural rules, and where Congress may provide "house-keeping" rules for its courts without closing them before those for whom they were intended with "procedure" as its pretext.
