MEMORANDUM

To: Madam Chairwoman, House Judiciary Committee
From: Industrious staff member
Re: Proposal to repeal 28 U.S.C. § 1367(b)
Date: 9/1/2007

## I. The Legal and Practical Effects of Repealing 28 U.S.C. § 1367(b)

Section 1367 codified the concept of pendent or ancillary jurisdiction, providing the statutory support for such jurisdiction that the courts seemed to require in *Aldinger*, *Kroger*, and *Finley*. Subsection (a) grants federal jurisdiction over supplemental claims that "form part of the same case or controversy", while subsection (b), by limiting a plaintiff's ability to file claims against non-diverse parties, codifies *Kroger*'s defense of *Strawbridge v. Curtiss*'s requirement of complete diversity.

If subsection (b) is repealed, jurisdiction up to the limits of Article III of the Constitution would be permissible, subject only to the discretion of the federal courts under 1367(c) to refuse to hear supplemental claims, and their discretion under § 1441(c) to remand entire cases that were removed from state court if questions of state law predominate.

There is nothing in the language of section 1367(c) suggesting that it is merely a codification of common law. Rather, it sets forth strict benchmarks that must be met if a federal judge refuses to hear a supplemental claim. In *Executive Software*, the Ninth Circuit concluded that a district court erred in remanding pendant claims under section 1367(c) when it failed to explain its reason for doing so. The court also held that only the statutory grounds provided in subsection (c), and not the more flexible considerations of *Gibbs*, provide a permissible foundation for remanding state claims.

The most significant practical effect of repealing 28 U.S.C. § 1367(b) would be an increase in the number of cases removed to federal court. At least one study shows lower win rates for plaintiffs in removed cases. Because it is alleged that federal courts are more 'defendant-friendly,' we may see a rise in the percentage of cases won by defendants.

## II. Change is Needed, but the Current Proposal is Too Radical

Commentators have expressed dissatisfaction with the language of section 1367(b) since its inception. Professor Freer criticized the apparent paradox that exists under section 1367(b) whereby plaintiffs are unable to assert claims against non-diverse parties even when defending against a counter-claim and in federal court through no action of their own. Revision of the current section 1367(b) is desirable. The ideal proposal, however, would amend or replace that subsection, rather than repealing it outright.

There are several arguments to be made in favor of a less restricted version of supplemental jurisdiction. Hearing related claims together promotes efficiency. Furthermore, forcing parties to litigate some claims in state court and some in federal might discourage them from taking advantage of a federal forum. Finally, on questions of state law, litigating in federal court eliminates the risk of prejudice against out-of-state defendants by state court jurors and judges (although discrimination studies have proved inconclusive).

However, allowing jurisdiction over supplemental claims to the limits of the Constitution is probably too extreme. District courts are courts of limited jurisdiction. Limitations of some kind on supplemental jurisdiction are desirable in order to counter-balance the liberal rules of joinder in the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, to prevent 'end runs' around *Strawbridge*'s principle of complete diversity, and to avoid unjustified congestion in the federal courts. Contrary to Professor Shapiro's suggestion that the scope of supplemental jurisdiction should be left to common law development, some legislative guidance is advisable in this area, as the confusion resulting from case law prior to the enactment of section 1367 confirms.

Subsection 1367(b) should be amended rather than repealed, and this revision should occur after further opportunity for empirical study of the relative competencies and caseloads of state and federal courts.
