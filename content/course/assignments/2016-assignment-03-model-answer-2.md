## Assignment 3 — Civil Procedure 2, 2016

### Question 1: JAMOL

The judge should have granted MIP's JAMOL. On a motion for JAMOL, the court views the evidence in the light most favorable to the non-movant (Outrage) and determines whether a reasonable jury could have found for Outrage (*Reeves v. Sanderson Plumbing Products*; *Scott v. Harris*). The court does not assess credibility and draws all reasonable inferences in favor of the verdict-winner.

Nevertheless, JAMOL is appropriate when the evidence is so one-sided that reasonable people could not disagree. Here, the defamation claim centered on the expert's statement that the BAM is effective in fewer than 50% of users. MIP's own internal documents — which the expert used as her source — show 70% effectiveness in clinical trials. While those trials are unreviewed, the expert's contrary interpretation of the same underlying data, without a peer-reviewed basis of her own, may be insufficient to defeat MIP's claim as a matter of law. Under *Celotex*, once MIP established a prima facie case through its own documents, Outrage needed to produce evidence sufficient to allow a reasonable jury to disbelieve MIP's evidence. The expert's reading of MIP's own data — essentially speculating that MIP's internal results were inflated — may not meet that threshold.

That said, the effectiveness of the BAM is acknowledged to be an "open question," and the expert had a basis for her opinion. A court could reasonably conclude that this was a close enough factual question that JAMOL was correctly denied. The argument that JAMOL should have been granted is the stronger position, but the denial was defensible.

### Question 2: New Trial

The judge should grant the new trial. The new trial standard — "great weight of the evidence" — is broader than the JAMOL standard. The judge may weigh the evidence and assess credibility to some degree. Here, the judge observed the trial and is well-positioned to conclude that the jury's verdict was against the great weight of the evidence.

MIP's own internal clinical trial documents suggest 70% effectiveness. The expert's disagreement was based solely on her reading of those same documents, without independent clinical testing, peer review, or other expert corroboration. The balance of the evidence arguably weighs in MIP's favor on the effectiveness question, supporting a finding that the expert statement was defamatory. The new trial should be granted.

### Question 3: Immediate Appeal

Outrage cannot immediately appeal a grant of new trial. The order is not a final judgment under 28 U.S.C. § 1291 — it simply directs the case to be retried. Outrage must wait for a final judgment following the retrial before appealing. The new trial grant is not immediately appealable as a collateral order (it does not meet the *Cohen* test because it is effectively reviewable on appeal from a final judgment). § 1292(b) certification is theoretically possible but unlikely to be granted here.

### Question 4: Jury — Concerns and Benefits

**Concerns:** The core dispute here — whether the BAM is effective in fewer than 50% of users — is a technical and scientific question. Jurors without relevant medical or statistical expertise may struggle to evaluate competing interpretations of clinical trial data. The danger is that jurors will decide based on narrative (a corporation vs. a public-interest watchdog) rather than the evidence.

The judge's summary judgment rulings highlight another concern: the judge found "lots of evidence" that Outrage installed recording devices but did not find it "convincing." On JAMOL, a court cannot make credibility determinations — but a judge would be able to do so in a bench trial, potentially leading to a more accurate result. The jury's inability to explain its verdict compounds this concern.

**Benefits:** The defamation claim involves questions about community standards for what constitutes actionable false statements versus protected opinion about a product's medical effectiveness. Juries are better suited than judges to determine what a community of reasonable people would understand as defamatory. The jury also provides a check on judicial power and brings lay common sense to bear on questions of reputational harm. In high-stakes commercial disputes involving potential fraud and public health, the jury's community-legitimacy function is valuable.
