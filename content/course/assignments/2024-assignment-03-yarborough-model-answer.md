## Question 1

### Motion to Seize

For the seizure rule to apply, the court must have SMJ, the object must be within the bounds of the DC, and the P must have an ownership interest.

The court likely has SMJ under § 1332, as Fishlegs is ME domiciliary, and Hiccup is incorporated in Delaware. One could argue the principal place of business is elsewhere, but that argument would likely not stand, as there is no other location that acts as the nerve center. The amount in controversy, from the $1.25M property valuation, is over $75,000.

Next, the object must be in Delaware. The object in question, the animation, has no clear physical location, similar to a debt or a bond. Assuming the object has been stolen, the technology in question is being used somewhere in New England. However, prior to the theft, as each of the three individuals had some control of the object in three different states at the same time, the location of the person using the software is likely not an effective way to analyze location. As Hiccup owns the object, under *Harris*, the location of the object should be the location of the owner — Delaware. Further, a functionalist argument points towards Delaware. Hiccup owns and controls all rights and interests of the animation, making it reasonable for parties to expect to litigate in Delaware. Similarly, a desire for certainty and minimizing arbitrary locations points towards Delaware.

Finally, Hiccup rightfully asserts an ownership interest and supports this interest with competent evidence (such as the contract assigning ownership of the artwork to Hiccup), as required by the FRCP.

However, the court may dismiss for lack of QIRJ. The court could attach the property pending the litigation, in this case the theft, making this a lawsuit against the property. *Shaffer* leaves open the question of if QIRJ even exists. Under interpretation 1, QIRJ survives. Assuming the property is in the forum state (see above), there can be QIRJ. The court must also consider the type of property in question, and the policy implications. This legal matter is not as over broad as the DE sequestration statute at issue in *Shaffer*, as the D was a part of a three-person company that incorporated itself in Delaware. Under interpretation number 2 of *Shaffer* — *International Shoe* limits QIRJ. This would still probably survive, as there are minimum contacts. Owning a substantial (1/3) stake in a company is similar to owning property in Delaware, meaning Fishlegs may have purposefully availed herself to the forum state. However, it is a movable object, potentially more like a car than a house. Fair and reasonableness factors go either way. As the defendant has contacts through incorporating her company there, meaning it is somewhat convenient. The forum has an interest in property laws of companies in their state. However, the evidence is likely not in Delaware. Finally, if the location of the animation is in Delaware, HCOL points to Delaware. The tort occurred "where the object was removed from the control of those who should have it," meaning Delaware.

Fishlegs would likely succeed in raising PDP concerns on this pre-hearing seizure compared to a post-hearing seizure (*Matthews*). The private interest factors are high for both parties. Hiccup has a property interest in the animation, which evokes concerns of livelihood, although the property in question is not subsistence like *Goldberg*. Similarly, although Fishlegs does not have a property interest in the animation, she is a co-CEO of the company, and it is her means of income. Second, the *Snaidach* factors show a risk of erroneous decision, compared to a post-hearing seizure. It is unclear who the decision maker is in this instance. A pre-hearing seizure is almost never constitutional, and there are no exceptional circumstances present (*Doehr*). The seizure requires no live testimony, nor litigation bounds, both of which would both reduce the risk of erroneous decisions. Third, the government has an interest in accuracy and low adjudication costs.

### Notice by electronic mail

The court will likely allow notice by electronic mail. The three members of Hiccup have established modes of communication via electronic mail, and they have no mailing address for Fishlegs. It was the most likely way to give notice, due to this established method of communication. Also, nothing came back, so no reason to think this was not effective (contra, *Flowers*).

## Question 3

To rule on summary judgment, the court will analyze the *Celotex* factors. Hiccup is the moving party and has the burden of proof at trial, so they must produce enough evidence on the issue that, if unrebutted, would show there is not a genuine issue of material fact. Hiccup produced two affidavits and a forensic report, showing that Ruffnut and Astrid were hacked, the hacker was in one of three states, and Fishlegs ceased communication with Ruffnut and Astrid. Drawing all reasonable inferences in favor of Fishlegs, a rational jury may be able find that she was not the culprit. Even if Hiccup met its burden of production, Fishlegs' affidavit shows a genuine issue of material fact, namely, who stole the technology. Fishlegs asserts that someone else must have stolen it, and her access has been uninterrupted.
