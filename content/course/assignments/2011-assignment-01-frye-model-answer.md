## Question One

The judge is likely to remand the case, either on procedural grounds or for lack of jurisdiction.

### Procedural Grounds

All defendants must sign the motion to remove, and here Fred did not sign it. The requirement that all defendants have to sign the motion to remove is meant to protect the interests of any defendant who wishes to remain in state court. Here Fred's interests were not taken into account. In order for the case to be remanded on this procedural ground, the remand petition must be filed within thirty days of the removal, or the case will stay in federal court, unless the court lacks SMJ. Because thirty days will be up very soon, the motion should be filed as soon as possible.

### Jurisdictional Grounds

The case could also be remanded for lack of jurisdiction.

#### *Arising Under*

There is no arising under basis for jurisdiction. The Weasleys' theoretical suit is the basis for jurisdiction, since the declaratory judgment act is not meant to broaden the federal courts' jurisdiction. The Weasleys would have brought a tort claim for negligence, which is a state law cause of action. The Weasleys could argue that there is a federal issue involved because the suit involves the FDA's warning, which would probably have been mentioned in their well-pleaded complaint. Under *Osborn* and the Constitution, it is likely the court could hear the case because it includes the tangential federal issue about the FDA. However, under § 1331 as interpreted in *Mottley*, arising under jurisdiction requires a federal law cause of action, which is not present here. The only possibility of arising-under jurisdiction is the *Smith*-*Grable* exception, but it probably would not apply here. First of all, the Weasleys do not necessarily have to prove that the FDA has authority to issue the warning or any other fact about the FDA in order to prevail on their claim. The fact that the employee was negligent is bolstered by the FDA's order, but the court could find negligence even without it. The FDA's shallow involvement in the facts of the case also indicates that the case will likely not involve a substantial federal issue, and it is unlikely that the court would find anything about the FDA's powers of regulation, etc. Furthermore, this is not an issue that all of the federal courts have to decide in the same way. Accepting this case could also "sweep-in" too many other cases, as any plaintiff whose case involves drugs regulated by the FDA could try to get their case into federal court. On balance the issues to be decided seem to be primarily state issues, so it is unlikely the case would fit under the exception.

#### *Diversity*

The complaint would allege at least $600,000 in damages, so the amount in controversy requirement of § 1332 is met. Libatius is domiciled in the state where its "nerve center" is located, which is in GA, since it directs its operations from there and its directors, officers, and scientists work there. It is also domiciled in the state where it is incorporated, which is DE. In order for diversity to exist, the Weasleys cannot be domiciled in either of these states.

The Weasleys are living in SC. However, it is unclear that they intend to stay in SC long-term. Generally starting a business in the state would probably be enough for an assumption that they are staying in the state long-term, but their letter to their mother that they intend to return to school indicates they are not settled and may move to a different state when they are done with school. There is at least a chance that a court would find them domiciled in GA.

However, even if the court did find they were domiciled in SC, the case is still improperly in federal court, and the court should remand on the basis that the Weasleys were defendants sued in the state where they are domiciled. Under § 1367, if the sole basis of federal SMJ is diversity, the defendant cannot remove if they are sued in the state where they live. Since this is a procedural ground and it is unclear where the court would find the Weasleys to be domiciled, the remand petition should be filed within 30 days.

## Question Two

Generally statutes of limitation (SOL) are considered procedural under both of the restatements. The SC court will apply its own law to procedural questions. The SOL has run in SC, and the judge will likely dismiss on SOL grounds.

This result is completely unfair. Since the defendant in the true case is bringing the declaratory judgment, it seems unreasonable that the defendant should get to pick the SC court, which will dismiss the case and possibly prevent the Plaintiff from bringing it later. The real Plaintiff would have brought the case in GA, and the GA statute of limitations will not run until April 2012. The Plaintiff is the wronged party, which is why he or she is generally allowed to pick the forum and to act as "master of their complaint" in bringing the claims he or she feels best capture the wrong suffered. This result curtails both of these rights, with the outcome that the Weasleys may be left with no way to redress their grievances. The result is a triumph of technical rules over substantive justice.
