## Question 1

### IRJ

Pugsley can argue that the NFT is not located in ME, so Lurch cannot repossess under USPL/sue IRJ. Because NFTs are incorporeal, this is a *Harris*-style problem. The court could locate the NFT at the owner's domicile (Pugsley in MA or Lurch's PPOB in ME), Lurch's place of incorporation (DE), where the code is (ND), or where the website servers are (ME). From a policy perspective, DE is the strongest choice. Using incorporation is easily administrable for courts and predictable for litigants, as it's easy to find out where a business is incorporated. ND makes less sense since only Lurch knows where it stores its data, and it would necessarily follow that an NFT could "exist" in many places if data is in a cloud that uses multiple places. ME is not ideal since Lurch could easily move its website operations (which have little connection to contents of NFT anyways).

Even if the court decides the NFT is in ME, we could argue that *Shaffer* did not leave IRJ undisturbed. Under a broad view of *Shaffer*, ME must have IPJ over Pugsley. Pugsley is domiciled in MA and there was no consent/waiver/tag so no GIPJ. ME's long-arm goes to constitution, so there must be minimum contacts linking Pugsley's interest in the NFT, the subject matter of the litigation, and the forum — which likely is not satisfied. Pugsley may not know that Lurch maintained their website from ME — since that was not advertised. Unless the court considers one sale with a ME business enough for minimum contacts, as it admittedly did in *McGee*, Pugsley likely prevails under a broad view of *Shaffer*. It likewise does not seem fair and reasonable to hail Pugsley into ME court, as he is potentially not aware he was doing business with a ME citizen, even though ME does have an interest in protecting its citizens from nonpayment.

### PDP

Pugsley should also argue that USPL did not afford him PDP and demand a pre-seizure hearing. Pre-judgment seizures typically require a hearing (barring extraordinary circumstances which are not present here) (*Doehr*). Like the pre-judgment seizure in *Doehr*, this seizure likely violates the *Mathews* test.

The private interests of Pugsley and Lurch seem to be balanced — the facts do not suggest that either are relying on this NFT or sale money for their livelihood, and both have a property interest. This NFT is not an essential item like a house or a car.

There is a high risk of erroneous deprivation. The seizure did not require any presentation of evidence or findings of fact before the deprivation (*Vitek*) or even a court's authorization. By allowing Pugsley the opportunity to be heard prior to the seizure with written findings of

The government always has an interest in accuracy, which would weigh towards allowing Pugsley a hearing before seizure.

## Question 2

Article III, Section 2 of the Constitution and 28 U.S.C. § 1332 both address the question of DJ in the federal courts. Article III, Section 2 authorizes federal judicial power to extend to "Controversies… between Citizens of different States." The Supreme Court has interpreted this broad grant to require only *minimal diversity*. This broad constitutional scope reflects the Framers' goal of providing a neutral federal forum to protect out-of-state litigants from local bias (home cooking). However, Congress has narrowed this power by requiring *complete diversity* (at least according to *Strawbridge*). The AIC requirement further restricts access to federal court.

The policy justification for § 1332's narrowing of Article III's scope is to respect federalism and prevent the federal courts from being overwhelmed by state-law disputes. By limiting DJ to completely diverse parties, Congress sought to reserve federal courts for cases truly at risk of local bias and to reduce their overall caseload. In practice, though, the complete diversity rule poorly furthers these goals. Local bias is unlikely to disappear simply because only one of many defendants happens to share a state with a plaintiff, and the rigid rule often forces multi-party disputes with largely out-of-state interests into state court, where bias can persist. Meanwhile, federal courts are already equipped to manage their caseload through other procedural tools (remand, Colorado River stay, etc.) without such an inflexible bar.

A more balanced approach would be to amend § 1332 to permit minimal diversity, aligning the statute more closely with the Constitution's broader grant. This change would preserve the availability of a neutral federal forum in genuinely interstate disputes, especially in an era where commerce and liability often cross state lines. The downside is that minimal diversity would increase the federal caseload and could blur the distinction between state and federal judicial responsibilities. But Congress could mitigate this by pairing minimal diversity with expanded discretionary abstention standards, ensuring that federal courts hear only cases with significant interstate dimensions and risk of state court bias. Overall, relaxing § 1332's complete diversity requirement would better reflect the original constitutional purpose of protecting against local bias in multistate controversies while maintaining manageable limits on federal jurisdiction.
