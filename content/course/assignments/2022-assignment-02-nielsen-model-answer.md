Buddy can move to dismiss for lack of SMJ and for failure to state a claim under 12(b)(6), but only the latter is likely to be successful.

## (1) SMJ

Buddy can argue no AUJ under § 1331 because no federal CoA (*Mottley*). Bob will respond that there's an embedded issue of federal law — whether Buddy violated the DDDA regulation by operating the drone while drunk — which is necessary to evaluating Bob's negligence *per se* claim. (*Smith-Grable*). But the regulation's meaning isn't disputed, unlike in *Grable*; rather, the fact that Buddy was drunk (a factual issue) is. If whether ∆ violated a federal law was all that was needed to get AUJ, federal courts would be flooded with state law claims hinging on federal law violations (*Merrill Dow*), which would frustrate the federal/state balance. So no *Smith-Grable*, and no AUJ.

Buddy can also argue no DJ under § 1332 because Bob is RI resident, destroying complete diversity (*Strawbridge*). But Bob will respond that he's lived in MA for four years, owns a condo there, and has registered to vote/obtained a drivers' license there. True, merely moving to a new state (especially for a time-limited activity like college) isn't enough to confer domicile (*Mas*), and we have no info suggesting Bob has affirmatively declared an intent to remain in MA (in fact, he has declared that he intends to move to FL with his wife). But the voter registration, drivers' license, and property ownership all suggest Bob is MA citizen, so there's probably DJ.

## (2) IPJ

No GIPJ because Buddy isn't really at home in MA (he lives in RI), nor is he "at home" there (*Daimler*), having only spent a day in MA. But there's SIPJ because MA has a long-arm that goes to constitutional limits, Buddy allegedly injured Bob in MA (so there are minimal contacts linking ∆ and the forum state (*Int'l Shoe*)), and the exercise of jurisdiction is fair/reasonable in light of Buddy being close to MA, MA's interests in adjudicating disputes involving its citizens, MA's being the location of key evidence (the hospital bills, eyewitnesses), and the likely application of MA substantive law.

## (3) Rule 12(b)(6)

Under *Iqbal*, Bob's complaint likely fails plausibility pleading. Paragraphs 4–6 are legally conclusory and constitute threadbare recitals of the elements of the two negligence causes of action. Paragraph 3 is also conclusory, because it simply states that the drone "caus[ed] injuries." That's enough to allege causation and injury, because getting hit by a drone will hurt, but it doesn't specify how/why those injuries were a product of Bob's negligence (element 2 of the tort). The key fact missing is Buddy's alleged drunkenness; paragraph 3 needs to include a statement that Buddy was drunk and this drunkenness caused him to carelessly operate the drone, which in turn led to Bob's getting hit.

Yet the plausibility pleading standard of FRCP 8(a)(2) and 12(b)(6) may not apply; if MA Rules 8/12 apply (as interpreted in *Violet v. Dash*), Bob's complaint is probably fine under the old notice-pleading standard (*Dioguardi*, suggesting the complaint need only present a discernable ground for relief). Here, since Bob has alleged that Buddy violated DDDA § 12, and has specified the date/location/nature of the key event (putting Buddy on notice), the court could discern that he is alleging Buddy did something wrong; it doesn't need to know exactly what at the complaint stage.

To decide whether FRCP or state rules apply, we need *Erie*.

- *York*: if FRCP applies, dismiss; if MA rules apply, don't dismiss. Apply MA rule/don't dismiss.
- Scalia *Shady Grove*: FRCP is valid b/c Congress delegated authority to promulgate FRCP in Rules Enabling Act and the rule doesn't abridge, enlarge, or modify a substantive right. Using *Sibbach*, the rule "really regulates procedure." FRCP is applicable b/c it directly speaks to the issue at hand (pleading standards). Apply FRCP/don't dismiss.
- Ginsburg *Shady Grove*: FRCP is valid for the same reason as Scalia test. It's hard to see how FRCP 12 isn't applicable; it directly speaks to pleading standards. Unlike in other cases, the federal rule doesn't set a floor with the state adding additional requirements; the state rule is actually a floor here, and the two are thus in direct conflict, which means apply FRCP/don't dismiss.
- Harlan: Pleading requirements don't change litigants' conduct and only affect what lawyers do. But could argue that pleading requirements change primary conduct by making corporations/institutional actors behave more boldly in light of fewer frivolous suits. If true, apply FRCP/don't dismiss.
