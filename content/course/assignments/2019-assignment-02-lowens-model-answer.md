## Question 1

I advise a limited appearance to contest jurisdiction over the person/property in both cases. *Shaffer* says that quasi-in-rem and in rem suits must be assessed using the *International Shoe* framework, so we look to whether the property seized is physically in the jurisdiction, and to the extent of the contacts among the "defendant's" interest in the property, the subject matter of the litigation, and the forum (although after *Burnham*, we don't know whether how much of *Shaffer* is good law).

**A), Illinois:** Arguably in 2019 bank accounts should be considered to "be" where the account holder is, not where the bank is — some people access their accounts online. But Hermione regularly traveled to the Illinois branch, so that argument doesn't work here; the account is in Illinois.

**A), NC:** The property was in NC.

**B), Illinois:** There is a good case Hermione did not intend or want her account to be in Illinois — it's a coincidence and even an *inconvenience* that it is there. As her attorney, I would point to *Shaffer* and argue that it is unfair and unreasonable to hold that the Illinois court has personal jurisdiction over my largely sedentary Missouri client's property when she has no interest in its being there. Unfortunately, this case might distinguish from *Shaffer* because Hermione regularly traveled to Illinois to access the account, so it's harder to say she did not associate the asset with Illinois. Moreover, the bank only existed in Illinois (perhaps deliberately to be subject to fewer regulations, which allows them to offer better interest rates), so there is, arguably, something inherently Illinois-specific about it. All that said, the bank account has no relation to the underlying dispute between Hermione and WeBuyDebts.

**B), NC:** The defendants had no interest in the property being in NC; it's pure coincidence. If *Shaffer* is taken seriously, this case should probably be dismissed (though the case would be even stronger if Fenrir accidentally went to NC by a wrong turn or somehow didn't know he was in NC).

We have even stronger procedural due process claims. Before a court seizes property, there must be reasonable attempts at notice.

**Illinois:** Though Plaintiff did make an effort to contact Hermione using an address on file (a baseline set by *Mullane*), a second attempt after unsuccessful delivery may have been necessary (*Flowers*). The equities favor Hermione: (1) WeBuyDebts.com has investigative capabilities, so looking further would be easy. (2) Some people/entities, including Hogwarts Bank *and another division of WeBuyDebts*, knew her address. (3) There is reason to believe that had she been contacted, Hermione would have acted differently — she could've paid off the $400 debt with the money in her account.

**NC:** In rem for real property does not require notice because it is assumed that the owner would keep tabs on her property and realize it were seized (*Pennoyer*). But the broom is not real property. It seems like the baseline of using the address you have on file (*Mullane*) should apply, and WeBuyDebts did not mail anything to Hermione.

## Question 2

We can attack UPSC's constitutionality on due process grounds because it provides insufficient opportunity to be heard before harming the defendant. *Sniadach* and *Doehr* are informative.

<!-- page: 2 -->

First, how does UPSC weigh Plaintiff's interests in seeking a remedy? Does UPSC require showing that the property is likely to soon become unreachable? Hermione never moves and her bank account was stable for months — we have a strong case seizing it was overkill (*Sniadach*). Does it consider the parties' pre-litigation interests in the property (*Doehr*)? That factor would also cut against seizing the bank account, though not the broom.

Second, what safeguards does UPSC include to prevent erroneously harming the Defendant? Since this case concerns property, not liberty, *Vitek* factors like opportunity to present witnesses or be represented is too much to ask. But aspects of "opportunity to be heard" like written notice (see discussion of *Mullane*/*Flowers*, supra), sharing documentary evidence, and some sort of hearing, especially in 2019 when communication is virtually costless, seems like a reasonable baseline. In Hermione's cases, inadequate notice and communication impacted the bank account seizure because Hermione could (and probably would) have paid what she owed if notified (thereby also advancing a government interest in facilitating out of court settlement). The broom case was a "documents case," resolved by the contract and the defendants' failure to pay. There, a pre-seizure hearing might not have affected much. Next, under UPSC, do judges or clerks authorize seizure? Judges mitigate risk to defendants because they're less likely to make major errors in the first instance, especially if a complicated contract matter with lots of documents like the broom case. UPSC should also consider the effects that seizure will have on the defendant (see, e.g., *Eldridge*). In the case of a checking account, it could be very damaging: automatic payments or outstanding checks will be bounced, and in Hermione's case, almost all of her assets became unavailable. Additionally or alternatively, Plaintiff could have to post a bond or defendants have a chance to post a counterbond (*Sniadach*) in order to mitigate the effects of seizure.
