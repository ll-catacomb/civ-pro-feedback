# Assignment 1 Model Answer

## 1. SMJ

There is SMJ through § 1332: Groucho is a citizen of TX; Zeppo is a citizen of DE, and Sylvania is a citizen of DE and OK, so there is complete diversity. *Strawbridge*. Groucho's claim against Zeppo is AIC sufficient (250k > 75k). So is Groucho's claim against Sylvania (150k > 75k).

## 2. PJ

The defendants might move to dismiss for lack of IPJ. Neither is from TX, and neither does more than 50% of its "business" in TX, so no G-IPJ. Given 4(k)(1)(A) and the Texas long-arm to constitutional limits, we look for minimal contacts to see if we can establish S-IPJ.

**Zeppo:** There are minimal contacts linking defendant, subject matter of litigation, and forum. *Shoe*. Zeppo met Groucho, experienced "DIS," and likely stole the password from TX. It would be fair and reasonable because Zeppo has already come to TX once so can come again, Groucho's convenience is high in TX, TX has an interest in protecting its citizens, and some evidence is there (Chico and his computer).

For **Sylvania**, it has few TX customers other than Groucho, it never "purposefully availed" itself to TX (no ads, word-of-mouth business, and Groucho even signed up in SD) and "stream of commerce" is insufficient. *Hanson*. Regarding fair & reasonable, TX has an interest in regulating allegedly wrongful conduct connected to its borders, but negligence may not have happened there (see below); TX is convenient for Groucho but inconvenient for Sylvania; evidence (Chico's testimony) is in TX.

## 3. HCOL

Whether contributory negligence is available requires an HCOL analysis. The question states that the TX federal court will apply TX choice of law rules. Whether the defense of contributory negligence applies here is a substantive issue for HCOL. Texas is a 2R state, so there is a presumption in favor of *lex loci deliciti*, but § 6 factors can overcome that presumption. *Lex loci deliciti* is complicated. Per the prompt, the negligence tort was complete at the location of "Duck" when it was stolen. Where was that? The artwork is incorporeal, and it does not consist solely of its code; the code is worthless without an interface to play it, and as this case demonstrates, code can exist in a number of places. To locate incorporeal things, we consider policy arguments.

- **South Dakota:** this is where the account-holder opened the Sylvania account (and here, the place where the account-holder relinquished total control of the item), but as this fact pattern demonstrates, this location could be gratuitous, depending on the vagaries of travel plans, and thus is both indefinite and accidental.
- Perhaps an analogy to *Harris v. Balk* suggests that an account follows the account-holder's current location (**Texas/California**). This is definite and ascertainable and might

comply with the holder's expectations. But it might not comply with company (Sylvania) expectations and means the location will be transient/uncertain.

- **South Carolina or Massachusetts:** "DIS" cannot usefully be in two places at once (or the judicial system would lack the certainty it wants regarding where the item is) and cannot be fractioned. Therefore, Boston, MA, and Columbia, SC (where Sylvania keeps records) makes little sense here.
- **Delaware or Oklahoma** are ascertainable and definite, and they have connections to the events. OK is likely stronger because Sylvania's principal place of business is a reasonable proxy for its system of records. Either choice might be fair to Groucho because he can be deemed to have had knowledge of these locations when he chose Sylvania as his storage. An analogy to *Harris v. Balk* may suggest the storage is a sort of debt owed to the account holder.

§ 6 factors likely won't persuade a court to apply TX law, which would allow for the contributory negligence defense. One can argue the center of gravity is TX, but Sylvania's negligence likely did not occur there (more likely that it occurred in OK, where the board of directors made decisions, or CA, where Zeppo opened Groucho's account). TX does not have a huge interest over conduct, or lack of (negligence), that happened elsewhere. Moreover, this rule is loss-allocating rather than conduct-regulating, so they may not have a strong interest in using their law. It also likely does not comply with the parties' expectations, since TX location was fortuitous to Sylvania.

If *lex loci deliciti* does not yield OK, § 6 factors might persuade a TX court to use OK law (no contributory negligence defense). The alleged negligence likely occurred there, where Sylvania's board of directors likely took the decision not to require 2FA. OK likely has an interest in how businesses conduct themselves within its borders, evidence about the relevant decisions is there so it may be in the interest of the judicial system, and it complies with the parties' expectations (see above).
