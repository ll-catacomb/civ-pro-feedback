# Assignment 2 – Model Answer 02

## Part 1: Legal Standards and Applicable Facts

§ 1391 governs MegaShop's motion to dismiss. In conjunction, § 1391(a) and (c) mean that venue lies in the District of South Carolina if that court has personal jurisdiction over MegaShop. The district court has in personam jurisdiction if the state court does. F.R.C.P. Rule 4(k)(1)(A).

Since the South Carolina long-arm statute extends to the limits of the U.S. Constitution, the Patils need only show that South Carolina state jurisdiction over the defendant would be consistent with Due Process: that the defendant has made "minimum contacts" with the forum state. *International Shoe*. They can argue under a theory of general or specific jurisdiction.

The Patils' strongest argument is that there is general jurisdiction. There are numerous Walmart stores, Sam's Clubs, distribution centers, and thousands of Walmart employees in South Carolina. This is far more than what was found insufficient in *Helicopteros*.

The Patils may also be able to argue that there is specific jurisdiction by showing limited contacts between the defendant and the forum. Such contacts might include (a) its sale of a product to a South Carolina resident, (b) its sale of a product to a resident who started her trip in South Carolina, or (c) its sale of a product that depicted beach scenes from a particular area, including South Carolina. The first two contacts above likely fail since neither shows that MegaShop was seeking the benefits or protections of South Carolina such that it "purposefully availed" itself of the forum. See *Hanson v. Denckla*. In addition, the fact that the lamp exploded does not seem to arise out of any of these contacts, which is required under a theory of specific jurisdiction. As a result, a theory of specific jurisdiction is unlikely to succeed.

In addition to showing that the defendant had minimum contacts with the forum state, the plaintiffs will also need to show that the exercise of in personam jurisdiction over the defendant would not offend notions of "substantial justice and fair play." *Burger King*. Since South Carolina law is unlikely to apply in this action (the accident did not occur in South Carolina and the evidence is not in South Carolina), one might argue that it is unfair for the trial to be held there. Nonetheless, MegaShop is a large company and can afford to defend far from home — especially since it has such a large presence in South Carolina. In addition, the interest of the forum state in providing redress to its citizens and the interest of the plaintiff in obtaining relief in a convenient forum suggest that the exercise of in personam jurisdiction would be fair.

## Part 2: Additional Facts and Discovery Plan

To defend against the motion to dismiss for lack of venue, the Plaintiffs should focus on obtaining information relating to: (1) the nature and extent of MegaShop's business in South Carolina; (2) the market in which it sells the lamp; and (3) the manufacture and design of the lamp. The first category of information will go to establishing the pervasiveness of MegaShop's contacts in South Carolina (to help support a theory of general jurisdiction). The second category of information will go towards proving that the defendant purposefully availed itself of the South Carolina forum. The third category will establish whether the manufacture or design of the lamp took place in South Carolina, thus enabling a showing that the cause of action arose out of those contacts.

To obtain these facts, the Plaintiffs should request document productions relating to the above categories of information, submit interrogatories to MegaShop's management, and depose several of the defendant's employees. The employees deposed should include those involved in the manufacture and design of the lamp, the sales heads responsible for determining where the lamp will be sold, and regional management that could provide information on MegaShop's activities in South Carolina.
