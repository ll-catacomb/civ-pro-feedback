## Question 1

**(a)**

Ginny would not prevail for lack of SMJ, IPJ, venue, or service of process: Nebraska District Court had SMJ because complete diversity existed (Ginny = Nebraska, Aberforth = Maine) and AIC was sufficient ($150,000). General IPJ existed because Ginny is a domiciliary of Nebraska. Venue similarly existed under § 1391(b)(1) (resident clause). Ginny was served personally. FRCP 4(e)(2)(A).

Aberforth could transfer the claim to Maine District Court because he would have been able to originally bring suit there. For IPJ, Maine's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the Constitution. The Maine federal court can piggyback on that statute. FRCP 4(k)(1)(a). IPJ will likely exist in Maine because Ginny purposely availed herself of Maine's laws and had minimum contacts. *International Shoe*. Maine was Ginny's destination. She stayed there overnight to attend a local beer festival. The events giving rise to a lawsuit occurred there. *Hess*. *Cf. WW Volkswagen*. Venue in Maine will exist because Ginny hit Aberforth with her car there. § 1391(b)(2).

**(b)**

Ginny would prevail on a 12(b)(6) motion: Before *Twiqbal*, Aberforth's complaint would have stated a viable claim. It includes (1) grounds for jurisdiction; (2) a short and plain statement showing that he is entitled to relief (that Ginny "negligently drove a car against the plaintiff"); and (3) a demand for relief ($150,000), in accordance with Rule 8(a) and Form 11. However, *Twiqbal* requires a claimant to allege material and essential facts that show the claim is plausible on its face (e.g., that Ginny ran a red light or was not paying attention to the road, i.e., breach of duty). If the district court were to cross out the conclusory statements (i.e., that Ginny was negligent) in Aberforth's complaint, it would leave nothing but the place and date of the accident. The complaint therefore does not state a claim upon which relief can be granted and should be dismissed.

**(c)**

Ginny would prevail on limitations grounds: Because Nebraska court was a happy court (IPJ and venue existed), Nebraska's choice-of-law rules apply. There are two *Erie* issues: whether the federal discovery or the Nebraska misconduct rule should apply and whether the federal one-year limitations (FLPA) or the Nebraska two-year limitations should apply.

*Discovery/Misconduct:* York — if FLPA applies, the outcome would be different and state law should apply; if Nebraska limitation applies, the outcome would be the same and federal law should apply. *Hanna* — common law rule, look at the twin aims of *Erie*; applying the federal discovery rule could lead to inequitable administration and forum-shopping because it would provide a more lenient rule for out-of-state plaintiffs (although on these facts it doesn't matter, could imagine facts on which it would; e.g., if Aberforth had not discovered his injury until April 2016 he could not have sued Ginny in Nebraska state court, but could in federal); state law should apply. *Harlan* — would alter pre-

litigation conduct; Aberforth might have waited to be examined because of the discovery rule.

*Limitations:* York — if misconduct rule applies, the outcome would be different and state law should apply; if discovery rule applies, the outcome would be the same and FLPA should apply. *Hanna* — FLPA is an applicable federal statute which is valid under the Constitution pursuant to Congress's Art. III power to create the lower federal courts; FLPA should apply. *Harlan* — no primary conduct effect; FLPA should apply.

The court should apply FLPA because Congress has passed a federal statute on the question and the state misconduct rule because applying the federal discovery rule would violate the twin aims of *Erie*. Aberforth's lawsuit should therefore be dismissed as outside the limitations period. However, if the court decides to apply either Nebraska's limitations period or the federal discovery rule, then Aberforth's lawsuit would not be dismissed on this basis.

## Question 2

**(a)**

Aberforth is a simple goat-herder, with no specialization in the law and possibly no lawyer. His complaint accords with the plain language of the federal rules (Form 11 and Rule 84) and should be honored. Moreover, it is unfair to deny him the opportunity for discovery, which might reveal that Ginny was drunk or not wearing her glasses when she hit Aberforth, facts he could not otherwise know. On the other hand, *Twiqbal* was designed to weed out meritless litigation, and perhaps Aberforth has no valid reason to believe that Ginny was driving negligently when she hit him. However, in a simple negligence suit, we aren't really worried about the expense of discovery. Therefore, his complaint should have been allowed to proceed.

**(b)**

The double-*Erie* issue in this case creates a hybrid law that frustrates the schemes for which the laws were originally created. If the Nebraska legislature had known that a one-year limitations rule would apply with its misconduct rule, it might have opted for the discovery rule instead. If Congress had known that a misconduct rule would apply with its one-year statute, it might have made the limitations period longer. It would be better policy for a court to evaluate the state and federal schemes as a package, rather than two separate *Erie* issues, thereby preserving the intent of one legislature, rather than frustrating both. Under either scheme applied as a whole, Aberforth's claim would not be dismissed.

Because Congress passed a statute for this particular occasion, courts should honor the federal scheme. Although this frustrates *Erie*, the Constitution contemplates that out-of-state plaintiffs would be able to avail themselves of federal courts, and Congress has

chosen to exercise its authority to provide a special rule for federal plaintiffs on this occasion. Supremacy concerns would suggest that federal law should therefore win out.
