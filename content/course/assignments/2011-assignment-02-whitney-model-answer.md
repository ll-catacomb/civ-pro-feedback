## 1.

### What would the answer to this question be under the three "Erie tests"?

Federal courts sitting in diversity apply state substantive law and federal procedural law. *Erie* helps us figure out what counts as "substantive" and what counts as "procedure."

*York* = "Outcome Determinative" Test. "Would applying the federal rule change the outcome of the case?" If it would → substantive → apply state law.

**Application:** Are pleading standards outcome determinative? We know (especially given the Durmstrang Supreme Court's articulation of *why* it was rejecting code pleading) that pleading standards can be outcome determinative; some cases that would make it through a 12(b)(6) under notice pleading get kicked out under code. Thus, outcome determinative → state law.

**Hanna Rule:** Two tests:

(1) if there's a valid and applicable federal law on point → apply it.

Difficulty: how widely do you interpret the federal rule (*Walker*);

(2) If the federal law isn't directly on point → use twin aims of *Erie* (avoid forum shopping, avoid inequitable administration).

**Application:** We are likely in (1) land because the two laws conflict. Though, argue that the federal move to code pleading should only apply to super complex (*Bell Atlantic*) and national security-ish (*Iqbal*) cases.

Also ask whether the federal rule is valid under the REA. SCOTUS can lay down general rules of procedure for district courts but those rules can't abridge, enlarge (etc.) substantive rights. Under *Hanna*, ask if the rule "really" regulates procedure (i.e., regulates the judicial process for enforcing rights and duties recognized by substantive law). Pleading standards sure seem to. Next, ask if the rule would modify (etc.) substantive rights. Court will likely say it doesn't (as far as I know, the Court has never knocked a rule down at this step). Thus, federal rule ok.

**Harlan Concur Rule:** Use state law if using federal would substantially alter primary conduct.

**Application:** As the State Supreme Court articulated, code pleading standards don't adequately deter harmful conduct. That "harmful conduct" is primary conduct. Thus, state notice pleadings apply.

### What role *should* the RDA play?

The RDA says that state law provides the "rules of decision" in diversity suits. But what counts as state law? Is it just what the states have written down or does it include state high court interpretations of the law? Answer: "state law" includes state high court interpretations of state rules. So, looking at the RDA in relation to *Erie*, the federal judge must treat the state high court's interpretation of the pleading standards as state law.

### What role *should* limits on D's ability to remove play?

§ 1441(b) prohibits defendants from removing to federal court diversity cases if ANY defendant is sued in his home state.

We're in federal court. That means the defendant was either (1) sued in federal court directly or (2) was sued in state court but, being not domiciled in Durmstrang, subsequently removed to federal court. Plaintiffs are, for the most part, the masters of their complaint — so what should we make of this?

It may depend on how this case got to federal court. Under (1), the plaintiff was willing to run the risk of heightened pleading standards by suing directly in federal court. What incentivized that? Better jury pool? More favorable judges? If that's the case, the judge may feel less likely to see the pleading standards, at least in this case, as that important (since the P likely crosses the plausibility threshold). (2) might be more interesting. Imagine the case of the disadvantaged P who was harmed by out-of-state-D's product. Say minimum contacts have been met and P can sue D in P's own state. D can now remove to federal court and disadvantage P. In that case we, especially for torts, feel particularly sympathetic to P and, accordingly, think out-of-state-D's strategizing shouldn't work. So, in the end, the story behind how they ended up in federal court sways our sympathies.

## 2.

First, given this is arising under, D could remove to federal court. Here they aren't. So, they likely think the pleadings cross the plausibility standard and are interested in other state-court-benefits. In that case, it just might not matter which pleading standard is used. Thus, forum shopping concerns are limited. But, we're talking about a federally created cause of action. Above we spent time debating that pleadings are really substantive. If that's the case and federal courts have required code pleading, perhaps we should consider the substantive law itself (the "merits") to have a built-in higher getting-in-the-door nature. If that's the case, by lowering the pleading standards state courts are, in essence, not only inequitably administering the law but, we might say, changing substantive federal law itself. Federalism concerns. But, even if you clearly think the "merits" of a case have nothing to do with whether a P can prove them before discovery, you're still faced with inequitable administering of the law. And, if you have a normative problem with the federal court applying code pleadings in diversity for that reason, and are thus twisting *Hanna* to find a way out of *Iqbal*, it's hard to see how one can also hold, with much intellectual consistency, that states shouldn't apply federal procedure to federal causes of action.
