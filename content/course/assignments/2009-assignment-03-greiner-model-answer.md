# Assignment 3: Alternative Response

*[The TAs all contended that federal law should govern the preclusive effect of a state rendering court's judgment in an arising-under case. The following is a draft response that goes the other way.]*

Although the question does not concern a federal court sitting in diversity, *Erie* principles have never been limited to the diversity jurisdiction context. Rather, per Harlan's concurrence in *Hanna*, *Erie* and its progeny have been understood to articulate basic principles of our federalist system. For this reason, *Erie* is cited as the foundation of the understanding that a state's highest court is the ultimate arbiter of what state law is, even if a state-law issue arises outside the context of a diversity case (which happens in, for example, criminal law cases involving the interaction between state criminal law and federal constitutional protections). Thus, *Erie* principles, with federalism in the background, are relevant here. Concerns of forum-shopping and inequitable administration of laws might lead one to believe that the same rule should govern the preclusive effect of a judgment in a case arising under federal law over which state courts have concurrent jurisdiction, regardless of whether the rendering court is a state court or a federal court.

That said, Congress exercises great power over the distribution of federal/state power in our system, and that power should be at its height when Congress creates the cause of action. What choices has Congress made here? First, it has passed § 1738, which courts have interpreted to require that (at a minimum) subsequent courts give at least the preclusive effect to a rendering court's judgment as would be afforded in the rendering court's state. If that interpretation holds when the subsequent court is a federal court, as *Migra* suggests, then Congress has told us that federal courts will sometimes have to give more preclusive effect to a judgment from a rendering state court than they would have had the same judgment come from a rendering federal court. According to Congress, that's life.

Second, in the Primus-Segundus situation, Congress has not chosen to exercise a power it could have exercised had it wanted to assure that the preclusive effect of § 1983 suits would be governed by federal law: it could have lodged exclusive jurisdiction in the federal courts over § 1983 lawsuits, the way it has with federal antitrust laws (for example). If state courts were unable to adjudicate § 1983 lawsuits, their preclusion principles would almost never be an issue.

The arguments in the first paragraph of this response prove too much; they apply to any difference that might matter to the outcome, per the discredited *York* test. Further, all they prove is that there should be uniformity of law between state and federal courts. But as *Semtek* showed, vertical uniformity can be achieved by requiring federal courts to apply state preclusion law, and that would work if we just allowed state preclusion law to apply even in arising-under cases. That feels odd, but is it really all that odd? Prior to *Erie*, federal courts used state procedural rules to adjudicate federal arising-under cases. True, we now have the FRCP, but that is because Congress (again, Congressional power is critical here) chose to pass the Rules Enabling Act. No one would seriously contend that *Erie* requires federal courts to have their own procedural rules. So our constitutional system can certainly survive some geographic variation, even among federal courts.

All this suggests that what matters here is whether the application of state preclusion law would threaten any federal interest, which we then might balance against state interests, per *Byrd* and *Gasperini*. As a general rule, Congress in § 1738 has said that it can live with the application of state preclusion law in the federal courts when the rendering court is a state court, and under § 1738, it does not matter whether the state (rendering) court is litigating a state or federal law cause of action. Thus, while a federal interest might be threatened in a specific case, Congress did not see a threat to federal interests sufficient to enact a general prohibition against using state preclusion law in arising-under cases (to the contrary, § 1738 affirmatively requires this). And it is hard to see what serious federal interest is threatened by refusing to allow Officer Devil two bites at the apple. New York should be allowed to apply issue preclusion in Segundus' lawsuit if it so desires.
