The primary issues on appeal are denial of jury trial, insufficiency of notice provided to the defendants, and overall denial of due process in this hearing.

## JURY TRIAL

The Seventh Amendment preserves the right to a jury trial for all causes of action in which the common law provided for a jury originally. To see if a jury right attaches, we find the closest analogue to the novel claim in eighteenth century common law (although this case doesn't really involve a novel claim), paying particular attention to the remedy sought, and look to see whether that claim would have carried the right to a jury. *Chauffeurs*. The plaintiffs in this case brought one cause of action, a common-law tort negligence claim allowed for under the Act, but requested two forms of relief, damages and an injunction. The tort action, heard by the King's Bench, is a claim at law. Regarding remedy, however, we have both damages requested and an injunction; damages typically means law/jury, injunction means equity/no jury. On one hand, the letter of apology seems like a pretty trivial claim, so we should yield to the damages and provide a jury trial. However, the plaintiffs only sought a total of $4,500 in damages, so perhaps the (potentially embarrassing and business-damaging) published letter places a greater burden on the defendant, meaning no jury right and trial to an ALJ would be OK. However, under *Beacon Theatres*, the court may be forced to try the damages claim in front of a jury before entertaining the claim for injunction, although the court's emphasis on collateral estoppel of the defendant's counterclaim in a subsequent action means that that case is distinguishable. If the nature of the plaintiffs' claims otherwise entitles the defendant to a jury trial, the public rights exception does not apply. Unlike OSHA in *Atlas Roofing*, the government in this case is not a party acting as a regulator; the USEA provides only a forum for adjudication. Hence, this case is actually much closer to the court's reasoning in *Granfinanciera* distinguishing *Atlas Roofing*, and Charles Townsend would retain its right to a jury.

## NOTICE AND OTHER DUE PROCESS

As far as notice is concerned, the statute imposes upon the plaintiff only the duty to serve by regular mail to the defendant's last known address a copy of the summons and complaint within 72 hours of the date of initiation. The plaintiff arguably complied with this statute, using the last edition of the yellow pages to locate an address. However, the additional step taken by the plaintiff, namely calling the phone number attached to the last known address and finding it disconnected, is reminiscent of *Jones v. Flowers*. In *Jones*, the Court held that the returned certified mail should have signaled to the plaintiff that the address was incorrect and that additional steps were required. Here, the disconnected phone number perhaps should have signaled that the address in the yellow pages was not current. In an age of the internet, in which companies regularly keep current addresses on their websites, this rule makes sense, and the plaintiffs should have known to search for a website when their call could not be completed.

We may first look at the *Mathews* factors to see if they provide guidance about other additional process due to the defendant. On one hand, requiring the plaintiff to bring in Secretary Galvin, or at the very least the letter directed to the Boston Globe, would have dramatically reduced the risk of error in these proceedings. The agency relied on not only a conclusory, out of court statement, but a secondary source's description of this statement. However, bringing in Secretary Galvin or forcing the plaintiffs to take steps to subpoena the letter would carry significant additional cost, so the *Mathews* factors do not provide a clear outcome. We may also look to *Vitek*, and reason by analogy that if we are to find a party liable for negligence, we should at least allow that party to challenge that there was some negligence. As it stands, this holding, written up by the administrative law judge during a 90 minute recess, could potentially have preclusive effect on future lawsuits and/or USEA proceedings (think about the number of voters who might sue!) through nonmutual offensive collateral estoppel and prevent the defendant from challenging this negligence in other fora. The additional process mentioned above seems justified given what little process was granted to the defendants in this proceeding.
