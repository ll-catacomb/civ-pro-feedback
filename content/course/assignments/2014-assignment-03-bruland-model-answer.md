# Assignment 3: Model Answer

## Question 1

### Statute of Limitations

The MA federal court will use the Greiner Happy Court rule to decide which state's (MA's or NH's) choice of law rules apply — and thus, which statute of limitations governs the case. Because the injury occurred in NH, venue was proper in NH under § 1391(b)(2). Moreover, NH's long arm statute goes to the limits of the Constitution, so there was statutory authorization for IPJ under Rule 4(k)(1)(a). Minimum contacts, however, is a harder question.

Olivanders could argue that it had insufficient contacts with NH because it did not purposely avail itself of NH's laws or of the right to do business in the state (*McGee*), working instead through an independent distributor (*McIntyre*) who was contractually obligated to make her own decisions. Thus, there was no specific link to NH and liability there was not foreseeable (*World-Wide Volkswagen*). Finally, because the distributor was given only a handful of wands at a time — with identifying stickers that would almost certainly be removed — Olivanders could argue that there was a mere trickle of (unbranded) commerce flowing into NH.

Parkinson, on the other hand, could point to Olivanders's encouragement to sell in NH as evidence of purposeful availment and of the foreseeability of being sued in NH. In addition, Olivanders might have foreseen that the distributor would sell in NH from the private investigator's report. Finally, Parkinson could also argue that NH should provide the choice of law because it has an interest in protecting its citizens (as expressed in its long limitations period).

If the MA federal court decides that there wasn't IPJ in NH, MA choice of law rules will apply, including MA's statute of limitations. Thus, Olivanders's motion will be granted and Parkinson's case will be dismissed as falling outside the limitations period. If, by contrast, there was IPJ in NH, the NH court was happy, so NH choice of law rules (including NH's statute of limitations) will govern and Parkinson's lawsuit can proceed.

### Claim Preclusion

If Parkinson's lawsuit proceeds under NH law, it will involve the same claim and the same parties as the first (dismissed) lawsuit. Because the MA court dismissed Parkinson's first lawsuit on statute of limitations grounds — which did not constitute dismissal on the merits — Parkinson's second lawsuit will probably not be claim precluded. It's possible, of course, that a NH court (or, in Parkinson's case, a MA federal court applying NH law) might give the dismissal more than the minimum preclusive effect (*Hart*). However, given NH's generous statute of limitations (three times the length of MA's!) it seems unlikely that NH would give MA's dismissal additional preclusive effect on policy grounds.

### Issue Preclusion

If Parkinson's lawsuit proceeds under NH limitations law, it will not be issue precluded. Although the issue of whether Parkinson filed her lawsuit too late under MA law has already been litigated,

## Question 2

Something seems wrong about the fact that, after neglecting to bring her case for two years and then filing in a court where she was time-barred, Parkinson can simply re-file in NH federal court and then transfer to the MA federal court right across the street from the state court that had originally dismissed her case. Parkinson is clearly forum-shopping, and both her failure to bring the case sooner and her poor choice of original forum hardly suggest that she's taking the litigation seriously. Allowing plaintiffs like Parkinson to re-file and proceed gives them no incentive to stay on top of their cases and file correctly the first time around. It also drains resources from defendants and courts.

However, as wrong as this particular situation appears, there are legitimate justifications for why the system works the way it does. In this case, MA decided that dismissals on statute of limitations grounds aren't on the merits and NH decided to allow plaintiffs a generous window to file their claims. Federalism argues that MA and NH should be able to experiment with different policies on these issues and to settle upon the ones that best further their citizens' interests. Federalism also demands that these policies be respected by other states and by the federal system.

Furthermore, although something seems perverse about Parkinson's being right across the street from the court that dismissed her original claim, the fact that the federal rules allow parties to transfer within the federal system generally furthers the interest of efficiency. Similarly, the MA federal court's ability to apply NH state law respects NH state policies (again, federalism) and facilitates diversity suits (although, admittedly, it is doubtful whether the justifications for diversity jurisdiction are as strong today as they were 200 years ago).

All in all, then, although there will inevitably be cases at the edges — like Parkinson's — that make us shake our heads, there are good reasons for why the system works the way it does. Additionally, it would be a little much to expect a procedural system that worked perfectly in *all* cases.
