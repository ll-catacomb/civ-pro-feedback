## Question 1

The motion to dismiss shouldn't be granted. SMJ is ok — no AUJ because there's no federal cause of action, but DJ is satisfied because there's complete diversity (PP = Oklahoma and VD = Texas) and AIC satisfied ($200,000 > $75,000).

Because court sitting in diversity, need to see 1) which limitations period applies and 2) if TXRCP 11 applies under *Erie*.

### SOL

The federal 4-year limitations period probably applies. Under *York*, the TX 2-year limitations period would apply because the limitations period determines the outcome of the litigation (if it applies → case dismissed; if it doesn't apply → not dismissed) and is therefore substantive so state law applies. Under Harlan's concurrence in *Hanna*, the 4-year period under federal law should apply because different limitations periods don't really affect pre-litigation primary conduct and therefore aren't substantive so fed law would apply. It's arguable that a limitations period might affect primary conduct like insurance coverage but it's more likely just time keeping. The *Hanna* majority and *Stewart v. Ricoh* point strongly towards applying the federal 4-year limitation because it is imposed by statute so 1) it's presumed valid and 2) it's applicable to the case because this is a civil action in a diversity court. Supremacy Clause states it trumps state law and therefore the 4-year period will apply. Both Scalia's and Ginsburg's opinions in *Shady Grove* would align with this.

### TXRCP 11

FRCP 11 probably applies. Under *York*, TXRCP 11 applies because what signature requirements are in place determines the outcome of the litigation (if TXRCP 11 applies → case dismissed; if FRCP 11 applies → case not dismissed). Under Harlan's concurrence in *Hanna*, FRCP 11 applies because signature requirements don't impact pre-suit primary conduct, so the rule is procedural and fed procedure rules apply. Under *Hanna* majority, because FRCP 11 conflicts with TXRCP 11 (TXRCP requires signatures on each page, FRCP doesn't), FRCP will be used if it's valid and applicable. FRCPs are presumed valid. Scalia would find FRCP 11 applicable because it "really regulates" procedure; we don't even need to look at TXRCP in this case. Signature requirements on pleadings are about as procedural as you can get and also don't abridge or modify any substantive state rights. Ginsberg would read FRCP 11 narrowly to accommodate the state law policy — here, to encourage attorneys to review pleadings before filing. Since FRCP 11 doesn't ban or even speak to additional signature requirements, she would probably find TXRCP 11 compatible with FRCP 11 and apply it.

Because both the federal 4-year limitations period and FRCP 11 probably apply, the motion to dismiss shouldn't be granted.

## Question 2

Scalia's construction of the *Hanna* majority test leads to equitable outcomes in both federal-state law conflicts present in Question 1.

With respect to FRCPs: they should control, as long as they are valid and "really regulate procedure." FRCP 11 should apply in this case and all other cases. It "really regulates procedure"; it does not abridge or modify any state substantive rights and therefore also doesn't encourage forum shopping. It does not bar any potential plaintiff from vindicating their rights in court. It may entail marginal federalism and separation-of-powers concerns in that the federal rules override state rules in district courts sitting in diversity, but this intrusion on state interests is slight, and a bright line, uniform rule that procedural rules apply across the board is workable and efficient. In contrast, Ginsberg's approach to accommodate state law policy in whether or not federal rules should apply is unworkable. Different federal courts would apply their federal procedural rules differently depending on the states they sit in; whether federal or state procedures applies would depend on how well states articulate the state policy behind their procedural rules rather; and judicial economy would be diminished by potentially requiring the litigation of every federal rule with respect to every state policy. Similarly, *York*'s "outcome determinative" test should not apply, as basically *any* procedural issue — from signature requirements to the paper size of pleadings — could affect the outcome of the case and therefore be substantive. Therefore, as long as FRCPs just regulate procedure and don't alter substantive rights, they should apply.

Similarly, ultimately federal statutes, as long as they are valid and applicable, should trump state laws under the Supremacy Clause. In the present case, it's unclear if Texas's limitations period is set by statute, common law, or even state constitution, making this a slightly harder case. The preemption of state common law is more palatable than preemption of a state constitutional provision. But what is clear is that the federal statute implicates the justifications for why *Erie* was created to begin with. The federal statute undermines federalism by altering substantive rights set by the state, like when a plaintiff can and can't sue. Further, the statute undermines equal protection under the law and implicates forum-shopping concerns, affording some plaintiffs a much longer time to vindicate their rights than others. However, there are also much stronger constitutional concerns regarding the federal government's authority to promulgate laws that are necessary and proper to carry out its functions. Sometimes this will require state laws to yield to federal law and even lead to inconsistent results between plaintiffs depending on where they are suing. Yet this inconsistency and intrusion on state substantive law cannot and should not surpass the constitutional grant of authority to Congress nor the Supremacy Clause.
