LB will seek a declaratory judgment that it was not negligent per se. There are four courts where this judgment could possibly be sought: NY State, NY Fed, NJ State, and NJ Fed Ct.

## NJ State

SMJ and venue are settled because it is a state court. There is likely general IPJ over PC because she is still domiciled in NJ. Though she is attending school in NY her domicile has not changed because there is a definite leaving point, i.e. graduation (*Mas*).

## NY State

As above, SMJ and venue are settled. There is likely not general IPJ over PC unless she is tagged in NY (*Burnham* plurality). More likely, by getting a bank account in NY and getting a credit card with a company that has its principal place of business and is incorporated in NY, PC purposefully availed herself of the laws and protections of NY (*McGee*) and because the alleged tort occurred in NY (*Griffis*) there are likely minimum contacts (*Int'l Shoe*). NY's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the constitution. Therefore, likely IPJ over PC.

## NJ Fed

IPJ is established as stated in the NJ State analysis via Rule 4(k)(1)(A). For SMJ, this is a state cause of action as § 2201 does not create a federal cause of action (Coercive Action Rule). Because CFPB promulgated the regulation that was violated there is possibly an embedded federal issue (*Smith*/*Grable*). Consumer protection, especially of especially vulnerable consumers, could certainly touch on serious federal interests, especially seeing as Congress was so concerned that it created an agency to promulgate regulations. But it is also likely that allowing everyday negligence claims to come into federal court could very well shift the balance of the federal-state caseload, sweeping many cases from state courts to federal courts, even where it is limited to credit cards sold over the internet. Overall, it is unlikely that a court would find an embedded federal issue here, but it is possible.

Even if there is no *Smith*/*Grable* exception there may be diversity jurisdiction. As established above, PC is almost surely still domiciled in NJ and LB is a citizen of NY because its nerve center is in NY (*Hertz*), and is incorporated in NY. This establishes complete diversity. The federal court, sitting in diversity, would apply the HCoL rules of the state where it sits (*Klaxon*). Applying the First Restatement, the court would locate PC's monetary injury in NY. The court would then apply the substantive law of NY, which would allow punitive damages and therefore the AIC would be met (no conflict with federal law so no *Erie* analysis). It is possible that a court could find that the injury was in NJ if it considered the fact that the parents were harmed as well, or that the injury occurred where the programmers of the website were and not where the victim was, but this seems tenuous at best.

Venue is laid under § 1391(b)(1) because PC is the defendant and is domiciled in NJ.

## NY Fed

IPJ over PC is established as analyzed in the NY State section via Rule 4(k)(1)(A).

Venue will likely be properly laid under § 1391(b)(2) because the application was filed in NY, the injury occurred there, and in short, a substantial part of the events took place in NY.

SMJ can get tricky. The *Smith*/*Grable* analysis is the same as above. The only difference is HCoL. NY uses the Second Restatement (*Babcock*) and will give the most weight to where the injury occurred but balance that against NY's interests in governing conduct within its territory, NJ's interest in protecting its citizens, and convenience (location of witnesses, evidence, documents, etc.). Because "where is the injury" carries the most weight, the court will likely apply NY law, allowing punitive damages (no conflict with federal law so no *Erie* analysis). There is an unlikely scenario in which NY could determine that NJ has such a pressing interest that NJ law should be applied. In that case punitive damages would be prohibited and SMJ would fail — the case would be AIC-deficient.

Finally, LB will have a tough decision to make about where it wants to file. All four courts will likely apply NY substantive law and will thus allow punitive damages. If federal courts really are more pro-big bank, then the best would be NJ Fed Ct. PC cannot dispute IPJ there. The pro-big-bank NY Fed Ct gives LB the best chance of getting the court to apply NJ substantive law, and venue will almost surely be laid properly.
