## Question

*Semtek* (CB page 1348) held that federal common law governed the preclusive effect of a judgment of a federal court sitting in diversity, but that the federal common law rule would be to borrow the state preclusion law of the state in which the rendering federal court sat. Thus, *Semtek* involved the preclusive effect of a judgment by a federal court adjudicating a state cause of action. What should the rule be in the reverse situation? That is, when a state court adjudicates a federal cause of action, what source of law should govern the preclusive effect of the resulting judgment, and what should the content of that law be?

To sharpen this question, imagine the following situation:

Suppose New York state law provides that non-mutual offensive collateral estoppel should be applied whenever possible, without regard to whether the plaintiff seeking to invoke the estoppel could have joined in the prior lawsuit. New York gives judges no discretion on this point. Thus, as a matter of state law, New York does NOT follow the limits on non-mutual offensive collateral estoppel that the Supreme Court articulated in *Parklane Hosiery* (CB page 1313), which stated the federal (common) law on this issue. Assume that New York state procedural law has a summary judgment procedure identical to that of Fed. R. Civ. P. 56.

Two friends, Primus and Segundus, were walking down a street in Manhattan when they were stopped by two cops, Officer Devil and Officer Angel. The officers were looking for thieves who had burgled a residence in the area. What happened next is a matter of dispute. It is undisputed that Officer Devil eventually used a police taser on both Primus and Segundus in arresting them. Primus and Segundus contend that the use of the taser was unjustified. Officer Devil contends that the two friends were drunk, responded belligerently to simple questions, and subsequently resisted arrest, forcing him to use the taser. Officer Angel contends that the two friends and Officer Devil all lost their tempers, and that the whole thing was an unfortunate incident that spiraled out of control. No charges were ever filed against either Primus or Segundus.

Primus filed a § 1983 action (alleging police brutality) against Officer Devil and Officer Angel in New York State court. Officer Devil removed the case to federal court, but the federal court granted Primus' motion to remand because Officer Angel had not agreed to the removal (in fact, Officer Angel wanted to litigate in state court). The case went to trial. A jury found that Officer Devil was liable for police brutality under § 1983, finding specifically that the two friends did not pose a physical threat sufficient to justify the use of a taser. The jury found, however, that Officer Angel was not liable.

After the Primus case was over, Segundus filed his own § 1983 action against both Officer Devil and Officer Angel in New York state court. Segundus moved for summary judgment on the issue of liability against Officer Devil, arguing that the issue of whether Officer Devil justifiably used a taser had been fully litigated in Primus' lawsuit.

---

What do you think the law should be, with respect to Segundus' summary judgment motion, in this sort of situation? Should New York state, federal common law, or something else govern the preclusive effect of the Primus judgment? If federal common law, should the federal common law rule be to allow the New York state court to use the New York law requiring non-mutual offensive collateral estoppel? Or should the federal common law be to require the New York state court to use the rule articulated in *Parklane Hosiery*, which would prevent the New York state court from applying non-mutual offensive collateral estoppel?

**NOTE:** This is a "should" question. It asks what the law SHOULD be in light of the policies and concerns discussed throughout the course, not what the law is. The quality of your thoughts is what matters. You will get no credit for doing research attempting to figure out what the law "is" in this situation.

In case it helps, here are some things you might want to consider in your answer (you need not address all of these questions, or indeed any one of them — these are just to help your thinking):

- Which of the policies *Erie* attempted to further are implicated by the fact situation above, and the general question being asked?
- *Hart v. American Airlines* (CB, page 1334) posed somewhat similar issues (although the posture there was different — how?), and purported to decide the case by reference to state policies. Are the ideas upon which the *Hart* court relied legitimate? How do they relate to *Colorado River*?
- It is clear that state courts use state procedural law when litigating state court lawsuits that involve a federal claim. Is that helpful here?
