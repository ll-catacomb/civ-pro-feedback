# Assignment 2 Model Answer

## Question 1

### 12(b) Motion to Dismiss

#### SMJ

There is SMJ under §§ 1331, 1367. Chani's claim against Leto arises under the federal RRA, and serves as an anchoring claim for the state negligence claim against Duncan, which arises out of the same case or controversy. The § 1331 anchoring claim makes § 1367(b) irrelevant. The court would likely apply supplemental jurisdiction because no novel issues of State law are raised, the state claim doesn't predominate over the anchoring claim, and there are no other compelling reasons for denying jurisdiction. *Gibbs*, § 1367(c).

#### IPJ

**Leto**

NY has GIPJ over Leto because Leto is domiciled, incorporated, in NY.

PA has GIPJ because its PPOB is in PA.

**Duncan**

NY likely has SIPJ over Duncan. NY has a long-arm statute that goes to constitutional limits and FRCP 4(k)(1)(a) allows federal courts piggyback off state courts IPJ. SIPJ is permissible under the DPC of 14A (*International Shoe*) because there are minimal contacts linking Duncan, the crash, and NY, and because the exercise of jurisdiction is fair and reasonable. Minimal contacts exist because Duncan conducted business — sale of the ticket — in NY, and served as an engineer in NY. Therefore, Duncan purposefully availed himself of NY such that he could reasonably anticipate being haled into court there (*McGee*). Duncan works in NY, he commutes to NY for work, and evidence of the alleged negligence may be in NY.

PA has GIPJ over Duncan because he is domiciled in PA.

#### Venue

Venue is improper in the NDNY. No substantial portion of events giving rise to the action took place in NDNY, § 1391(b)(2), and Duncan does not reside in NY, § 1391(b)(1), thus, we can move to dismiss or transfer the suit on this ground.

Since Duncan is a PA domiciliary and the crash occurred in Philadelphia, venue would be proper in EDPA. Also, the negligent act happened in Manhattan, thus under § 1391(b)(2), venue is proper in SDNY.

#### Failure to State a Claim

NY State and federal laws conflict on whether the suit should be dismissed based on the COL clause in the contract. If using NY law, the COL clause is invalid and only simple negligence is required, so the case will proceed. If using PA law, COL clause is invalid and recklessness is required, so the case will be dismissed. Under federal law, COL is valid, PA law applies, therefore the case will be

dismissed. The Erie issue is between NY and federal law, since a choice of federal law leads to the same result as PA law.

**York:** The difference between state law and federal law is outcome-determinative, thus NY state law would be used, and the case will proceed.

**Hanna majority (Scalia or Ginsburg):** Since the law at issue is FCL, the court will apply the twin aims of *Erie*. Federal law encourages forum shopping, because out-of-state plaintiffs seeking to enforce a COL clause will file in federal court, while those seeking to avoid an unfavorable COL clause will file in state court. This also results in inequitable administration of justice since out-of-state defendants sued in state court can remove to federal court to ensure the enforcement of a COL clause, while in-state defendants would not have the option. Therefore, NY state law should apply.

**Harlan concurrence:** If COL clauses represent agreements between sophisticated parties, they are likely to affect the primary conduct of plaintiffs because they may engage in more risky behavior knowing that favorable law protects them; therefore are substantive and NY law applies. There is an argument that COL clauses don't affect primary conduct of plaintiffs in adhesion/standardized contracts because most plaintiffs lack the bargaining power to negotiate COL clauses and are unlikely to be aware of these clauses within habitual documents, like train ticket receipts. Therefore, COL would be procedural, federal law applies and the case would be dismissed.

## Question 2

The Court might transfer the case either to EDPA or SDNY. Both are happy courts because there is SMJ, IPJ, and proper venue in both.

The EDPA is better for Duncan. PA is a First Restatement state, which means that PA courts will apply the substantive law of the state where the last act required to complete the tort occurred. Here, that would be the harm caused to Chani, which occurred when the train crashed in PA. Therefore, EDPA would apply PA law, where Chani must prove that Duncan was reckless to recover, and the case would be dismissed.

SDNY could be as favorable for Duncan. NY is a Second Restatement state, so presumption of *lex loci delicti*, then consider whether the center of gravity test privileges NY law under § 6 factors. The injury occurred in PA; the conduct causing the injury occurred in NY; Duncan is domiciled in PA; the relationship between Duncan and Chani is centered in NY — § 145. However, like in *Neumeier*, ignoring PA's policy requiring proof of recklessness in a case which involves a PA domiciliary does not further the substantive law of NY and would actually result in exposure of a NY domiciliary, Leto, to greater liability. Therefore, *lex loci delicti* should apply. However, this analysis could turn out differently because the issue is the standard to be applied to whether certain (in)actions constitute misconduct, which *Babcock* said should be governed by the law of the place where the (in)actions occurred, so EDPA is clearly a better option for Duncan.
