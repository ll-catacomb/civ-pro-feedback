## Question 1

Pugsley should object to the lawsuit on the grounds that the Maine court lacks in rem jurisdiction (IRJ). Note that under either version of *Shaffer*, the Maine statutory scheme authorizes the seizure and Maine has a long-arm statute that extends to constitutional limits.

First, if IRJ survives post-*Shaffer* (via the *Burnham* Scalia opinion), IRJ is only proper if the NFT is deemed to be located in Maine. As with the debt in *Harris*, it is unclear where incorporeal objects like NFTs are "located." There are plausible arguments for North Dakota (location of Fester code/data), Massachusetts (domicile of Pugsley), Maine (PPOB of Lurch), or Delaware (Lurch's place of incorporation). Given that only one copy of the NFT exists and was always hosted on Fester's servers in North Dakota, Pugsley could argue that the NFT was located there. However, that could make customers susceptible to suits wherever a company maintains servers, which is both unpredictable and could include many states. Lurch might argue that the NFT was located in Maine because it owns Fester and has its PPOB there. But under that line of logic, Pugsley can argue the NFT was located in Massachusetts once he *purchased* it from Lurch. Delaware, therefore, might provide the best answer because a company's place of incorporation is both easy to ascertain and unlikely to change.

Second, if *Shaffer* implies that IRJ now requires an *International Shoe* analysis, Pugsley should argue that he did not purposefully avail himself to Maine law. Lurch will probably claim that Pugsley did so by transacting business with a Maine-based company (analogous to *McGee*). But Pugsley never stepped foot in Maine and perhaps had no idea that Lurch's PPOB was there. Pugsley should also argue that IRJ would not be fair/reasonable. The extent of his contacts to Maine is limited and Maine is inconvenient (commuting from Martha's Vineyard). However, Maine is convenient for the plaintiff, may be the location of witnesses (like sales representatives), and Maine has an interest in protecting corporations that operate there.

Pugsley should object to the seizure on the grounds that it violated his procedural due process rights (*Eldridge*). Both parties have substantial interest in retaining ownership of their property, and there is no indication Pugsley needs the NFT for subsistence. Compared to an alternative seizure process that provides for pre-seizure hearing and a litigation bond (*Doehr* concurrence), the USPL carries a high risk of error. No judge signs off on the seizure, no sworn affidavits are required, and the lack of bond requirement means that the market has not evaluated the claim's viability. Lurch will probably respond that there is no risk of wasting the NFT that continues to be hosted on Fester while the proceeding plays out. But there is no government/public urgency to warrant pre-hearing seizure (*Fuentes*) and the USPL does not require an immediate post-seizure hearing.

## Question 2

Article III, Section 2, Clause 1 of the U.S. Constitution and 28 U.S.C. § 1332 both address the exercise of diversity jurisdiction by federal courts. Both sources of law provide that a federal court can adjudicate a lawsuit between citizens of different states. But while Article III could extend to any dispute with minimal diversity, *Strawbridge* interpreted § 1332 as requiring complete diversity. § 1332 also imposes an AIC minimum ($75,000). Supposedly, the limits of

§ 1332 narrow the scope of DJ to cases in danger of "home cooking," where there is both much to lose and a substantial risk that state courts will be unfairly biased in favor of citizens of their own state. In my view, however, § 1332's limitations are arbitrary and no longer serve a valid purpose in twenty-first century litigation. Modern technology and transportation have facilitated movement between states and diminished the importance of state identity. At the same time, however, that interconnectedness has given rise to complex disputes that arise between citizens from many states and involve challenging choice-of-law questions. Given a state court's likely preference and expertise in applying their own law, federal courts *should* exercise DJ to specialize in adjudicating these increasingly complicated multistate disputes.

With this in mind, § 1332's AIC and complete diversity requirements prevent federal courts from exercising DJ in cases that they may be more qualified to adjudicate. If the goal is to have only exceedingly challenging choice-of-law cases appear in federal court, fine, but DJ should turn on the complexity of the dispute, not whether it exceeds an arbitrary AIC threshold or happened to meet the complete diversity requirement. The Supreme Court should reconsider *Strawbridge*. Congress should amend § 1332 to remove the AIC threshold and in its place, grant federal courts discretion to adjudicate complex disputes that meet the minimal diversity requirement. To be sure, this will cause some uncertainty among litigants and force federal courts to weigh their relative expertise in resolving each lawsuit. But federal courts can develop a balancing test of relevant factors to guide their discretion as was the case for abstention doctrine (*Colorado River*). The Supreme Court should weigh in if circuit splits develop (and therefore avoid a *Lacy*-like result).
