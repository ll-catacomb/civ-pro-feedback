**(a)** There are two main avenues available: (1) challenge the VBHA on due process grounds or (2) challenge the sufficiency of Gringotts' notice on due process grounds.

**(1)** Pati can allege that the VBHA is invalid because it provides insufficient process. Various aspects of the VBHS can be challenged.

**First**, the statute's timing may be unreasonably short, at least when looked at in the aggregate. The two-week period to answer the action, the expedited two-week period before trial, and the seven days that Patil had to vacate her home might each alone be legitimate, but together may not afford enough time to provide process. In ascertaining whether more process should be required, we look to *Matthews*. Here, where (1) the private interest — one's home, usually their largest asset — is threatened, (2) there is a high likelihood that a few more days would significantly reduce the risk of error, and (3) neither the government nor Bank have meaningful interests in *not* waiting (the extra days add no more administrative costs to the government and only marginally more cost, if any, to the bank), the *Matthews* test may point strongly in favor of providing more time, and to the conclusion that the timeframe afforded insufficient process.

**Second**, the statute denied the opportunity to appeal. While *Vitek*'s mini-code doesn't mention right to appeal as a core element of due process, its absence here further exacerbated the unduly short timeframe. Had the timeframe been short, but Patil been allowed to appeal, perhaps the scheme as a whole would have provided sufficient process. But where, as here, there is a very small window for activity and no opportunity to appeal a (perhaps hasty) decision, there is a stronger argument that due process has been denied.

**Third**, VBHA provides no right to counsel. Patil is unlikely to succeed in arguing this, alone, to overturn the ruling, because *Lassiter* suggests that a government need pay for counsel only where physical liberty is at stake (and if counsel isn't available to keep one's children, it almost surely isn't available to keep one's home). However, the absence of counsel again exacerbates the short timeframe because a lay person might need more time to investigate and to comply with court procedures.

**Fourth**, the VBHA denied the opportunity to take discovery. Again, while not required under *Vitek*, given the lack of access to counsel and the short timeframe, the inability to see the bank's evidence might have made it near-impossible for Patil to defend herself. That said, there is little factual dispute in this case, and the basis for the foreclosure is pretty obvious, so lack of discovery is, as applied, less problematic.

**Fifth**, there was no right to a jury (even though Patil doesn't want one, if it gets the ruling thrown out, it's a worthwhile challenge). The 7th Amendment doesn't apply to the states, so she had no federal constitutional jury trial right, but federal appellate judges like juries, and the absence of a jury might weigh on how a court sees the whole scheme.

**(2)** Patil can likewise allege that Gringotts failed to adequately provide notice of the suit by only posting on facebook. Though it is sufficient under the VHBA, Patil might argue that her status indicating that she would be away from the computer — and thus unable to see posts — was enough to warn Gringotts that notice via facebook wouldn't reach her, and that under *Flowers* they would need to notify her another way. On the other hand, in *Flowers* there was affirmative proof of lack of receipt, whereas here, Gringotts would have had to have taken extra steps to ascertain that Patil would be away from her computer. Still, if it is incumbent on the notice-provider in *Flowers* to read the notification that the mail was not delivered, it's not a great stretch to require similar diligence here, requiring a good-faith effort to ensure that Patil actually would see the post. In addition, *Greene* indicates that situations in which it's more likely that notice will fail may require heightened diligence. Regardless of VBHA's allowing notice-by-facebook, the nature of the medium likely requires Gringotts to be at least somewhat more conscientious.

It's particularly salient that until the facebook post, Patil and Gringotts had communicated — effectively — solely through the mail, and that it is odd and perhaps unforeseeable that Gringotts would suddenly choose to provide electronic notice. That said, while it might be fair for Patil to assume that she would receive notice by mail, and thus to assume that the scheme she set up with her mother would suffice, it's highly irresponsible for her to take a 15-day trip to the countryside when days earlier, Gringotts alerted her that they planned to foreclose. Nonetheless, Patil's foolishness doesn't relieve Gringotts of their notice requirement (see *Mennonite Board of Missions*), and so she has a strong argument here.

**(b)** In order to avoid a jury trial, Patil should only ask for an injunction — and not damages — because injunctions sound in equity, and thus have no right to a jury.
