## 1

### A

It is unclear whether *Shaffer* is limited to its facts or whether all in rem now follows *Shoe*. If the former, MA doesn't have an egregious scheme that holds property hostage to compel a GIPJ waiver. Instead, this scheme operates like garden variety in rem. If *Shaffer* means in rem now follows *Shoe*, we need a basis for IPJ over Picard or, possibly, minimum contacts among house, MA, and lawsuit's subject. Either way, we have them. MA has GIPJ over Picard. The house is in MA, and the suit is about who owns it.

### B

No. The alternative is a seizure after judgment for the bank. Use *Matthews* (*Matthews* applies to old property, *Doehr*).

#### Private Interest

Somewhat strong. Picard has life savings in home, as do many (but not all) homeowners. No litigation bond or extra damages will compensate Picard if he prevails. Becoming unhoused while litigating to get one's home back is serious hardship. And because this isn't loss of income, homeowners probably can't get new benefits to finance housing in meantime.

But this isn't welfare (*Goldberg*), people won't literally die. Borg owns most of equity in house (unlike *Doehr*, where accuser had no preexisting interest in house), so it has interest too. And Borg's interest is strong: there's legislative finding that inability to foreclose immediately is creating liquidity crisis. Borg arguing it has title to house, so its seizure proportionate (unlike *Doehr*), and it can use § 6 to argue it needs to seize now to repair and sell (thus assure liquidity of the market). Without the statutory finding, this might seem suspect, but courts should probably defer to that legislative judgment about empirical facts.

Viewed as a whole, these factors don't point decisively in one direction.

#### Risk of Erroneous Determination

Low. For another kind of case, it'd be huge. A bank just needs to bring right papers and file an affidavit, then it can seize. We have a clerk instead of a judge. And the clerk has no discretion and doesn't even get to assess merits: if paperwork proper, clerk *must* issue seizure order even if they have doubts. No litigation bond, bank doesn't owe *any* damages if it loses — there little incentive against filing borderline claims.

But this kind of eviction seems straightforward. If a mortgagor hasn't paid, they're delinquent. The bank must come with a mortgage contract showing it's entitled to foreclose if payments missed and then swear under penalty of perjury that the mortgagor missed payment. (True, it's

just allegation and not evidence (*Doehr*), but payment that *doesn't* get made doesn't create evidence.) This isn't like *Doehr*, where we'd benefit from hearing other side. All the evidence is documentary (*Matthews*), what can Picard even say to rebut it? This system bad in general, but in the unique context of document-driven mortgage, the risk of error seems low.

#### Public Interest

High. We have legislative finding that the nation facing dire threat because banks can't seize and sell quickly enough. To be fair, making Borg wait for judgment before it can seize wouldn't increase cost to government — pre-judgment seizure doesn't eliminate hearing, just changes order of steps.

Overall, factors mixed.

## 3

### A

Yes. Can sue both in DistMass.

**SMJ**

Borg suit is state-law COA against non-diverse defendant. No DivJur, needs SSMJ. Worf suit is anchoring claim. Federal COA, AUJ. The core of both suits is the allegation that the foreclosure, seizure, and eviction were improper. Borg and Worf were two parties acting together to produce all three. Suits share CNOF and are convenient triable unit, so § 1367(a) applies. Because the anchoring claim is AUJ, § 1367(b) is irrelevant. Under § 1367(c), there is no reason not to exercise SSMJ — no novel/complex issue of state law, state-law claim and federal claim are roughly equal parts of suit, no exceptional circumstances.

**IPJ**

Borg MA citizen, Worf in MA. Mass courts can exercise GIPJ over both, so DistMass can too (4(k)(1)(A)).

**Venue**

Proper in DistMass. Both Ds subject to IPJ in MA, so both citizens of MA, so venue proper in DistMass under § 1391(b)(1). All events giving rise to claim took place in DistMass so also proper under § 1391(b)(2).

### B

Picard would need to drop theft COA. We have no information on historical analogs, but we know that an injunction is an equity lawsuit, and damages mean an action at law with a jury trial right. Since Picard would seek compensatory damages for theft, he'd be seeking a legal remedy with > $20 AIC, Borg would have right to jury trial.
