# Assignment 3: Model Answer
**TA Model Answer**

Dear Judge:

## Issue (1)

We don't need to do a HCOL analysis because SOL is the exemplar instance of procedural for the broader HCOL analysis. Also no Erie; under *York*, state SOLs apply, at least when there is no competing federal SOL.

Whether to grant Olivanders' request to dismiss the lawsuit as barred by statute of limitations depends on how we come out on a Greiner Happy Court Rule analysis of the original federal court filing in NH (which has diversity jurisdiction over claim between MA-citizen Olivanders and NH-domiciled Pansy Parkinson). Venue is proper in NH because the major event giving rise to it — the explosion — occurred in New Hampshire. IPJ is slightly more tricky because it depends on if NH's long arm statute (the limits of the constitution, i.e., Minimum Contacts — *International Shoe*) can pull Olivanders into NH state court, and therefore federal court since federal courts use the IPJ rule of the state courts they sit in, Rule 4(k)(1)(A). The strongest argument is that Olivanders purposely availed himself of the NH market by encouraging Luna Lovegood to sell in NH. If NH/NH-District Court has IPJ over Olivanders, then the transferor court was a happy court, and this should be a change only in courtroom so the motion to dismiss should be denied because Pansy filed within the 6 year SOL period for NH, which applies under *Ferens*. If, however there was no IPJ, the motion should be granted because the 2 year SOL period for MA has expired.

## Issue (2)

Generally for intersystem preclusion, the second court must apply at least as much preclusive effect as the rendering court. However, it's somewhat of an open question after *Hart* if the subsequent court can apply more. The MA dismissal was 'not on the merits for claim preclusion.' So if you decide to go with the floor rule, the MA dismissal would not preclude because though this is the same claim with the same parties, the final judgment was 'not on the merits for claim preclusion,' and so would not be preclusive in MA; a policy choice explicitly made by the MA legislature when they crafted the 'limitations system act.'

## Issue (3)

If NH was a happy court then the issue of complying with the NH statute of limitations scheme has never been litigated, because NH's longer statute of limitations is a different issue. If NH is not a happy court, then we are applying the SOL of MA, which was an issue already litigated, clearly necessary to the dismissal decision between the exact same parties.

## On the "Should"

It does seem that allowing this lawsuit to go forward confounds many of the policy intentions that MA and NH had in mind by combining their SOL and preclusive effects in one bill: a happy court transfer that allows for the plaintiff to be back in MA, using the longer SOL of NH, but still rely on the lack of preclusive effect from the initial MA dismissal. It seems that the intent of the MA statute is to aggressively keep litigants from sleeping on their rights, without punishing them too much in later more lenient forums. This then might be an example of that working out just fine. Furthermore, with regards to other hypothetical plaintiffs, none could be stuck with the short end of a reverse scenario of filings/transfers that created a preclusive NH dismissal with a shorter MA SOL because the NH initial lawsuit wouldn't have been dismissed as beyond their SOL. Though it seems to confound policy intents to bundle, I don't see much practical problem with how this would play out.

This also points to questions about preclusion and SOL generally. As a policy decision it seems reasonable to deter parties who don't look up the SOL and file anyway wasting everyone's time, by not letting them just keep refiling. So maybe this preclusive effect leeway and the SOL state policy gaming more broadly is ripe for giving more preclusive effect to deter such frivolous lawsuits. On the contrary, people who file outside of SOL aren't using this filing to test claims, or game the system — they are usually just uninformed, and maybe our system should be more forgiving of that.

Finally, this raises some federalism issues, since at the end of the day we have a MA federal district court that is going to be allowing a case to go forward (on the same legal claims), that was dismissed by the MA state court that is probably just across the street. If the federal district court system of sitting in diversity and applying state laws allows for some form of gaming that enables plaintiffs to circumvent a state court decision by forum shopping/swapping, that might be a problem. However, I also think that part of the point of having so many different overlapping court systems is to let people make mistakes, or find the system that works for them so they can litigate their actual claims for relief — like a million dollar hospital bill.
