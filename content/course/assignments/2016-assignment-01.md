### Background Facts and Law

*(Note: all of the following is fictional.)*

South Carolina uses the First Restatement for horizontal choice of law. North Carolina uses the Second Restatement. Recall that, as stated several times in class, the choice of law analysis for a federal court in state A is the same as that for a state court in state A. Thus, a North Carolina court facing a horizontal choice of law problem would use the Second Restatement.

South Carolina state law requires a plaintiff who alleges that she was injured in a slip-and-fall tort accident[^1] on a business property to prove that the business proprietor was reckless in leaving the slippery conditions. This "recklessness" standard means that a plaintiff injured in a slip-and-fall accident would have to show that the business knew about the slippery conditions, but despite that knowledge, failed to clean up properly. Under North Carolina law, however, a plaintiff need only prove negligence in a slip-and-fall case. Negligence means a failure to exercise due care under the circumstances. A plaintiff can prove negligence without proving actual knowledge of the slippery conditions (by, for example, proving that the business proprietor failed to monitor a premises adequately for slippery conditions).

Both North Carolina and South Carolina state law include "quiet title" causes of action identical to the Michigan cause of action that the plaintiff asserted in *Grable v. Darue*.

[^1]: In a "slip and fall" case, the plaintiff walks on the business' premises and is injured due to slipping on unsafe, slippery floor conditions.

In late January of 2014, Congress passed (and President Obama signed) the Emergency Home Mortgage Act (the "EHMA"), Section 1 of which reads as follows:

*Section 1: Importance of this Act and the Programs It Creates*

*The Congress hereby finds that homeowners in the United States are just emerging from a crisis in paying their mortgages that was unprecedented in the history of our nation or even the world. The financial consequences of this crisis were catastrophic to the United States economy and, thus, to our national security. Accordingly, the provisions of this statute are perhaps the most important to have been passed by Congress in its history. We intend this statute to assure that no such crisis ever occurs again.*

Under the EHMA, each state was granted the power, as a matter of federal law, to change the terms of any contract or mortgage relating to a home so as to make those terms more favorable to a homeowner. The EHMA also empowered the state to issue an order transferring title of a piece of property from one person/corporation to another. The EHMA requested that each state appoint a state "mortgage czar" to exercise this modification power. Most states, including South Carolina and North Carolina, duly appointed mortgage czars, and by law empowered their mortgage czars to exercise all of the powers of the EHMA. Mortgage czars had these powers with respect to property that lay within their respective states.
