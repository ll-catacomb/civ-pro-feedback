# Assignment 3 Model Answer

## Question 1

I would not support abolishing the right to a jury trial in civil cases, but would amend it to guarantee a jury only when there is an issue of material fact.

The law/equity divide doesn't draw efficiently on judges' and juries' skillsets. Fraud actions rely on determinations of credibility, and restitution reflects fairness to remedy harms in a community — both credibility and fairness determinations are thought to be jury strengths, but here are relegated to a judge. This suggests that the law/equity and law/fact frameworks are in tension. Rather than "preserving" the jury right along law/equity, the law/fact divide should control. Juries should resolve factual and credibility disputes, where community values and diverse perspectives are particularly salient. Judges should resolve issues of law, reflecting strength in interpreting complex and sophisticated statutes and promotion of legal uniformity.

This could impact the Public Rights Exception because the jury right would no longer turn on the COA. Administrability would marshal against juries in PR cases, and the interest in federal uniformity is particularly strong when Congress creates a federal COA to displace local law. The strength of these interests suggests that the PRE from *Atlas Roofing* should be retained under the amendment.

## Question 2

**SMJ:**

§ 1331: No. There's no AUJ because battery is a state claim. § 2201 doesn't provide jurisdiction because the would-be plaintiff (Splinter) could not have brought suit. *First Federal*.

§ 1332: Yes. There is complete diversity because Rafael/April (N.J.) are diverse from Splinter/Bebop (N.Y.). *Strawbridge*. AIC is greater than $75k ($80,000 + $20,000 = $100,000) for both Bebop and Splinter individually.

**IPJ:** New York has general IPJ over Splinter and Bebop because they are "really at home."

**Venue:** Venue is proper for Splinter in S.D.N.Y. under § 1391(b)(2) because substantial events occurred in Manhattan (the attack/lecture), and he is a resident under § 1391(b)(1), (c)(1).

### 2(a)

Yes. Rafael would have an affirmative claim preclusion defense because Splinter was obligated to bring a 13(a) counterclaim in LS1.

*13(a):* Splinter had an opposing claim against Rafael arising out of the same T/O (13(a)(1)(A)), with no jurisdiction issue (13(a)(1)(B)). Splinter might argue the battery was not the same T/O because the justification defense was based on the lecture and not the battery, which happened at a different time/place. But *Heyward* tells us T/O is broad, with high interest in efficiency. The court in LS1 had to have handled evidence regarding the battery to determine whether the lecture justified it. Thus, LS1 dealt with enough similar evidence to render same T/O, making Splinter's counterclaim compulsory.

*Claim preclusion:* Because same T/O, identical parties, judgment final and on the merits (judgment after a jury trial), Splinter is claim precluded.

### 2(b)

Yes, Splinter should argue that April is claim precluded. The T/O in LS2 is identical to LS1 because the lecture and the beating are the same. Per above, the judgment was

## Question 3

I would not favor this proposal unless it incorporated PDP protections.

**Disadvantages:** Federal notice requirements are significant (at times requiring multiple notices by multiple means), reflecting a policy judgment that would-be plaintiffs are substantially burdened when they lose the opportunity to vindicate their rights. That value would be undermined if plaintiffs like Bebop lost the opportunity to claim $80k in medical bills without sufficient notice. And while there is an interest in quick resolution of lawsuits, that policy balance is already reflected in SOL periods that limit the timeframe for bringing suit. Considering *Mathews v. Eldridge* factors also suggests that insufficient notice would undermine private interests (lost claims) and raise the risk of erroneous determination, since adverse parties would not always be notified to represent their rights.

**Advantages:** There is a powerful gov't interest (*Eldridge*) in more affordable and efficient administration of the law, and a public interest in fewer lawsuits. This rule would support those interests by encouraging litigants to bring claims, leading to consolidation and reduced burden on the court system. That said, cases might become so unwieldy under this rule that courts would be forced to chop them up under Rule 42, and any administrative gain might be lost, all while risking parties' opportunities to vindicate their rights. But from a fairness perspective, the rule could lead to more uniform outcomes between individuals — for example, it might be more likely that Bebop & Splinter's claims would receive similar treatment if heard in a single case than if they were split.
