## WW's Challenges

WW should challenge the USEA's decisions on two procedural grounds. First, WW should challenge the decision as a violation of its Seventh Amendment right to a jury trial. Under *Chauffeurs v. Terry*, a court evaluates the right to a jury trial by (1) comparing the statutory action to 18th century actions brought in courts of England, determining whether the suit would have been brought in a court of equity or in a court of law, and (2) examining the remedy sought is legal or equitable in nature.

Here, the first factor could go both ways. WW will want to argue that Margolis' suit is most analogous to a historical action for negligence. WW can argue that Margolis is arguing that WW breached its duty to Margolis (and other electors) to provide enough election ballots by failing to exercise due care, and that such a breach led to Margolis' pain and suffering. USEA will reply that this is more analogous to a third-party breach, because of the contract between WW and Massachusetts. Either way, the historical analogy is muddled and does not clearly point to one over another. The second factor, however, weighs in WW's favor, as Margolis' suit was for damages, a type of relief that is clearly legal. Since *Chauffeurs* made clear that the second factor is the most important, this test seems to weigh in favor of WW.

Following *Atlas Roofing*, the USEA will likely argue that this situation falls under the public rights exception to the right to a jury trial. Such an exception applies when (1) the government is involved on one side of the dispute and (2) the government is acting as a regulator/sovereign, not an economic actor. Here, WW has a strong argument that this exception does not apply, as the government is clearly not a party — it is an action between two private parties, Margolis and WW, adjudicating private rights. Under *Granfinanciera*, such factors clearly point to a right to a jury trial.

Second, WW should challenge the decision as a violation of its 14th Amendment due process rights. Here, a court might look to *Vitek* for guidance on what is required of a new adjudicatory scheme for it to comply with procedural due process. Here, many *Vitek* factors are absent. The USEA categorically does not provide for live testimony, making its decision based solely on a written record submitted by the parties, with no in-person hearing. The written statement issued by the USEA did not elaborate on the evidence relied on and the reasons for its decision. Though the USEA provided notice, there is no further description of notice provisions, and no set procedures for a defendant's opportunity to respond. Moreover, though *Vitek* explicitly states that the lack of discovery or appeal does not *per se* render a system unconstitutional, their absence removes significant safeguards which might otherwise mitigate the unavailability of the other protections listed above.

## Margolis' Challenges

Margolis should challenge USEA procedures on right to counsel grounds, especially because she explicitly requested counsel in her petition before the USEA. This argument is likely an uphill battle. Per *Lassiter*, there is no guarantee to counsel in civil proceedings; even further, the case illustrates the presumption that there is no right to counsel unless personal liberty is at stake. In any case, the controlling test here is that in *Mathews v. Eldridge*, which weighs (1) the affected private interest(s), (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation, and (3) the government's interest as counterbalanced against the private interests implicated.

Here, the private interest at stake — whether or not Margolis will receive damages for her pain and suffering — seems relatively minor, especially in light of *Lassiter*, which denied

counsel in a case involving parental rights. If the termination of parental rights is not a private interest of sufficient importance to warrant counsel, it is unlikely that Margolis' request for damages — though arguably required to make her whole — is enough to overcome *Lassiter*'s presumption, especially since no personal liberty is at stake.

Though the risk of erroneous deprivation may seem high, since Margolis herself states that she knew very little about the law when she initiated the proceeding, USEA will likely argue that there is no risk of "deprivation" since Margolis is simply asking for damages. However, if measuring two counterfactual states of the world — one in which Margolis has access to counsel and one in which she does not — it seems likely that Margolis would secure a better outcome in the former.

Finally, the government has a significant interest in adjudicating election disputes quickly, because of the extraordinary importance of electoral integrity. Moreover, the cost of providing counsel to all those aggrieved would be noteworthy, especially in relation to the relatively minor interest involved. Thus, *Mathews* would likely point to no right to counsel for Margolis.
