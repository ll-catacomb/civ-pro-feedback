## Facts

Assume the following (all parts of which are totally fictional).

Both New Hampshire and Massachusetts have long-arm statutes allowing their court systems to exercise jurisdiction "in any case in which such exercise would be consisting with the United States Constitution."

In January of 2007, Congress passed the National Medical Malpractice Reform Act (the "NMMRA"). The NMMRA took a wide variety of steps affecting the medical profession and regulating the provision of malpractice insurance. As relevant here, however, the NMMRA also included certain provisions regarding the establishment of a national registry of radiological images.

---

**Section 601: Findings:** The Congress finds that the process of detecting cancerous masses suffers from the lack of a national depository of radiological images that would allow radiologists to compare images for a particular patient to images in the depository that are known to be cancerous or non-cancerous. The lack of a depository causes thousands of mistaken or missed diagnoses of cancer each year, costing the Medicare and Medicaid programs millions of dollars and having a deleterious effect upon interstate commerce.

**Section 602: Establishment of a National Registry:** The Secretary of Health and Human Services is hereby directed to establish a National Registry of radiological images. The Secretary shall establish a classification system of such images, indexing them in a manner that he shall determine.

**Section 603: Deposit of Electronic Images:** Any provider of medical services who receives any funds under the Medicare or Medicaid programs, and who produces, receives, examines, or obtains any radiological image of any patient, shall forward an electronic copy of that image to the National Registry, along with all information about the patient required to enable the Secretary to index the image pursuant to Section 602 of this statute.

---

As soon as the NMMRA was passed, several medical providers announced that they would refuse to comply with portions of it. One of these providers was St. Mungo's Medical Center. "St. Mungo's Medical Center" is actually just the doing-business-as name of St. Mungo's, Inc., a corporation incorporated in Delaware and whose sole facility was an enormous, multi-purpose medical center in Boston, MA. All of St. Mungo's directors and officers maintain their offices in Boston.

Shortly after the passage of the NMMRA, St. Mungo's chief executive officer Madame Poppy Pomfrey issued a series of press releases. Irma Pince was born in New Hampshire in 1969. She suffered from grinding poverty through much of her life. She succeeded in graduating from high school at age of 18, but from then until after age 40, she was homeless, and she lived either on the streets or in homeless shelters in either Boston, MA or Concord, NH,[^1] depending on when she wanted a change of scenery and when she could get a ride from one place to the other. In early 2009, she stopped going regularly to Concord, and lived continuously in a particular homeless shelter located in Boston. From 2009 to early 2011, she traveled to Concord twice. Both times were around Christmas, when she got a ride to Concord, where she stayed for about two weeks. There, she saw friends and her only surviving family, an older brother. Her older brother was also homeless, and he lived his entire life on the streets of Concord.

In early 2011, a librarian at the Boston Public Library took an interest in Pince. This librarian found Pince a rich patron, who lived in Concord. The rich patron provided Pince a small income that would continue on two conditions: (i) she must enroll in college and live in college dorms during the school year, and (ii) she must come to Concord during the summers to live in a room in the patron's house. Accordingly, Pince enrolled in the UMass Boston program of library sciences in the Fall of 2011. She spent the summers of 2011 and 2012 in her patron's house in Concord.

In 2008, while still homeless, Pince began suffering from a pain in her left hip. She saw a St. Mungo's advertisement in a New Hampshire newspaper while she was in Concord, NH. Accordingly, the next time she was in Boston, she went to St. Mungo's. Suspecting cancer, a general practitioner there ordered an MRI of Pince's hip. The radiologist who reviewed the resulting MRI images followed St. Mungo's policy, meaning that she neither consulted any image in the National Registry nor provided an electronic copy of Pince's image to the National

Registry. The radiologist pronounced that Pince had no cancer or other major disease in her hip, and Pince's general practitioner told Pince to take ibuprofen for her pain.

In June of 2012, while she was living in her patron's house, Pince's hip pain became unbearable. She saw a doctor in a New Hampshire medical facility, who took the unusual (but cost-saving) step of requesting that the MRI images from the 2008 MRI at St. Mungo's be forwarded to a Concord-based radiologist. The Concord-based radiologist compared Pince's MRI image (the one taken at St. Mungo's) with several in the National Registry and preliminarily diagnosed her as having bone cancer. A biopsy confirmed the diagnosis.

In August of 2012, Pince sued St. Mungo's in New Hampshire State Court. In her complaint, Pince sought $2 million in compensatory damages and $10 million in punitive damages.[^2] The complaint alleged that the St. Mungo's radiologist's failure to consult the National Registry created by Sections 601-603 of the NMMRA constituted negligence per se.[^3] For the purposes of this assignment, a plaintiff can argue that a defendant commits "negligence per se" if she shows that the defendant violated a statute that was designed at least in part to protect the safety of persons such as the plaintiff. In this case, Pince is alleging that the St. Mungo's radiologist's failure to consult the National Registry constituted negligence under Massachusetts or New Hampshire Law.

[^2]: You may assume that under applicable law Pince may sue St. Mungo's for the acts or omissions of its employee physicians and radiologists.
[^3]: For the purposes of Civil Procedure class only, here is an explanation of "negligence per se": suppose a plaintiff alleges that a defendant has failed to exercise due care under the circumstances and that as a result the plaintiff was injured (the classic "negligence" cause of action). Ordinarily, a plaintiff would have to identify what due care under the circumstances would require for a particular fact situation (before demonstrating that the defendant did not do what due care required). If, however, a plaintiff can demonstrate that the defendant's conduct violated some other law designed at least in part to protect persons like the plaintiff, then the plaintiff can say that the defendant's conduct is a "per se" violation of due care. The idea is that it MUST be the case that the defendant failed to exercise due care because the defendant violated some external provision of law. Here, Pince alleges that St. Mungo's failure to follow the requirement of the NMMRA (when the St. Mungo's radiologist did not consult the national registry) was a violation of the NMMRA, and thus constitute negligence per se.
