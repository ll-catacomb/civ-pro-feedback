Assume that Massachusetts has the following law.

- **Negligence cause of action, two ways to prove breach:** The elements of the tort of negligence are (i) that the defendant breached the duty of due care that all people owe one another, (ii) that the breach caused the harm the plaintiff suffered, and (iii) that the plaintiff suffered quantifiable harm. The plaintiff may allege and/or prove element (i) in two ways:
    - By alleging/proving facts showing that the defendant failed to act with sufficient care for the plaintiff's interests under the circumstances (proceeding in this way is called "simple negligence"); or
    - By alleging/proving that the defendant's actions violated some state or federal law designed, at least in part, to protect people like the plaintiff (proceeding this way is called "negligence per se").
- **Massachusetts Rules of Civil Procedure 8 and 12** have texts identical to Federal Rules 8 and 12. In the 2012 case *Violet v. Dash*, however, the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court held, "Massachusetts rules of civil procedure implement the philosophy of notice pleading. In our courts, a short plain statement of the relevant facts giving rise to the claim for relief is sufficient to survive a motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim."

Massachusetts has only one federal district court, the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts. Massachusetts has a long-arm statute going to constitutional limits.

In early 2021, Congress passed Drunk Drone Driver Act (the "DDDA"). The DDDA included the following provisions:

- **Section 11:** The Congress finds that as flying drones become cheaper, and as purchasers begin to use them more over populated areas, the number of individuals operating drones while drunk has increased, threatening health, safety, and interstate commerce.
- **Section 12:** The Consumer Products Safety Commission ("CPSC") is hereby directed to promulgate regulations prohibiting the operation of drones while the operator's blood-alcohol content is above .08.
- **Section 13:** The CPSC shall have the power to sue any individual who operates a drone while their blood alcohol content above .08 for a civil penalty of up to $10,000 per violation, regardless of whether any injury to person or property results from such violation.

The Consumer Products Safety Commission, acting pursuant to its authority under DDDA Section 12, promulgated a federal regulation with the force and effect of law prohibiting the operation of a drone while the operator has a blood-alcohol content above .08.
