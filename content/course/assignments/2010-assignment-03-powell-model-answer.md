# Assignment 3: Model Answer

---

Pansy's attempt to use Luna's settled suit against W&W will likely be unsuccessful. Pansy's most compelling invocation of Luna's suit is via nonmutual offensive collateral estoppel (NMOCE).

Federal common law will govern Pansy's state court NMOCE claim. *Semtek*. Issue preclusion may be invoked when the new action presents (1) the same issue, (2) that was actually litigated, and (3) was necessary to the decision. *Cromwell*; *Russell*. NMOCE additionally requires that the plaintiff could not have entered the original suit. *Parklane Hosiery*.

It is clear that Pansy's suit would address the same tortious interference and constitutional issues as Luna's. Luna and W&W's completion of discovery and filings are almost certainly sufficient to satisfy the "actually litigated" requirement.

However, Pansy will face a great—I think insurmountable—hurdle in convincing the court that the issue was decided. While the judge's ruling must have resolved the constitutional and tortious interference issues, an oral bench ruling with a written decision to follow is hardly a final decision. Opinion-writing is highly valued in the judicial system because it forces judges to thoroughly review the issues. While the writing may confirm a judge's instincts, it may alternatively lead a judge to change an untenable position and reverse her decision. Thus, W&W would not have been allowed to appeal the judge's announced decision. § 1291. Since Luna settled the case before the judge issued a written decision, W&W can likely convince the state court that the judgment was not final and thus the issue was never decided.

In the unlikely event that the state court finds the above three elements apply, it will need to consider the appropriateness of NMOCE. In evaluating NMOCE, the courts are concerned that permitting it misincentivizes plaintiffs to "wait on the sidelines" until another plaintiff has a favorable ruling, and causes unfairness to a defendant who did not vigorously defend against initial claims or who may have additional procedural opportunities in subsequent cases. See *Parklane Hosiery*. Therefore, *Parklane Hosiery* prohibits a plaintiff from invoking NMOCE if she could have joined in the initial suit.

Pansy most likely could not have joined in Luna's federal suit. While Luna's complaint alleges that the court had both arising under and diversity jurisdiction over the claims, I think it was only permitted to sit in diversity.

W&W will be eager to show that Luna's suit was based on § 1331 arising under jurisdiction because then Pansy could have joined the suit under § 1367 supplemental jurisdiction. Under § 1367, claims that "form part of the same case or controversy" can be brought together. If Pansy could have joined Luna's suit she will fail the *Parklane Hosiery* test in trying to issue preclude W&W. This limits plaintiffs' incentives to wait on the sidelines. If, though, as I argue below, Pansy's claim were based only on § 1332 diversity, Luna would not have been able to join the suit. She would have needed to seek inclusion under FRCP 20, which is denied under § 1367(b) when supplemental jurisdiction will destroy complete diversity. (Here it would—Luna and W&W are clearly Massachusetts citizens.)

Federal jurisdiction of Luna's suit was under § 1332 diversity. Tortious interference with a contract is a state claim, so Luna's § 1331 claim must be via the exception annunciated in *Smith* and *Grable* for claims that implicate significant federal issues. Certainly Luna's claim relied on the EIA, a federal statute; however, that is insufficient to get into federal court. See e.g. *Shoshone*; *Empire Healthchoice*.

Rather, there must be a contested and important federal issue that doesn't upset Congressional division of power intentions. *Grable*. W&W will argue that the constitutionality of the EIA and the protection of voting rights are important federal issues that would benefit from the expertise and consistency of the federal judiciary.

However, opening the federal courts' doors simply because a party has stated a Constitutional argument would swing the doors wide open and burden the federal judiciary with claims better handled in state courts. Unlike the significant bond issue in *Smith* and tax issue in *Grable*, a statute discussing the outsourcing of elections is insignificant. See *Merrell Dow* n.12 (reconciling *Smith* and *Moore* based on the importance of the issues at stake). In addition, permitting an arising under claim would upset Congress's intended division of power. First, states run elections and should thus deal with any ensuing litigation. As a corollary, since each state conducts its elections differently, there would be no benefit to the consistency of the federal judiciary. Further, Congress did not provide a federal cause of action. Though not dispositive, this is revealing. Unlike the quiet title actions at issue in *Grable*, the EIA could obviously invite federal claims. Thus Congress's silence reflects its intention to leave federal courts out of the issue. See *Grable* (discussing *Merrell Dow*); *Empire Healthcare*.

Therefore, the District Court was sitting in diversity when it entertained Luna's claim. Pansy would have been unable to join that suit, thus satisfying *Parklane Hosiery*'s final prong.

Nonetheless, I do not think NMOCE will be available to Pansy because of the quality of the initial "decision."
