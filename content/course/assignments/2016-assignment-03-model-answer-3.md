## Assignment 3 — Civil Procedure 2, 2016

### Question 1: JAMOL

The judge was correct to deny MIP's JAMOL. JAMOL is appropriate only when, viewing all evidence in the light most favorable to the verdict-winner (Outrage) and drawing all reasonable inferences in Outrage's favor, no reasonable jury could have found for Outrage (*Reeves v. Sanderson Plumbing*). The court may not weigh evidence or make credibility determinations on JAMOL.

The defamation claim turned on whether the expert's statement that the BAM is effective in fewer than 50% of users was false and defamatory. The true effectiveness rate is an open question. A reasonable jury could have credited the expert's interpretation of MIP's FOIA-obtained clinical trial documents over MIP's unreviewed internal figures. The jury also could have found that the expert statement was a defensible opinion rather than a false statement of fact, which would defeat defamation liability. Either conclusion would support Outrage's verdict. Because a reasonable jury could have found for Outrage on these grounds, the JAMOL was correctly denied.

### Question 2: New Trial

The judge should grant MIP's new trial motion. The new trial standard permits the court to weigh evidence and grant a new trial when the verdict is against the great weight of the evidence, even if a reasonable jury could technically have reached it. The trial judge has broad discretion and, having observed the witnesses and evidence, is in the best position to assess credibility.

On the record here, the expert's opinion rested entirely on her interpretation of MIP's own unreviewed clinical trial documents showing 70% effectiveness. Without independent clinical evidence or peer review supporting the sub-50% conclusion, the weight of the evidence favors MIP's position. If the judge concluded that the expert's methodology was unreliable or that the jury gave undue weight to Outrage's narrative of corporate fraud at the expense of MIP's actual effectiveness evidence, a new trial is warranted. The conduct-related claims (recording devices) resulted in summary judgment for Outrage; the remaining defamation question is narrow and technical. The great weight of the available evidence on that question supports MIP.

### Question 3: Immediate Appeal

Outrage cannot immediately appeal the grant of a new trial. An order granting a new trial is not a final judgment under 28 U.S.C. § 1291 — the case continues. Outrage must await a final judgment from the new trial before bringing an appeal. The order does not qualify as an immediately appealable collateral order because it does not finally resolve a separable issue, and review after final judgment is adequate. Interlocutory appeal under 28 U.S.C. § 1292(b) requires certification by the district court, which is unlikely to be granted for a routine new trial grant.

### Question 4: Jury — Concerns and Benefits

**Concerns:** A central concern is juror competence on technical scientific and medical evidence. The core question — whether the BAM works in fewer than 50% vs. more than 70% of users — requires understanding clinical trial methodology, statistical analysis, and expert credibility. Jurors without such background may substitute narrative sympathy for analysis.

The opacity of jury verdicts is also a concern. Juries do not explain their reasoning. Courts reviewing JAMOL or new trial motions must infer what the jury found, making error correction difficult. A judge could have been asked to make specific findings of fact, providing a cleaner record on appeal.

The trade secrets and trespass claims were resolved on summary judgment, meaning the jury only decided the defamation question. But the salience of Outrage's surreptitious recording activities — even though those claims were decided in Outrage's favor before trial — may have influenced the jury's assessment of MIP's motives and credibility unfairly.

**Benefits:** The defamation context involves community-norm questions: what reasonable people would understand as a defamatory statement about a product's medical effectiveness, and whether a public-interest organization's publication was made with the requisite fault. Juries are traditionally the appropriate body to apply community standards. The jury's verdict also carries democratic legitimacy that a bench verdict lacks. In a case pitting a corporation against a public-interest watchdog over product-safety speech, a community verdict may be more socially credible than a judicial one.
