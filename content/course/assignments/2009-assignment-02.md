## Question

The South Carolina Bank Health Act (the "SCBHA") was adopted by the South Carolina legislature and signed by the governor in June of 2008. Relevant portions of the SCBHA read as follows:

**Section 1: Statement of Purpose**

The Legislature finds that banks in this state and across the nation face an unprecedented liquidity crisis due to bad mortgage loans, and that this liquidity crisis poses a grave threat to the public interest due to the citizenry's great reliance on banks. Among the causes of this crisis are:

- an inability of banks holding mortgages to foreclose and to evict delinquent homeowners promptly, which prevents banks from quickly seizing and selling the property and removing bad loans from their books . . . .

**Section 5: Summary Eviction: Notice**

Any bank holding a mortgage on a property located in South Carolina may institute summary foreclosure and eviction proceedings against a mortgagor in default of a loan by filing a complaint in a Housing Court of the relevant county. Upon filing of such a complaint, the county sheriff of the Housing Court shall immediately give notice of the action to the mortgagor/homeowner. Notice of the action may be given to the mortgagor in any of the following ways:

(a) via hand delivery/personal service on the mortgagor;

(b) via posting of notice on the relevant property; OR

(c) via electronic notice, if the mortgagor has provided a cell phone number and email address to the bank on the mortgage application. Electronic notice under this subsection shall consist of both of the following:

> (i) a telephone call to a cell phone number previously provided by the mortgagor to the bank; AND
>
> (ii) an email attaching the complaint and summons in .pdf form to an email address previously provided by the mortgagor to the bank.

The sheriff of the county Housing Court is deemed to have complied with subsection (c)(i) of this section if he/she leaves a message on a voicemail system associated with the cell number notifying the mortgagor of the Summary Eviction action, the Housing Court Case number, and the address of the Housing Court.

**Section 8: Summary Eviction: Proceedings**

(a) If, in ten days after the Housing Court sheriff has given notice to the mortgagor of summary eviction proceedings under Section 5, the mortgagor fails to file an answer to the Summary Eviction action, or fails to otherwise appear before the court or notify the court of the mortgagor's intent to dispute the summary eviction, the mortgagor is deemed to have consented to foreclosure and eviction, and the bank may proceed forthwith to claim title to the property and sell it to satisfy the amount of the outstanding loan. A Housing Court judgment to this effect shall issue immediately and is final. No motions to reconsider or for relief from judgment shall be entertained, and no appeal will lie.

(b) If instead the mortgagor files an answer disputing the Summary Eviction action, the Housing Court judge shall without exception hold a trial on the issue within seven weeks of the answer's filing. The Housing Court shall issue its ruling orally from the bench at the close of trial, which ruling shall be reduced to a written judgment that same day.

(c) If Housing Court renders judgment in favor of the bank, the mortgagor shall vacate the premises within ten days, with the bank to take possession at the end of such time. If, however, within that ten-day time period, the mortgagor files notice of an intent to appeal the judgment, possession shall remain with the mortgagor, provided, that the mortgagor posts a bond with the court in an amount sufficient to cover the monthly rental value of the property multiplied by 6.[^1]

Helga Hufflepuff, an uneducated farmhand, was and is a domiciliary and citizen of South Carolina. Gringotts Bank, Inc. was incorporated in and has its principal place of business in North Carolina (specifically, in an office building located in the Eastern District of North Carolina). In January of 2008, Hufflepuff signed a mortgage contract with Gringotts Bank so as to buy a log cabin just on the South Carolina side of the North Carolina-South Carolina border in York County. In her mortgage application, Hufflepuff provided her personal cell phone number and her work email to the bank. The amount of the corresponding loan was $40,000; the assessed value of the cabin was $45,000. The contract obligated Hufflepuff to pay $2,000 per month in principal and interest until the loan was repaid.

Hufflepuff made the February–April payments but missed the May and June payments. She had realized she would have trouble continuing to pay $2,000 per month.

On July 1, 2008, in a telephone conversation (Hufflepuff used her cell phone), Gringotts demanded that Hufflepuff resume payment of $2,000/month. When Hufflepuff responded in the call that she definitely would never do so, Gringotts informed her that it considered the loan in default, and that it intended to foreclose on the loan, seize the property, and sell it. Gringotts told Hufflepuff that someone would notify her by calling her cell phone (the bank verified that it had the right number) when Gringotts initiated a summary eviction proceeding, and that she could have as few as ten days to respond.

On July 2, 2008, the farm Hufflepuff worked for announced that it had gone bankrupt and would close its doors and shut down all systems (including email) on July 9. In despair, Hufflepuff decided to go on a two-week camping trip by herself. She left on July 10, 2008. She took her cell phone with her, but discovered that it did not receive a signal out in the woods where she was camping. On July 11, 2008, Gringotts filed its Summary Eviction Complaint in South Carolina Housing Court in York County. That day, the York County Housing Court sheriff called Hufflepuff's cell phone number and left a voicemail message notifying her that Gringotts had filed a Summary Eviction action and giving the Housing Court Case number as well as the address of the Housing Court. Also on that same day, the Sheriff sent an email attaching the complaint and summons in .pdf form to Hufflepuff's work email address, but the email was returned as undeliverable.

On July 25, when Hufflepuff returned from her trip, she heard the cell phone voicemail message. She immediately went to York County Housing Court and obtained a copy of the relevant papers; a Housing Court clerk told her that she had missed the deadline to file an answer, that she had thereby consented to the eviction and to the seizure of her log cabin, and that a judgment had already issued. Hufflepuff asked if a lawyer could be appointed at state expense to represent her, and was told no. Hufflepuff was forced to leave her log cabin on August 1, and the property was sold that same day.

On August 15, Hufflepuff won the South Carolina state lottery, and is now a millionaire.

Hufflepuff is angry about how she was treated. She wants to sue York County to make sure that no one else gets treated the same way; she also wouldn't mind getting a money judgment against York County to teach it a lesson, although she doesn't need the money. She says that she really could have used a lawyer on July 25, when she went to Housing Court. She also says that she could have asked her sister for a loan to "pay something to somebody to let me keep the log cabin for a while until we got things worked out" if that would have made a difference. Hufflepuff heard somewhere that she might do better in federal court, but above all she wants to avoid a jury trial because she's afraid that the jury might end up finding out that many years ago she was a drug dealer and was convicted on a cocaine charge.

### Question (a)

Can Hufflepuff sue York County in federal court? On what grounds and legal theories? Is she likely to succeed? Discuss fully.

### Question (b)

How can Hufflepuff best avoid a jury trial in her federal court lawsuit?
