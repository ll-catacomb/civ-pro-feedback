# Assignment 2 – Model Answer 01

Since the cause of action is in tort, and does not involve intellectual property or a specific plot of land, the only applicable venue statute is 28 U.S.C. § 1391, sections (a), which addresses venue in diversity suits, and (c), which addresses venue for corporations. Venue lies against MegaShop, the single defendant, in the District of South Carolina (SC) if it "resides" there, § 1391(a), that is, if it was subject to personal jurisdiction there on September 2, when the Patils' suit was filed, § 1391(c). Under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 4(k)(1)(A), in turn, MegaShop was subject to personal jurisdiction in the federal District of SC if it was subject to the personal jurisdiction of its state courts. Since the state long-arm statute permits personal jurisdiction over a defendant to the fullest extent consistent with the U.S. Constitution, the venue inquiry collapses to the single question of whether personal jurisdiction over MegaShop is proper in SC.

Given the facts of the case, we can almost certainly demonstrate general, and possibly also specific, jurisdiction over MegaShop in SC. Through its (predecessors') continuous, systematic association with SC in operating Wal-Mart stores throughout the state, MegaShop has made itself amenable to suit there even where unrelated to its specific SC-related actions. See *Perkins v. Benguet*. While plaintiffs often find that demonstrating specific jurisdiction is less burdensome, there are some potential weaknesses to a specific jurisdiction argument in this case. Under *International Shoe*'s multi-factor test, it is clear that MegaShop will not be inordinately inconvenienced by litigation in SC, and that the level and nature of MegaShop's business activity in SC constitutes "purposeful availment" of the state's business. See *Hanson v. Denckla*. However, the connection between MegaShop's SC business and the substance of the Patils' suit is somewhat tenuous.

To be sure, Padma Patil started her trip in the state, SC has an interest in adjudicating its residents' claims, and the tacky lamp's beach scenes included SC beaches, which could indicate MegaShop's efforts to connect the tacky lamp to SC. However, the lamp was purchased in North Carolina (weakening our argument that its scenes connect it to SC), and the Patils' injuries occurred in Virginia. Thus, depending on SC's choice of law rules, either North Carolinian or Virginian tort law, rather than South Carolinian, will apply. Moreover, none of the relevant events (except Padma's departure) took place — and none of the relevant evidence can be found — in SC. Thus, in this case, general jurisdiction may be easier to demonstrate.

In order to develop the case for specific jurisdiction, we need to tie the tacky lamp to SC. Thus, we need to know whether the tacky lamp was actually available for purchase in SC, and if so how many were sold there, which we can find out through interrogatories but which MegaShop might plausibly claim is more readily available to us through the MegaShop sales and inventory documents we will also request. Additional information about the lamp itself, like where and by whom it was manufactured, should also be available through interrogatories and document requests.

In order to develop the case for general jurisdiction, we need to know whether MegaShop's presence in SC is sufficiently systematic and continuous. See *Perkins*, *Helicopteros*. We can use interrogatories and document requests to gather information about how many stores it has in SC, when they opened, and whether Wal-Mart's presence in the state has been continuous. A deposition of a MegaShop executive would be helpful on this score, either a central planner or someone responsible for the Southeast region of the country.
