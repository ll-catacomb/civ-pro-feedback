1. State courts in which to sue Montoya — options are FL, VT, TX and MI. (OK Westley would concede has insufficient relationship to the subject matter of the litigation therefore no specific IPJ under *Int'l Shoe*).

State courts are courts of general jurisdiction, so any would hear the case under SMJ.

**PJ:** The issue is where Westley can argue for PJ.

**FL** — FL likely has general IPJ. Westley can argue that Montoya's domicile shifts to FL when he registers to vote, gets a license, and opens post office box. Additionally, the fact that he never changes the address on his website indicates that he still considers FL "home." Montoya will argue a MI domicile, but it is likely that court would hold that, despite his not paying taxes (which he doesn't pay anywhere) he was in FL with an intention to stay in the sense of no natural preset leaving date.

If Montoya convinces the FL court that he is still domiciled in MI, specific IPJ will likely fail in FL. All states have long arm to the limits of the constitution, so the standard for specific IPJ is minimal contacts among the defendant, the subject matter of the litigation, and the forum, plus fairness and reasonableness. FL, apart from Montoya's bank account (which is not connected to the computer repair), lacks minimum contacts to the subject matter of the litigation.

**VT** — General IPJ not satisfied. Montoya appears to have never been in VT, much less make it home and domicile.

Specific IPJ will likely fail in VT. Specific IPJ — sufficient minimum contacts among defendant, forum, and subject matter of litigation are likely missing because, although the accident occurred there, it did so because of the plaintiff's "unilateral activity" (*Hanson*). True, VT law will likely apply for SOL, but absent minimal contacts and thus no IPJ, this likely does not matter.

**MI** — General IPJ may be possible. For domicile, you're from where you're from until you get where you are going. Westley can argue that Montoya is not settled in FL because he stayed so mobile while in FL.

Specific IPJ could pass in MI. Montoya has sufficient litigation-related contacts with MI and because the item was shipped to MI and Westley was in MI, as Montoya knew during the sale negotiations.

**TX** — General IPJ not satisfied. Montoya has not set up significant life ties to TX. No voting, license, taxes, home buying etc.

Specific IPJ will likely fail in TX. Specific IPJ will likely fail for lack of contacts between subject matter and forum. Check was cashed in TX, but the whole transaction took place in OK and MI, and the injury occurred in VT.

**HCOL:** SOL is always procedural for HCOL purposes. Use forum law for procedural issues. SOL in FL and VT — 4 years. Westley should sue Montoya in one of these states. He should choose FL because that's where PJ is. A court in MI and TX will dismiss because their 2-year SOL.

Overall, FL is the best place to sue, so long as we can persuade the FL court that Montoya is a FL domiciliary and obtain general jurisdiction. If so, then FL will use its 4-year SOL, and the case is not time-barred.

2. Yes, Westley can sue in fed court.

**SMJ:** No **AUJ** because no federal issue. Product liability is state law issue. No *Smith-Grable* exception because there is no federal issue, much less an embedded one.

**Diversity** — If Montoya is domiciled in FL, see above, then we have complete diversity (*Strawbridge*). Montoya will argue domicile still in MI which would destroy diversity if successful and get case out of federal SMJ. **AIC** $110k–$200k is greater than minimum $75k, so AIC satisfied.

**IPJ:** Court will piggyback on PJ of state court sits in under 4(k)(1)(a) so see IPJ discussions above, especially the PJ argument for FL.

Under Diversity SMJ, the case can be heard in fed court if they find that Montoya is a FL domiciliary. For HCOL and IPJ reasons discussed above, FL is the best place to sue in fed court. Procedural success of the claim will turn on Montoya's domicile.
