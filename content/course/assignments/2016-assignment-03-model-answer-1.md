## Assignment 3 — Civil Procedure 2, 2016

### Question 1: JAMOL

The judge was correct to deny MIP's JAMOL. A JAMOL is appropriate only when no reasonable jury could have found for the non-moving party — i.e., when the evidence, viewed in the light most favorable to Outrage (*Reeves v. Sanderson Plumbing*), is insufficient to support the verdict. The court may not make credibility determinations or weigh evidence; it must draw all reasonable inferences in favor of the verdict-winner.

The defamation claim centered on the expert statement that the BAM was effective in fewer than 50% of users. MIP claimed 70% effectiveness; the expert, reviewing MIP's own clinical trial documents, concluded the rate was below 50%. The true effectiveness rate is an open question. A reasonable jury could have found the expert's analysis credible and concluded that the statement — that the BAM worked in fewer than 50% of users — was a defensible, non-defamatory opinion based on disclosed underlying data. Alternatively, a reasonable jury could have found that Outrage's publication lacked the requisite fault for defamation. The existence of competing expert interpretations of MIP's own unreviewed clinical data is precisely the kind of factual dispute that juries are designed to resolve. The JAMOL was correctly denied.

### Question 2: New Trial

The judge should evaluate MIP's new trial motion under the "great weight of the evidence" standard. Unlike JAMOL, a new trial may be granted when the verdict is against the great weight of the evidence, even if a reasonable jury could have reached it. The trial judge, who observed the witnesses and evidence firsthand, has broad discretion.

Here, there are grounds to consider granting a new trial on the defamation claim. The expert's opinion was based on clinical trial documents obtained via FOIA request. MIP's internal documents suggested 70% effectiveness. The expert's methodology — her interpretation of those documents — was the central dispute. If the trial judge believed the jury gave insufficient weight to MIP's evidence about effectiveness, or that the expert's testimony was not credible, she could conclude the verdict was against the great weight of the evidence and grant a new trial.

However, the judge must be cautious about using new trial motions to simply override jury verdicts she disagrees with. The "great weight" standard is higher than mere disagreement. Unless the verdict is clearly against the weight of the credible evidence — a conclusion that is difficult to sustain when the underlying issue (BAM effectiveness) is genuinely contested and subject to expert disagreement — the judge should deny the new trial motion.

On balance, given the genuine factual dispute over effectiveness and the availability of reasonable interpretations supporting Outrage's position, the judge should deny the new trial motion.

### Question 3: Immediate Appeal of New Trial Grant

If the judge grants MIP's new trial motion, Outrage cannot immediately appeal. Under 28 U.S.C. § 1291, courts of appeals have jurisdiction only over "final decisions" of district courts. A grant of a new trial is not a final decision — it sends the case back for further proceedings. Outrage must wait until after the new trial results in a final judgment before appealing the grant of the new trial.

This rule prevents piecemeal appeals and promotes judicial efficiency. The only exception for interlocutory appeal in this context would be if the district court certified the issue under § 1292(b), which requires the district court to determine that the order involves a controlling question of law and that immediate appeal would materially advance the litigation — unlikely in this circumstance.

### Question 4: Concerns and Benefits of Jury

**Concerns:** Juries may lack the technical expertise to evaluate complex scientific or medical evidence, such as conflicting expert analyses of clinical trial data about the BAM's effectiveness rate. Jurors may be swayed by sympathy or by presentation style rather than the underlying merits. Jury verdicts are also opaque — unlike bench decisions, they provide no reasoned explanation, making them harder to review on appeal and possibly inconsistent across cases.

Additionally, juries may be susceptible to anchoring on dramatic or emotionally resonant aspects of Outrage's investigation (the surreptitious recording devices, the "fraud" allegations), even on a narrow defamation question.

**Benefits:** Juries introduce community-norm assessment — particularly relevant for defamation claims, where the standard of what counts as a defamatory statement of fact versus protected opinion may properly reflect community standards. Jury decisions distribute the adjudicatory function across a cross-section of the community, enhancing legitimacy and reducing the appearance of judicial bias. In cases involving accusations of corporate fraud and media investigations, a jury verdict may carry more public credibility than a bench decision.
