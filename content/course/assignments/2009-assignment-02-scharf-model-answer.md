# Assignment 2 Model Answer

## Part (a)

Hufflepuff can sue York County, a state actor, in federal court under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, alleging deprivation of her Fourteenth Amendment procedural due process rights of notice and opportunity to be heard.

Since a liberty interest was not implicated, the presumption rests against Hufflepuff having had a right to court-appointed counsel. *Lassiter*. The *Mathews* test is unlikely to yield an overturning of the *Lassiter* presumption in this case, so this would not be a good claim for Hufflepuff.

### Notice

Notice must be reasonably calculated to inform the notified party of the pending action. *Mullane*. It would be difficult for Hufflepuff to argue that the voicemail and email sent were not reasonably calculated to inform her — she had provided both to her bank, and she had been warned that she would be notified by phone of her eviction.

However, where notice is known to be ineffective, there is a responsibility to make further reasonable attempts at notice. *Flowers*. Here, both attempts at notice were arguably ineffective — the notice sent to Hufflepuff's email address bounced and the call to her cell phone did not result in a connection.

This argument turns on the constitutional "effectiveness" of a voicemail message as notice. While there is nothing to indicate that a voicemail would be an unconstitutional method of notice where it meets the *Mullane* reasonably calculated test, that the call to Hufflepuff presumably went straight to voicemail without ringing (she was in an area without service) might tilt the facts towards a holding that this voicemail constituted ineffective notice, requiring further reasonable attempts.

Hufflepuff's claim is not without merit, but it is unlikely to be successful — her provision of her phone number and email address as preferred forms of communication are sticky facts in the context of *Mullane*, and the voicemail will likely be tough to *Flowers* away, as the Sheriff had no reason to know that Hufflepuff would not retrieve the message in a timely fashion.

### Opportunity to Be Heard

Hufflepuff could argue that she was not given a sufficient opportunity to be heard pre-deprivation. The controlling test is that in *Mathews*, which weighs: (1) the affected private interest(s); (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation; (3) the government's interest as counterbalanced against the private interests implicated.

(1) The affected private interest is a significant one — loss of a home. The procedural safeguard of allowing a foreclosed mortgagor to stay in her home pending appeal is offset by the hefty bond requirement.

(2) The risk of erroneous deprivation is great, due to the short windows for filing an answer or appeal. The likely economic position of those being foreclosed against and their resultant relatively limited access to counsel is relevant in this context.

The additional burden of longer windows would be limited. The statute already allows for up to seven weeks before trial. Increasing the length of the ten-day window for filing an answer to the summary eviction notice thus seems unlikely to so heavily weigh on the government's interest as to negate the risk of erroneous deprivation present in the statute as it stands.

(3) The government's interest in ensuring the rapid processing of foreclosures is great, if § 1 of the SCBHA is to be taken at face value.

In sum, this is a tricky argument for Hufflepuff, but it is probably her best claim. The short windows and the high bond requirement are good facts for Hufflepuff. *Sniadach* stands for the proposition that deprivation of the indigent can be a trip-wire — many of those being foreclosed on will assumedly rely on their foreclosed property as their only form of shelter. Depriving them with so few safeguards and in such rapid fashion weighs *Mathews* factor 1 heavily against the constitutionality of the SCBHA.

## Part (b)

Hufflepuff should drop her claims for monetary damages, and seek only equitable relief. The Seventh Amendment preserves the right to jury trial, which at the time of its enactment extended to legal but not equitable actions. The remedy sought is the most important factor governing the right to a jury trial. *Chauffeurs*.
