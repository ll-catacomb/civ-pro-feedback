## Question 1

SC federal court must have FSMJ over Gringotts' lawsuit for it to bring it there. 28 U.S.C. § 2201(a) allows a declaratory judgment to proceed in federal court if the imaginary lawsuit (the lawsuit that the DJ pre-empts) meets FSMJ requirements. So, for Gringotts to sue in SC federal court there must be FSMJ over the slip-and-fall tort action Helga plans to bring against Gringotts.

No AU jurisdiction for the slip-and-fall because no federal cause of action, nor *Smith-Grable* exception because no embedded federal question. No diversity jurisdiction either because the AIC is $20,000, less than the $75,000 AIC necessary to get into federal court under diversity. Maybe no complete diversity of citizenship (*Strawbridge*) either: Gringotts is a citizen of Delaware and NC because its PPoB is likely NC (headquarters + board/shareholder meetings in Durham). Ambiguous where Helga's domiciled. The last place she had official residence was NC, so that's her assumptive domicile, but she may have become an SC domiciliary since she started sleeping in SC in addition to going to school there. Further, she evinced intent to stay in SC by trying to buy a house there. Could go either way, but most likely SC. If she's a SC domiciliary, then there's diversity; if she's still an NC domiciliary, then no diversity.

There may be AU SMJ for the lawsuit over #4 Privet Drive. Because Gringotts is suing under a quiet title action which is a state law claim, no federal cause of action so the Well-Pleaded-Complaint (*Mottley*) rule isn't met. However, there may be a *Smith-Grable* exception to the WPC rule if there's a federal question embedded in the state claim, the federal question is "contested and substantial," and granting SMJ would not jeopardize the state-federal division. Embedded within Gringotts' action to quiet title is a question of federal law — the constitutionality of EHMA. This affects whether Gringotts properly refused the new terms the mortgage czar imposed and therefore whether Gringotts owns #4 Privet Drive. This embedded federal question is contested and substantial — scholars disagree as to its constitutionality, and section 1 of EHMA says its provisions might be "the most important" to have ever been passed. Granting SMJ may jeopardize the state-federal division however because this case involves land and contract law, traditionally thought to be in the purview of the states. But EHMA's already seeking to regulate this, so granting SMJ is overall probably OK. In which case, there is AU SMJ for Gringotts' suit.

There cannot be SMJ under diversity jurisdiction because the AIC is $65,000 < $75,000. Further, there may not be complete diversity (see above analysis).

Gringotts cannot sue on both claims in a single lawsuit because the two claims are not part of the same case or controversy nor do they share the same operative facts. This is a requirement for supplemental jurisdiction under § 1367 both where the anchoring claim has AU SMJ and where there's diversity SMJ. The issues/facts involving slip-and-fall center around whether Gringotts was negligent/reckless to not clean the puddle, whereas

the issues/facts involving quiet title center around the purchase-and-sale agreement and EHMA's legitimacy.

Suppose the two claims did involve the same case and controversy. Gringotts could not use the slip and fall as the anchoring claim because it is not a federal claim (no AU) and is less than $75,000 (no diversity). Further, the court might exercise discretion under § 1367(c) and decline to hear the supplemental tort claim if it thinks the quiet title claim "substantially dominates" the anchoring tort claim. So the action to quiet title must be the anchoring claim under AU (diversity would not be available because the anchoring claim is $65,000 < $75,000). So if the two claims involved the same case and controversy, they could be in a single lawsuit under supplemental jurisdiction.

Alternatively, if Helga is domiciled in SC and if the lawsuits are part of the same case or controversy, Gringotts would not need to use supplemental jurisdiction at all. It could bring both claims simultaneously under diversity jurisdiction, there being complete diversity (SC v. NC & DE) and $65,000 + $20,000 = $85,000 > $75,000 under the diversity jurisdiction "stacking" rule.

## Question 2

Gringotts is bringing a DJ in SC federal court, which uses the First Restatement. A federal court uses the COL analysis of the state it sits in (*Klaxon*), so First Restatement COL rules apply. Whether negligence or recklessness standard applies is a substantive COL problem, so the federal court will ask "where's the blood" and therefore use SC law because Helga slipped and fell in SC. SC uses recklessness as a standard in slip-and-falls, so Gringotts will likely prevail because no one at Gringotts actually knew of the puddle. Actual knowledge is a necessary element of recklessness.

If Gringotts sued in NC fed court, then the Second Restatement applies and the NC court would cut the case into issues and evaluate which state's law should apply to each issue according to where the "center of gravity" of the transaction was and what state interests were involved (*Babcock*). The tort occurred in SC, so that's the "center of gravity"; further SC has interests in citizens' safety in its territory. The defendant is an SC citizen and perhaps Helga is too, which also speaks to SC state interests. But if the NC court recognized Helga as an NC citizen, then NC has state interests to apply NC law. On balance NC will probably apply SC law, so Gringotts would prevail (see above). If NC applied NC law, then Helga would likely prevail because Gringotts failed to monitor the puddles when it should have.
