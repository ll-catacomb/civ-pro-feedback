## Question 1

This would be nonmutual offensive collateral estoppel. It is fairly clear that an issue at dispute in this case – whether the oral modification was made – was litigated (*Cromwell*) and was decided (*Russell*) in the previous case. Thus, the issue in this case is whether a decision made by a federal administrative agency – in which a second party was prohibited from joining – will be accorded preclusive effect in a federal district court.

As a threshold issue, only decisions of a certain quality are accorded preclusive effect, i.e. if litigants were afforded a "full and fair" opportunity (*Parklane*). Under *Elliott*, an administrative determination will be accorded preclusive effect where the agency is "acting in a judicial capacity" where parties have "adequate opportunity to litigate." Similarly, RJ § 83 suggests preclusion "where an administrative forum has the essential procedural characteristics of a court."

The limited evidentiary procedures in the USEA and, in the Boston Five case, the refusal to order depositions makes a preclusive effect unlikely. The *Elliott* hearing consisted of over 100 witnesses and 150 exhibits. Similarly, the Restatement states that at a minimum the parties must have access to procedural elements allowing "the parties to obtain evidence." While this should be balanced against the need for the speedy resolution, such a balance should not be used to spirit away a defendant's ability to defend itself in subsequent suits.

Of course, in offensive nonmutual estoppel even if the judgment is of sufficient quality it is in the court's discretion to allow or deny preclusion (*Parklane*). If fairness considerations counsel against the application of preclusion, the court will likely demand the issues be relitigated.

Here the application of NMCE seems unfair. The subsequent proceeding affords significantly more advantageous procedural opportunities for Townsend. The very problems suggested by the quality determination suggested above would be remedied by a proceeding in district court. It seems particularly unfair to Townsend to allow offensive nonmutual preclusion in an original district court action when the first action was started in an agency with reduced protections at the option of the original plaintiff. Also, to the extent that preclusion is about maximizing judicial efficiency by avoiding repetition, it is relevant that such a limited administrative proceeding may have little predictive value for the outcome of a full trial on the merits in a federal court.

## Question 2

Charles Townsend is likely to challenge the Boston Five decision on two grounds. First, that the agencies denial of their demand for a jury trial violated the Seventh Amendment. Parties are entitled to a jury trial where, on balance, two factors support their claim (*Tull*): (1) where the 18th-century action would have been brought in a court of law, rather than equity, and (2) where the relief sought is legal, rather than equitable, in nature. In this case, the Boston Five's action, though deceptively contractual in appearance, likely has no historical analogue. The relief the Boston Five sought was mixed; the relief they received was legal only. *Tull* seems to suggest that the relevant inquiry is the relief sought, not received, which makes sense. Considering that historically only equity courts could supply both remedies requested, and that the equitable relief seems the more significant in this case, the denial probably does not violate the Seventh Amendment.

In any event, this case may be covered by the "public rights" exception (*Atlas Roofing*). The right asserted in this case is likely public – more like an OSHA fine than a fraudulent conveyance claim. The initial suit included the government in its sovereign capacity and *Nordberg* suggests that a qualifying public right can be asserted by or exist against the government in its sovereign capacity so long as it is closely entwined with a federal regulatory system. The problem is that this is a federal cause of action existing against a state government and so a lot will depend on how expansive the court reads this exception.

Second, they will likely claim that the procedures employed by the agency are a violation of Fourteenth Amendment due process because they categorically do not provide for live testimony and in the present case denied the request to depose the Secretary of State.

Due process requires a balancing between the private interests at stake against the government's interest and any public interest. (*Goldberg*) This case falls squarely between *Goldberg*, requiring at a minimum a fair opportunity to defend oneself by confronting witnesses and presenting arguments and evidence orally, and *Eldridge*, where the court did not require live testimony as it was unlikely the testimony would materially supplement the written record.

Since the statute prohibits live testimony, a fair adjudication is unlikely if the record cannot be completed by deposition of the relevant parties to the litigation. Moreover, the additional cost to the system is low since both parties would bear their own deposition costs – the only cost is that inherent in adjudicating from a well established, as opposed to a poorly established, record. Consequently, it seems likely that Townsend would succeed on a due process claim.

*Note: This situation is not covered by § 1783.*

*It is worth noting that this analysis is somewhat problematic since it implies that if the second defendants were to bring their suit in the USEA, that the first agency decision would have preclusive effect since the procedures are the same. This type of forum shopping is never desirable.*

*One could argue that the action resembles a tortuous interference with contract claim that probably would have sounded in law at the time of the enactment of the Seventh Amendment (then as a specialized application of trespass on the case). However, this would be true only if the claim was intentional interference, negligent interference with contract remains almost universally unrecognized in American jurisdictions even today.*
