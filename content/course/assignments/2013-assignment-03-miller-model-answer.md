# Assignment 3 Response

## 1)

The claims Peverell has against Delacour, Derwent, and DMC all arise from the same transaction or occurrence (or related occurrences), the tonsil surgery. Therefore, under the proposed rule Peverell had to join all three defendants in his initial lawsuit if possible. However, DMC's principal place of business is in South Carolina, making it a domiciliary of South Carolina like Peverell, so joining DMC in the initial lawsuit would have destroyed complete diversity and would therefore have been invalid.

Since Peverell could have joined Derwent in the initial lawsuit, proposed Rule X would preclude Peverell from suing Derwent now. The suit against Derwent should be dismissed.

Since Peverell could not have joined DMC in the initial lawsuit, proposed Rule X has no effect, and Peverell can proceed with his lawsuit against DMC.

## 2)

The proposed rule should not be adopted. The primary argument in favor of adopting the rule is the improved judicial economy of disposing of all claims related to the same transaction or occurrence at once, the same justification for the compulsory counterclaim rule. The supplemental jurisdiction that allows the compulsory counterclaim system to work (§ 1367(b) would never oust jurisdiction over a counterclaim because a counterclaim is not a claim "by a plaintiff"), however, does not apply to this rule and therefore judicial economy is not automatically improved as it is under the counterclaim rule. As the Peverell case illustrates, since there is no supplemental diversity jurisdiction DMC could not have been joined in the federal court lawsuit, and the rule would likely not have a large effect on the number of lawsuits filed.

The rules of non-mutual offensive and defensive collateral estoppel, where they apply, already disincentivize filing multiple lawsuits. If a state (state law matters, see *Semtek*) allows nonmutual defensive collateral estoppel, any issues the plaintiff loses in the first lawsuit are automatically lost in the second, and any issues won in the first lawsuit cannot be used to preclude the second defendant even if he could have been joined in the first lawsuit. Thus, the plaintiff has nothing to gain and everything to lose by litigating the same issues multiple times. The plaintiff would also obviously have to bear the exorbitant costs of multiple lawsuits even in the best case scenario. Thus, the rule accomplishes little that a rule allowing NMDCE could not.

Any minor judicial economy gains could be offset by the costs of litigating the application of the new rule itself. The rule requires joinder of all parties against whom the plaintiff *knows* she has a claim for relief. Determining who the plaintiff "knew" she had a claim for relief against at the time she filed the lawsuit could be a difficult and fact-intensive inquiry that defendants could use to drag out litigation unnecessarily. Additionally, the proposed rule requires joinder when the claims arise out of the same or related transactions or occurrences. As *Nesbit* and *Jones v. Morris Plan Bank* demonstrate, the scope of this phrase seems to be dependent on the facts and desired policy outcome of a specific case and context, making it hard to predict and inviting litigation.

<!-- page: 2 -->

In addition to these increased litigation costs, the proposed rule could also lead to unjust outcomes. The same phrases that invite increased litigation, i.e. "knows" and "same transaction or occurrence," could also frustrate an otherwise valid claim if decided incorrectly by a court, or construed in a way that would have been difficult or impossible to predict *ex ante*, *see Jones*. The debatable judicial economy gains that the proposed rule would bring are outweighed by the costs of potential errors that the rule creates, and the rule should not be adopted.
