# Assignment 3 Model Answer

When a state court adjudicates a federal cause of action, federal general common law — specifically the *Parklane* rule — should govern the preclusive effect of the resulting judgment. The alternatives would frustrate well established and prudent principles of judicial economy and fairness by encouraging forum shopping and the inequitable administration of justice. *See Erie*; *Semtek*. The alternatives would also promote excessive judicial abstention.

## Forum Shopping

Take for instance the Segundus lawsuit. If the reviewing state court does not adopt the *Parklane* rule, and instead follows New York preclusion law by applying offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel, then the reviewing court will likely find Devil liable. This creates a strong incentive for future defendants, especially those who are involved in suits with multiple potential plaintiffs, to secure the *Parklane* rule, which would improve the odds of preventing the use of offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel in future lawsuits. In order to take advantage of *Parklane*'s balancing test in a subsequent action, such defendants would need to remove to federal court in the initial suit (note: *Parklane* suggests that its holding is only binding on reviewing courts when the rendering court is a federal court). The defendant's incentive to remove would likely cause an increase in federal litigation. If, however, a federal common law rule (shaped by *Parklane*) governs the preclusive effect of state court judgments on federal causes of action, then the two regimes would have uniform preclusive structures, and the defendant's incentives to litigate in federal court (or vertically forum shop) would be weakened.

## Inequitable Administration of Law

The lack of a uniform *Parklane* rule would also lead to the inequitable administration of the law. For instance, if Angel had consented to removal in the first case, then the subsequent Segundus litigation (assuming both defendants opted to remove again) would have been governed by the *Parklane* rule. This would have resulted in an outcome in defendants' favor because of *Parklane*'s balancing requirements. Because they did not remove, however, the rendering court's judgment (in the absence of requiring a federal common law rule) is governed by New York preclusion law, which virtually guarantees a finding of liability. Hence a lack of a uniform preclusion framework allows the substantive outcome (a finding of liability under § 1983) to vary based on whether defendants can meet removal requirements. The ability of removal to dictate the substantive outcome of cases, and the resulting bias against defendants, is something *Erie* was particularly keen to prevent. Although we don't have identical barriers to removal here (*Erie* was a diversity case) we still get similar inequitable administration of law unless we adopt a uniform preclusion law that governs all judgments of federal causes of action in the same manner.

## Counter-Arguments and Abstention

Critics of an approach using the *Parklane* rule could argue that a federal common law rule that allows a state court to adopt the New York rule may do more to promote fairness in the application of laws. For one, the preclusion approach favored by New York state law might do a better job of reducing the incidence of inconsistent results (there would be less risk of the defendant losing in suit #1 and prevailing in suit #2 on the same issue). *Hart* echoes this principle of fairness in its decision to adopt offensive collateral estoppel. By arguing that it would prevent defendants from being able to re-litigate issues already decided, *Hart* also suggests that judicial economy, or the desire to avoid duplicative litigation, is also a justification for a strong preclusion policy.

Nevertheless, even if there is some merit to the fairness arguments in *Hart*, giving state courts the choice to follow New York preclusion law would ultimately be improper because it would allow a New York court to routinely follow *Hart* by invoking notions of judicial economy and disposing of litigation that it otherwise had the authority to consider. Such an approach would violate the principles in *Colorado River*, which articulated a federal court's obligation to exercise jurisdiction over cases that Congress authorized it to hear barring proof of exceptional circumstances. Under *Colorado River*, the fact that litigation is duplicative is not enough of a reason to refuse to hear a case. Since we are dealing with federal causes of action, created by Congress, similar principles should bar a rule that would allow a state court adjudicating such a case to summarily apply offensive collateral estoppel and dispose of arguments in a federal cause of action without at least engaging in *Parklane*-like balancing.[^1]

[^1]: Note from Prof. Greiner: This last paragraph implicates principles not taught in Civ Pro — namely, a state court's duty to litigate a federal cause of action over which it has jurisdiction. In *Alden v. Maine*, the Supreme Court held 5-4 that state courts do not always have to litigate federal causes of action if they do not want to. Assignment 3 is designed to get students to think about what the law should be based on the assigned reading, not what the law is.
