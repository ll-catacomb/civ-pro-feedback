## Assignment 1, Question

Assume the following (all parts of which are totally fictional).

California Revised Statutes, § 3333, provides:

> In any action sounding in tort, involving a plea for damages for emotional distress, and not involving the death of the plaintiff or any member of the plaintiff's family, a plaintiff who proves that she has suffered emotional harm shall be entitled to an award of exactly $20,000, no more and no less.

The damage limit described above applies in full to the common law tort of "Outrage" or "Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress" in California. Oregon law does not specify the amount of damages that can be awarded in Outrage lawsuits, and appellate courts in Oregon have upheld damages awards as high as $5,000,000.00.

Both California and Oregon recognize the tort of Outrage and define it as follows: "One who by extreme or outrageous conduct intentionally or recklessly causes severe emotional distress to another is subject to liability, and shall pay damages as appropriate under the law, for such emotional distress." Both states define "recklessness" as the "failure to exercise due care to eliminate a known risk of harm." Under the law of both Oregon and California, the tort of "Outrage" is not complete until the plaintiff suffers the emotional distress that gives rise to the claim.

California follows the Second Restatement on Choice of Law. Oregon follows the First Restatement on choice of law. Both states have the doctrine of "respondeat superior," meaning that a company is liable in tort for the actions of employees that are within the scope of the employees' duties.

Weasley's Wizard Wheezes, Inc. ("WWW") is incorporated in Delaware. It manufactures all of its nearly magical internet products in its enormous manufacturing facility just outside of Portland, Oregon, where it employs about 2000 people. WWW's plant is

considered one of the best places to work in the state, featuring a fully complete gym with a swimming pool, a first class dining facility, and live musical performances every Friday at 5:30 pm. The Mayor of the City of Portland regularly visits the facility to make sure that all is well at one of the City's biggest employers.

WWW's owners are Fred and George Weasley; between them, they own 65% of the company's voting stock. Fred is the Chief Executive Officer, the Chief Operating Officer, and the Chief Financial Officer. George serves as both General Counsel (he obtained his law degree from Hogwarts in 2003) and the Chair of the Board. Both are domiciled in Sacramento, California, where they enjoy life in their large two-house compound just outside the city limits. They have a small building on the compound with five rooms in it that they use as their offices, where they make all major decisions on behalf of the company. Their two executive assistants, Angelina Johnson and Oliver Wood, came to work in this small building, which also had a fully wired room for board meetings. The compound also has a single-building work area, where Fred and George create their latest prototypes.

The Weasleys were excited. They had just created a new product, the Weasley's Amazing Machine (the "WAM"). The WAM represented the next generation of WWW's line of Internet-based gaming products. It consisted of a full body suit that the user could wear while standing or sitting on a specially designed platform that could simulate the movements of anything from an airplane to an earthquake. The result was a total immersion in the simulation. The WAM came with a menu of over 1500 simulation-based games that a user could experience, all of them interactive and based on simulated worlds that existed on the Internet.

The Weasleys wanted to make a big splash with the WAM, and they thought the best way to do so would be to provide a group of WWW's 30 best customers with a free copy of the device for their exclusive use for a period of two months. They hoped that the users would create Youtube videos about the WAM that would go viral and attract more viewers than Gangnam Style. Accordingly, the Weasleys contacted 30 customers to invite them to a party at their Sacramento compound. The invitations were sent via gmail (which does not reveal the email recipients' physical locations).

The party, which occurred on February 23, 2013, was a roaring affair; Psy (a Korean K-pop star) came to perform for the 30 customers, the Weasleys, and their two assistants, Johnson and Wood. No alcohol was served, however, as each customer had been instructed to bring a vehicle with adequate trunk space for a special "end of party gift." During the party, the guests were treated to a tour of the Weasley's Sacramento compound, including a 30-minute look inside of Fred & George's workroom. Fred and George gleefully described how they had created the 30 WAM prototypes from scratch entirely within this workroom. At the end of the party, the Weasleys presented each of

the 30 customers with his or her own WAM. The customers were overjoyed. Each loaded up his or her WAM and headed home.

One of these customers was Nymphadora Tonks, who preferred to go by her surname, "Tonks." Tonks drove her WAM back to her house in Hogsmeade, Oregon, just across the California/Oregon border. She installed the unit in her bedroom, fired it up, and called the office where she worked to say that she would be taking a three-week vacation. Too embarrassed to say how she really planned to spend the time, she told her office that she would be on a romantic getaway with Remus drinking wine in the Napa valley. She and Remus spent the next 20 days more or less continuously in the WAM bodysuit, taking turns playing the games. They were both delighted. At the end of the three-week WAM binge, both returned to work, but they continued to enjoy the WAM for a few hours in the evening and a little more on the weekend.

In late June of 2013, while surfing the Internet from her home in Hogsmeade, Tonks stumbled across a new website and blog called "AllAboutWWWFans.com." When she went to the site, she was horrified to see, on the first page, pictures of Remus and herself in various states of undress in their home bedroom. After she clicked on a panel that said, "Click here if you want to learn more about these losers!", she found text that described accurately her height and weight, that stated that she exercised regularly and had good reflexes, and that she had told her officemates that she would be away on a 3-week vacation in the Napa valley but that she had really spent 8 hours a day in "a new WWW product not on the market yet." She explored the website further and found private information, including photos, of each of the attendees of the WAM party.

That night, Tonks and Remus threw their WAM in the trash. The next day Tonks sought psychiatric counseling for grief and shock from a counselor in Sacramento. The counselor diagnosed her with post-traumatic stress disorder.

The day after, Tonks consulted a partner in the law firm for which you work.

You are an associate in that law firm. The partner for whom you work asks for your help. She says that she has some indication that WWW was collecting private data on WAM users without the users' knowledge or permission, and that Tonks wants to sue WWW for the tort of Outrage. But, says the partner, "I'd like to try to stay out of federal court if at all possible. Federal courts have been issuing bad rulings lately on Outrage suits, and I'm worried that we won't win the case. That said, I'd still like to hear whether the federal courts might be an option for us." The partner asks you to write a memo discussing the following question: in what court systems might Tonks be able to file a lawsuit against WWW? What would be the advantages and disadvantages of each in terms of the availability of damages? How do you respond? *(Note from Professor Greiner: You may assume that there is no federal law applicable to damages in this case, so that the question of damages is governed by state law.)*
