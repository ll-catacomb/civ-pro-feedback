## QUESTION 1

There is likely diversity jurisdiction.

There is no AUJ. Negligence is a state law claim. There is no well-pled federal cause of action, *Mottley*, nor any intertwined federal issue (*Smith-Grable*).

There is likely diversity jurisdiction. Perrin is still a Texas domiciliary. Perrin only travels to California for one week every month, not enough to manifest an intention to remain there. *Mas*.

Tuon is either a New York (likely) or California domiciliary; either way, she's diverse from Perrin.

Damodred's domicile has likely changed to New York. Though originally a Texas domiciliary, she's moved to NYC and expressed interest in buying property. While her three-month-long stays in other places didn't change her domicile, and nor did simply going to school in NYC, looking to purchase property supports an intention to stay. Plus, Damodred doesn't have a time or plan to leave.

However, if the court finds otherwise, there's no complete diversity, meaning no diversity jurisdiction. *Strawbridge*. Perrin could argue for supplemental jurisdiction, but her claim against a non-diverse defendant falls within the § 1367(b) ouster; supplemental jurisdiction isn't possible here.

Ishamael is a Nevada domiciliary. Having never incorporated his business, the relevant citizenship is his own.

The amount-in-controversy is satisfied: for each defendant, the value of the claim against them, $100k, exceeds $75k.

## QUESTION 2

Maybe California has personal jurisdiction over all three defendants, but not Nevada, Texas, or New York. All relevant states have a long-arm statute going to the limits of the Constitution, so federal courts can piggy-back. 4(k)(1)(A).

### *California.*

No defendant is domiciled there, so there's no general personal jurisdiction over any of them. But there could be specific jurisdiction.

<!-- page: 2 -->

Minimum contacts are likely satisfied for both Tuon and Damodred. The accident occurred in California. Tuon traveled to California, rented space there, and trained Damodred from there (though since the call was virtual, she might dispute its location as gratuitous); Damodred did the same to operate the construct. Thus, both purposefully availed themselves of California laws. Fair and reasonable factors support specific personal jurisdiction over both: it's convenient to hale both into California court, it's convenient enough for Perrin (who flies there once a month), and California has an interest in product and consumer safety within its borders.

Specific jurisdiction over Ishamael is harder. He hasn't traveled to California and he trained Tuon in Nevada. He sold the construct in Nevada with no control over where it went. *Volkswagen*. Tuon acted unilaterally taking the construct there. *Hanson*. Fair and reasonable factors: it's inconvenient for Ishamael, though convenient for Perrin.

### *New York.*

There's general jurisdiction over Tuon and Damodred (assuming the court considers her a New York domiciliary). Asserting specific jurisdiction over Ishamael is harder.

Minimum contacts: Ishamael transacted with a New York domiciliary, Tuon, gaining economic benefit. *McGee*; *International Shoe*. But Ishamael didn't target New York in his marketing, and Tuon's not the plaintiff. He insisted that Tuon come to Nevada to receive training and complete the sale; in *McGee*, by contrast, the sale was completed while the plaintiff was in California.

Fair and reasonable factors disfavor specific jurisdiction over Ishamael. Maybe New York has an interest in monitoring its residents' unsafe online transactions, but that's a leap (and privacy violation). There aren't relevant witnesses or evidence regarding Ishamael's negligence in New York.

### *Nevada.*

There's general jurisdiction over Ishamael and likely specific jurisdiction over Tuon, who purposefully availed herself of Nevada laws by traveling there to take possession of the construct. But fair and reasonable factors might counsel against specific jurisdiction: though she can get there, evidence of her training of Damodred would be in California, not Nevada, and it could be inconvenient for Perrin. Nevada likely couldn't assert personal jurisdiction over Damodred, who's never been, so there are no minimum contacts.

### *Texas.*

A lawsuit isn't possible here. Two of the three defendants have never been, and Texas has no connections with the litigation subject matter.

## QUESTION 3

<!-- page: 3 -->

California law likely applies, enabling a contributory negligence defense.

The question of available defenses is substantive, not procedural, so we need an HCOL analysis. Federal courts apply the HCOL rules of the state in which they sit (*Klaxon*).

California, Texas, and Nevada: First Restatement states use the law of the state where the harm/injury occurred. Perrin was injured in California. Contributory negligence applies.

If the suit is heard in New York: Second Restatement states begin with California, where the injury occurred. The "center of gravity" test doesn't support overriding the 1R presumption. The injury happened in California, Perrin's relationship with Tuon/Damodred centers on California, Tuon/Damodred's guilty conduct occurred in California — California law will apply. Nevada's interest in safe user training is probably trumped by California's in regulating dangerous conduct and products within its borders.
