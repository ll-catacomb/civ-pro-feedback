## Assumptions

Assume the following:

- that South Carolina has a two-year statute of limitations on tort claims;
- that Georgia has a three-year limitations period on tort claims;
- that South Carolina follows the First Restatement approach to choice of law;
- that Georgia follows the Second Restatement approach to choice of law;
- that South Carolina has a declaratory judgment act statute identical in all ways to 28 U.S.C. § 2201, so that South Carolina state courts can issue declaratory judgments just as federal courts can do so, but that neither the state statute nor the federal statute affects any court's subject matter jurisdiction; and
- that the United States Food and Drug Administration (the "FDA") regulates the language that must appear on the labels of prescription drugs, with an eye toward assuring that these labels include information sufficient to allow a consumer to make an informed choice about the dangers of any drug and/or its side effects.

## Facts

Libatius Borage, Inc., was incorporated in Delaware in 1972. Libatius makes drugs to treat various skin maladies. Its latest product is called Essence of Dittany; Libatius' marketing materials call the drug a "nearly magical cure-all that can help relieve symptoms for a variety of ailments," but thus far, the FDA has only approved Essence of Dittany for use to treat a skin disease called spattergroit, a most grievous affliction of the skin that causes gruesome pockmarks and blemishes. Libatius directs all operations from its corporate offices in Atlanta, Georgia, where all of its directors, officers, and scientists live. But it manufactures Essence of Dittany, and all of its other drugs, in its plant in Allendale, South Carolina, just north of the Savannah River. The Allendale plant is Libatius' only manufacturing plant, and the company ships its products from this plant to pharmacies around the world. Indeed, over 85% of the company's several hundred employees work in the Allendale facility.

When the FDA approved Essence of Dittany as a treatment for spattergroit,[^1] it issued an order requiring Libatius to put the following warning on the label of each bottle for Essence of Dittany: "Warning: persons suffering from toenail fungus should not take this drug, as life-threatening side effects may result." Double-blind randomized experiments had shown that a person who took Essence of Dittany when she already had toenail fungus had a dramatically increased chance of having the fungus spread to and attack vital organs.

[^1]: Prior to Libatius' discovery of the Essence of Dittany drug, the only other treatment for spattergroit known to be effective was too unpleasant and embarrassing to be recommended for widespread use.

Due to an oversight by one of the supervisors in the Allendale plant, in late 2008, a set of 500 bottles of Essence of Dittany did not carry the exact language the FDA required. Instead, the labels on these bottles said only, "Warning: persons suffering from toenail fungus should not take this drug."

One of those bottles was shipped to a pharmacy in Georgia, where it was purchased by Fred Weasley in February of 2009. Fred and George had grown up together in Athens, Georgia. During high school, the two had started a mail-order novelties and gag gifts business called "Weasley's Wheezes" specializing in sulfur-laced whoopee cushions. The business grew quickly. In 2007, both Fred and George entered college at the University of South Carolina's campus in Columbia, South Carolina, but after completing their first years in May of 2008, they took a leave of absence from USC to open an actual physical shop on Devine Street in Columbia. In a letter dated May 30, 2008, they assured their mother, Molly, that they intended to return to USC at some point, but said that business was simply too good at the moment to waste time in the classroom. As of today, neither has re-enrolled at USC, and they continue to operate Weasley's Wheezes out of their Devine Street shop. They live in an apartment upstairs from their shop.

As noted above, in February of 2009, Fred bought one of the Essence of Dittany bottles with the shortened warning phrase from a pharmacy in Georgia when he and George were in Georgia visiting Molly. Both Fred and George had recently been diagnosed with spattergroit, and had received a prescription for Essence of Dittany from a South Carolina doctor. Both Fred and George also had toenail fungus. While staying in Molly's Georgia home, both Fred and George took the Dittany, and the next day, both were in an Athens hospital receiving intensive medical treatment. Both survived, and had fully recovered by the end of April, 2009, but each spent three weeks in the hospital and medical bills for each totaled over $200,000.

In May of 2011, Fred and George wrote Libatius a letter demanding $300,000 each in medical bills, pain, and suffering. They stated in their letter that the negligence of the Libatius employee in the South Carolina plant had caused a defective warning label on the bottle of Essence of Dittany that Fred had purchased. Fred and George contended that had the warning label included the language "as life-threatening side effects may result," they would not have taken the drug. As evidence of negligence, the letter pointed out that the label had not used the FDA's required language, and thus that Libatius had violated an FDA order. Fred and George demanded that payment of $300,000 to each of them, for a total of $600,000, be made by November of 2011, or else they would sue Libatius in Georgia federal court.

You are Libatius' lawyer. After consulting with your client, you thought your best chance of winning a lawsuit was by litigating it in South Carolina state court, since Libatius employed a lot of people in South Carolina, and you were sure that you can somehow put the idea in the heads of the local judge and jury that a successful lawsuit might cause Libatius to fire employees at the plant. You decided to try to seize the forum. On August 16, 2011, you sued both George and Fred in South Carolina state court and served both with process the same day. Your complaint requested that the court issue a declaratory judgment, under the South Carolina declaratory judgment statute, that your client owed nothing to Fred or George as a result of their taking the Essence of Dittany. Among the arguments you raised was that South Carolina's 2-year statute of limitations expired no later than April of 2011, so any lawsuit George and Fred might file would be time-barred.

On August 18, 2011, Fred left for a long-planned safari in Africa. George hired a lawyer on August 23, 2011, but due to an unexpected breakdown in cell phone communications, no one could get in touch with Fred, so the lawyer could only represent George. On September 6, 2011, George's lawyer filed a petition for removal in federal court, signed only on George's behalf, and thus removed the lawsuit from South Carolina to federal court.

You want to get the lawsuit back to South Carolina state court. The date is now September 27, 2011. You also want to win the lawsuit on your statute of limitations argument.

## Questions

**Question 1:** Discuss whether the judge is likely to grant a motion you might file to remand the case back to South Carolina state court. Be sure to discuss all possible arguments supporting a remand. Also, is there any reason to file this remand motion quickly?

**Question 2:** Now assume that the federal court does in fact remand the case back to state court. You are now back in South Carolina state court. Will you win on your statute of limitations argument? Should you?
