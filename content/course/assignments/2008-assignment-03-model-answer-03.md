# Assignment 3 – Model Answer 03

## Question 1: Federal Subject Matter Jurisdiction

Yes. In a declaratory judgment action, the court must evaluate who would have otherwise brought the lawsuit (a "coercive action") and determine if there would have been subject matter jurisdiction over that suit. The Boston Five could have sued Charles Townsend under the EIA, a federal law. Thus, there is federal subject matter jurisdiction over Charles Townsend's declaratory judgment action.

## Question 2: Appellate Jurisdiction

There is likely appellate jurisdiction over Townsend's appeal under the collateral order doctrine. Under *Cohen*, an order on a "collateral matter" may constitute an appealable final judgment if it (1) conclusively determined the question, (2) resolved an important question independent of the merits, and (3) is effectively unreviewable on appeal from the final judgment.

The decision on this issue was concluded and closed. By granting the stay and stating that it would apply claim and issue preclusion to the USEA, the district court essentially made a final determination that it would not look into the facts of this case (assuming the District Court can give preclusive effect to the USEA hearing's decisions). The decision to stay the case was collateral to the merits of the case, and involved an important question, as it means that Townsend's declaratory judgment action would not be tried in the district court. Finally, a decision to stay this declaratory judgment action is essentially unreviewable on appeal, because the district judge's stay amounts to a refusal to adjudicate the claim. The idea behind the stay is that, once the USEA decides the issues relevant to the claim, the federal court would then honor that determination as res judicata.

If the collateral order doctrine is insufficient, then there is probably not appellate jurisdiction under any other theory. Townsend is unable to appeal the order under § 1291 as a final order because the stay is not otherwise a "final order" — it did not resolve any claim. He cannot appeal under Rule 54(b) because the action does not present more than one claim for relief. It also may not be reviewed as an interlocutory appeal: Rule 23(f) is limited to review of class certifications in class action suits; § 1292(a)(1) applies only to the grant, denial, or modification of an injunction; and the requirements of § 1292(b) are not satisfied because there were no findings raising an issue involving a controlling question of law. Finally, a writ of mandamus is unlikely because it should issue only in extraordinary circumstances.

## Question 3: Appropriateness of the Stay

The stay is inappropriate, because the procedures of the USEA do not meet the standards required to make its decisions worthy of preclusive effect. Thus, the district court's rationale for granting the stay does not justify its decision. In *Colorado River*, a similar stay was justified on the grounds that there was an interest in promoting efficient judicial administration. Here, however, because the decisions of the USEA do not deserve preclusive effect, it is likely that waiting for the decision of the USEA will not save such resources.

According to the Restatement, a valid and final adjudicative determination by an administrative tribunal has the same preclusive effect as a judgment of a court. However, in order for an administrative proceeding to have this preclusive effect, it should provide: (1) adequate notice to persons bound by the adjudication; (2) the legal right to present evidence and legal argument; (3) a formulation of issues of law and fact in terms of the application of rules; (4) a rule of finality; and (5) a conclusive determination of the matter in question.

The USEA proceeding seems particularly suspect under requirement (2) in that it will resolve factual disputes about whether the ballot order increase took place based only on a written record. The USEA proceedings do not allow for live testimony. The USEA, therefore, will be making decisions without the evidentiary benefit of live witness testimony. Furthermore, the USEA's reliance on the newspaper article means that its decision will be based, in part, on an article that may be entirely biased or unreliable. This problem could be exacerbated by the fact that the USEA cannot compel the testimony of critical witnesses. Finally, because there is no right to appeal, these problems cannot be resolved in a later proceeding. In effect, giving preclusive effect to the decisions of the USEA would raise serious Due Process concerns because the USEA proceeding likely does not offer all of the protections required under this clause.

On another note, giving preclusive effect to the decision of the USEA may not be appropriate because it involves voting, an important federal right. Cf. *Gjellum* (involving state agencies).
