## Q1

*Matthews* governs this PDP issue. The courts will consider (i) private interests of the party; (ii) risk of erroneous deprivation; and (iii) the government's interest (fiscal/administrative burdens). In this case, Buffy's private interests in receiving damages (rather than say, her personal liberty being at stake) is low compared to the parental rights issue in *Lassiter*, which was still not enough for appointed counsel. In addition, there is nothing super-complex about this case, so the risk of erroneous determination is average. These factors thus do not outweigh the government's interest in reducing fiscal/administrative burdens associated with providing counsel.

## Q2

*Rule 65* governs this Preliminary Injunction (PI) issue. There is a circuit split as to whether the four factors are required, or merely balanced. Either way, for Buffy, court is likely to deny the motion for PI (lack of irreparable harm).

(i) Buffy's likely to succeed on the merits:
   - a. Duty: Assumed on the manufacturer/distributor's part;
   - b. Breach: A reasonable manufacturer/distributor of a self-driving car would take ordinary care in inspecting and ensuring such car would navigate accurately and stop appropriately prior to selling/sending it to customer's home;
   - c. Causation: If manufacturer/distributor inspected the car prior to sending it to Buffy's house, it would know its navigation/stopping was defective and would not have sent it to Buffy's home;
   - d. Damages: Buffy incurred physical harm and her pickup was damaged due to the self-driving car coming onto the curb (evidenced by dent to the pickup and her medical records).

(ii) Immediate/Irreparable harm: Because the injury already occurred, there is not much the court can do other than provide damages to Buffy, which is not irreparable harm. This factor would likely be determinative in denying the PI.

(iii) Balance of equities: Both parties have a risk of incurring only monetary harm whether or not PI is granted.

(iv) Public interest: This is a purely private dispute, with little affect on public interest either way. There is, for example, no regulatory schemes at issue.

## Q3

*Fed. R. Civ. P. 56 (FRCP 56)* governs Motion for Summary Judgment (MSJ). MSJ shall be granted if movant shows there is no genuine dispute of material fact and movant is entitled to judgment as matter of law. *Celotex* governs the MSJ analysis. Court would likely grant Buffy's MSJ on the two elements:

a. **Plaintiff bears burden of persuasion at trial as to elements ((ii) breach of duty of care and (iii) causation) of negligence claim:** Buffy would need to prove that the

<!-- page: 2 -->

manufacturer/distributor breached their duty of care and that such breach caused her and her sister's injuries.

b. **Since moving party in this case is plaintiff, J. Brennan's dissent in *Celotex* tells us that she also bears the burden of production:** Buffy's able to offer two affidavits suggesting there was a lack of due care by the manufacturer/distributor in inspecting the trucks that were sold. The affidavits further demonstrate such lack of due care was the reason Buffy was hit by the self-driving car, and suffered injuries. **This evidence, if unrebutted by the non-moving party, would cause a trier of fact to only find in favor of the Plaintiff (*Celotex*).**

c. **Since Buffy as the moving party and plaintiff met her burden of production, the burden shifts to XH/Faith (non-moving parties).** XH and Faith can submit Chase's affidavit, which on its face suggests that there may be a genuine issue of material fact. However, the court is likely to base its judgment on the photos and evidence submitted with the MSJ (*Scott*), which, even when viewed in the light most favorable to the non-moving party, show that it is more likely than not that there was in fact a lack of due care by the manufacturer/distributor which caused Buffy's injuries.

However, if VA law § 6.7 (VRCP 56) applies, Buffy's MSJ will be denied because she failed to submit the required psychiatrist analysis regarding mental distress, causing a vertical choice of law issue (*Erie*):

**York:** Outcome Determinative —
- Applying FRCP 56 for this MSJ would be outcome determinative. As such, we'd use the VA law (MSJ denied).

**Hanna Majority (for FRCPs): Valid and Applicable —**
- **Validity:** Measured by the REA (28 U.S.C. §§ 2071–2072(b)), and does not abridge, enlarge or modify a substantive right (*Sibbach*).
- **Applicability:**
  - Scalia: Construe FRCP 56 according to its text. Since it regulates the procedure of filing a MSJ, must use federal law (MSJ granted).
  - Ginsburg: Narrowly Construe FRCP 56; may claim VRCP 56 does not exactly "conflict" with FRCP 56, and instead requires additional documentation, so use state law (MSJ denied).

**Harlan:** Primary conduct —
- In Buffy's case, the VA General Assembly found that lawsuits may deter investors from investing in industries, thus causing some pre-litigation conduct of some parties. If the court agrees with the VA General Assembly that applying FRCP 56 would cause primary conduct of the parties to change, they will determine that FRCP 56 has wandered into a substantive sphere and therefore will apply the state law (MSJ denied.)

## Q4

*Liberty Mutual* governs this issue. In order to be able to file an appeal, there must be a final order (judgment) from the trial court. Because the finding on liability did not finally dispose of Buffy's prayers for relief, the issue is not appealable to the court of appeals. As in *Liberty Mutual*, none of the exceptions apply.
