# Assignment 1 Model Answer

## A) SMJ

Both defendants would move to dismiss from fed court for lack of SMJ. Groucho is domiciled in TX, Zeppo in DE, and Sylvania in DE (incorporation) or OK (PPoB); suits are for 150K or 250K, both >75K. TX federal court likely has SMJ due to diversity jurisdiction.

### IPJ

Both defendants would move to dismiss for lack of IPJ. TX has a long-arm statute to the limits of the Constitution, so TX fed court has same under 4(k)(1)(A).

Zeppo traveled to TX to visit Chico, setting off the events that culminated in the alleged theft; first experienced DiS in TX; and likely discovered Groucho's password there. The cause of action against Zeppo thus arose out of an act in TX, so TX jurisdiction likely to be fair/reasonable (*McGee*). Because these are key events in the subject matter of the litigation, TX likely has specific IPJ over Zeppo.

Like the *Luban* defendant, who didn't target AL but merely knew her rival lived there, Sylvania will argue that it didn't solicit ads in or otherwise target TX — Groucho wasn't even in TX when he signed up for the product (in SD) or heard about it from his European friend, so Sylvania probably didn't even know he lived there. Like the *Hanson* trust, which wasn't subject to Florida jurisdiction because it only continued a relationship when its beneficiary moved, Sylvania's only connection to TX is that a customer who bought the product somewhere else returned to the state. Even then, Sylvania has a stronger objection to TX jurisdiction, because it may not have received any money from Groucho while in the state (unclear if it's a subscription service or a one-time fee). TX asserting jurisdiction may offend fair play — Sylvania never solicited any business there and may not know any of its customers were there, because only Groucho's unilateral action brought its products to TX.

TX likely lacks personal jurisdiction over Sylvania.

## B) Possible Defenses

Applying TX HCoL rules, the location of the tort controls unless there are overriding factors (Second RS). So we have to locate the negligence, meaning locating DIS — difficult, as DiS is incorporeal.

- Sylvania is domiciled in DE/OK. DE has an interest in regulating its corporations, especially given its unique approach to corporate regulation, but unlikely that any decisions made there. OTOH, because programmers do work in OK, plausible claim that Sylvania's corporate decisions are there.
- Data storage centers in SC/MA, but nothing else links Sylvania to these states. Because Sylvania and other internet-based corporations could choose any warehouse to store its servers, little reason to locate it here.
- First Sylvania control over DiS in SD, where Groucho bought DiS and uploaded it to Sylvania's server. Might make sense to locate this one action in SD, but policy repercussions probably uncontrollable: Sylvania could simultaneously be located anywhere a customer accessed it, implicating the same concerns as for IPJ (no reason Sylvania would expect to be in SD), and a bigger company (Amazon) could conceivably be "located" in every state at once.

- CA: Last Sylvania control over DiS. Even fewer reasons to locate negligence here, as neither Sylvania nor Groucho had reason to believe DiS or Sylvania systems generally would ever be accessed in CA.
- TX: Groucho accessed DiS multiple times, discovered his loss, and got any benefit from DiS in TX. Sylvania could easily see DiS being downloaded in TX, so constructive notice that software accessed there.

After locating the tort, most likely in TX or OK, TX will consider the section 6 factors (Second RS) to determine which state's law applies.

*Location of evidence:* Unlikely in TX, as no Sylvania-directed activity there. Maybe some evidence in the states where the data was stored, but likelier that the programmers would be best-positioned to testify about their data practices, pointing towards OK.

*Convenience to parties:* Despite being right next to OK, Groucho will claim TX is the most convenient to him. Sylvania could argue OK/DE most convenient, but their own business structure — multistate data storage, international customers — indicate location isn't too much of a problem for them. Points towards TX.

*Protecting own citizens:* Like NY in *Babcock*, TX will want to protect its citizen Groucho from anachronistic interpretations of the law, and any interpretation of tort law that tries to assign physical location to internet "objects" is anachronistic, mitigating against CA or OK law. DE and OK have an interest in regulating their own corporations, but unlike in *Neumeier*, no clear evidence that the injury occurred there rather than in Texas. Points towards TX.

*Policy goals:* The policy concerns which led the *Neumeier* court to (perhaps regretfully) deviate from *lex loci delicti* aren't present here: in that case, an ON resident was trying to get around ON's allowed defenses and applying NY law would create liability for NY residents where none existed for similar ON residents. Here, rather than subjecting defendants to harsher penalties, the plaintiff stands the risk of more liability — TX's contributory negligence scheme stands to help Sylvania, the defendant, instead of the forum-shopping *Neumeier* plaintiff. Displacing the default rule and applying OK law won't advance any of TX's relevant substantive law purposes of incentivizing both plaintiffs and defendants to exercise care (such as original username/password combinations). Points towards TX.

The court is thus likely to apply TX law, allowing Sylvania to assert the defense of contributory negligence.
