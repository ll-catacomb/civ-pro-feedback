## Assignment

**First:** In 2009, in response to the financial crisis that led to the Great Recession, Congress passed the Deceptive Investment Practices Act of 2009 (the "DIPA"). As relevant to this question, the DIPA provided as follows.

> Any business or individual who takes out a loan from a financial institution or a bank because of, in whole or in part, of a false or misleading statement or omission (orally or in writing) by an officer or official or agent of such bank or financial institution, may sue the bank or financial institution for a statutory penalty of $10,000 (no more, no less) for each such loan taken out. The right to relief in this statute is in addition to, and not instead of, any other relief that such business or individual might have in law or equity. Any suit pursuant to this section shall be brought within eight years of the allegedly false or misleading statement or omission.

**Second:** You may assume for the purposes of this Assignment that if a bank officer makes a false or misleading statement or omission while offering a loan to a potential borrower, and that the statement or omission caused the borrower to take out the loan, the borrower may sue the bank on the theory that the loan contract should be rescinded (meaning that the contract should be declared null and void). This legal theory would be called fraud in the inducement to contract. This is a state law cause of action. Every state has some version of this cause of action. Assume that all states have limitations periods of at least six years on this cause of action.

**Third:** For the purposes of this assignment, the preclusion laws of Delaware, Idaho, and South Carolina are all the same as federal common law.

In March of 2011, Peter Pettigrew, a domiciliary of Idaho, represented by the law firm of Antioch, Cadmus, and Ignotus, sued Gringotts Bank, a Delaware Corporation with its principal place of business in Oklahoma City, Oklahoma. Pettigrew filed his lawsuit in the United States District Court for the District of Delaware. Pettigrew alleged that in 2010 and early 2011, he had taken out 10 loans of varying amounts each to finance a business he was attempting to start up in his home town of Boise. Each time he took out a loan, Pettigrew alleged, the loan contract stated that the annual interest rate would fluctuate monthly, that is, that the rate would be the Knut rate plus 5%. The Knut rate, which is based on a complex calculation of foreign currency valuations, is known to fluctuate wildly and unpredictably. According to Pettigrew's complaint, each time he took out a loan, the Gringotts loan officer in Boise stated words to the effect of, "Don't worry about the Knut rate. We've never charged a customer more than 12% per annum, and we won't charge you any more than that, either." Pettigrew alleged that in fact, however, just after he took out his last loan, the Knut rate spiked to 25%, and Pettigrew began receiving monthly demands from Gringotts for payments based on an annual interest rate of 30%. Pettigrew further alleged that from late 2009 to early 2011, Gringotts had instructed its loan officers to provide similar oral assurances about the Knut rate and Gringotts' interest rate practices to all would-be Gringotts borrowers. Accordingly, Pettigrew sought to represent a class of all persons who took out loans based on the Knut rate from Gringotts Bank from late 2009 to early 2011. Pettigrew requested $100,000 in statutory penalties for himself and, for each member of the class, $10,000 in statutory penalties for each loan Gringotts made based on the Knut rate. No other relief was requested.

In March of 2012, after discovery (and the collapse of settlement discussions), the district court certified the class under Fed. R. Civ. P. 23(b)(3). Discovery had demonstrated that there were about 25,000 members of the class across the country, and a total of around 40,000 loans had been taken out with the Knut interest rate clause. After appropriate notice was given, no class member opted out.

The parties agreed to try the case to the court. A three-week trial was held in April of 2014, and in July of 2014, the Honorable Severus Snape issued findings of fact and conclusions of law holding for Gringotts. Judge Snape found that Pettigrew had proved that in his individual case, the Gringotts loan officer had made the oral statement regarding a 12% interest rate ceiling, and that this statement was false. But Judge Snape found that given his financial situation in 2009 to 2011, Pettigrew would have taken out the loans had no such statements been made. Thus, Pettigrew failed to prove that the false and misleading statements caused Pettigrew to take out the loans, as DIPA required him to prove, and so his individual DIPA claim failed. Further, Judge Snape found that Pettigrew had failed to prove that Gringotts had a bankwide policy of having its loan officers make oral statements regarding a 12% interest rate maximum, so the classwide DIPA failed.

Pettigrew filed a notice of appeal, and the appeal is still pending.

In August of 2014, two new lawsuits were filed.

**First,** Gringotts sued Pettigrew in Idaho state court, alleging that Pettigrew had in early 2011 stopped making payments on his Knut-rate loans and demanding damages in the amount of the unpaid payments. Pettigrew answered and counterclaimed. In his counterclaim, Pettigrew stated only a claim under Idaho state law for fraud in the inducement (see above). His argument was that due to the false and misleading statements made to him when he took out his Knut-rate loans, which made him take out his Knut rate loans, his loan contracts with Gringotts should be declared null and void (*i.e.*, rescinded), and thereby making him liable only to repay the money he actually borrowed (under a restitution theory) but not any interest. In his answer, Pettigrew similarly contended that the misleading statements to him were a defense to Gringotts' collections lawsuit, because without those statements, he would not have taken out the loans. Gringotts moved for summary judgment on Pettigrew's counterclaim, citing preclusion principles. Pettigrew moved for summary judgment on Gringotts' loan collection lawsuit, citing preclusion principles. Assume that Idaho rules of civil procedure are identical to the federal rules.

**Second,** Gringotts sued Ludo Bagman, a South Carolina domiciliary, in South Carolina state court. Gringotts' complaint alleged that Bagman had taken out three loans from Gringotts branch in Columbia (SC), each based on a Knut rate contract, in the total amount of $50,000 from Gringotts in 2010, and that while he had paid each month the amount that would have been due had the annual interest rate been 12%, he had not paid the amount that was due under the written contract (especially after the Knut rate spiked). Bagman counterclaimed, alleging two legal theories. First, he alleged a DIPA theory. That is, he alleged that when he took out his Knut rate loans, the Gringotts bank officer in Columbia had orally assured him that Gringotts would never charge more than 12% interest, and that these assurances had caused him to take out the loans. On that basis, he sought $30,000 in statutory penalties under the DIPA. Second, Bagman alleged a state law fraud-in-the-inducement theory. That is, he alleged that because the false oral assurances had caused him to take out his Knut rate loans, his loan contracts with Gringotts should be declared null and void (*i.e.*, rescinded), thereby making him liable only to repay the money he actually borrowed (under a restitution theory) but not any interest. Gringotts moved for summary judgment on both of Bagman's counterclaims, citing preclusion principles. Assume that South Carolina's rules of civil procedure are identical to federal court rules.
