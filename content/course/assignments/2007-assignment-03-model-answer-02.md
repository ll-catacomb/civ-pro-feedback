Memorandum
To: Overseeing Partner
From: First Year Associate
Subject: Appeal from Summary Judgment in *Jones v. Clinton*

Applying the *Celotex* standard for summary judgment, to succeed on a motion for summary judgment, the defendant must show that plaintiff presented no plausible evidence as to a material element of each cause of action. If the plaintiff has demonstrated that the record includes sufficient facts that would allow a rational jury to infer the material elements of a cause of action, summary judgment is not appropriate.

The trial judge strayed from this standard because, rather than limit her review to the existence of alleged facts, she entered into a subjective analysis of the quality or believability of the evidence. This analysis should be reserved for the jury.

The quid pro quo and hostile workplace claim are the likely best candidates for success on appeal because Ms. Jones, as noted by the trial judge, did not appear to suffer the sort of "emotional distress so severe in nature that no reasonable person could be expected to endure it…" necessary to the IIED claim. I think the quid pro quo and hostile workplace claims are more likely to succeed because they depend more on the environment and outside influences on Ms. Jones, and less on her subjective reaction.

## Quid Pro Quo Sexual Harassment

The material elements necessary for the quid pro quo claim include (i) that Ms. Jones job was adversely affected, and (ii) her refusal of Mr. Clinton's sexual advances caused the adverse effects. Here, Ms. Jones testimony alleged facts sufficient for a rational jury to infer the veracity of each material element.

In a case involving allegations of licentious conduct, the only likely witnesses are the plaintiff and defendant. Therefore, factual allegations found in the two parties' testimony will often be the only evidence available to a plaintiff to prove her case to a jury. The trial judge found that there was "nothing in plaintiff's… testimony… showing that plaintiff's reaction to Governor Clinton's alleged advances affected tangible aspects of her… terms [or] conditions… of employment." This is just not true. Ms. Jones' testimony alleges that the terms of her employment were adversely altered: her responsibilities were not only reduced but deracinated: She testified that, upon returning to her job following maternity leave, "[She] didn't have any work to do. [Her] work had been gone. [She] was sitting there doing nothing." (Pl.'s Depo. at 14.) She also testified that she was deterred from applying for promotions. (Id. at 5). She also argues that her salary was affected, claiming that her salary was reduced by "a grade." (Id. at 7). Although the employment records also provided in the evidence conflict with this claim, Ms. Jones asserted that she would dispute the contradictory evidence, and this discrepancy should weigh against a grant of summary judgment. (Id.) It is for the jury to decide which of these sources is more dependable. Moreover, Jones provides testimony that her job environment was adversely affected: she was physically isolated, treated coldly, and not given flowers on Secretary's day. (Id. at 17-18). A rational jury could include that the amalgam of these alleged alterations to Ms. Jones' workplace do suffice a detriment suffered by her.

The causation element of this claim is a bit more tenuous, but could still be inferred by a rational jury. The testimony shows that the detriment occurred after her refusal of Gov. Clinton's advances, Gov. Clinton was friends with her boss, and he had the power to exert authority over her boss' actions. Moreover, the plaintiff testifies that when Gov. Clinton's bodyguard propositioned her to meet with the Governor while his wife was away, he was aware of the name of her boyfriend. (Id. at 45). There is little to explain the Mr. Ferguson's detailed knowledge of Ms. Jones' personal life unless the Governor was taking an active interest in Ms. Jones personal affairs. Therefore, evidence in the record indicates the possibility that Gov. Clinton caused the detriment to Ms. Jones work environment. A jury should decide the verity of the claim.

## Hostile Workplace and IIED

The allegations and evidence necessary for the hostile workplace claim to survive summary judgment are almost identical to those necessary for quid pro quo sexual harassment. Rather than evaluating the factual allegations of Ms. Jones, however, the trial judge seems to inappropriately harp on her behavior and her reaction to the hostile environment she alleges that she suffered. (Opinion p. 161-163). Ms. Jones' ability to tolerate a hostile environment is not dispositive of her exposure to the hostile environment. As discussed for the quid pro quo claim, and using the same evidence, a jury could well find that, despite her sang froid in the face of her hostile work environment, she did actually suffer adverse effects because of her refusal of Gov. Clinton's advances.
