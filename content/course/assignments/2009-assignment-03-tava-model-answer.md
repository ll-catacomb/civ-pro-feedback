# Assignment 3 Model Answer

When a state court adjudicates a federal cause of action, federal common law (as articulated in *Parklane Hosiery*) should govern the preclusive effect of the resulting judgment.

As the Court noted in *Semtek*, the preclusive effect of a case cannot easily be classified as procedural or substantive law. On the one hand, the preclusion philosophy of a reviewing court would not affect the "primary conduct" of the parties in litigation, because it would not shape the underlying decisions of the parties that led to the dispute (*see* Harlan in *Hanna*). Yet it may be outcome determinative (*see Guaranty Trust*). A defendant who lost in a prior case would be held liable again in a court that broadly applies offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel but be able to successfully defend herself and prevail in a court that does not.

Indeed, as the post-*Erie* line of cases suggests, the purpose of the procedure/substance distinction is largely predicated on policy grounds, geared towards ensuring that federal and state courts do not overly encroach onto each other's domain. Such an observation likely guided the *Semtek* Court. Given that *Semtek* involved a diversity case implicating state causes of action, the Court erred on the side of caution to protect federalism. By borrowing state preclusion law in such a context, federal courts ensure that they do not overly shape the future consequences of state law, thus respecting state courts' superior knowledge over complex issues of state law and the weight they deem appropriate to accord to a prior state-law judgment.

Conversely, a state court adjudicating a federal question case should apply federal preclusion law. Yet because the nature of the issues being litigated is federal rather than state (unlike in *Semtek*), the source of the federal common law regarding preclusion should also be federal in nature. Thus, the state court should "borrow" the *Parklane* approach to offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel. This approach would mirror *Semtek*'s rationale and ensure that the future impact of federal law would largely be shaped by federal courts, the "experts" in such law. Moreover, this approach would further the "twin aims" of *Erie* and enhance judicial efficiency.

First, applying *Parklane* would ameliorate vertical and horizontal forum shopping by ensuring that one uniform offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel framework would apply to all federal causes of action everywhere. In contrast, allowing state preclusion law to attach would promote such behavior. Given that state courts are courts of concurrent jurisdiction, a plaintiff could select a state forum that has an offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel framework in her favor.

Admittedly, the vertical forum shopping concern is not as severe in this context as in a diversity case; unlike in *Semtek*, a defendant in a federal question case could not be "boxed" into her home state court. Yet removal is still subject to limitations — for instance, a defendant must gain the approval of all her co-defendants.

Second, if state and federal courts were to both apply the *Parklane* approach to offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel to federal causes of action, federal law would be more consistently applied. Based on a plaintiff's choice of forum, a defendant may face two different outcomes were state courts to apply their varying approaches to offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel. Here, if Angel had initially agreed to removal in Primus' lawsuit, a federal court would have adjudicated the decision. In such a situation, Segundus' choice of forum is crucial. If Segundus chooses to litigate in federal court, which would apply *Parklane*, he would likely not be granted offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel (given that he could have easily joined Primus' case). Thus, the defendants could re-litigate and win. In contrast, if Segundus chooses to litigate in NY state court, under its preclusion law, he would likely be granted offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel and the defendants would lose.

Third, applying the *Parklane* approach would enhance judicial economy. While *Hart* sought to improve judicial economy by giving greater preclusive effect to a rendering court's decision (which here would favor NY preclusion law), the *Parklane* approach promotes judicial economy in an alternative manner by putting parties "on notice" and structuring incentives. If parties knew that *Parklane* would always attach to federal causes of action, regardless of where litigated, parties would have an incentive to join in litigation early on, rather than "waiting and seeing." Further, since the *Parklane* approach is a case-by-case balancing test, it would be more just — providing for an individualized fairness assessment of whether offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel should attach based on the specific parties in play.
