# Assignment 2 – Model Answer 03

MegaShop's motion to dismiss for lack of venue is very likely to fail. Under § 1391(a), venue is proper in any judicial district where the defendant resides if the case is in federal court under diversity jurisdiction, and under § 1391(c), a corporation is deemed to reside in any district in which it is subject to personal jurisdiction. Therefore, venue is proper if MegaShop is subject to personal jurisdiction in South Carolina.

Rule 4(k)(1) provides that the federal court can exercise personal jurisdiction if MegaShop is subject to personal jurisdiction in South Carolina, and the court will likely find that MegaShop is subject to personal jurisdiction in that state under a theory of general jurisdiction and possibly under specific jurisdiction as well. Since South Carolina's long-arm statute provides that the court may exercise personal jurisdiction in any case when such exercise is consistent with the Constitution, only a due process analysis is necessary to determine if an exercise of jurisdiction is proper.

General jurisdiction is appropriate if the defendant's activities in the state are so substantial that it should reasonably expect to be subject to a suit there on any claim and would not suffer an inconvenience from defending there. MegaShop is a massive corporation with stores in every state, including a likely large presence in South Carolina. It is very possible that the court will find that MegaShop has sufficient contacts — such as a significant number of stores, employees, sales revenue, and directed marketing — with the state that it should reasonably anticipate having to litigate in South Carolina. The court will also likely find that, being such a large corporation, MegaShop will suffer little to no inconvenience litigating in the state.

The court may also find that specific jurisdiction exists under the minimum contacts test laid out in *International Shoe*. The picture on the product indicates that it was in part intentionally aimed at South Carolina, suggesting purposeful availment. If the defective product is sold in South Carolina, the state would have an interest in regulating the product to protect its citizens. The plaintiff is also a South Carolina resident, and the court is likely to take into consideration the interest of the plaintiff in having the trial near her home. MegaShop, on the other hand, is a large corporation that could likely defend itself in South Carolina with little to no inconvenience. However, unless there is proof that the defective product was also sold in South Carolina, the court is less likely to find specific jurisdiction since the injury occurred outside the state and the contacts with South Carolina are largely unrelated to the cause of action.

During discovery, it will be important to determine the nature and extent of MegaShop's business in South Carolina and information on the manufacture and sales of the lamp. This could be accomplished through the use of interrogatories, depositions, and requests for production. Depositions of the regional manager and national sales manager would likely be appropriate. Interrogatories can be used to identify the appropriate employees for deposition. Requests for production should include documents relating to the manufacture of the lamp and sales figures on the lamp, particularly sales in South Carolina. It would be important to discover if the flaw was limited to a single batch or even just that one lamp, or if it was a widespread problem that affected all of the lamps and perhaps other similar lamps, and to what extent, if any, the defective products were sold in South Carolina. We should also request documents regarding the extent of MegaShop's business in South Carolina.
