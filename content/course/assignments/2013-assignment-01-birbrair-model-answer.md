**Question 1:**

St. Mungo's ("SM") may move to dismiss by arguing that the New Hampshire state court lacks personal jurisdiction over it. Because state courts are courts of general jurisdiction, the court has subject matter jurisdiction over the complaint. It is unclear, however, whether it has PJ over SM.

NH lacks general jurisdiction over SM, which is incorporated in Delaware and conducts all of its business in MA. Because NH's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the Constitution, the state statutory and federal PJ inquiries merge. To find specific jurisdiction under the Due Process Clause, there must be sufficient minimal contacts between the defendant, the forum and the litigation (*International Shoe*).

Pince can argue that NH has PJ over SM because SM purposefully availed itself of the right to conduct business in NH by running advertisements in its newspapers. Pince will note that some of the ads are adapted to NH, suggesting that SM could have anticipated being haled into court there (*World-Wide Volkswagen*).

SM could respond, however, that the ads are not strongly targeted (for example, they run in general newspapers and are not specifically sent to sick people). Furthermore, SM bars its employees from performing services in NH and does not send products to the state. If the SM could anticipate being sued in NH based solely on the newspaper ads, then SM would be subject to PJ in all 50 states, a curious result the court will likely avoid by granting the motion to dismiss. The court may also find that NH lacks an interest in providing a forum for this dispute, which does not explicitly involve businesses or services located in NH (though it does involve one of its citizens), and that Pince has minimal interest in litigating in NH since MA is easily accessible. In addition, most of the evidence, witnesses and documentation involved in the case would likely be located in MA. This would also be a basis for moving to dismiss on forum non conveniens grounds, but for the reasons already expressed, the motion would probably fail.

**Question 2:**

Pince cannot contest PJ or venue; she has spent years in Boston and went to SM to receive treatment, creating sufficient minimum contacts. Although she was tagged in NH, one need not be served in a state to create PJ there. Venue can also be laid in MA because most of the events giving rise to the claim occurred in Boston.

Pince could argue, however, that the MA federal court lacks SMJ over the state law negligence claim. 28 U.S.C. §2201 does not create a federal cause of action, so one must look at the suit the coercive action plaintiff would have brought to determine whether the suit arose under federal or state law. In order to get into federal court, there must be either a federal question or complete diversity between Pince and SM and an amount-in-controversy of $75,000 or more.

Diversity depends on whether Pince is domiciled in NH or MA. SM is domiciled in DE (incorporation) and MA (nerve center). Pince's domicile is trickier. She obviously began in NH, and typically "you are where you are until you get where you're going." Although she has not lived there regularly since 2009, her brother and patron remain there, she spends her summers in NH, and she has sought medical and legal services there recently. It is also unclear whether she will return to MA while she receives cancer treatment, which may weigh in favor of finding that she is domiciled in NH.

On the other hand, though being a student in MA wouldn't normally change her domicile (*Mas*), the two years she spent there between 2009 and 2011 might have. She lived in a homeless shelter at the time, though, which might not suggest an intention to stay. If she is domiciled in NH, complete diversity and AIC ($12M) are met. If she is domiciled in MA, a motion to dismiss for lack of SMJ will be granted unless the *Smith/Grable* exception applies to Pince's state cause of action.

The court might find that the suit necessarily raises an important federal issue embedded in the state law cause of action (whether the NMMRA is constitutional). The court would then need to consider additional *Smith/Grable* factors, such as how many cases would be swept from state courts into federal court, whether the case needs a federal forum, and whether the issue is a pure question of law or a mixed question of law and fact. The court would likely decline to make a *Smith/Grable* exception, however, because it would sweep a great deal of negligence per se cases into the federal system.

Lastly, Pince may ask the MA federal court to grant a *Colorado River* stay while the NH state suit is pending. The precondition of a parallel state court proceeding among the same parties adjudicating mostly the same issues, is met. Ordinarily, the court should still go forward, though it will consider a number of factors, including which court is more convenient for parties and witnesses (in this case, both are convenient), which case has moved further along (both are new), any evidence of special Congressional intent (there is none), and judicial efficiency. Thus, because of the strong presumption against granting such stays, except in the 7th Circuit, a motion to stay will likely fail.
