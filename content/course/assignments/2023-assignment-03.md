## Substantive Assignment

**Question 1:** Would you support a proposal to change United States federal court law and practice (including U.S. Const. amend. VII) to discontinue the use of the jury in civil cases? Explain why or why not. Your discussion of whether to continue juries should include a discussion of what our current system allows juries to decide, i.e., the allocation of power between judges and juries. It should also include some assessment of what the federal court system believes to be the strengths and weaknesses of judges and juries.

**Question 2:** Assume for the purposes of this assignment that the preclusion law of the states of New York and New Jersey is identical to federal law, and identical to the law that we have learned through (and including) class period 20. Assume also that both New Jersey and New York have declaratory judgment statutes identical in all ways to § 2201, and that their rules of civil procedure are also identical in all ways to the federal rules. New York has four federal district courts; the Southern District of New York includes Manhattan. New Jersey has only one federal district court, D.N.J. In this question, please consider all elements of a doctrine the question raises, even if you think one will be dispositive. Note also that because this assignment covers doctrines completed through the end of Class Day 20, you should not discuss issue preclusion in your response.

Splinter the Rat was born in the sewers of Manhattan. Infected by a radioactive slime at an early age, he became a national leader on the topic of civil procedure. He founded a law school for other creatures affected by the same radioactive slime. He taught classes in the sewers of his birth, deeming them, not unreasonably, the cleanest places in Manhattan. Splinter's teaching skills were not always the best, however, or at least, so his students thought. Splinter invited Professor Bebop, a domiciliary of Albany, NY, to help with a class on preclusion. The class did not go well. In particular, students April Mestre and Rafael Kleiner, both martial arts adepts and both domiciliaries of New Jersey, were enraged. They planned for several days, scouting out the sewer network and picking their perfect time. When the day arrived, April and Rafael lay in wait for Splinter in a dark sewer tunnel and, using ninja weapons, beat up

the hapless Rat. Two months later, April and Rafael were visiting Albany and unexpectedly encountered Bebop. The two seized the moment and also beat him up. The incidents caused $80,000 in medical bills and $20,000 in pain and suffering to each of Splinter and Bebop.

Both New York and New Jersey have a tort called "battery," which provides a cause of action to a plaintiff when a defendant intentionally causes bodily harm to the plaintiff via the use of fists, flippers, or weapons. The law of both states provides a defense to a battery claim called "justification," which states that if there was sufficient reason for the bodily harm at issue, the defendants are not liable.

Fearing that they were unpopular among the judges of their home state of New Jersey, Rafael and April decided to seize the forum. April said to Rafael, "I tell you what, I'll hire us a law firm, you just sign the papers and leave everything to me." Rafael said, "Thanks, just let me know what you need me to do, and I'll leave everything to you." April hired the law firm of Shredder & Donatello to represent them, with Shredder deciding to handle the case personally. Both April and Rafael signed retainer agreements with the firm, but then at the last minute, April decided to try to keep herself out of the litigation. April got Shredder to file a lawsuit naming only Rafael as plaintiff and naming Splinter as the only defendant in the Southern District of New York, seeking a declaratory judgment that any battery the two had committed against Splinter should result in no damages because the horrible civil procedure lecture Splinter and Bebop delivered on the fateful day justified the battery. Shredder worked exclusively with April on the decisions in the litigation through the jury trial. As soon as Splinter received notice of the lawsuit, he called his friend Bebop to tell him all about it. Bebop wished Splinter well.

The case proceeded to a jury, which rendered a verdict, and the Southern District judge rendered judgment in accordance with the jury's verdict.

**Question 2(a):** If, after the Manhattan case went to judgment, Splinter wanted to sue Rafael in a New Jersey federal court for damages, would Rafael have any Civ Pro defense? Why or why not?

**Question 2(b):** If, after the Manhattan case went to judgment, April wanted to sue Splinter in a New York federal court for a declaratory judgment that any battery Rafael and she committed was justified, would Splinter have any Civ Pro defense? Why or why not?

**Question 2(c):** If, after the Manhattan case went to judgment, Bebop wanted to sue Rafael in a New Jersey federal court for damages, would Rafael have any Civ Pro defense? Why or why not?

Note: You may answer subsections (a) through (c) jointly or separately as you prefer.

**Question 3:** There is a proposal to amend the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure to provide that the doctrine of claim preclusion will apply in a subsequent lawsuit anytime a person (or corporation or unincorporated association) could have intervened in a prior lawsuit, regardless of whether they received notice of the prior lawsuit, and regardless of whether they did in fact intervene. What would be the advantages and disadvantages of such a proposal? Would you be in favor or against? Explain.
