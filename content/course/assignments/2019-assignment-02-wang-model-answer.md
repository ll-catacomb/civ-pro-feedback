## Q1

**i.** Applying the three-part *Matthews v. Eldridge* test, procedural due process is triggered in the IL seizure. Hermione's interest here is important: in the bank account is all her money to support the life of herself and her children, including rent, food, and heating. WeBuyDebts.com also has a property interest ($700), but the risk of erroneous deprivation and the value of additional procedural safeguards are great. The same-day one-judge judgment, the one-sided, conclusory allegation in the complaint, and the lack of bonds posted create great risks [*Doehr*]. No extraordinary situation justifies the deprivation of due process before seizure [*Fuentes*], and no post-seizure hearing alleviates the harm [*Mitchell*]. According to procedural due process, then, Hermione should have reasonable notice [*Mullane*] (particularly, more than one attempt [*Jones v. Flowers*], especially given WeBuyDebts.com's "beyond outstanding" investigative team), and the opportunity to be heard [*Sniadach*].

For the NC seizure, Hermione's interest is still great ($20,000 in profit to improve her livelihood). Although risk of erroneous deprivation is smaller (more specific, sworn affidavit-based complaint and purchase/sale agreement), the judgment is still same-day, unnoticed, and unbonded, without the opportunity to be heard or receive relief. Further, signing the purchase/sale agreement doesn't waive basic procedural due process rights [*Fuentes*].

**ii.** Quasi-in-rem suits are also subject to in personam jurisdiction analysis, requiring minimum contacts [*Shaffer*], and attaching the IL property is only ok if the cause of action arises out of it. However, the cause of action arises out of failure to pay AmEx in MO, so the IL court had no jurisdiction to seize the bank account. One can argue that Hermione purposefully availed herself in and benefited from IL by continuing to use the IL account and physically traveling there (by bus) to access the bank account, but it might not be fair or reasonable for Hermione to be expected to be sued in IL simply because of that [*International Shoe*]. Also, holding stocks in the forum state is not sufficient by itself in *Shaffer*, so neither should having a bank account.

For the in rem suit, NC is the domicile of neither Hermione (MO) nor Fenrir (SC), but this might not be relevant as it's the broom that's being sued, and the broom is seized while in NC. However, it is unclear whether in-state seizure is alone sufficient [*Burnham*], and further, the case is distinguishable in that the *Burnham* husband "availed himself of the benefits provided by CA," while Fenrir/the broom was just passing by NC (the contract was with dealer in VA; Fenrir purposefully availed himself in VA, not NC).

## Q2

**i. Procedural Due Process:**

1. **Notice:** (1) UPSC needs to require notice reasonably calculated, under all circumstances, to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections [*Mullane*]. If UPSC now permits WeBuyDebts.com's attempt at notice, it probably should not. Hermione's apartment address is readily available; mailing to her address involves small expense; and receiving notice of lawsuits at home address is traditional and expected. (2) UPSC needs to require notice to include detailed explanation of reasons for actions being taken, and the defendant's right (e.g., to appear by a written pleading) [*Aguchak*, *Finberg*].

2. **Opportunity to be heard:** We might argue that UPSC needs to provide opportunity to be heard, and further, that such opportunity should be before the seizure [*Fuentes*].
   - a) The default period should be reasonable to minimize risk of erroneous deprivation, allowing time for defendant to answer. The current UPSC allowed writ of seizure to be served to the bank the day after the court order, giving the defendant no time to find a lawyer or prepare for the case.
   - b) The UPSC should provide opportunity for defendant to get relief to minimize risk of erroneous deprivation.

3. **Level of specificity:** UPSC needs to require plaintiff to provide specific information before the seizure [*Doehr*]. Bottom line, conclusory allegations shouldn't be sufficient. This is more of an issue in IL than in NC. For example, it could require statements written under the penalty of perjury, establishment of likelihood of success on merits, and documentary evidence (much of which was present in NC). Constitutionality of the current UPSC is dubious as it allowed the IL seizure.

4. **Bond:** Constitutionality would've been stronger had UPSC required bond to be posted, and allowed counterbond from defendant to get the property back.

5. **Relationship of the lawsuit and the property seized:** UPSC needs to require a strong relationship: plaintiff cannot attach a property just in case she wins [*Doehr*]. Unclear whether the IL seizure satisfies this.

**ii. Jurisdiction:**

1. The UPSC should require either in personam jurisdiction to be established for quasi-in-rem suits, or that the cause of action arises out of the property [*Shaffer*], as *Pennoyer* ruled that the Due Process Clause poses limits on in personam jurisdiction. Thus, minimum contacts should be required [*International Shoe*]. Since minimum contacts isn't satisfied even when shareholders had stocks in DE [*Shaffer*], Hermione having a bank account in IL shouldn't satisfy it either.

2. The in rem lawsuit poses serious notice and opportunity to be heard issues. In this case, it is a broom moving with the possessor, so the possessor can be notified. However, if it were a property elsewhere, the possessor might not receive notice.
