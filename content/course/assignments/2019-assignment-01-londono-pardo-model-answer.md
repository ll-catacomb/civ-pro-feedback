# Assignment 1 Model Answer

## Question 1

Yes, Florean can sue Bathilda, Tonks, and Chang Corp in a single lawsuit.

### SMJ

Florean will sue Bathilda and Tonks for breach of contract. No AUJ, as breach is state law. No federal question is embedded under *Smith-Grable*. Diversity jurisdiction applies. Parties are diverse: Florean domiciled in Oregon; Bathilda is a citizen of Delaware (place of incorporation) and California (PPoB); Tonks is a citizen of Delaware (inc) and Idaho (PPoB). Plaintiff completely diverse from the defendants. (Defendants do not need to be.) AIC is above $75K at $225,000 > $75,000, the difference between the agreed price and amount paid (and thus Florean's damages).

Florean will sue Chang for interference with contractual relations. No AUJ, as state law cause of action. No federal question embedded. Diversity jurisdiction applies; parties diverse: Florean domiciled in Oregon; Tonks a citizen of Delaware (inc) and Idaho (PPoB). AIC above $75K at $225,000 > $75,000 (the amount of damages if the tortious interference suit succeeds).

### PJ

Florean will likely be able to establish PJ over the three parties in Delaware and Oregon. California is not likely an option. Idaho is not an option, as Tonks is the only one with exposure in that state.

**Delaware:** Delaware has PJ over Chang, Bathilda, and Tonks under General IPJ. All three are actually at home in Delaware because they are all incorporated there.

**Oregon:** Oregon likely has PJ over Chang, Bathilda, and Tonks.

Can establish Specific IPJ over Bathilda and Chang given that they do business in that jurisdiction. Under minimum contacts test (*Int'l Shoe*), there is: 1) a state long arm statute and 4(k)(1)(a) allows the federal court to piggy-back, and 2) minimal contacts are met. Both Bathilda and Chang availed themselves to Oregon; they do business with that state, Bathilda had in-person negotiations at Florean's farm. Fair and just for them to get hailed to Oregon; Chang's conduct occurred there (OR has an interest in regulating that), and Bathilda negotiated a contract with an OR farmer (*McGee*).

It is less clear if Oregon has Specific PJ over Tonks, but it likely does. Minimal contacts are more likely met because Tonks knowingly purchased the rights to 150K of Bathilda's bushels from Florean, which included a contract signing. Tonks' inspectors participated in checking the corn that had come from Oregon; knew it was buying bushels coming from Florean from Oregon, or, at the very least, should have taken the time to figure out where the corn was coming from. Unlike *Hanson*, where one of the parties unilaterally moved after contract was made, here Tonks contracted to receive products from a party, and onus was to know location at time of contract. Likely Specific IPJ over Tonks in Oregon.

**California:** Likely no Specific IPJ over Chang because does business exclusively in Washington & Oregon, incorporated in Delaware, and PPB in Washington. Any contacts are too attenuated — it strains credulity to imagine that a decision to close the Oregon bridge would affect specific parties in CA; did not purposely avail itself of OR. (*WW Volkswagen*). CA has general IPJ over Bathilda; at home. Specific IPJ over Tonks: 1) long arm & 4(k)(1)(a), and 2) minimum contacts met by doing business with Bathilda.

**Idaho:** Tonks is the only one with any contacts there. No PJ.

### Venue

Within both Delaware and Oregon, only one district. Venue proper in D. Delaware because all defendants "reside" there (incorporation → IPJ). Venue proper in D. Oregon because substantial part of events occurred there (corn harvest, bridge closure, transport beginning), or because subject to the court's PJ. If California were an option, Eastern District because substantial part of the events happened there.

## Question 2

The two possibilities are Delaware or Oregon. Florean would be most likely to succeed if he sued them in Delaware. Federal court sitting in diversity will use the HCOL rules of the state in which it sits. (*Klaxon*)

### HCOL

**If Florean sues in Oregon:** Oregon is a 1st Restatement state, so it will use the law of California with regard to interference with contractual relations against Chang. This is because the last event necessary to complete the breach was the hand delivery of the second letter to Dobby in Sacramento, CA. California has the "intentional" standard for interference with contractual relations, which means that Florean will have to show that Chang interfered with the corn contract by shutting down the bridge "with the intention of causing [Florean] to suffer damages." This is a higher standard to meet than a negligence standard.

**If Florean sues in Delaware:** Delaware is a better place to sue. It is a 2nd Restatement state, and will take into account a more holistic consideration with respect to HCOL. Because Florean is based in Oregon, Chang Corp does business in Oregon, and the interference (shutting down the bridge) happened in Oregon, Delaware will see that Oregon was the "center of gravity" for the transaction. (*Babcock*) Delaware will thus determine that it should use Oregon's law, which follows the "negligent" standard for interference with contractual relations. Florean is more likely to succeed if Oregon's law is used given the lack of clarity regarding Chang Corp's intention in shutting down the bridge. He should sue in Delaware.
