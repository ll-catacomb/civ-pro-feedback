First, Townsend may argue that it was improperly denied its right to a jury trial. This claim will likely satisfy the two-part test described in *Granfinanciera*. Since the Boston Five's complaint is based on a negligence theory (heard by the King's Bench (law) before the merger of law and equity), the first part of the two-part test comes down in Townsend's favor. The second part of the two-part test is more equivocal, since the remedy sought by the Boston Five includes both damages (law) and injunctive relief (equity). The appeal court's decision on whether the remedy is more legal or equitable in nature may hinge on which of the two remedies is seen by the court to "dominate" the action. However, when both legal and equitable relief is sought, the court must preserve the right to a jury trial whenever possible (per *Beacon Theatres*). [NOTE TO STUDENTS FROM PROF. GREINER: YOU WILL NOT HAVE READ BEACON THEATRES BY THE TIME YOU DID THIS QUESTION, AND THE TAs WILL NOT EXPECT YOU TO DISCUSS IT; THIS LAST SENTENCE IS DESIGNED TO HELP YOU IN EXAM STUDYING.] Given the appellant-friendly valence of both parts of the two-part test, it's likely that the appeals court will recognize Townsend's right to a jury trial. The appeals court may consider whether the Boston Five's claim is appropriately heard by an ALJ, since the procedures in this case arise out of a federal statute, and thus may present an issue of "public rights" that can be adjudicated in an administrative hearing. However, this case is not one in which the government is involved in its sovereign capacity under a statute that creates enforceable public rights (like *Atlas Roofing*), but private rights between individuals (in particular, a common law tort claim). This is a one-two punch for the ALJ trial: the case does not represent a public rights adjudication, and the ALJ probably cannot seat a jury. That may mean the end of this statutory scheme, but . . . who cares?

Second, Townsend may argue that it was denied procedural due process, in at least three ways. First, the Boston Five's attempt to notify Townsend does not meet the notice standard put forth in *Mullane*. When the Boston Five were notified that Townsend's previous phone number had been disconnected, they were put on notice that the contact information in the phone book was inaccurate and were therefore required to take additional reasonable steps to contact Townsend (per *Jones v. Flowers*). The appeals court is likely to find that a simple web search would have been just such a reasonable step; in this case, a step that would have led the Boston Five straight to Townsend. This is not a situation in which Townsend was a missing person, and a single letter to a last known address was the best the plaintiffs could do (in contrast with *Dobkin v. Chapman*). Indeed, it's possible that the appeals court may consider the "regular mail" provisions of the USEA to be inadequate and strike down the statute as not requiring sufficient notice (per *Wuchter v. Pizzutti*). Second, the seventeen days that elapsed between the Boston Five's unsuccessful attempt to contact Townsend and the start of the trial was insufficient for Townsend to properly respond (per *Roller v. Holly*). Third, Townsend should have had the opportunity to challenge the Globe article as part of its fundamental right to cross-examine witnesses (per the seven-element mini-code of civil procedure set out in *Vitek*). This procedural failure goes to the heart of due process — by allowing to go unchallenged a hearsay statement by a politician to the news media, the USEA procedures undermined the validity of the trial. Even the *Mathews* factors can be said to support Townsend's position: the judgment damaged Townsend's bottom line and reputation, the risk of error is vast when evidence cannot be challenged, and the state governments already have a well-established procedure in place for adjudicating common law tort suits (use of which in this case would not present any special burden on the government or any of the parties).
