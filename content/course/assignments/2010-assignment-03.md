## Question

Assume that Massachusetts law is identical to federal law in all respects regarding preclusion.

Assume the following background: On January 2, 2006, Congress passed the Election Improvement Act of 2006 (the "EIA"). One of the reasons Congress passed the EIA was in reaction to a growing practice among states, including Massachusetts, to outsource the administration of municipal elections. Essentially, states (including Massachusetts) were hiring contractors to print ballots, provide voting machines, hire poll workers, count votes, and report final results to secretaries of state.

Section 405 of the EIA provided as follows:

> The Congress finds that the practice of outsourcing the conduct of municipal elections, while valid under federal law, poses grave risks to citizens' right to vote, as protected by the United States Constitution. Accordingly, should any state contract with a private entity (the "Contractor") to conduct a municipal election in that state, that contract shall be deemed to include a term imposing upon the Contractor the duty to assure that a sufficient number of ballots are available in each polling location so as to prevent undue delay in voting. This term shall not be waivable by the state.

The EIA did not provide a federal cause of action for any aggrieved party.

In early 2009, Massachusetts Secretary of State William Galvin contracted with Weasley & Weasley, Inc., a corporation organized under the laws of Massachusetts with its headquarters in Boston, to administer all aspects of the 2009 City Council and mayoral elections in Boston. When these elections were conducted in early November of 2009, however, there were widespread reports of long lines at polling locations around Boston. According to a report a week later in the Boston Globe, the delays were due to Weasley & Weasley's mistake in not printing enough ballots for the election. The article stated that Weasley & Weasley had estimated the number of ballots that would be needed by looking at turnout for the 2005 Boston municipal elections; in the 2009 election, however, the departure of several incumbents in the City Council had enlivened the overall contest, and turnout had been much higher than in 2005. The Boston Globe article detailed how every person knowledgeable about Boston municipal elections had predicted that turnout in 2009 would far exceed that of 2005.

Two people voting that day were Luna Lovegood and Pansy Parkinson. Both had had to wait in line from 7:30 am until 3:45 pm on Election Day to vote, and thus neither had been able to attend work that day. Both had been born in and had grown up in Boston and had recently turned 18, but had decided to take a year off between high school and college to work for Luna's father, Xenophilius, researching Crumple-Horned Snorkacks. They were paid $100 per day for this service, but because they had been unable to come to work on Election Day, they each lost the $100 they would otherwise have earned.

In August of 2010, Luna and Pansy went off to college, Luna at the University of New Hampshire and Pansy at UMass Boston. Xenophilius bought Luna a condo in Durham, NH to live in while she was at school. Luna fell in love with New Hampshire. She decided she wanted to live there forever. She intended to major in journalism at UNH and to start her own magazine, to be based in Manchester, NH, focusing on stories no one else would print.

Neither Luna nor Pansy was able to get over the anger they felt at the day's wages they had lost on Election Day of 2009. In August of 2010, on her second day of school, Luna sued Weasley & Weasley in Massachusetts federal district court, alleging in paragraph two of her complaint that the court had "subject matter jurisdiction over this action under 28 U.S.C. §§ 1331 and 1332." She claimed Weasley & Weasley's failure to provide a sufficient number of ballots for the election constituted a tortious interference with her employment contract with Xenophilius, and that this violation was intentional, willful, and malicious. She sued for $100 in actual damages and $100,000 in punitive damages.

Weasley & Weasley answered, denying liability. Its answer asserted that the EIA provision purporting to insert a sufficient-number-of-ballots term in the Galvin/Weasley & Weasley contract was unconstitutional as beyond Congress' powers, and because the term had never been validly inserted, there had been no wrongdoing on its part. After conducting discovery, Luna moved for summary judgment, and Weasley & Weasley cross-moved for summary judgment. On November 7, 2010, after hearing oral arguments on the motions, the judge announced (orally) that she thereby granted Luna's motion for summary judgment and denied Weasley & Weasley's motion. The judge further stated that she would decide an amount of damages at a later date and enter final judgment in accordance with the summary judgment grant and the damages decision then. On November 12, the parties reported to the court that they had reached a settlement agreement (terms undisclosed), and pursuant to that settlement agreement, Luna dismissed her case with prejudice. Luna later reported to Pansy that she received a sizeable check from Weasley & Weasley but was not allowed to disclose the amount.

Pansy has contacted you about filing a lawsuit in Massachusetts state court based on a tortious interference claim, just as in Luna's lawsuit. She is interested only in suing in Massachusetts state court, nowhere else. She wants to know what use she can make of the result in Luna's case to help her lawsuit. Can she make any use of it? To the extent it is unclear whether Pansy CAN make use of the result in Luna's lawsuit, SHOULD she be able to do so? Explain.

As applied to the facts of this case, the Restatement (Second) of Torts, § 766, provides as follows: "If [Weasley & Weasley] intentionally and improperly interferes with the performance of a contract . . . between [Luna] and [Xenophilius] by inducing or otherwise causing [Luna] not to perform the contract, [Weasley & Weasley] is subject to liability to [Luna] for the pecuniary loss resulting to [Luna] from the failure of [Luna] to perform the contract." Assume punitive damages are available if Luna proves her case on this tort.

You should NOT consider the merits of this lawsuit.
