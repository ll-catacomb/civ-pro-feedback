## Assignment 2, Question

The Virginia Bank Health Act (the "VBHA") was adopted by the Virginia legislature and signed by the governor in June of 2008. Relevant portions of the VBHA read as follows:

### Section 1: Statement of Purpose

The Legislature finds that banks in this state and across the nation face an unprecedented liquidity crisis due to bad mortgage loans, and that this liquidity crisis poses a grave threat to the public interest due to the citizenry's great reliance on banks. Among the causes of this crisis are:

. . .

-- an inability of banks holding mortgages to foreclose and to evict delinquent homeowners promptly, which prevents banks from quickly seizing and selling the property and removing bad loans from their books . . . .

### Section 5: Summary Eviction: Notice

Any bank holding a mortgage on a property located in Virginia may institute summary foreclosure and eviction proceedings against a mortgagor in default of a loan by filing a complaint in a Housing Court of the relevant county. Upon filing of such a complaint, the bank/plaintiff shall immediately give notice of the action to the mortgagor/homeowner. Notice of the action may be given to the mortgagor by delivering to him or her a copy of the summons and complaint in any of the following ways:

- (a) via hand delivery/personal service on the mortgagor;
- (b) via posting on the relevant property, accompanied by mailing via first class mail to the address of the relevant property; OR
- (c) via electronic notice, if the mortgagor has provided an address on a well-known social media platform (such as Facebook) as well as an email address to the bank on the mortgage application. Electronic notice under this subsection shall consist of EITHER of the following:
  - (i) notice given using the social media platform (such as a posting on the mortgagor's Facebook page) notifying the mortgagor of the Summary Eviction action, the Housing Court Case number, and the address of the Housing Court; OR
  - (ii) an email attaching a copy of the complaint and summons to an email address previously provided by the mortgagor to the bank.

<!-- page: 3 -->

### Section 8: Summary Eviction: Proceedings

- (a) If, in fourteen days after notice has been given to the mortgagor of summary eviction proceedings under Section 5, the mortgagor fails to file an answer to the Summary Eviction and mortgage foreclosure action, or fails to otherwise appear before the court or notify the court of the mortgagor's intent to dispute the action, the mortgagor is deemed to have consented to foreclosure and eviction, and the bank may proceed forthwith to claim title to the property and sell it to satisfy the amount of the outstanding loan. A Housing Court judgment to this effect shall issue immediately and is final. No motions to reconsider or for relief from judgment shall be entertained, and no appeal will lie.
- (b) If instead the mortgagor files an answer disputing the Summary Eviction and mortgage foreclosure action, trial to the Housing Court judge shall without exception ensue within two weeks of the answer's filing. This is a hard deadline that shall not be appealable. Neither party shall be entitled to take discovery, and the case will be tried to the judge without a jury. Either side shall be entitled to present evidence according to the Virginia Rules of Evidence and to be represented by counsel at its own expense. The Housing Court shall issue its ruling orally from the bench at the close of trial, which ruling shall be reduced to a written judgment that same day.
- (c) To facilitate the Housing Court judge's provision of written judgments, each party may submit proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law to the judge on the first day of trial, which the judge may choose to adopt as part of the written judgment.
- (d) If Housing Court renders judgment in favor of the bank according to subsections (a) or (b) of this section, the mortgagor shall vacate the premises within seven days of the judgment's issuance, with the bank to take possession at the end of such time.
- (e) There shall be no appeal from a Housing Court judgment.

Padma Patil, an uneducated farmhand, was and is a domiciliary and citizen of Virginia. Gringotts Bank, Inc. was incorporated in and has its principal place of business in North Carolina (specifically, in an office building located in the Eastern District of North Carolina). In January of 2013, Patil signed a mortgage contract with Gringotts Bank so as to buy a log cabin just on the Virginia side of the North Carolina-Virginia border in York County, located in the Eastern District of Virginia. As part of her mortgage application, Patil "friended" Gringotts on her Facebook page and provided her work email. The amount of the corresponding loan was $60,000; the assessed value of the cabin was $70,000. The contract had an unusual clause in which the interest rate on the mortgage would go up if interest rates on U.S. Treasury Bonds went down. Initially, the mortgage obligated Patil to pay $1200 per month.

Each month, Gringotts sent Patil a bill via regular mail, to her log cabin address, for her mortgage payments, and Patil made the February-April payments. But in May, interest rates on U.S. Treasury Bonds fell, and Gringotts notified Patil by regular mail letter to the log cabin

address that she would be obligated to pay $2000 per month in principal and interest. Patil received the letter and wrote back to Gringotts. She explained that she had in fact received the letter regarding the higher mortgage payment, but that she had not understood the unusual interest rate clause in her mortgage, and that she would never be able to pay $2000 per month. The Gringotts loan officer responded by letter to her log cabin address that if she failed to pay the $2000 per month, it would consider the mortgage in default and would foreclose. In May and in June, Gringotts mailed Patil mortgage bills for $2000. Patil mailed checks for $1200 in May and June.

On late June, 2013, Gringotts mailed Patil a demand letter requiring her make up the $1600 shortfall and begin payment of $2000/month. When Patil immediately responded via letter that she definitely would never be able to do so, Gringotts mailed her a letter informing her that it considered the loan in default, and that it intended to foreclose on the loan, seize the property, and sell it.

On July 2, 2013, the farm Patil worked for announced that it had gone bankrupt and would close its doors and shut down all systems (including email) on July 9. On July 7, Padma found a job working at another farm, this time as a farm supervisor. The new job had a substantially higher salary, and Patil realized (after a night doing complicated math) that she would be able to make the $2000/month payments after all. To celebrate, Patil decided to go on a two-week camping trip by herself. She left early in the morning of July 10, 2013. She asked her mother, aged 73 (and a retired state forest ranger who had never seen a computer in her life), to stop by her house every day to feed her dogs and horse, and to check her mail. Her mother was to open her mail and, if there was anything important, to light a signal fire on a nearby hilltop (where her Mother lived). Before leaving, Patil changed her Facebook page so that anyone using it would see the following message there: "Just got new job! Will be away for two weeks in the country without internet access--see you when I get back! xoxo OMG LOL".

Patil checked the hilltop every day of her camping trip, but saw no signal fire from her mother. On the afternoon of July 10, 2013, Gringotts filed its Summary Eviction Complaint in Virginia Housing Court in York County. That afternoon, a bank employee posted a note on Patil's Facebook page providing her with Housing Court Case number as well as the address of the Housing Court.

On July 25, when Patil returned from her trip (she stayed an extra day), she checked her Facebook page and saw the notice of the lawsuit. The next day, she went to York County Housing Court and obtained a copy of the relevant papers; a Housing Court clerk told her that she had missed the deadline to file an answer, that she had thereby consented to the eviction and to the seizure of her log cabin, and that a judgment had already issued. Patil asked if a lawyer could be appointed at state expense to represent her, and was told no. Patil was forced to leave her log cabin on August 1, and the property was sold that same day. Patil had no place to live until first paycheck at her new job, so she had to camp out in the woods for a few weeks in a nearby national forest.

Patil is angry about how she was treated. She wants to sue York County to make sure that no one else gets treated the same way; she also wouldn't mind getting a money judgment against York County to teach it a lesson and to supplement her new paycheck. She says that she
