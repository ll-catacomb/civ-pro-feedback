**1.**

**A. Law/equity.** Under current civil procedure rules/Seventh Amendment interpretation, whether a particular party can demand a jury trial turns on whether it is an action in law or equity. This is a vestigial historical structure dating back to 1600s England, but it does carry some advantages. Under *Tull* (especially as interpreted by *Chauffeurs*) the remedy sought is often determinative; this allows juries, which sit temporarily, to judge cases where the remedy is a one-time transfer of money, while judges implement and oversee ongoing injunctions.

**B. JAMOL.** FRCP 50 allows a litigant to ask the judge presiding over a jury trial for judgment as a matter of law at the close of the other party's case and again after an adverse jury verdict. Because the standard for granting such a motion is that a reasonable jury could not find against movant, granting such a motion either suggests a fear that the seated jury is unreasonable (50(a) motion) or accuses the completed jury of having acted unreasonably (50(b)). However, this process can be efficient: if a judge grants a 50(b) motion but is reversed above, there is no need to retry the entire case, as there is a contrary jury verdict that can be entered immediately.

**2.**

As drafted, your proposal leaves too much uncertainty. Repealing the Seventh Amendment's guarantee of a jury trial in certain civil cases is not equivalent to abolishing their use. Under your proposal, Congress could still provide for jury trials by statute, or could give courts the option of when to sit juries (see below). If you intend to rid the courts of juries in civil trials, this policy should be part and parcel of the repeal. If you instead intend to throw the question of jury availability to Congress, you should propose the framework that you think appropriate to answer this question going forward.

Perhaps Congress should attach the right or option of having a jury trial to specific causes of action that it deems particularly well-suited to determination by a jury. As a starting example, this would likely include actions that reflect the conscience of the community, such as suits alleging defamation or obscenity. Alternatively, rather than choose to avoid having to determine jury availability for each cause of action independently, Congress might either amend the FRCP (itself an interesting procedure, as amendments to the Rules do not typically start with Congress) or enact a statute specifying factors that judges should consider in determining whether to permit parties to try their civil cases to a jury.

However you and Congress address the above concerns, the second element of your proposal will also likely require a major re-think of the current standards by which judges "gatekeep." Because there would be no live testimony at trial, absent a change in the summary judgment standard we would ask a judge to assess evidence without making a credibility judgment to determine if there are disputes as to material facts (summary judgment standard) and then, if there are disputed issues of material facts, to assess the exact same evidence while making credibility judgments during a paper-based "trial" to resolve the disputes. Requiring judges to sequentially evaluate the same evidence at different standards would prove untenably artificial.

It is unclear how Congress and the courts would respond to this newly bizarre role of motions for summary judgment. One option would be to abolish summary judgment and instead

either allow the parties to move for final judgment after a certain period of discovery (much as they can currently move for summary judgment) or have the courts set a scheduled time for a final decision much as they currently schedule a trial date. If FRCP 56 is indeed removed, there will be greater pressure on courts to control their docket via 12(b)(6) motions to dismiss, as every case that survives that hurdle will be entitled to a considered decision on the merits by the judge. This might accelerate the apparent return to code pleading of the past decade. As commentaries about *Twombly* and *Iqbal* have pointed out, a heavy hand at the motion to dismiss stage requires significant pre-complaint investment by plaintiffs. Such investments are often much more expensive (for the plaintiff) than is finding the same information in the discovery process, so we may worry about inefficiently increased costs of litigation.

I also have several other concerns about the feasibility of placing such emphasis on deposition evidence. It will greatly raise the stakes and combativeness of the discovery period, as it will be not merely foundational but instead the whole ball game. This could greatly tax judges' ability to manage cases — for example, they may need to make evidentiary rulings during depositions to prevent the transcripts from being saturated with hearsay or otherwise invalid evidence. The prospect of a more intense discovery process might further incentivize judges to aggressively grant 12(b)(6) motions.
