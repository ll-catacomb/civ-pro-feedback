## Q1

### IPJ

### Erie Arguments

State and federal law conflicts on whether lawsuit should be dismissed for: (a) FNC, (b) failure to attach medical bills (FRCP 12(b)(4)), (c) statute of limitations (FRCP 12(b)(4)). If using state law, dismiss. If using federal law, do not dismiss. Since issues that seem procedural (attaching medical bills, limitations period) can be substantive for VCOL, do Erie analysis.

**York:** If ND common law used, court would dismiss case for FNC (ND law focuses on a non-US-plaintiff and events in Canada). If federal law used, primacy of plaintiff's forum choice, private interest of ND defendant to litigate close to home in ND (where alleged misconduct occurred), public interest in deciding controversy implicating ND company may counsel against FNC dismissal. Outcome determinative. Use state rule — dismiss case.

Given plaintiff's failure to attach medical bills, court would dismiss case under ND statute. If federal law used, case might not be dismissed because no medical bill requirement under FRCP 8(a). Outcome determinative. Use state rule — dismiss case.

If state law used, court would dismiss case because statute of limitations has expired (plaintiff filed suit 26 months after defendant shipped medicine; limitations is 24 months). If federal law used, court would not dismiss case because 24-month statute of limitations still running (plaintiff filed suit 22 months after becoming ill). Outcome determinative. Use state rule — dismiss case.

**Harlan Hanna:** ND FNC common law affects primary conduct. Law may incentivize companies to take greater risks in transactions involving non-US-plaintiffs in non-ND locations, because of FNC protection. Substantive issue, use state law — dismiss case.

ND statute requiring medical bill attachment does not affect primary conduct, since it furthers procedural purpose of ensuring complaint to recover damages is supported by evidence of medical bills. Use federal law — do not dismiss.

Differing limitations period unlikely to affect parties' primary conduct, e.g. business decisions. Limitations period serves procedural purpose of preventing courts from being overwhelmed by outdated cases. Use federal law — do not dismiss, because limitations not expired.

**Scalia Hanna:** No valid and applicable federal provision; FNC is FCL (*Piper*). RDA controls. Since state law requires dismissal but federal law may not require dismissal, failure to apply state law may encourage plaintiffs to forum shop to avoid dismissal. Use state law, dismiss. (No equal protection problem, plaintiff is non-US (*Piper* — American plaintiffs are masters of complaint)).

FRCP 8(a), which does not require attachment of medical bills, is valid under REA since it "really regulate(s) procedure" (*Sibbach*) and applicable since it involves pleading requirements. Use federal law (Supremacy Clause) — because FRCP 8(a) does not mention medical bills, do not dismiss.

Source of law is congressional statute. Valid exercise of congressional authority under Congress's power to create the lower federal courts (and thus prescribe some rules for them), as augmented by the NPC, if needed. Applicable since statute's text reflects congressional intent to prescribe limitations period for personal injury lawsuits. Use federal law (Supremacy Clause) — do not dismiss.

**Ginsberg SG:** Same validity and applicability as Scalia. Use state law — dismiss.

Construing FRCP 8(a) narrowly, FRCP 8(a) is compatible with ND statute. FRCP 8(a) provides the basic requirements for complaint, ND statute adds medical bills requirement. Same validity and applicability as Scalia. No unavoidable conflict, use state law — dismiss.

Even construing congressional statute of limitations period narrowly, unavoidable conflict with state law, since both laws provide different markers for when limitations starts to run. Same validity and applicability as Scalia. Since unavoidable conflict, use federal law — do not dismiss.

## Q2

Different source of federal law in each argument. ND FNC common law conflicts with federal common law FNC doctrine (*Piper*). Medical bill pleading requirement conflicts with rule of civil procedure. ND statutory limitations period conflicts with congressional statute.

## Q3

Ginsberg argument for pleading requirement may result in incoherence. FRCP 8(a) stipulates certain pleading requirements, which strike balance between filtering out frivolous complaints while ensuring it is not overtly onerous for pro-se litigants to file complaint. Stitching FRCP 8(a) together with ND statutory requirement of attaching all corresponding medical bills defeats the latter purpose. Also confusing to litigants since pleading requirements are found in two places. One solution is for ND to incorporate FRCP pleading requirements into its statute, along with medical bill requirement — so litigants can refer to pleading requirements in one place.

Mixing state law for length of limitations period, which legislatures may have calculated based on one marker for when it starts to run, with federal law that has different marker for when period starts to run may result in incoherence. Solution would be to apply Ginsberg SG Erie analysis above, and use federal law.

## Q4

The York argument to dismiss case for FNC furthers Erie purpose of federalism. By using state law, courts are respecting how federal government should not usurp state powers beyond its limited enumerated powers. The York test is neutral regarding separation of powers since the court is applying court-made common law. York promotes equal protection among US plaintiffs since the FNC common law rule only mandates dismissal of complaints by non-US-plaintiffs. By applying state law, it addresses prudential concern that federal courts were crowding out state law.
