## Question 2

The court will likely reject the injunction request. Elements in interpretation of FRCP 65 (in some circuits (e.g., 9th Cir.) these are factors, in others, they are requirements) (*Thanecoat*):

(1) Likelihood of success on the merits: The facts make it likely that Buffy will win on the merits. (i) We presume everyone owes a duty of care (given); (ii) Buffy likely will prove that XH and/or Faith breached the duty by not inspecting the trucks for malfunctions given how many bumps and scratches they had before hitting the sisters; (iii) Buffy likely will prove that had XH and/or Faith investigated the truck, she wouldn't have been hit (but-for causation), and that (iv) the truck's hitting her caused her physical and emotional harm.

(2) Irreparable harm: Buffy probably cannot show that without the injunction she would suffer irreparable harm. Buffy might argue that without the truck, her physical therapy would necessarily be delayed, causing a loss to bodily health (which might count as irreparable because body parts are irreparable for the purposes of preliminary injunctions), but this is a stretch because Buffy already "required care in a nearby Philadelphia hospital…before making her recovery." This indicates that the costs are mostly already borne and might therefore be compensable through money. Further, physical therapy might be possibly done satisfactorily later (after trial) if she has already recovered.

(3) Balance of the Equities: This injunction seems disproportionately harm the defendants for the benefit it gives. Even if we presumed there would be irreparable harm if Buffy didn't make it to therapy immediately, providing a self-driving truck for the sole purpose of driving her to and from therapy, which could be done with taxi or Uber money or, health permitting, public transportation is disproportionate.

(4) Public Interest: If the cars really are malfunctioning, putting more on the street (which is what Buffy is requesting) would endanger the public.

## Question 3

If state law, Giles Act applies, as Buffy gave no psychiatrist's affidavit, deny motion.

If federal law, apply *Celotex* (Brennan, J., dissenting). Buffy has burden of persuasion at trial and therefore must affirmatively produce evidence that if uncontradicted would compel a reasonable trier of fact to rule in her favor even though the judge reasonably construed the facts in the light most favorable to XH/Faith. Even in the light most favorable to XH/Faith, Buffy produced strong evidence of (ii) and (iii). For (ii) Buffy has showed with Maclay's affidavit that Maclay told

herself that three of the trucks hit polls and wrote it off to herself as a "one-time malfunction;" a reasonable person might think that a bigger cause for concern. Chase didn't investigate the scratches further like a reasonable person might have given she knew the vehicles were supposed to be programmed to avoid that and Maclay didn't give an explanation. For (iii), Angel's affidavit showing he was able to match numbers of the trucks is strong evidence that it was their failure to investigate the malfunctions which led to Buffy to be hit. The burden of production shifts to XH/Faith. XH/Faith with Chase's affidavit raised a genuine issue of material fact: if, as Chase avers, Chase and Maclay ran and relied upon the standard tests, they likely did act with due care, rebutting (ii). Likely, the motion for Summary judgment fails. There is a chance, however, the judge could grant it by analogizing it to the uncontradicted, object of the physical damage to the trucks, for which XH/Faith offer no explanation, to the video in *Scott*.

*Erie* implicated:

*York*: Dismissal of case would change outcome (no way Buffy can win if Giles Act applies on these facts, whereas she might under FRCP 56). Therefore, substantive, so use state law.

Harlan: One might argue that summary judgment rules are unlikely to affect how people act prior to any litigation, but as Giles Act § 6.7(a) states, maybe the heightened standard changes investors' conduct and attracts them to the state before litigation. If so, substantive and use state law.

Scalia: FRCP 56 is likely valid because it "really regulates procedure" (*Sibbach*, 2072(b), never found a FRCP invalid; courts approve them after all). Applicable to the situation because this is about summary judgment.

Ginsberg: Validity analysis is the same as Scalia. More willing than Scalia to render FRCP 56 inapplicable given the expressed state interest. FRCP 56 says the movant must show "no genuine dispute as to any material fact and that the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law." Maybe here there is no issue of material fact, but the lack of the affidavit (on which the rule is silent) is another way not listed in the rule, that the movant is not entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. Therefore, probably apply state law.

In sum, the tests point towards using state law, and therefore the denial of the motion per the Giles Act.

## Question 4

Only final orders are appealable (28 U.S.C. § 1291). Summary judgment here, a finding of liability *without* relief, is not a final order; none of the exceptions apply, as this case is procedurally identical to *Liberty Mutual*.
