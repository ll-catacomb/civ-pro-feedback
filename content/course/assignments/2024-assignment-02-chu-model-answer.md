## Assignment 2

Snow can't dismiss the case based on a lack of SMJ since there is DJ: Peta is domiciled in CA, while Snow was incorporated in DE with its PPoB in TX. The AIC is $400k — greater than $75k.

Snow can force transferring the case if S.D. Cal. is unhappy. Snow could argue for a lack of IPJ. CA has a long-arm statute that goes to constitutional limits (4(k)(1)(a)).

- **GIPJ**
  - S.D. Cal likely has no GIPJ over Snow. Snow is really at home in DE/TX. For *Daimler* at home, Snow has too limited a fraction of business in CA to make it unique — well below 50%.
- **SIPJ**
  - There is an argument for a lack of minimum contacts in CA. Snow conducted sales exclusively through an intermediary, which makes minimal contacts a tough call (*McIntyre*). Peta might argue that Snow has more than a single sale in CA per year (*McIntyre* Breyer), but it is unclear whether 10 sales are sufficient.
  - On fairness and reasonableness, CA is convenient to Peta, but TX is more convenient to Snow. Snow's PPoB and factory are in TX, and it has no factory or office in CA. Evidence is split — the injury happened in CA, but the manufacturing defects likely took place in Snow's factory in TX. CA has asserted an interest in protecting its residents from out-of-state dangerous products, while TX has asserted an interest in protecting its businesses from meritless product liability lawsuits (see the CA and TX Legislatures). Nevertheless, since both CA and TX are R1 states, CA substantive law will apply.

Venue is proper under § 1391(b)(2) since the product was bought and sold in S.D. Cal., and the injury happened in S.D. Cal.

If Snow can force a transfer, it would want to transfer to N.D. Tex. or W.D. Tex. Snow can also transfer to DE, where GIPJ exists, and § 1391(b)(1) venue is proper, but there is no benefit in doing so since none of the *Gilbert* factors point to DE. Snow is at home in N.D. Tex. (PPoB), and venue is proper under § 1391(b)(1). W.D. Tex. is likely happy too. For IPJ, Snow's products are all produced in W.D. Tex., so there's an argument for *Daimler* at home. If not, there are likely minimum contacts since TX's long arm goes to constitutional limits (4(k)(1)(a)), and Snow purposefully availed itself of the privilege of conducting activities within W.D. Tex. by manufacturing there. Venue is likely proper under § 1391(b)(1) (if Snow is *Daimler* at home in W.D. Tex.) or § 1391(b)(2).

Even if S.D. Cal is happy, Snow may still transfer if the *Gilbert* factors outweigh the presumption for Peta's choice of forum. The *Gilbert* factors are unlikely strong enough to defeat that presumption. For private interest, evidence and witnesses are likely split between CA and TX (see above, IPJ discussion). For public interest, jury duty burden is also split, as the case is related to both CA and TX: the plaintiff is a CA resident, whereas the defendant is a TX business. Both CA and TX have an asserted interest in this type of cases (see above, IPJ discussion).

If Snow can transfer the case to TX, it will want the court to apply TX pleading laws to take advantage of TRCP 8(a)(4). A federal court sitting in diversity uses HCOL rules of its state (*Klaxon*). If S.D. Cal. is happy, the court will use CA HCOL rules, which results in CA procedural requirements (*Ferens*). Because most things arguably procedural are usually procedural (e.g., SoL), CA HCOL rules would mean applying CA procedures and no TRCP 8(a)(4). If S.D. Cal. is unhappy, the court will use TX HCOL rules, resulting in TX procedures, including TRCP 8(a)(4). TRCP 8(a)(4) imposes a requirement absent in federal law, so we need an *Erie* analysis.

- **York:** Peta's complaint does not comply with TRCP 8(a)(4). If federal law applies, the case will survive. If TX law applies, the case will be dismissed. Use state law.
- **Hanna (Scalia):** FRCP is valid (§ 2072; *Sibbach*) because it "really regulates procedure." FRCP's plain text covers pleading requirements and requires only a "short, plain statement." Use federal law.
- **Hanna (Ginsburg):** FRCP is valid, see Scalia. However, Ginsburg is willing to creatively interpret the FRCP: federal law is silent on whether plaintiffs need to include the Testifying Expert section and merely sets the minimum, so there is no conflict between federal law and state law. Use state law.
- **Harlan:** While it could be argued that applying TRCP 8(a)(4) only affects lawyer conduct (what to put on paper), it likely affects investment decisions as well (as the TX Legislature suggests), which is primary conduct. Use state law.
