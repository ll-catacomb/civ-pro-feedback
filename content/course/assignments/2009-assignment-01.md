## Question

Assume Maryland, Virginia, and DC all follow the First Restatement approach on choice of law, that all three "states" follow federal forum non conveniens law, and that all three states have long-arm statutes that allow their state courts to exercise jurisdiction when allowed by the due process clause.

Peter Pettigrew was born and raised in South Carolina, where he lived until he attended first college (entering in 2005), then law school (entering in August of 2009) at the University of Virginia ("UVa"), which is located in Charlottesville, Virginia, in the jurisdiction of the United States District Court for the Western District of Virginia. In April of 2009, upon finding out that he had been accepted to UVa Law, Pettigrew decided to save on tuition by becoming a Virginia resident. Accordingly, with his parents' help, he placed a down payment on a small condo in Charlottesville, registered to vote there, and obtained a Virginia drivers' license. After law school, Pettigrew planned to move to D.C. to work for the Justice Department.

Dedalus Diggle, Pettigrew's friend, also was born and raised in South Carolina. Like Pettigrew, Diggle attended UVa undergrad, but upon nearing graduation in 2009, he took the Foreign Service exam, passed with flying colors, and was posted in August of 2009 to Barbados. The order sending Diggle to Barbados stated that his assignment was to last until further notice.

Before leaving for Barbados, Diggle decided to loan his car to his girlfriend, Ginny Weasley, a domiciliary and citizen of Washington, D.C. Diggle frequently visited Weasley in D.C. during Diggle's college years. In addition, the Ernie McMillan Car Repair Shop, Diggle's favorite car servicing location, was located in D.C. When in D.C. for a visit, Diggle would take the car to Ernie's for servicing.

Just before he left for Barbados, Diggle visited Weasley in D.C. and took the car around to Ernie's for a checkup. Ernie told Diggle that the car had a slow power steering fluid leak, and that he should have it fixed within the next few weeks. In his excitement for his departure to Barbados, however, Diggle forgot to tell Weasley about the leak.

In November of 2009, unbeknownst to Diggle, Pettigrew and Weasley began having an affair. To demonstrate her love, Weasley decided to treat Pettigrew to a beach vacation in Ocean City, Maryland. Weasley drove Diggle's car down to Charlottesville, Virginia, picked Pettigrew up, and the two drove to Ocean City, Maryland. After a blissful week, the two began the return trip in the car back to Charlottesville.

While still in Maryland, near the Maryland/DC border, with Weasley driving, the car spun out of control and hit a tree. Weasley called a towing service, which towed the car to Ernie's Repair Shop. (Ernie subsequently looked at the car and discovered that it had no power steering fluid in it.) Pettigrew was taken to a hospital in DC and treated for his injuries, which required an extended hospital stay and resulted in bills of over $100,000. After his hospital stay, he returned to Charlottesville to continue school.

Pettigrew broke up with Weasley. He then sued both Weasley and Diggle in Maryland state court for negligence. His theory against Diggle was that Diggle's negligent failure to tell Weasley to get the steering fluid leak fixed had caused the accident. His theory against Weasley was that she had been negligent in not getting the car checked out upon receiving it from Diggle.

You are Weasley's attorney. Your excellent friend from Harvard Law School, Luna Lovegood, is Diggle's lawyer. You meet with Lovegood, and both of you decide that Maryland state court juries are extremely pro-plaintiff. You would prefer to litigate in Virginia federal court if you could, but you believe that anywhere is better than Maryland state court. Lovegood just wants to do whatever is best for Diggle, and she agrees that anywhere would be better than Maryland state court.

### Question (a)

What is your best strategy for getting the case to Maryland federal court? Would it help to try to get one defendant out of the case?

### Question (b)

Suppose you're able to get the case to Maryland federal court. Can you get the case to Virginia federal court? Why or why not?

### Question (c)

Now suppose you're unable to get the case to Maryland federal court. What strategies might you pursue to force Pettigrew to litigate somewhere else? (Where else?) You should treat Washington, D.C. as just another state for purposes of this question.
