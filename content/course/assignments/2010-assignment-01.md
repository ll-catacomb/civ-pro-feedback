## Assignment 1: Question

Assume the following background facts and law (note all of this is fictional). In late January of 2010, Congress passed (and President Obama signed) the Emergency Home Mortgage Act (the "EHMA"), Section 1 of which reads as follows:

> **Section 1: Importance of this Act And the Programs It Creates**
> The Congress hereby finds that homeowners in the United States face a crisis in paying their mortgages that is unprecedented in the history of our nation or even the world. The financial consequences of this crisis are catastrophic to the United States economy and, thus, to our national security. Accordingly, the provisions of this statute are perhaps the most important to have passed Congress in its history.

Under the EHMA, each state was granted the power, as a matter of federal law, to change the terms of any contract or mortgage relating to a home so as to make those terms more favorable to a homeowner. The EHMA requested that each state appoint a state "mortgage czar" to exercise this modification power. Most states duly appointed mortgage czars.

Helga Hufflepuff was born in Charlotte, North Carolina, and lived there with her parents through high school. Her parents died in her senior year of high school, though, and as she had no other relatives nearby, she decided to leave the Charlotte area for college to pursue her dream of a career in international business in New York. She got into the Honors College at the University of South Carolina, and in an effort to save money on tuition, she decided to establish residency in South Carolina. Accordingly, using the money her parents had left her, she bought a small house in Columbia, South Carolina, paying $80,000 down and signing a mortgage requiring her to pay $2,000/month. The transaction closed in February of 2010.

The mortgage contract was with Gringotts Bank, Inc., which was incorporated in Delaware. Gringotts did most of its business in South Carolina, where its loan portfolio included everything from business and car loans to mortgages. Its primary operational headquarters was in Durham, North Carolina, and it held its board and shareholders' meetings there. Its CEO was Lord Voldemort, who lived in the Durham area. Lord Voldemort, a multi-millionaire, had vehemently opposed the EHMA, arguing that it was beyond Congress' powers under the Constitution. He had donated substantial amounts of money through various front entities to members of Congress who opposed the EHMA, and had purchased campaign ads on TV stations around the nation arguing that the EHMA was another big government effort designed to kill American economic freedom.

Hufflepuff made the February–May payments but missed the June and July payments. She had realized in late April that she would have trouble continuing to pay $2,000 per month. Accordingly, she applied for a modification to the terms of her mortgage from the South Carolina EHMA mortgage czar. In late May, the South Carolina mortgage czar sent Gringotts Bank (copy to Hufflepuff) an "Order" purporting to modify the terms of Hufflepuff's contract so that she only had to pay $1,500 per month. The Hufflepuff Order was one of several that the South Carolina mortgage czar issued to Gringotts.

Initially, Gringotts announced that it would comply with these orders and informed Hufflepuff that she need only pay $1,500/month on her mortgage. But Lord Voldemort, back from a 2-month period of viewing his yacht in a series of races, heard of the modifications of various mortgage contracts from Gringotts' website. Incensed that the EHMA was being used/enforced, Voldemort returned to Gringotts' central offices in North Carolina. There, he instructed the bank staff to ignore all mortgage czar modification orders.

On July 1, 2010, Gringotts notified Hufflepuff in a telephone conversation that it considered the South Carolina EHMA mortgage czar's "Order" to be unconstitutional and demanded that Hufflepuff resume payment of $2,000/month. When Hufflepuff responded in the call that she definitely would never do so, Gringotts informed her that it considered the loan in default, and that it intended to foreclose on the loan, seize the property, and sell it. Within the space of four weeks, the bank used available South Carolina legal procedures to evict Hufflepuff from the house and to sell the property. Hufflepuff lost her down payment and is currently homeless as she attends her classes.

In early September, Hufflepuff wrote a letter to Gringotts threatening to file a breach of contract lawsuit (on the theory that her mortgage contract was properly and constitutionally modified by the South Carolina mortgage czar's order) in one month against Gringotts in North Carolina state court.

You represent Gringotts. Lord Voldemort has been an excellent client of yours for some time, bringing tens of millions of dollars in business to your 2,400-attorney law firm over the past three years. You are anxious to please him at all costs. Lord Voldemort is currently facing lawsuits for civil fraud filed by various business partners in North Carolina state court, so he is anxious to avoid having the Hufflepuff litigation occur there. He wants to litigate in South Carolina federal court. Never a patient man, Voldemort doesn't want to wait for Hufflepuff to sue Gringotts, and wants to file suit in two weeks. What advice can you give this valued customer?
