# Assignment 3 – Model Answer 01

## Question 1: Federal Subject Matter Jurisdiction

There is likely federal subject matter jurisdiction here. Because this is a declaratory judgment action, the district court would have to decide if there was subject matter jurisdiction over an imaginary suit; if so, then the court does have federal subject matter jurisdiction. This is a major exception to the well-pleaded complaint rule. As applied, given the explicit grant of a cause of action (triggering arising-under jurisdiction) for suits in Section 4 of the EIA, Townsend can likely proceed in a declaratory judgment action because the Boston Five could have sued Townsend in an imaginary suit under EIA § 4.

## Question 2: Appellate Jurisdiction

There is likely appellate jurisdiction over this action, but on fairly narrow grounds. Under the collateral order doctrine, as articulated in *Cohen v. Beneficial*, there are three factors relevant to this inquiry. First, the order must be collateral to the merits — that is, it must be separable from the merits. Second, the prior decision itself has to be final as to that issue: the order must finally resolve the issue to which it relates. Finally, a later appeal itself would be too late and the rights would be lost.

As applied to this case, the *Cohen* exception would seem to apply. The stay in this case would be collateral to the merits in allowing the USEA to hear the case. Second, the stay appears to be final as to the issue of hearing the case to full conclusion (i.e., reaching the merits). There may, however, be one way out of this with the judge's phrasing of his stay order. The judge expects the claim to be resolved by the USEA; however, should the claim not be resolved by the USEA in a way that is satisfactory to the judge, he would in effect be able to reopen the case, and the stay would not be final. As to the third factor, save for the judge's looseness in language, it seems as though if the stay is not lifted, claim preclusion might kick in, preventing an appellate court from effectively reviewing the stay.

The other bases for appellate jurisdiction are unlikely to work. A stay is not otherwise a final decision. Rule 54(b)'s element of more than one claim among many is not met. § 1292(a)(1) only applies to refusal to issue an injunction. Finally, § 1292(b) appealability requires (1) a controlling question of law; (2) substantial ground for difference of opinion; and (3) that it materially advance the ultimate resolution of litigation. Importantly, a § 1292(b) appeal requires both permission of the District Court and the Court of Appeals, and neither court made any of the necessary findings. A writ of mandamus is an extraordinarily unlikely action here and unnecessary.

## Question 3: Appropriateness of the Stay

The district court's stay seems inappropriate in (prospectively) honoring the USEA's administrative procedures. An agency's adjudications, according to the Restatement, would be honored if the following standards are met: (a) adequate notice; (b) right to present evidence and opportunity to be heard; (c) formulation of issues of law and fact; (d) a rule of finality specifying when a final decision is entered; and (e) such other procedural elements as might be necessary.

Section 3 of the EIA is woefully inadequate; due process issues are raised. Element (a) seems to be satisfied in the fact pattern because Townsend receives adequate notice. Critically, however, element (b) is missing because the EIA does not empower the USEA to compel witnesses — including, but not limited to, the Secretary of State in Townsend's request. Without the ability to compel even written affidavits from witnesses, the adjudication is stripped of its essential function — investigation. Those who can voluntarily obtain the information will almost always win, a clear process violation. Taken together, *Elliott* and *Marrese* in the civil rights context indicate that any preclusive effect given to an agency — whether issue or claim preclusion — must be contingent on adequate processes at the agency level. Those processes do not exist with the USEA; that is, EIA § 3 is insufficiently robust.
