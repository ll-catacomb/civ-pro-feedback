## Assignment

For the purposes of this assignment, assume the following.

New Jersey uses the first restatement for horizontal choice of law. Both New York and New Jersey have long-arm statutes that go to the limits of the federal constitution. Both states have declaratory judgment statutes identical to the federal declaratory judgment statute. New Jersey substantive (in both the horizontal and vertical choice of law senses) tort law prohibits punitive damages for negligence actions. New York substantive (in both the horizontal and vertical choice of law senses) tort law allows unlimited punitive damages for negligence actions. Both New York and New Jersey have four-year limitations periods for negligence lawsuits.

In the tort law in both New York and New Jersey, there is a doctrine called "negligence per se." Roughly speaking (and sufficient for the purposes of this problem), the doctrine of negligence per se states that an actor is negligent if, without excuse, the actor violates a statute or a regulation that is designed to protect against the type of harm that the actor's conduct causes, and if the victim of the harm is within the class of persons the statute or regulation is designed to protect.[^1]

Note that negligence is a state law cause of action, but the statute or regulation giving rise to a negligence per se argument could be state or federal.

New Jersey has only one federal district, the United States Federal District Court for the District of New Jersey. New York has four federal districts; Manhattan is located in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York.

<!-- page: 3 -->

In 2012, the United States Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (the "CFPB"), a federal agency with the power to regulate financial products and transactions involving consumers (but not to create a federal cause of action), promulgated a regulation entitled "Credit Card Default Rates: Duty to Warn." In relevant part, the regulation specified as follows:

> **Credit Card Sold Over the Internet**
>
> § 5633: Any financial institution offering credit cards on an Internet website shall at all times include the following information, in at least 13-point typescript and in a clearly visible location, on the webpage in which such offer shall be contained:
>
> (a) The credit limit of the credit card offer; and
>
> (b) The interest rate or rates offered; and
>
> . . . [Other requirements not relevant to this problem]
>
> (h) A statement of the percentage of previous customers who have credit card accounts with the financial institution and whose accounts the financial institution has declared to be in default.

Accompanying the regulation was the following CFPB commentary:

> *The purpose of subpart (h) of section 5633 is to protect consumers. Potential credit card customers should know whether the financial institution from which they are contemplating purchasing credit card services has a high rate of consumer default. Consumers might be able to use this information to evaluate whether the institution's offers seem "too good to be true" or are being marketed to persons who will, in truth, unable to meet the terms of the credit agreements.*

Neither Congress nor the CFPB created a federal private cause of action by which a plaintiff might enforce section 5633.

LupinBank, Inc. is incorporated in New York. 90% of its employees, including all of its customer service representatives, work in a skyscraper in the city of Trenton, New Jersey. Its Board, Chief Executive Officer, and upper level management work in its office in Manhattan, New York City, and the bank's corporate strategy, financial strategy, and operational decisions are made from its Manhattan office.

<!-- page: 4 -->

LupinBank offers credit cards, including its signature "Wolf Card," over the Internet. On its website, LupinBank says, "We do business with anyone anywhere. The WolfCard is your best bet for financial success and independence. Get one today! Click HERE for your card!".

A customer clicking on the relevant location is taken to a webpage that says, "LupinCard: Make yourself into a wolf! Bad credit history? No problem! We work with all types!" Farther down the webpage appears the following information, in 13-point type:

> **CFPB Required Disclosures**
>
> LupinBank provides the following information in compliance with CFPB Regulation 5633
>
> (a) WolfCard credit limit: $2000 initial offer, may be increased after a review of credit history.
>
> (b) Interest rate: Super-Low-Low-Low 75% APR
>
> [Other information]
>
> (h) Percentage of customers in default: click HERE

You are a lawyer in LupinBank's general counsel's office. On January 14, 2014, one of the bank's credit officers walks into your cubicle in the New Jersey high-rise and shows you the following letter:

<!-- page: 5 -->

> **Law Offices of Creevey & Creevey, LLC**
>
> January 6, 2014
>
> **Demand Letter**
>
> LupinBank:
>
> This letter communicates the demand on behalf of our client that you pay her the sum of $80,000.00 to compensate her for the damages the negligence of your company caused her. The relevant facts are as follows:
>
> On December 12, 2012, our client, Penelope Clearwater, age 22, while at her college dorm room in Manhattan, NY, visited the LupinBank website. From the papers LupinBank subsequently sent her, we can tell that her visit occurred from 3:10–3:30 pm Eastern time. She clicked to the WolfCard webpage and reviewed the terms. Upon seeing the "CFPB Required Disclosures," she went to the portion dealing with subsection (h), and clicked on the "HERE" link. She was taken to a page that said, "This page under maintenance. Please check back soon." From looking at your regulatory filings, we can tell that had the webpage contained updated and accurate information on consumer default, Ms. Clearwater would have discovered that approximately 62% of WolfCard credit card customers end up in default on their cards. Had she known about this astronomically high rate of default, she would not have (as she subsequently did) obtained a WolfCard.
>
> Your failure to provide this disclosure violates CFPB regulation 5633(h) and therefore constitutes negligence per se. As a result of your negligence, Ms. Clearwater did obtain a WolfCard. Subsequently, she was unable to pay for the charges (particularly given the exorbitant 75% APR). Her college bank account in Manhattan was subsequently seized by a debt collector, and she had to ask her parents (who live with Ms. Clearwater in her Jersey City, NJ home) to ask for financial support to finish her semester. She suffered actual financial losses and accompanying emotional distress in the amount of $20,000.00, and if forced to sue you, we will seek punitive damages in the amount of $60,000.00.
>
> Please remit a check in the amount of $80,000.00 to my client's home address, #4 Privet Drive, Jersey City, NJ, 00713.

Through a phone call to Creevey and Creevey, you verify that the only cause of action that Clearwater contemplates asserting against your client is a negligence per se theory based on the violation of CFPB § 5633(h).

Through further investigation, you discover that LupinBank's website programmers are located in the basement of a Manhattan building. On December 12, 2012, from approximately 2:45–4 pm, the programmers were doing maintenance on the portion of the LupinBank website dealing with CFPB-mandated disclosures, and they had taken the subsection (h) portion of the site down. When the credit card officer asks you whether you think that there could be liability here, you reply, "I'm not sure. That's something a court would have to decide."

<!-- page: 6 -->

In fact, you'd like to get a court to decide it. You'd rather not wait around to be sued. You believe that federal courts are pro-big-bank, so you'd like to litigate in federal court if possible.

## Question

In what court or courts could LupinBank initiate litigation? What would be the nature of the lawsuit? Why is this court, or why are these courts, able to hear the suit LupinBank would file? What court or courts would be best from LupinBank's point of view? Discuss.

[^1]: Some jurisdictions may limit the application of the doctrine of "negligence per se" to cases involving physical harm. Assume that no such limit applies for the purposes of this exam. So, a plaintiff can assert a negligence per se cause of action alleging only monetary harm.
