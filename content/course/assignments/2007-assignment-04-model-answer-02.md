## Question 1

The Watertown voters could try to use the USEA decision against Charles Townsend (CT) through offensive nonmutual collateral estoppel (ONMCE). The likelihood of their success, however, is questionable. First, because ONMCE attaches only to issues actually decided, they will be limited by the USEA decision's scope. The voters would clearly want to preclude re-litigation of whether CT caused the delays they experienced. But CT might claim that the issue in the USEA decision – whether CT's breach caused delays in Boston – did not encompass the issue in this case – whether CT's breach caused delays in Watertown. As such, ONMCE would likely be available only for the limited question of whether CT breached their delivery contract.

Second, the voters would have to prove that the USEA decision merits preclusion. Under the Second Restatement, administrative proceedings can be used for ONMCE only if (1) they meet the preclusion requirements for judicial decisions and (2) they "entailed the essential elements of adjudication." For the first, the court will look to the fairness test put forward in *Parklane Hosiery*, the factors of which cut both ways here. The Watertown voters could not have joined the USEA action; § 4 of the EIA prohibited it. On the other hand, CT may not have had incentive for zealous litigation; the Boston Five's damages request was effectively nominal. Although one could argue that CT would have known the USEA suit would have triggered more substantial losses through issue preclusion, that logic is cyclic – the question before the court is precisely whether preclusion attaches. Finally, suit in the district court may offer significant procedural advantages (e.g., the ability to solicit live witness testimony on waiting times, to join responsible third-parties, and to depose the secretary). And this last factor may tilt the balance in CT's favor.

In any event, the USEA proceeding may also fall short on the essential elements question as the procedural disparities just mentioned also indicate that the USEA proceedings may not be of sufficient quality to merit preclusive effect. CT was able to submit depositions and affidavits refuting the case against them, but they were refused the chance to challenge the only evidence of their breach – the secretary's statements. Critically, this might run afoul of § 83(2)(b)'s fair opportunity to rebut provision, or, § 83(2)(e)'s opportunity to obtain evidence requirement. As such, the Restatement appears to counsel against the availability of ONMCE.

## Question 2

To overturn the Boston Five (BF) decision, CT will have to show that the USEA violated their Seventh Amendment jury rights, which will depend mostly on whether they had such a right at all.

Under *Tull*'s two part test, the right exists as it did at common law, which can be determined based on (1) the cause of action and/or (2) the remedy demanded. The cause of action here – a violation of federal election law – seems initially without a clear common law analogue. Pointing to BF's request for lost wages and citing *Granfinanciera*, however, CT could argue that the BF cause was actually simple tortious interference. If so, the right would certainly have attached to BF's common law analogue in tort. But this conclusion seems unlikely. BF's stated claim involved election law violations and the court may reject such a tortured interpretation.

As to remedy, CT could argue that BF's demand for lost wages was a legal remedy, to which jury rights attached at common law. *Atlas Roofing* and *Granfinanciera*, however, cabin this conclusion: because Congress can assign to agencies adjudication of newly created public rights, not only must the remedy be legal, the right asserted must be a private one.

There is, without doubt, a strongly public element to the right in this case. The right is a statutory construct, and it serves to protect the sanctity of elections – a uniquely public concern. Nonetheless, there is also a private side: the right neither "belongs to nor exists against the [] government;" and although *Granfinanciera* does reject the government-as-a-party requirement for public rights, § 5's bypass option implies that individuals, not the public, hold and can pursue the right's vindication. Ultimately, though, Congress did not merely re-cast a common law action in statutory terms. Private interference with voting rights is only actionable pursuant to the EIA's scheme, and may be considered an integral part of that scheme. Therefore, under *Granfinanciera*, the right involved will likely be seen as a public one, to which no jury right can attach.
