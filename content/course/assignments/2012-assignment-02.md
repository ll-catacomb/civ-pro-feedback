## Substantive Assignment

Assume the following, all of which might be (and probably is) completely fictional: South Carolina has only one federal district court. South Carolina's limitation period for all tort cases is four years. Virginia's limitations period for all tort case is three years. In 2007, Congress passed a statute entitled "A Uniform Tort Limitations Period for Federal District Courts," which provided as follows: "Notwithstanding any state law to the contrary, in any action sounding in tort filed in or removed to a federal district court, the action must be commenced within two years of the event triggering the plaintiff's entitlement to relief, or else the action is subject to dismissal upon motion." South Carolina follows the First Restatement of choice of law, while Virginia follows the Second Restatement. Both South Carolina and Virginia have long-arm statutes that allow their courts to exercise jurisdiction to the limits of the federal constitution.

On July 1, 2008, due to a inability to raise operating capital on faltering credit markets, the multilayered entities that previously ran the "Walmart" national chain of superstores in the United States sold all of their assets (including the stores and the "Walmart" brand name) to a single corporation, MegaShop, Inc. MegaShop was incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in New York. Megashop executives decided to keep the Walmart brand name and all of its stores, the operations of which remained essentially unchanged. Indeed, industry observers commented that consumers "never knew the difference." (Assume the Walmart business entities that were bought in this transaction were the Walmart business entities that you know and love in real life.)

On July 15, 2009, Ms. Padma Patil, a resident and citizen of South Carolina, was traveling in her car on the way to see her sister, Parvati, who lived in Alexandria, Virginia, which is located in the Eastern District of Virginia. On her way through North Carolina, Padma stopped at a Walmart store and bought a tacky lamp as a present for her sister. The shade of the lamp pictured scenes from famous South and North Carolina beaches, including a tacky picture of famous surfer Nicholas Flamel (a South Carolina native) posing in Myrtle Beach (in South Carolina), along with a series of shots of sports hero Victor Krum at South of the Border, a novelty shop and restaurant location on the South Carolina side of the North Carolina/South Carolina border. When Padma arrived at her sister's house and plugged in the gift, the lamp exploded, causing serious injuries to both Patils. Both were hospitalized in a Maryland hospital, and each ended up incurring over $90,000.00 in medical expenses.

You are a partner in the law firm who represents the Patils. On behalf of both Patils, your law firm filed an action in South Carolina federal district court on September 10, 2012. The complaint alleged that MegaShop, Inc. negligently designed and manufactured the lamp, and that it also was strictly liable in tort for the Patils' injuries. On September 25, 2012, the attorney for Megashop contacted you and suggested that the parties consent to a change of venue to the Eastern District of Virginia. After researching recent jury awards in the Eastern District of Virginia and comparing them to those of the District of South Carolina, you agree. On October 1, 2012, the case was transferred to the Eastern District of Virginia. On October 2, Megashop's attorney filed a motion to dismiss, alleging that
