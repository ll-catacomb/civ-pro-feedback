Repealing 1367(b) could possibly end such bizarre cases as *Guaranteed Systems, Inc. v. American National Can Co.* 1367(b) attempted to codify much of the case law that occurred in cases like *Kroger*. It sought to prevent plaintiffs from circumventing *Strawbridge*'s requirement of complete diversity. 1367(b), however, produced a number of odd results, many of which are noted by Professor Freer on page 304 of the casebook. Plaintiffs become disadvantaged in a number of cases, particularly when counterclaims are brought against them and they become de facto defendants. As cases like *Guaranteed Systems* show, even though the plaintiffs are defending against counterclaims, they are still treated as plaintiffs as far as 1367(b) is concerned.

Repealing 1367(b) would have a number of interesting results in diversity cases. In cases like *Aldinger* and *Kroger*, the Court emphasized that it was not enough that Article III gave the courts constitutional power to hear related claims; they needed additional statutory authority to exercise that jurisdiction. This is what 1367 sought to do. Subsection (a) gave the courts the ability to use supplement jurisdiction to the limits of Article III. The only exception to this power was section (b). In diversity cases the "same case or controversy" requirement was not the only hurdle parties had to cross in order to use supplemental jurisdiction. Plaintiffs also had to follow complete diversity in almost all cases.

Repealing subsection (b) would make subsection (a) the only requirement necessary to exercise supplemental jurisdiction and would essentially repeal *Strawbridge* and would make much of the case law prior to 1367 invalid since the statute was meant to codify such decisions. As long as the additional party was considered part of the same case or controversy, supplemental jurisdiction would be allowed. Presumably some courts would follow the definition given in *Gibbs* to this concept. However, even the decision in *Gibbs* would be put in doubt as some could argue that 1367(a) was the only definition Congress wanted to give to the idea of "same case or controversy."

However, 1367(c) would still remain. Subsection (c) could be seen as either replacing *Gibbs* or working in conjunction with it. Either way, the ability to keep cases in the federal system would become much more discretionary for district court judges. Cases like *Guaranteed Systems* may not be given supplemental jurisdiction by district court judges. However they may rely more on subsection (c) (and possibly on *Gibbs*) in order to dismiss such cases on jurisdictional grounds. District courts might be able to hear more of the cases they want as a result.

There is no doubt, however, that the elimination of subsection (b) would cause many more cases to end up in federal court. This might have positive effects of relieving some of the burden on the overloaded state systems. It would also increase efficiency as claims that are part of the same case or controversy would be kept together instead of being split between the two systems. Similar cases would not have to be conducted twice. Moreover, the system would be more fair in its treatment of plaintiffs who had counterclaims brought against them, yet were left powerless in federal court under the current version of 1367.

On the other hand, this would cause many more state cases to be heard in front of federal judges. This leads to the larger discussion of diversity jurisdiction. Many critique diversity jurisdiction as a violation of federalism since legitimate state issues are dealt with by federal courts. Moreover, diversity jurisdiction limits the ability of state law to develop through case law. An increased scope of supplemental jurisdiction would only magnify problems such as these.
