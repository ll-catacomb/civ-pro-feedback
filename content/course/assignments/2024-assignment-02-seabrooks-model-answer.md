## Snow Can't Dismiss for Lack of SMJ

This suit has DJ. Snow is incorporated in Delaware, with its PPoB/nerve center (*Hertz*) in Texas. Peeta is a domiciliary of California. (*Strawbridge*). AIC is sufficient ($400,000 > $75,000).

## Snow May Object to IPJ

Three (maybe 4) ways to get GIPJ. None are applicable to Snow.

1. Snow is not at home in California (see analysis above).
2. Snow is not "essentially at home" (*Daimler*) because significantly less than 50% of Snow business is conducted in CA.
3. No evidence Snow consented to suit in CA.
4. There is no evidence Snow was tagged in CA.

### SIPJ is close.

California's extent-of-the-Constitution longarm, with FRCP 4(k)(1)(A), means that it welcomes these tort suits, but minimum contacts may be lacking under *International Shoe* (e.g., no advertising in CA, single-page website, no trade shows). Moreover, Snow should emphasize that Capitol sells goods into CA, not Snow directly. An intermediary makes it harder to find SIPJ (*McIntyre*). Especially true absent evidence that Snow directed its actions at California (*Calder*). The Breyer *McIntyre* concurrence leaves *some* room for an argument regarding SIPJ (i.e., how much business = a lot of business; 10 units/year cuts it close), but ultimately, the lack of contacts and purposeful availment probably overcomes.

The fair and reasonableness factors are also close. Though the defendant's contacts are not extensive, traveling to California is probably not too inconvenient and Snow products are sold there. Moreover, California is interested in providing justice to its plaintiffs and regulating tortious conduct within its jurisdiction. Evidence of the harm is in California, though evidence of the negligent manufacturing is probably in Texas.

## Venue is Proper

Venue is proper under § 1391(b)(2) because the sale and injury occurred there.

If Snow can win the IPJ argument, then SDCA is unhappy. Transfer is mandatory (*Goldlawr*). NDTX would be a happy venue because that is where Snow is PPoB, providing GIPJ and proper venue (§ 1391(b)(1)). Maybe WDTX (also GIPJ, because it's anywhere in TX) since that is where the bow is manufactured — therefore substantial portion of events (§ 1391(b)(2)). If the S.D. Cal. is happy, the court would consider if it *should* transfer. Snow must overcome a presumption for plaintiff's choice, but if transfer is in the interest of justice (*Gulf Oil Corp*), the court may allow it. Access to evidence split between SDCA, NDTX and WDTX. The premise of the accident is SDCA. One could argue that the people of Texas have stronger interest in regulating manufacturing in Texas. Conversely, a jury in CA may have strong interest in protecting plaintiffs. Congestion is equally problematic in either district. Access to witnesses and Peeta's hospitalization probably weigh against transferring to CA.

**Assuming Snow is able to transfer, Tex. R. Civ. P. 8(a)(4) would probably apply.**

Ultimately, TRCP is procedural, so the choice of law analysis does not matter much. But if happy court to happy court, California's choice of law rules would follow to Texas. If unhappy to happy, then Texas's choice of law rules would apply. Again, either way, TRCP would apply.

*Erie* analysis is necessary to resolve conflict between Texas pleading rules and federal pleading rules.

- ***York*:** The choice of whether to apply TRCP is outcome-determinative. If TRCP applies, Peeta's suit will likely get dismissed (see analysis below). Therefore, use **state** law.

- ***Harlan* Primary Conduct:** The stated purpose of Texas's law is to shield manufacturers from liability. This alters the conduct of both plaintiffs and manufacturers by deterring frivolous lawsuits and theoretically allowing Texas businesses to invest in the production of consumer products. Use **state** law.

- **Hanna Majority:** FRCP is valid because of the Rules Enabling Act. It does not abridge any substantive right (*Sibbach*). Therefore, it really does regulate procedure. Read with eye towards the FRCP's plain meaning, FRCP 8 addresses the issue regarding pleading procedure. Therefore, apply **federal** law.

- **Hanna (Ginsburg):** Validity analysis stays the same, but applicability can be read more narrowly. Here, the FRCP sets a minimum pleading standard. Texas's law imposes more limits, but does not directly conflict with the FRCP (*Walker*). Therefore, may use **state** law.

Snow should move to dismiss under 12(b)(6) (or state equivalent). Though Peeta does not have a "Testifying Expert." Therefore, the complaint can be dismissed under TRCP 8.
