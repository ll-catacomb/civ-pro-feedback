# Assignment 2: Model Answer

---

Structurally, before considering which court would be the most advantageous for Jones, it is essential to determine in which courts she will be able to sue at all. Because the alleged facts giving rise to the complaint took place in Arkansas, it is likely that Jones will be able to establish minimum contacts and get IPJ over Clinton there. *International Shoe*. Given Clinton's obvious connection to both Arkansas and Jones, he will not be able to escape IPJ under *World-Wide Volkswagen*. Similarly, because Clinton's residence and domicile are in NY it is a certainty that Jones will be able to get IPJ over Clinton in his new "home" state. Since IPJ will likely be satisfied in either state, it will also be the case that Jones has IPJ over Clinton in federal court in both Arkansas and NY. FRCP 4(k)(1)(A). California is a bit trickier. Although it would seem that Jones can bring suit there because she is a resident, it is unlikely that she will be able to establish that Clinton satisfies the minimum contacts requirement. *International Shoe*.

Back to Arkansas and NY: because Jones's claim ($200k) satisfies the amount in controversy requirement (28 U.S.C. § 1332) and because complete diversity exists between the parties (*Strawbridge*), Jones likely will be able to establish SMJ in either federal court. Venue is also not a problem in either NY or Arkansas federal court. Under § 1391(a) jurisdiction is permissible in the defendant's home state (i.e., NY), and SMJ in Arkansas is possible under § 1391(b), which allows jurisdiction in the locus of a substantial part of the events.

Having established that the two states available are likely NY and Arkansas, and that federal court in either state is also likely available, the question becomes which forum provides a greater advantage. This is a question of pleading standards. Given the recent decisions in *Granger* and *Skeeter*, it is likely that both NY and Arkansas state courts will apply notice-pleading standards, whereas federal courts are likely to apply the heightened *Iqbal*/*Bell Atlantic* "plausibility" standard. Jones's other lawyers may have failed to satisfy the third prong of Arkansas's Title VII ("adverse consequences were orchestrated by the defendant because of the plaintiff's refusal to engage in a sexual relationship"): while the complaint spells out the details of the alleged meeting between Clinton and Jones, at no point does it concretely link her workplace treatment to Clinton, and so is likely insufficient under the *Iqbal* standard (which requires more than just that the facts suggest the possibility of the defendant's liability) to recite conclusory statements like "It was a pretext for the real reason which was that she was being punished for her rejection of the various advances made by Clinton." ¶ 39. As such, we're going to want to be in the court with the lowest bar for pleading requirements (and *DioGuardi* suggests that the notice standard is lower than it seems).

While both Arkansas (First Restatement) and NY (Second Restatement) will likely apply Arkansas substantive law, for horizontal choice of law purposes the motion to dismiss standard will likely be considered procedural, so each state court would apply its own procedural law. Both states have a notice standard for motions to dismiss, making the question one of removal. Because Clinton would almost certainly remove to federal court in Arkansas within the required 30 days (§ 1441(a)) but would be unable to under § 1441(b) (precluding defendants from removing to federal court in their home state), the optimal court for Jones is NY state court.

But could she get the state pleading standard in federal court? This is going to be tough, but there are several vertical *Erie* arguments. The issue is that it sure looks like state and federal law (Rules 8 and 12) directly conflict. Jones's best argument would be under *York*: since the question would be dispositive of the issue (outcome determinative) state law would apply. Similarly, Jones could try a *Hanna* twin-aims argument, noting that forum shopping and inequitable administration of the laws are both implicated here (since the different pleading standards would be guiding Clinton's choice to remove). Harlan's concurrence in *Hanna* is a problem: Jones is fighting an uphill battle if she tries to argue that the parties' primary conduct was affected by the different rules applied in federal and state court (put differently, pleading standards were likely not on Clinton's mind when the "occurrence" occurred). Finally, while it is also possible that, under *Walker*, Jones could argue for a narrow reading of *Iqbal*, limiting it to its high-discovery cost and public persona facts, since those same facts likely apply with regard to President Clinton the chances of success here are bleak. Of course, the biggest problem here is that a controlling federal rule appears to be directly in conflict with the state rule, and that the federal rule is valid under the Rules Enabling Act because it does not substantially affect a substantive right. 28 U.S.C. § 2072.

This paints something of a rosy picture, though; it seems highly unlikely that a federal court would apply the state pleading standard. Clinton would remove if Jones filed in Arkansas, elevating the standard to *Iqbal*, creating a problem for Jones in terms of the third prong of Title VII. Thus, Jones should bring this case in NY state court, sticking Clinton in his adopted backyard.
