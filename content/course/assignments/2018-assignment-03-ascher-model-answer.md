## Question 1

SMJ, PJ, and venue are all proper in both D. Me. and D.N.H.

IPJ is OK in a Me. state court because it has a longarm statute going to the limits of the constitution, and because Ginny's purposeful availment action of driving in Me. and getting into an accident there provides minimal contacts and makes the exercise of jurisdiction fair and reasonable. IPJ is OK in N.H. state court because Ginny is domiciled there. IPJ is thus OK in N.H. or Me. federal court under FRCP 4(k)(1)(A).

SMJ is OK in any federal court because Ginny is domiciled in N.H., Aberforth in Me., and damages > $150K. Venue lies in D.N.H. because the defendant is a domiciliary of N.H. § 1391(b)(1). Venue lies in D. Me. because all of the events giving rise to this claim took place in Me. § 1391(b)(2).

This means I can file in federal court in either D.N.H. or D. Me. N.H. state court uses notice pleading (requiring only "a short, plain statement showing plaintiff's entitlement to relief," *Dioguardi*). Me. state and federal court will each use *Twiqbal*'s plausibility pleading standard. N.H. federal court will probably use plausibility pleading too: Rule 8(a) is a valid (see Rules Enabling Act) and applicable procedural law, so a court sitting in diversity will apply it (*Hanna*). Rule 8(a) = plausibility pleading (*Twiqbal*).

N.H. state court is the only forum where I can get notice pleading, which is good for me since it's a lower burden. And if I file in N.H. state court, Ginny cannot remove to federal court, so I definitely get the notice pleading standard. That said, I probably have enough underlying facts to satisfy plausibility pleading, and as a result I may wish to sue in Me. to avoid the possibility of home cooking in N.H. It's a close call, but I think the benefit of notice pleading outweighs the potential harm of home cooking — N.H. state court it is!

## Question 2

**Jurisdiction & Venue**

The plaintiff is a domiciliary of Maine. The defendant is a domiciliary of New Hampshire. The amount in controversy, without interest and costs, exceeds the amount specified by 28 U.S.C. § 1332. Venue is proper under 28 U.S.C. § 1391(b)(2) as all of the events giving rise to this claim took place there.

**Allegations**

On March 15, 2018, defendant Ginny Weasley negligently hit plaintiff Aberforth Dumbledore with her car. At 10:30 pm, plaintiff was walking his dog, wearing a bright orange reflector jacket. He heard screeching tires and was then struck by car. Harry Potter and Ginny Weasley got out of the car, with Weasley exiting the driver's side. Plaintiff smelled alcohol on Potter's breath.

In April 2018, plaintiff visited his doctor, who diagnosed him with life-threatening hip vasculature degeneration. The doctor stated this condition was caused by physical trauma, of the type experienced in a car crash. Plaintiff underwent surgery, and the sum of his medical costs is $150,000.

Defendant failed to act consistent with ordinary care when she drove drunk. Defendant caused the accident when she drove into Plaintiff. Harm has resulted from the accident in the form of vascular degeneration.

## Question 3

The appeal should be dismissed. First, the district court has not yet entered a final order in the case under § 1291 — though it has reached a finding on liability, because Ginny's appeal was immediate, it has not yet disposed of Aberforth's prayer for relief (*Liberty Mutual*). Because there is only one issue, Rule 54(b) does not apply. Because this is a damages action, § 1292(a) does not apply. It is extremely unlikely that the trial judge would certify the issue for appeal under § 1292(b) since there is no disagreement over a controlling question of law necessary to the disposition of a case. If the trial judge did certify it, there's no way the court of appeals would hear it. There is no collateral order, so *Cohen* doesn't authorize an immediate appeal. And certainly the appellate court would not have to think twice about denying a petition for a writ of mandamus: here, there's no reason for hurry, it's not clear the trial court erred, and neither the balance of the equities nor the public interest demand the writ.
