# Assignment 2: Model Answer

NC has SMJ under § 1332 because complete diversity exists between Slughorn, citizen of NC (central nerve) and DE (incorporation) and Honeydukes, citizen of SC (central nerve and incorporation). The amount in controversy is above $90,000. Venue is also proper under § 1391(b)(2) or otherwise § 1391(b)(3), if there actually is IPJ.

## Argument 1: No IPJ

First, Honeydukes can argue that there is no IPJ, which would require the lawsuit to be dismissed, and the Felix Felicis (FF) returned. NC District Court can use 4(k)(1)(A) to use NC's long arm statute, which goes to the limits of the constitution (general or specific jurisdiction). There is no general jurisdiction because Honeydukes is not domiciled in NC and no tag (*McArthur*).

Honeydukes can argue no specific jurisdiction because it does not have minimum contacts with NC (*International Shoe*). Honeydukes can argue that it did not know Slughorn was in NC, since the website had no business address and wiring money to a NC bank account is not enough to know that the money is actually going to a company in NC. Therefore, it did not purposefully avail itself of NC laws (*McGee*). Honeydukes can also argue that NC's long arm statute does not specifically say that there is specific jurisdiction for out-of-state buyers who miss installment payments (*Hanson*). Slughorn can argue that it only advertised via its website, and its website was full of pictures and statements attesting to its NC location. Slughorn also required that customers pay via electronic transfer to a bank in NC.

## Argument 2: Erie Challenge

Second, Honeydukes can raise an Erie challenge. The most relevant federal rule is FRCP 65 (preliminary injunction). FRCP 65 requires a movant to show likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, a balance of the equities, and that an injunction is in the public interest. Under FRCP 65, Slughorn would be unlikely to get a preliminary injunction because it will not suffer irreparable harm (can sue for damages later).

Under a *York* analysis, Honeydukes would want to argue that the outcome of either test would be the same. However, given the different standards between the two tests, that is unlikely to be successful. Therefore, using federal law would change the outcome of the lawsuit, so need to use the state law.

Under the *Hanna* majority analysis, Honeydukes should argue that there is a valid (rule doesn't abridge a substantive right, REA) and applicable FRCP (rule determines whether property can be seized before a full trial). Slughorn could argue that FRCP 65 is inapplicable because preliminary injunctions are used to prevent people from doing certain things, not a tool of property repossession. *Walker* supports a narrow interpretation of FRCPs. If Rule 65 is NOT applicable, we'd probably use state law. There may be some federal common law right-of-replevin procedure that Slughorn might use, but (i) we didn't learn about it, and (ii) using the "twin aims of Erie" test (which we should use when we're talking about federal common law) almost always results in state law.

Under the Harlan concurrence analysis, Honeydukes would want to argue that using the federal rule would not change the pre-litigation conduct of the parties (parties would not take method of litigation into account when forming contract; federal rules wouldn't require different insurance policies), so the federal rule should apply. Slughorn could argue that he would have drafted a different contract, such as requiring more payment upfront, had he known he would have had to meet the high standard of preliminary injunction. If this is true, then the FRCP affects the preliminary conduct of the parties, and state law should apply.

## Argument 3: Procedural Due Process Violation

The third issue that Honeydukes can raise is that the procedure under NCRCP 65 violates procedural due process (PDP) because there is insufficient opportunity for Honeydukes to be heard before its property was seized (*Vitek*). PDP applies even in temporary deprivations (*Sniadach*). Honeydukes should argue that there is no reason for urgency that overrides their right to a hearing pre-deprivation (*Fuentes*). Slughorn could argue that it retains a property interest in the FF, and therefore has an interest in preventing waste to the property, especially if it can show that there is a limited quantity of FF (*Mitchell*). Honeydukes should also argue that Slughorn was not required to post a bond (*Fuentes*; *Doehr*), it had no opportunity to get its property back, such as posting a counterbond (*Fuentes*), and that there was no immediate post-deprivation hearing. Slughorn can argue that it was required to make a substantial showing of facts in his complaint before the property was seized (not just a conclusory allegation that he was entitled to the goods, *Fuentes*), he was required to file additional written evidence including affidavits by witnesses with knowledge of the events, that the decision to seize the property was made by a judge (*Mitchell*), and that there is no obligation on Honeydukes to file anything in court to get their property back since post-seizure, there will be a trial to determine final possession.

## Argument 4: Jurisdiction Over Property

The final argument that Honeydukes could make is that since NCRCP 65 authorizes repossession of property, it is a writ of replevin. A writ of replevin is analogous to a quasi in rem procedure, where courts can generally only exercise jurisdiction over property that is within their jurisdiction (*Shaffer*). Honeydukes can argue that NC district court does not have jurisdiction, since the property was in SC when the NC court authorized the sheriff to seize it.
