**You likely cannot move to dismiss under 12(b)(1)-SMJ:** Bob likely doesn't have AU-SMJ but likely has D-SMJ. He'll argue he satisfies *Smith/Grable*, but will fail. His state claim contains a federal issue (must show DDDA violation to prove negligence per se), but each *Grable* factor goes against him: the federal issue isn't significant (states regularly adjudicate BAC-levels); granting AU-SMJ to negligence per se claims implicating federal laws will flood federal courts; and the DDDA violation concerns a factual issue (BAC-level).

Bob likely has D-SMJ because he's likely domiciled in MA, you're domiciled in RI, and the $200K AIC exceeds $75K. He'll argue his voter registration, driver's license, MA property ownership, and MA wife all show intent to stay in MA, meaning his domicile changed from RI to MA. You'll argue going to school isn't enough to change domicile (*Mas*) and that he inherited (didn't buy) MA property and intends to move to FL soon, showing he doesn't intend to stay in MA. Bob's case likely prevails granting him diversity given voter registrations, drivers licenses, and property ownership are compelling indications of domicile.

**You cannot move to dismiss under 12(b)(2)-PJ:** You're subject to Specific-PJ in MA. D-MA will follow MA's long-arm statute going to the limits of the Constitution (4(k)(1)(a)). You have minimal contacts with the forum and litigation because you flew your drone in MA and it hit/injured Bob in MA. It's fair/reasonable for MA to exercise jurisdiction because you have sufficient MA contacts (see above); MA is convenient for you (you've shown you can go to MA for the day) and Bob (MA resident); MA is interested in protecting Massachussettans from drunk drone operators; MA has evidence and witnesses; and as place of injury, MA law will likely apply to many substantive issues.

**You cannot move to dismiss under 12(b)(3)-Venue:** § 1391(b)(2) grants venue because substantial events leading to Bob's claim occurred in MA (see above).

**Your ability to dismiss under 12(b)(6) depends on *Erie*:** MA's notice pleading standard differs from FRCPs 12(b)(6) and 8(a)(2) as interpreted in *Iqbal* creating an *Erie* issue. **Under MA's standard, Bob's complaint survives 12(b)(6)** because it includes sufficient information to put you on notice about what you're charged with. It describes the day you allegedly operated a drone that struck Bob and the DDDA section you allegedly violated. **Under FRCPs as interpreted in *Iqbal*, Bob's complaint doesn't survive** because it doesn't plausibly allege an MA negligence cause of action. The court will ignore the second half of ¶3 as conclusory about

injury causation and ¶¶4–5 as mere recitations of MA's negligence cause of action's elements. The remaining information doesn't nudge the complaint from possibly to plausibly giving rise to negligence. ¶¶1–2, 6, and the first half of 3 don't describe facts suggesting you plausibly failed to act with care or that your BAC-level was high enough to plausibly establish a DDDA violation and negligence per se.

If MA law governs, your motion fails; if the FRCPs govern, your motion succeeds. Two *Erie* tests suggest state law governs and two suggest the FRCPs govern.

**Under *York*, MA law governs.** The choice between MA's pleading standard and the federal *Iqbal* standard determines the outcome of the motion (see above) thus state (MA) law governs.

**Under *Hanna*-Scalia, the FRCPs govern.** FRCPs 12(b)(6) and 8(a)(2), as interpreted in *Iqbal*, are valid under § 2072 because they "really regulate procedure" (*Sibbach*) since they concern complaint-writing. Scalia holds they're applicable because they purport to govern what a complaint must factually allege to state a claim upon which relief can be granted, which is what this conflict concerns. Because the FRCPs are valid and applicable, they govern.

**Under *Hanna*-Ginsburg, the FRCPs likely govern.** FRCP 12(b)(6) and 8(a)(2), as interpreted in *Iqbal*, are valid for same reasons as *Hanna*-Scalia. Though *Hanna*-Ginsburg tries to interpret FRCPs to render them inapplicable and respect states' policies/interests, doing so here is unlikely possible because the FRCPs are more restrictive than MA's standard. Without a plausible interpretation that deconflicts the FRCPs, they're likely applicable and thus govern.

**Under Harlan, MA law likely governs.** *Iqbal*'s reasoning partially depended on idea that pleading standards impacts primary conduct by burdening parties with discovery, distracting them from jobs and other day-to-day activities. If an issue impacts primary conduct, it's substantive and thus state law governs, so here MA law governs. You'll argue pleading standards don't impact primary conduct, are procedural, and thus the FRCPs govern since pleading concerns complaint writing.
