## Applicable Law and Facts

New Hampshire and Maine each have only one federal district, D.N.H. (for NH) or D. Me. (for ME). Both states have long-arm statutes that go to the limits of the Constitution. Both have state rules of civil procedure identical in text to the federal rules of civil procedure. In the 2015 case of *Malfoy v. Patil*, the Supreme Court of Maine held that Maine, under the Maine rules of civil procedure, followed the federal plausibility pleading doctrine articulated in *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal* in interpreting the Maine Rs. C. P. 8, 12. In the 2016 case of *Longbottom v. Parkinson*, the Supreme Court of New Hampshire held that New Hampshire did not follow the federal doctrine articulated in *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal* in interpreting New Hampshire Rs. C. P. 8, 12, and instead continued to follow the notice pleading doctrine as exemplified by *Dioguardi v. Durning*.

You are a solo practitioner in Portland Maine. In September of 2018, Aberforth Dumbledore, a domiciliary of Maine, walks into your office. He tells you that on March 15, 2018, while wearing a bright yellow orange reflector shirt as he walked his dog, he was crossing the street in front of 12 Grimmauld Place, Portland at 10:30 at night. He heard a screech of tires and was then hit by a car hard enough to fall down. Two individuals got out of the car, later identified as Ginny Weasley and Harry Potter, with Ms. Weasley exiting from the driver's side of the car. Ms. Weasley stayed near the car, while Mr. Potter approached Aberforth. Aberforth detected a mild smell of alcohol on Potter's breath. At the time, Aberforth reported that he was not hurt, but Potter gave Aberforth contact information for both Potter and Weasley in case things turned out badly. The contact information demonstrated that Potter and Weasley were residents of New Hampshire. In the following weeks, Aberforth felt no discomfort. In late April of 2018, however, Aberforth visited his personal physician. The physician noticed an unusual swelling on Aberforth's left hip. Subsequent examination revealed that Aberforth had a life-threatening degeneration of his hip vasculature. The physician said that the degeneration was most likely caused by trauma to the area, such as might occur in a car accident. Surgery was immediately performed, and Aberforth racked up $150,000 in medical, physical therapy, and medication bills.

You do some investigation. You find from official records that Potter and Weasley were both born and raised in New Hampshire and that they attended a local high school. After graduation, they lived at the same address in Manchester, New Hampshire and continue to work there.

You decide to file a lawsuit against Ginny Weasley for negligently hitting Aberforth with a car. The elements of the cause of action for negligence are: that the defendant (i) acted in a way inconsistent with ordinary care under the circumstances, (ii) that the failure to act with ordinary care caused some accident or event, and (iii) that the plaintiff suffered injuries as a result.

### Question 3

Continue to assume that Aberforth sues Ginny in the United States District Court for the District of Maine. He demands damages in the amount of $150,000, and no other form of relief. After discovery, Aberforth moves for summary judgment on the issues of whether Ginny was drunk and when she drove into Aberforth, and that her driving negligently caused her to hit Aberforth with her car. The district court grants the motion, reserving the issue of the amount of damages to be awarded for a later trial. Ginny immediately appeals the summary judgment grant the United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit. Aberforth moves to dismiss the appeal.

Should the First Circuit grant Aberforth's motion to dismiss Ginny's appeal? Why or why not?
