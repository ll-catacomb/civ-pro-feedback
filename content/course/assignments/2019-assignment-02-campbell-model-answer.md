## Procedural Due Process

In both suits, there was probably inadequate notice. WeBuyDebts had to make reasonable efforts to notify Hermione under *Mullane*. In the IL suit, mailing to the PO box could be inadequate. WeBuyDebts says it has good investigative abilities, so maybe they should have figured out the PO box was closed. If they were told by the post office that Hermione no longer received mail there, they would likely have to take further action (*Flowers*). They also had her address from the broom contract, so mailing only to the PO box seems unreasonable. In the NC suit, they did not even allege making any effort to provide notice, and they had her address, so they most likely don't meet the *Mullane* standard.

The IL suit probably required a pre-deprivation hearing. *Eldridge* factors likely favor Hermione. WeBuyDebts' interest was just based on their $700 debt, which seems small for a large debt-buying company. No pre-existing interest in the account. They could likely afford to wait for Hermione to pay. No evidence that Hermione would attempt to evade collection. Hermione has pressing interest in her bank account. She's poor and needs her money now. Failing to have a hearing pre-deprivation increases risk of wrongly preventing Hermione from accessing her whole account (which has $200 more than WeBuyDebts is even asking for). *Cf. Doehr*. Hearing would allow judge to conclude that seizure was unnecessary, or to issue a narrowly tailored seizure order (e.g., set aside $700 in the account).

Maybe the NC suit required a pre-deprivation hearing, too, but the argument isn't as strong. *Eldridge* factors don't clearly favor Hermione. WeBuyDebts retained interest in broom under the contract, and Fenrir was about to go and sell it in a way that would impair WeBuyDebts' ability to reclaim it. Hermione and Fenrir likewise have interest in broom, but not in a way that outweighs WeBuyDebts'. Relatively minimal risk of erroneous deprivation without hearing beforehand because the contract and the non-payment is really all the evidence necessary to establish that WeBuyDebts is entitled to the broom.

## Personal Jurisdiction

NC probably didn't have jurisdiction over the broom. The item needs to be in the territory of the state, and the broom wasn't in NC when the order was issued. But it was in NC when the order was executed, and perhaps that's enough. But under *Shaffer*, Hermione and Fenrir must also have an interest in having the broom in NC. Hermione can say they didn't have such an interest. Fenrir was only taking the broom through NC to get to VA to sell it. The broom's presence in NC was incidental. But WeBuyDebts will say that Fenrir nevertheless intentionally brought the broom into NC, so Hermione and Fenrir did have an interest in having it there. On balance, it seems unfair for NC to exercise jurisdiction over the broom when it was only going to be there for a couple hours in Fenrir's car en route to VA.

IL may not have had jurisdiction over Hermione's account. Account was in IL, but did Hermione have interest in having it there? She will say no. It's just where she happened to bank. But she did physically go to IL to bank at Hogwarts, so it's clearer that she's intentionally maintaining an IL bank account. Not just online banking with a bank that happens to be in IL. Since the bank is close enough for her to go regularly, it seems fair for her to be haled into IL court over the account. Contacts are definitely stronger than in *Shaffer*.

## Statute

Compare statutory requirements against those in *Vitek*. *Vitek* was about deprivation of liberty, not property, though, so the statute can perhaps be more flexible. But the seizures here are pre-judgment, whereas *Vitek* is for the whole process, so statute should perhaps be stricter.

What the statute probably needs, in light of the caveats above: reasonable attempt at notice, as well as a hearing either before or shortly after the seizure. At that hearing, parties can present and cross-examine witnesses. There is a neutral decision maker. The decision maker sets forth the basis of its decision in a written statement, or at least has to make a finding that the evidentiary standard is met (judge cannot just rubber stamp requests for seizure). And the party receives notice of these rights. If the statute doesn't provide for any of these things, Hermione can argue it doesn't meet the constitutional minimum for due process.

<!-- page: 3 -->

I would also look for the evidentiary standard for a pre-judgment seizure (lower standard = less likely to be constitutional). A bond requirement would make the statute stronger, and a counter-bond option (or other way for Hermione to retain access to her property) even more so. A right to interlocutory appeal could also strengthen the scheme. The statute should also give adequate time to get an attorney and prepare a defense (*Roller*). Absence of those things strengthens the argument against constitutionality.

Also, the statute most likely would violate the due process clause to the extent it purported to confer jurisdiction over property where the *Shaffer* requirements aren't met.
