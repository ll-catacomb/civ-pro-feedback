# Assignment 3 Model Answer

## Question 1

I would reject this proposal. While judges may be better at providing uniformity (stare decisis), construing complex written documents (like patents), or overseeing long-running disputes, the courts believe that juries may be better at resolving disputes of fact, providing community views (they can act as a cross-section of the community), and judging credibility. Additionally, plaintiffs may feel that justice has been denied to them if only a single judge decides their claim (rather than multiple "peers"). Discontinuing all civil jury trials would eliminate these potential benefits. Moreover, the current allocation of power between judges/juries supports the idea that we — as a community — see value in juries. Baked into the "law/fact" test is the idea that juries are better than judges at the tasks listed above. Additionally, the "law/equity" test weighs in favor of a jury when the plaintiff seeks remedies at law (e.g. damages), covering a significant number of cases. While juries may be far from perfect (ex: they may introduce discriminatory bias), eliminating jury trials would (1) threaten people's faith in the legal system (given our long-standing belief in juries) and (2) may lead to less just outcomes in some cases.

## Question 2(a)

For Q2 answers, L1 = original lawsuit against Splinter and L2 = proposed lawsuit.

**SMJ:** There is complete diversity (Splinter domiciled in NY, Rafael in NJ) and $100k AIC > $75k, so § 1332 provides diversity SMJ.

**IPJ:** NJDC has GIPJ over Rafael (since domiciled in NJ, 4(k)(1)(A)).

**Venue:** § 1391(b)(1) says NJDC can lay venue in a suit against Rafael (Rafael only defendant).

**Preclusion:** Applying Rule 13(a) shows that Splinter's L2 claim was a compulsory counterclaim in L1. First, the claim in L2 arises from the same T/O as the claim in L1. Both lawsuits focus on the beating of Splinter by Rafael/April in the sewer network (same actions, same time). While the "justification" claim in L1 would involve facts beyond the beating itself (e.g. the class), both lawsuits would involve evidence of the beating. Second, no additional parties would have been brought in if Splinter raised the counterclaim in L1. Third, the judgment was final and on the merits (judgment entered after a jury trial). No special exceptions apply (e.g. claim wasn't subject of separate pending action). Because Splinter's claim was not raised then, it may not be brought now.

## Question 2(b)

**SMJ:** There is complete diversity (Splinter domiciled in NY, April in NJ) and $100k AIC > $75k. § 1332.

**IPJ:** SDNY has GIPJ over Splinter (since domiciled in NY).

**Venue:** § 1391(b)(1) says SDNY may lay venue (Splinter only defendant). § 1391(b)(2) says SDNY can lay venue (beating occurred in Manhattan).

**Preclusion:** Splinter can successfully raise claim preclusion as a defense if (1) April and Rafael are in privity, (2) the claim in L1 is the "same" as the claim in L2 (T/O test), and (3) judgment in L1 was final/on the merits.

*Privity:* April likely had substantial control over Rafael's conduct in L1 given that she is the one who hired the law firm and Rafael let April "handle everything." Thus, she was in privity with Rafael.

*T/O:* Rafael and April both beat up Splinter on the same day using the same plan.

*Final/"On the Merits":* Judgment was final; and judgment was on the merits (jury trial).

Therefore, April is likely claim precluded.

## Question 2(c)

**SMJ/IPJ:** See 2A (Bebop domiciled in NY, like Splinter; same AIC).

**Venue:** § 1391(b)(1) says NJDC can lay venue (Rafael only defendant).

**Preclusion:** Claim preclusion will not apply. Bebop was not a party in L1 (and was not in privity with any party in L1), and it would be a denial of due process to apply preclusion to him. The T/O "same claim" analysis is closer. Both Bebop and Splinter were beat up by the same students, and the students were triggered to beat them up because of a single class taught by Bebop and Splinter. Thus, the "justification" legal issue and evidence is likely to have significant overlap in L1/L2. However, the assaults occurred in different states and months apart; evidence to prove assaults will likely be disparate (e.g. different CCTV, different witnesses).

## Question 3

I would reject this proposal. Potential advantages would be increased efficiency (fewer "redundant" cases for an already burdened court system) and uniformity (claims evaluated/determined at the same time). However, disadvantages would be severe. A party that never received notice of L1 but *could* have intervened (and thus would be precluded under the new rule), would have had no notice or opportunity to be heard (due process violation). To avoid this, people would have to constantly monitor the filing of new lawsuits to see if anyone was bringing a lawsuit where they could intervene. This goes far beyond the current claim preclusion, which only bars "same party" claims (brought by you or someone you were in privity with), not claims of unrelated 3rd parties. This could even lead to invidious "gaming" where defendant gets plaintiff P1 to *quietly* bring smaller claim to block future litigation from P2 (who might have a bigger claim or better resources).
