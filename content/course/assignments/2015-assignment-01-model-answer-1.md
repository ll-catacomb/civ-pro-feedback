LupinBank will try to get a declaratory judgment that it was not negligent. LupinBank can pursue its claims in state and federal courts in New York and New Jersey.

A federal court will have SMJ over a declaratory judgment lawsuit only if that court would have SMJ over the as-yet-unfiled suit they seek to preempt (Greiner Imaginary Lawsuit Rule).

**SMJ:** LupinBank wants to be in federal court but likely lacks Federal Question Jurisdiction (as CFPB's regulation is not meant to create a federal cause of action). LupinBank could argue that there's a federal issue embedded in a state law cause of action because the regulation relates to federal interests (*Smith*/*Grable*), but that would be a tough sell given the volume of negligence claims and the potential consequences of letting them into federal court. Without FQJ, LupinBank must turn to diversity jurisdiction to get a declaratory judgment in federal court on Penelope Clearwater's presumptive state law negligence claims. Diversity jurisdiction requires complete diversity (all plaintiffs citizens of different states from all defendants) and sufficient amount-in-controversy (§ 1332).

Clearwater is domiciled in NJ (her permanent address is in NJ, she lives there, and she hasn't declared intent to stay anywhere else permanently) (*Mas*). LupinBank is a citizen of NY because NY is its principal place of business (according to the nerve center test (*Hertz*)) and because it is incorporated there. As the parties are citizens of different states, they satisfy complete diversity.

The next test is AIC: Clearwater wants $20k in compensatory damages and $60k in punitive damages. New Jersey does not permit punitive damages, whereas New York permits unlimited punitive damages. See below re which state's law applies.

**IPJ:** As Clearwater is a domiciliary of NJ, both state and federal courts in NJ will be able to exercise jurisdiction over her (Rule 4(k)(1)(A)). There would likely only be general IPJ over Penelope in NY if she were tagged there (*Burnham*). More likely, NY courts could assert specific IPJ in New York given that a substantial portion of the relevant events occurred there and because Penelope established minimum contacts with the state and the tort arose from those contacts (*Int'l Shoe*). By attending school there and doing business with a NY corporation, Penelope also availed herself of the laws of New York state (*McGee*).

**Venue:** In New Jersey, venue is laid under § 1391(b)(1) because Penelope Clearwater is the defendant (in the declaratory judgment action) and is domiciled there. In New York, venue is laid under § 1391(b)(2) because a substantial portion of the events occurred there.

Keeping all of that in mind, the options:

**NJ State Ct:** Though the suit would be viable (IPJ over Penelope Clearwater, SMJ because negligence per se is a state cause of action, venue properly laid), LupinBank prefers federal court.

**NJ Fed Ct:** NJ is a First Restatement state, so it applies the substantive law of NY, the place where the injury was felt (where Penelope signed up for the card and where her bank was). Because NY law applies, Penelope CAN seek punitive damages, meaning she meets the AIC requirement. This option is a solid one for LupinBank, though they might be concerned about NJ Fed Ct offering a slight home-state advantage to Penelope.

**NY State Ct:** Though the suit would be viable (IPJ over Penelope Clearwater because the harm was felt in New York, SMJ because negligence per se is a state cause of action, venue properly laid), LupinBank prefers federal court.

**NY Fed Ct:** NY is a Second Restatement state, so it applies the laws of whatever state has the greatest interest in the issue at hand. If the court determined New Jersey to have the greatest stake in damages, it would bar punitive damages, meaning the AIC requirement wouldn't be met. If the court applied NY damages law, AIC would be met and it could issue a judgment. Given that the center of the parties' relationship appears to be in NY, and that the injury was felt there, it is unlikely the court would apply NJ substantive law.
