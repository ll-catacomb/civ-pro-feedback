- **Section 202:** A seller who claims ownership of an incorporeal piece of property deemed to be located within this state due to a buyer's failure to make one or more installment payments pursuant to a contract with an agreed-upon purchase price may, if they can do so without violence and if the contract so provides, repossess the item at issue, provided:
    - The seller gives reasonable notice to the buyer of the installment payments missed and the total amount due, as well as the intention to repossess if the arrearage is not paid in full; and
    - Within a reasonable time after such repossession, the seller initiates an in rem lawsuit in a court of this state over the piece of property seized seeking a declaration that due to the buyer's failure to make appropriate payments, the seller is now the legal owner of the property.

If the court, pursuant to such lawsuit, finds that the seller has proved that the seller's claim of ownership was proper under applicable law, the court shall declare the seller the owner of the property, and shall issue such orders as may be necessary to require the seller to refund the amount of payments the buyer did make against the purchase price, less the amount of seller's damages (or damage to the property) and expenses in the litigation not including attorneys' fees. ## Question 2 (suggested 400-450 words against limit of 850)

In multiple places in the class thus far, we have seen the Constitution and some other source of law speak to the same concerns. In each of these instances, the Constitution provides a broad set of limits within which the government can operate, and some other source of law (such as a statute, a rule of civil procedure) narrows the range of what is permissible. In each of those instances, the parties or the court system must operate within both the broader set of Constitutional limits and the narrow range set by the other source of law. And in each case, it is not clear that the narrow range of the non-Constitutional source of law does a very good job of furthering the goals/policies of the area.

Write a short paragraph or two naming one area we've studied with this legal structure. In your mini-essay, begin by identifying the Constitutional provision and the non-Constitutional source of law, and briefly explain the contours/limits each imposes. Include a brief discussion of whether, in your view, the non-Constitutional source of law does a good job of furthering the goals or policies of the area. Suggest reforms or alternatives or changes to the non-Constitutional source of law and discuss briefly advantages and disadvantages of your proposal.

To guide your writing, an example of a strong response appears below in italics. You may **not** use the topic of this example (the arising under basis of subject matter jurisdiction) in your writing.

*Article III section 2 clause 1 and 28 USC 1331 both address whether a federal district court may exercise "arising under" jurisdiction. Both sources of law provide that a federal district court can adjudicate a lawsuit that "arises under" federal law. But while Art. III extends to cases that have any federal issue, possibly even any nascent federal issue (*Osborn*), *Mottley* interpreted 1331 as allowing AUJ only when the plaintiff asserts a federally created cause of action. The *Smith-Grable* doctrine provides an exception when a federal issue is embedded in a state cause of action, but such embedding rarely occurs, and even embedding is not enough on its own. Supposedly, the narrow interpretation of 1331 serves the value of federalism because it limits the number of cases in which the availability of AUJ provides a federal forum, meaning more cases end up in state courts. In my view, however, 1331's limit makes little sense. Historically speaking, the problem is not that the number or importance of cases for the state courts to adjudicate has decreased over time; as state statutes have become more common and more complex, state courts have more, and more important, cases to decide. The problem is that federal statutes have become more common and more complex even faster than their state counterparts. If the federal courts are to specialize in adjudicating federal issues, their SMJ should grow even more than the state courts. Any so-called "power grabs" are the result of Congress' passing too many statutes.*

*With this in mind, *Mottley*'s well-pleaded complaint rule does a poor job of furthering the goals of the arising under power. If the goal is to have some but not all cases with federal issues end up in federal*
