## Question

Imagine that the State of Durmstrang has a set of rules of civil procedure that are worded exactly the same as the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. In particular, the wording of Durm. Rs. Civ. P. 8(a)(2), 12(b)(6) are identical to those of Fed. R. Civ. P. 8(a)(2), 12(b)(6). However, in January, 2011, the Durmstrang Supreme Court issued an opinion in the case of *Finch-Fletchley v. Macmillan*. The following language was included within it:

> Although the verbiage of Durm. Rs. Civ. P. 8(a), 12(b) is identical to that of Fed. Rs. Civ. P. 8(a), 12(b), we specifically decline to follow the recent Supreme Court cases of *Bell Atlantic v. Twombly* and *Ashcroft v. Iqbal*. In our view, these two cases were unfaithful to the concept of notice pleading and impose far too high of a burden on plaintiffs with meritorious claims who need access to discovery tools to generate evidence to prove their cases. The problems with *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal* go beyond their abandonment of the plain language of the rules. Nor is the problem here limited to the idea that plaintiffs with legitimate claims might not file suit, thinking that doing so would be futile under the "plausibility" standard. Rather, the problem is that the Supreme Court of the United States seems to have forgotten that lawsuits, particularly those sounding in tort, have a purpose beyond compensating victims: to deter wrongful conduct, particularly by powerful companies in a position to hurt many citizens of Durmstrang at once. In our view, were we to follow the *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal* decisions in our interpretation of our own Durmstrang rules, the result would be an insufficient level of deterrence of harmful conduct among companies doing business in our state. We instead adhere to the notice pleading standard, as exemplified by the case of *Dioguardi v. Durning* and Form 11 of the Durmstrang Rules of Civil Procedure (which is identical to Form 11 of the Federal Rules).

### Part 1 (Suggested words: 500–550)

Should the United States District Court for the District of Durmstrang, litigating a case in which the only basis of jurisdiction is diversity and in which the relevant horizontal choice of law rules result in the application of Durmstrang substantive law, be required to apply Durmstrang law (as articulated in *Finch-Fletchley v. Macmillan*) when adjudicating a motion to dismiss under Rule 12(b)(6)?

Your answer to this question can be a single essay, or one broken down into subparts, whatever you prefer. Your answer should, however, address all of the following sub-issues:

(a) What would the answer to this question be under the three "Erie tests" we have learned in the class thus far?

(b) What role, if any, does the Rules of Decision Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1652, play in the analysis?

(c) What role, if any, does, should limits on when defendant/defendants are allowed to remove play in the analysis?

**Note:** Except for subpart (a), this is a "should" question. I'm asking what you think the answer should be. To write a good response, you need to consider the principles we've learned in class, as well as the competing policies behind the various Erie and motion-to-dismiss cases we studied. But in this question (except for subpart (a)), I'm not asking you to predict what a court would do when faced with this question.
