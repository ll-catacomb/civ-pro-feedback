## Substantive Assignment

### Applicable Law

Under Washington and Oregon state law, a plaintiff may sue for a tort called negligent interference with contractual relations. To prevail in this cause of action, a plaintiff must prove that the defendant:

(i) committed an act or omission that is a departure from ordinary care (i.e., negligent),
(ii) that the plaintiff knew, or should have known, would cause the plaintiff to suffer damages in the form of an inability to receive the benefit of a valid contract not involving the defendant, and
(iii) that the plaintiff did in fact suffer such damages.

Under California, Delaware, and Idaho state law, there is no tort for negligent interference with contractual relations. There is, however, a cause of action called intentional interference with contractual relations. To prevail in this cause of action, a plaintiff must prove that the defendant:

(i) committed an act or omission *with the intention* of causing the plaintiff to suffer damages in the form of an inability to receive the benefit of a contract not involving the defendant, and
(ii) that the plaintiff did in fact suffer such damages.

For both the negligent and the intentional version of the interference with contractual relations tort, the last event necessary to complete the tort is the breach of the contract with which the defendant interfered.

All states relevant to this assignment have a standard cause of action for breach of contract. To prevail on a common law breach of contract claim the plaintiff must show: (1) the plaintiff and defendant made a valid enforceable contract, (2) the plaintiff performed his/her part of the contract or offered a justification for nonperformance, (3) the defendant failed to perform his/her part of the contract, and (4) damage resulted to the plaintiff. The plaintiff must prevail on all elements to win.

Oregon and Washington follow the First Restatement for horizontal choice of law. Delaware, California, and Idaho follow the Second Restatement.

Oregon, Idaho, and Delaware have only a single federal district. Washington has two federal districts, the Eastern and Western. The city of Potomac is located in the Eastern District of Washington. California has four federal districts. The city of Sacramento is in the Eastern District of California.

Oregon, Idaho, Delaware, Washington, and California all have long-arm statutes that go to the limits of the Constitution.

### Facts

Florean Fortescue was a farmer. He leased federal land in Oregon, where he was born and lived his entire life, to grow crops. His primary farmland was located in an isolated area of northwestern Oregon. Due to recent fires, washouts, and other natural disasters, Florean's farmland was accessible only by a logging road that, in the sleepy town of Hogsmeade, Oregon, passed over the Hogsmeade Bridge. The town of Hogsmeade raised revenue by charging any vehicle larger than a passenger car a toll to cross the bridge.

<!-- page: 3 -->

In early 2017, the Commissioners of the town of Hogsmeade decided that the Hogsmeade Bridge, which was also used by the town's few residents, was in need of repairs, although the repairs were not urgent. They contracted with Cho Chang Contracting Corp., incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Potomac, Washington, to do the repairs. Chang Corp. does business exclusively in the states of Washington and Oregon. Chang Corp. advised the Commissioners that the repairs would require that the Bridge be shut down for approximately two weeks. The contract for the repairs stated that Chang Corp. would take care to schedule the shutdown "keeping in mind the needs of farmers in northwestern Oregon to access the bridge for the purpose of delivering their harvests to markets in the central and eastern portions of the state, and to give due notice to such farmers of the dates of any shutdown." Otherwise, the contract allowed Chang Corp. to choose the time of the shutdown, so long as all repair work was complete by January of 2021.

In January of 2018, Florean signed a contract with Bathilda Bagshot Industries, Inc., incorporated in Delaware. The contract was negotiated via email exchange, after in-person negotiations occurring at Florean's farm and Bathilda's headquarters, with Florean writing from FloreanFortescueFarmer(at)gmail.com and Bathilda writing from BathildaBagshotBuyer(at)gmail.com. It was signed by both parties using an online signature verification service. In the contract, Bagshot agreed to buy 300,000 bushels of Florean's corn at $2.00 per bushel. The contract specified that Florean was to deliver the corn to a facility in Sacramento, California, adjacent to Bagshot's headquarters. Delivery was to be made at any time during the month of September, 2018. According to the contract, Bagshot had seven days to inspect the corn. If within those seven days, Bagshot believed that the corn's condition was not in accordance with industry standards, it could refuse delivery, in which case either party could sue the other for breach.

In February of 2018, Bathilda sold the rights to 150,000 bushels of Florean's corn to Ted Tonks Industries, Inc., incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Idaho. The sales contract was negotiated over the telephone, with calls made between Bathilda's offices in Sacramento and Tonks' offices in Boise, Idaho. The contract was signed on the Internet via an online signature verification service.

On Friday, August 31, 2018, a fleet of approximately 300 semis gathered at a distribution facility in one of Florean's farms, loaded up 1,000 bushels of corn each, and set off for Sacramento. They drove through part of the night, but when they arrived at the Hogsmeade Bridge, they found that Chang Corp. had closed it for repairs. The fleet of semis was unable to get through. Upon hearing of these events, Florean immediately drove to Potomac to meet with Chang Corp.'s Chief Executive Officer, Ms. Marietta Edgecombe. Edgecombe told Florean that she, from her office in Potomac, had scheduled the Hogsmeade job herself. She agreed that she could have scheduled the Hogsmeade Bridge repairs at any time, from August of 2018 to February of 2019 (note that there is no farm harvest in Oregon in January or February), but stated that she thought it best to "get those repairs out of the way." Florean demanded that Chang Corp. reopen the bridge immediately to allow his corn harvest through, but Edgecombe said, "No, now that we've started, trying to stop will cause the bridge to be closed even longer."

The Hogsmeade Bridge remained closed for about two weeks, during which time Oregon experienced unseasonably hot weather. The fleet of semis finally arrived in Sacramento on September 17, 2018. Bathilda and Tonks immediately sent in their inspectors, and one of Florean's farm hands who had accompanied the fleet, a Mr. Dobby Kreacher, also examined the corn's condition.

Initially, the Bathilda and Tonks inspectors believed that the corn was up to industry standard. On September 18, Bathilda and Tonks each hand-delivered a form letter to Dobby in Sacramento telling him that they accepted the corn. Two days later, however, they each hand-delivered a second letter to Dobby, who was still in Sacramento, stating that further inspection of the corn had revealed it to have been damaged from exposure to the heat, and that they would pay only $1.25 per bushel for the corn. Subsequently, each wired $187,500.00 into Florean's bank account.

**Note:** if the actions of Bathilda and Tonks constituted a breach of contract, the last event necessary to complete the breach was the hand delivery of the second letter to Dobby in Sacramento.

### The Client's Situation

You were a lawyer Florean has consulted in the past. Florean called you in a rage. He told you that Bathilda's and Tonks' second letter constituted a breach of contract. The corn was fine, Florean tells you. Each of Bathilda and Tonks, separately, owes him 150,000 × ($2.00 − $1.25) = $112,500.00. He wants to sue them in federal court.

Florean was also in a rage about Chang Corp.'s closure of the Hogsmeade Bridge. He said that if the corn was defective, then Chang Corp. should have to pay him $112,500 × 2 = $225,000.00 for the money he lost. (Assume, which is true, that applicable law will allow him to sue "in the alternative," meaning that Florean can try to recover $112,500.00 from each of Bathilda and Tonks on the theory that the corn was fine, and in the same lawsuit try to recover "in the alternative" $225,000.00 from Chang if it turned out that the court ruled that the corn was defective.)
