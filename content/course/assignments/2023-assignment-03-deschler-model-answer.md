# Assignment 3 Model Answer

## Question 1

I would not support a proposal to discontinue the use of the jury in civil cases. Such an amendment would offer little gain to the civil legal system, but would come with a high cost. Currently, the federal court system trusts juries to make certain findings of fact, and almost no findings of law. The judicial system trusts juries, and not judges, to judge the credibility of witnesses, both expert and lay, but only in non-equitable cases. The role of the jury is thus limited to only factual questions in legal cases (as opposed to legal issues, or equitable cases). The civil legal system already has safeguards in place to protect against incompetent or rogue juries. The system allows judges the power to preemptively remove the power of a jury via summary judgment or JAMOL, when a judge concludes that no reasonable jury could side with one of the parties. Judges can also overrule a jury after the fact, via Rule 59 and the motion for a new trial. Additionally, various doctrines, such as the public interest factors in a forum non conveniens analysis, allow judges to proactively make a jury's job easier. As such, I believe that little would be added to the system by removing juries, as the system already controls for potential problems. Much would be lost by such a move — trust in the civil legal system would likely drop as the right to have a case heard by a jury of one's peers disappeared in the civil sense. Decisions over whom to trust and what to believe would be made by individual judges, unelected, and more likely to be biased than a larger panel of one's peers. The loss of trust and introduction of potential bias are not worth any small gains made by removing juries from the civil system.

## Question 2

There are no SMJ issues (Splinter is NY, Rafael NJ, AIC met, so use § 1332 for all cases), no PJ issues (everyone being sued in home forum, so general IPJ), and no venue issues (via § 1391(b)(1)) with any of the lawsuits. Questions arise in each case regarding counterclaims or claim preclusion. For all questions, judgment in first lawsuit (Rafael suing Splinter in NY) is final and on the merits via jury verdict.

**(a)** Rafael can raise forfeiture of a compulsory counterclaim, because Splinter had to bring a compulsory counterclaim under Rule 13(a) in the original suit. Splinter's suing Rafael for damages would come from the same transaction or occurrence that Rafael's DJ in the Manhattan case came from. Counterclaim might require adding April, but court should have jurisdiction via diversity, as she is NJ resident, so no problems with FRCP 13(a)(1)(B). If Splinter did not raise the counterclaim in the Manhattan suit, it expires, since it was compulsory. If he did raise it, then he would be claim precluded from suing again in NJ because he is the same party, the judgment is final and on the merits (after the jury trial), and per above it came from the same transaction and thus is the same claim.

**(b)** Claim preclusion issue. Same transaction or occurrence is easy: beating of Splinter by April and Rafael at issue in both cases. Rafael's DJ went to judgment, so no issue there.

Only question is whether they are the same party. April is literally different from Splinter, but likely in privity via substantial control. Clear from facts that April controlled Rafael: Shredder "worked exclusively with April," April removed herself from suit after Rafael signed retainer, Rafael said "I'll leave everything to you." **(c)** Bebop was not a party in Rafael's initial lawsuit, so should not be claim precluded, as that would be a due process violation. Additionally, it is debatable whether beating of Bebop is same T/O as beating of Splinter (beatings were different, but cause of beatings the same, could be close). Claim preclusion is unavailable to Rafael.

## Question 3

The advantages of such a proposal would be to lower the number of cases on the federal courts' dockets and combine litigation on major issues (which is the goal of many complex litigation doctrines). A major negative would be claim precluding people who had no notice of a lawsuit, thus removing their right to relief without due process. This would create issues for notice/opportunity to be heard, as well as incentivize people to know about as many lawsuits happening as possible, to find out if they should/need to intervene. Large companies will be more able to monitor the dockets of all federal courts than individual people, leading to an imbalance of power as corporations will (1) intervene in earlier suits and (2) will be aware that individuals should have and raise the issue that they have not. Complex litigation is already balanced towards repeat players with large amounts of resources, and this change would further that trend. Additionally, this proposal would result in many, many more interventions, which would overcomplicate relatively simple cases, and likely remove power over the lawsuit from the parties who are actually litigating that suit. On the whole I would be against the proposal.
