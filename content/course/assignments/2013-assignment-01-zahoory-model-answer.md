**Question 1:**

NH's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the Due Process Clause, so the only IPJ issue is the constitutional one. St. Mungo's can make a limited appearance in NH state-court to argue that any attempt by NH to exercise IPJ over Mungo's would violate its Due Process rights.

Mungo's can argue that being hauled into a NH state court would violate "traditional notions of fair play and justice" because Mungo's lacked minimum contacts with NH. (*International Shoe*). Other than advertising in NH, Mungo's has done nothing to demonstrate that it has purposefully availed itself of NH law. All of Mungo's medical business occurs at its medical center in Massachusetts (MA). While Pince may argue that Mungo's specifically directed its advertisements to the "Granite State," this sole reference to NH in an advertisement, one that serves no functional purpose other than to catch eyes, seems insufficient.

Ultimately, even though it is a close call, it does not seem that NH's interests are significant enough to support exercising IPJ over Mungo's. Pince can argue that it wouldn't be difficult for Mungo's to litigate in NH, while it would be difficult for her to come to MA because of her bone cancer. It may also be true that NH has an interest in protecting its residents from negligent medical practices. However, Mungo's can argue that MA state law applies to this case and Mungo's never purposefully availed itself of NH law. Also, considering that the alleged tortious conduct occurred in MA and all the witnesses and evidence are in MA, Mungo's can make a strong argument that it would be more efficient to hear this case in MA.

**Question 2:**

Considering that Pince repeatedly made trips to MA to obtain medical treatment at Mungo's, a federal court in MA applying 4(k)(1)(a) to piggy back on MA's long-arm statute would have specific personal jurisdiction over Pince.

Additionally, since the district where Mungo's filed its suit is also where the events underlying this cause-of-action took place, venue is also proper.

However, Pince could argue that the MA federal court in which this case was filed lacks SMJ. According to the coercive action rule, a MA federal court can only have SMJ over Mungo's 28 U.S.C. 2201 action if the "imaginary" plaintiff (Pince) could have filed the case in that federal court herself. Accordingly, Pince must argue that this court would have lacked both federal question jurisdiction (FQJ) and diversity jurisdiction over this case had Pince filed it in MA federal court.

While Pince's claim has a cursory relation to federal law because of the NMRA, her cause-of-action arises out of state tort law. Thus, Mungo's would have to argue that Pince's state-law claim implicates important federal issue and should fall under the *Smith-Grable* exception. It is unlikely that Mungo's could rely on the *Smith-Grable* exception to argue for FQJ. First, unlike in *Smith*, Pince's right to relief does not depend on the constitutionality of a federal law. Even if the NMRA being held unconstitutional may affect Pince's negligence-per-se argument, Pince could still make a tort case in state-court for negligence. Second, the federal interest in this case is weak considering the federal government has minimal interests in resolving common medical-malpractice cases that are traditionally handled at the state level. Third, by hearing this case, the federal court risks upsetting the federal-state balance by sweeping in too many state cases into federal courts. Even though there don't seem to be any significant disputed issues of fact, it is unlikely that a court balancing the federal and state interests in this case would recognize FQJ under the *Smith-Grable* exception to FQJ.

However, Mungo's could probably bring its case to federal court relying on diversity (§1332). Pince was born in NH and thus began her life as a NH domiciliary. Relying on *Mas v. Perry*, Mungo's could argue that the few years Pince spent living in a Boston homeless shelter after four decades in NH were not enough to demonstrate her intention to stay in MA. According to *Mas*, attending university in a different state is not necessarily enough to change a person's domicile. Thus, the time she spent living in MA from 2011–2012 was not enough to make her a domiciliary of MA. Since Pince is probably domiciled in NH, and this case meets the amount-in-controversy requirement, a MA federal court could probably exercise diversity jurisdiction over this case.

Considering that the MA court would have diversity jurisdiction over this case, Pince could ask the federal court to stay its hearings and ultimately abstain from hearing this case. However, looking at *Colorado River* this argument is likely to fail. Federal courts have an "unflagging" duty to hear the cases properly before them and the differences between this case and *Colorado River* are significant. First, there is no hint that Congress preferred that these cases be heard exclusively in state court and there is no complicated state regulatory regime in play. Second, the state-court case started only a month before the federal case. Third, since Pince travels to Boston regularly, it wouldn't be difficult for her to make it to the federal courthouse in MA. Finally, this case does not involve issues of special concern to states, considering malpractice suits are heard in federal courts regularly. Ultimately, looking at the *Colorado River* factors, it is unlikely that a federal court with proper SMJ could abstain from this case.
