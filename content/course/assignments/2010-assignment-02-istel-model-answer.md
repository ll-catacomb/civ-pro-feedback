# Assignment 2: Model Answer

---

Jones can likely only get IPJ in Arkansas state and federal court, and NY state and federal court. Jones has general jurisdiction over Clinton in NY (a court in the state in which a defendant resides has general jurisdiction over the defendant), and IPJ in Arkansas under the minimum contacts analysis: Clinton's acts giving rise to the complaint occurred in Arkansas (*International Shoe*); it was thus reasonably foreseeable that he should anticipate being hauled into court there (*World-Wide Volkswagen*). Federal court adopts the jurisdictional authority of the court of the state in which it sits. FRCP 4(k)(1)(A).

Jones has SMJ in both federal courts given the diversity of the parties and that the amount in controversy is over $75,000. 28 U.S.C. § 1332. Venue is proper in both states: § 1391(a)(1) establishes venue in the judicial district where any defendant resides (NY); and § 1391(a)(2) establishes venue where a substantial part of the events occurred (Arkansas). Because there are no minimum contacts in California, IPJ is unlikely there.

Given her options, Jones should choose her forum based on which court applies the most advantageous pleading standards. While federal courts are likely to apply *Iqbal*/*Twombly*, where Jones would have to prove that the facts alleged are plausible and not just possible, state courts are likely to use notice pleading standards (*Granger*), which just require that the defendant be on notice of the claim (*Conley*) and apply even to nearly incomprehensible complaints (*Dioguardi*). While Jones's complaint will almost certainly survive a 12(b)(6) motion under the latter standard, it may not satisfy the third prong of Arkansas's Anti-Discrimination statute under the heightened *Iqbal* standard: while the complaint specifies the details surrounding Jones and Clinton's interaction, it does not concretely establish a plausible link between their interactions and her adverse employment consequences, or how Clinton was directly involved. For instance, the complaint asserts that the reason they gave for her employment transfer was untrue, but does not provide direct evidence supporting its conclusory statement that the "real reason" for her transfer was because of her denial of Clinton's advances (see ¶ 39).

As such, Jones should try to keep her complaint in state court to be evaluated under notice pleading's lower standard. Both NY (Second Restatement, interest analysis) and Arkansas (First Restatement, place of injury) will apply Arkansas substantive law. Regardless, the 12(b)(6) standard is likely to be considered procedural for horizontal choice of law purposes, meaning both NY and Arkansas will use their own procedural law, and notice pleading still applies. To avoid removal, Jones's first choice should be litigating in NY state court (a defendant cannot remove in his backyard on a diversity claim—§ 1441(b)), over Arkansas state court where Clinton could remove.

If Clinton did remove, Jones would try to argue that since it is a diversity case, and the twin aims of *Erie* are implicated, state law should be used. Jones's first hurdle would be arguing either that (1) the state and federal laws don't conflict; or (2) that there is not a federal rule on point.

Jones would have a hard time arguing that state law and federal law do not conflict, given that federal courts have interpreted FRCP 8 and 12 under *Iqbal* to imply a much higher standard than state courts have used. Using *Walker*, Jones could try to argue that *Iqbal* should be interpreted narrowly to avoid a direct conflict where possible. However, given the political concerns of the suit (how it might affect their jobs if politicians could get sued on conclusory allegations), and its consequent similarities to *Iqbal*, it is likely that the courts will find a direct conflict.

If the court found a direct conflict between state and federal law, Jones would have a hard time arguing *Erie* choice of law analysis, given that when there is a controlling federal rule, it takes precedence over state policy (REA; *Hanna*). Here, this seems applicable given that (1) the rule applies to the issue at hand; and (2) is valid under the REA, in that it does not "abridge, enlarge, or modify the substantive rights of any litigant" (i.e., that it is procedural).

To avoid this, Jones could argue *Iqbal* is not a federal rule, but just the court's interpretation. Without a federal rule on point, Jones could argue that the *Erie* analysis should be used.

If Jones made it past this hurdle, she would have to avoid Harlan's concurrence in *Hanna*, where she would have a hard time arguing that the availability of different rules drove the conduct giving rise to the complaint (it is unlikely that Clinton considered pleading standards when interacting with Jones).

Instead, Jones could argue under *York* that since the outcome would be different in applying federal law as opposed to state law, state law should be applied. (NB: *York* is no longer good law.)

Jones's best argument would be *Hanna*'s majority. Jones would show that this is exactly the kind of case implicated by forum shopping and the inequitable administration of laws, in that Clinton would decide to remove to federal court solely to achieve a different result. And, *Hanna*'s majority overrides Harlan's concurrence.

Overall, because federal court could go either way on whether *Erie* should be employed, and even if employed whether state law should govern, it would be best to keep the case in state court, preferably in NY where Clinton can't remove.
