## Substantive Assignment

Pennsylvania has three federal district courts: the Western, Middle, and Eastern districts. Philadelphia is in the Eastern District of Pennsylvania. New York has four districts: Northern, Eastern, Southern, and Western. Manhattan is located in the Southern District of New York; Albany is located in the Northern District of New York. Pennsylvania follows the First Restatement for horizontal choice of law. New York, as you know, deploys the Second Restatement approach.

All states relevant to this assignment have a cause of action for negligence. The elements of this cause of action are that the defendant owes a duty of care to the plaintiff (we all owe a duty to each other to act with ordinary care), that the defendant breached this duty by failing to act with ordinary care under the circumstances, and that the breach caused quantifiable harm to the plaintiff. In all states relevant to this exam, a plaintiff can sue a defendant for negligence if the defendant failed to exercise ordinary care in maintaining a mode of transportation, such as a railroad coupling. That said, regarding railroad safety, the law of some states requires more than simple negligence before a plaintiff may recover. In Pennsylvania, for example, a plaintiff injured by the actions or inactions of a railroad employee regarding railroad safety must prove that the employee was reckless, meaning that the employee disregarded a known risk. In New York, in contrast, a plaintiff need only prove simple negligence. This difference between negligence versus recklessness is substantive for both horizontal and vertical choice of law rules.

A federal statute called the Railroad Recovery Act ("RRA") creates a cause of action allowing a plaintiff injured by an action of an officer or employee of a railroad to sue for damages, if such action (i) was taken in the course of the officer/employee's company duties, and (ii) would constitute a tort in the law of the state in which the negligent act or omission occurred. Note that under the RRA, it is irrelevant in which state the injury occurred.

Many contracts include so-called "choice of law clauses." These clauses specify that the law of a particular state will govern any lawsuit arising among the parties that relates to their relationship. Such clauses can govern tort lawsuits relating in any way to the parties' relationship as well as breach of contract lawsuits. They can also govern lawsuits against employees of a company who made the relevant contracts. Federal common law provides that choice of law clauses are enforceable. Both Pennsylvania and New York law render such clauses invalid.

All states relevant to this exam have long-arm statutes that allow their courts to exercise personal jurisdiction to the extent permissible under the U.S. Constitution.

Leto Railroad Lines was a New York corporation with its principal place of business in Philadelphia, PA. Leto operated a small, high-speed railroad line between Philadelphia and Manhattan, New York. Leto trains had two cars each, with the cars connected by a standard railroad coupling. Because of the coupling's importance to the safety of the train, Leto policy required that the train's engineer, typically the only employee on each Leto train, inspect it at the station right before the train departs on each run.

On June 20, 2023, Chani, who had lived her entire life in Pittsburgh, PA, was in New York to see her mother, Jessica, who lived in Albany, New York. She decided to take a Leto train to visit her father, Stilgar, who lived in Philadelphia. After driving a friend's car to New York City, she purchased a train ticket at Leto's Manhattan ticket office. The engineer on the train that would make the run to Philadelphia that day, Duncan, sold Chani her ticket. Duncan had grown up with his family in Philadelphia and continued

to live there. To purchase the ticket, Chani signed a contract (Duncan provided it to her) that included the following clause:

> **Applicable law:** The parties to this contract agree that any lawsuit arising out of, or relating in any way, to the buyer's Leto train trip will be governed by the law of Pennsylvania. In particular, the standard of care owed by Leto or any of its employees to the buyer of this contract will be governed by Pennsylvania law.

After signing the contract and purchasing her ticket, Chani boarded the two-car train. Duncan, the train's engineer and driver, failed to inspect the coupling connecting the two cars before the train left the Manhattan station. About 15 minutes after the train crossed into the city limits of Philadelphia, the train's two cars decoupled, causing a crash. Subsequent examination demonstrated that the coupling was faulty in a way that a proper inspection would have uncovered. Chani suffered injuries in the crash.

In August of 2023, Chani sued both Leto and Duncan, demanding $100,000 from each, in the United States federal court for the Northern District of New York. Chani sued Leto under the RRA. Against Duncan, her cause of action was simple negligence. The parties have agreed that for the purposes of this lawsuit, Duncan's failure to inspect was negligent but not reckless. You are a partner in the law firm representing Duncan.
