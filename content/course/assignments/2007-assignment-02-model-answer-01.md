## Injunctive Relief Against Picasso and SPAT

In order for the court to issue a preliminary injunction, CLAP must be able to show that 1) CLAP will suffer irreparable harm without relief; 2) there's some likelihood of success on the merits; 3) the balance of equities favors CLAP; and 4) an injunction would not be against public policy. CLAP will most likely fail the first two requirements, and therefore, fail to get injunctive relief.

**Irreparable Harm.** It would be hard for CLAP to argue that the consummation of the deal between Picasso and SPAT would lead to irreparable harm. CLAP can be paid for the projected lost profits in the "Eric Cartmen" posters and for increased profits in other posters resulting from increased foot traffic. CLAP could try to claim irreparable harm based on the fact that it would be difficult to calculate total lost profits—particularly profits from foot traffic—with any accuracy, especially since the Connecticut store was to have capitalized on the current public interest in the "Eric Cartmen" painting.

**Success on the Merits.** Bracketing procedural issues, the case against Picasso and SPAT is very strong on the merits. The words of the contract clearly indicate that CLAP has exclusive right to product posters, and there's no mention of a geographical limit. However, there are serious personal and subject matter jurisdiction issues that will likely prevent CLAP from obtaining injunctive relief.

**Personal jurisdiction:** While there's no problem of personal jurisdiction with Picasso—Picasso is a citizen of MA and domiciled in MA—there's a problem with SPAT because SPAT is a citizen of ME and CT and has no continuous or systematic contacts with MA. SPAT has never sold products in MA, doesn't maintain an office in MA, and the subject of the suit only involves SPAT's CT stores. Unless CLAP can convincingly argue that SPAT advertises or solicits in MA, intentionally targets MA, or that sales of its goods in CT causes effects in MA, there's no personal jurisdiction over SPAT.

**Federal Question Jurisdiction.** Under 28 USC § 1331, the claim must arise under federal law and do so as part of the plaintiff's case. CLAP's complaint against Picasso and SPAT is essentially a state breach-of-contracts claim. While it's true that Picasso could sue CLAP in federal court for copyright infringement under 17 U.S.C. 502, 504, CLAP cannot sue first for declaratory judgment in federal court merely in anticipation of a defense to a possible federal claim by Picasso. The only way CLAP can get around this rule is to try to ask for declaratory judgment that it's not a copyright infringement for CLAP to sell its reproductions in CT. Here, it would be important to argue that Picasso can get into federal court himself, even though the facts underlying his dispute is essentially a contracts claim.

**Diversity jurisdiction.** Under 28 USC § 1332, there must be complete diversity and damages must equal or exceed $75K. There's no complete diversity in this case because both CLAP and Picasso are citizens of MA. However, if CLAP were to only sue SPAT (and assuming away personal jurisdiction problems), this case might possibly go forward. If the amount-in-controversy only includes the costs that CLAP has borne so far, the amount's far below the required 75K. However, if it also includes the lost profits in the CT store—and it must appear to a legal certainty that the claim is really for less than the jurisdictional amount to justify dismissal—then there's a chance for diversity jurisdiction. If CLAP only sues SPAT, however, it cannot bring Picasso into the case under supplemental jurisdiction. Under § 1367(b), there's no supplemental jurisdiction where a plaintiff would join a defendant that's not diverse.

**Supplemental Jurisdiction.** If CLAP is somehow allowed to sue under federal question jurisdiction, there could be a problem with supplemental jurisdiction. The tort claim arises from the same nucleus of operative facts. The judge might still decline to exercise jurisdiction because of jury confusion or because the facts mainly indicate state claims. However, the fact that it's not a novel state claim and it doesn't predominate over the case weighs toward granting jurisdiction.

**Public Interest.** The injunction would not disserve the public interest because this is a private contracts dispute with few public implications.

**Balance of Equities:** The balance of equities favors CLAP because CLAP could possibly lose some incalculable amount of profit, whereas SPAT and Picasso's losses are smaller and more contained.

## Draft Complaint

DISTRICT COURT OF MASSACHUSETTS
CIVIL ACTION NO. ___________

CHARMING LOVELY ART POSTERS, INC.,
Plaintiff,

vs.

STRANGE PRODUCTS AND TASTES, INC. and HARRY PICASSO,
Defendants.

**COMPLAINT FOR DECLARATORY AND INJUNCTIVE RELIEF**

### Introduction

This is an action for declaratory relief that Charming Lovely Art Posters, Inc. ("CLAP") will not infringe Harry Picasso's copyright under 17 U.S.C. § 502. CLAP also seeks injunctive relief preventing the consummation of the deal between Strange Products and Tastes, Inc. ("SPAT") and Picasso, and damages against SPAT for tortious interference with contractual relations.

### Jurisdiction and Venue

This Court has subject matter jurisdiction over this action pursuant to 28 USC § 1331 because the case arises under federal copyright law. This Court has supplemental jurisdiction under § 1367 over the tortious interference claim.

This Court has authority to grant declaratory relief requested pursuant to § 2201(a).

This Court has in personam jurisdiction over Picasso because Picasso is a citizen of, and domiciled in, Massachusetts. This Court has in personam jurisdiction over SPAT because of the effects its commercial activity has in Massachusetts.

Venue is proper in the District of Massachusetts pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 1391 because Picasso resides within this Court's district.

### Parties

Plaintiff CLAP is a corporation incorporated in Delaware and having its principal place of business in Massachusetts.

Defendant SPAT is a corporation incorporated in Maine and having its principal place of business in Connecticut.

Defendant Harry Picasso is citizen of, and domiciled in, Massachusetts.

### Factual Allegations

Plaintiff and Picasso entered into a written contract on July 15, 2007 giving Plaintiff exclusive right to produce posters of the painting "Eric Cartmen in Guernica, Reclining."

In reliance on this contract, Plaintiff proceeded to produce 200 poster-pictures of the work, intending to sell them in a Connecticut store newly opened for this purpose.

In September however, Picasso negotiated a deal with SPAT, to permit SPAT to produce and sell the same "Eric Cartmen" posters in its Connecticut stores.

Picasso then threatened to sue Plaintiff for copyright infringement if Plaintiff proceeded with its plans to sell the "Eric Cartmen" posters in Connecticut.

### Prayer for Relief

Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Court grant him judgment as follows:

Declare that CLAP's intended sale of the "Eric Cartmen" posters in Connecticut is not a violation of 17 U.S.C. § 502, 504.

Issue injunctive relief to prevent the consummation of the proposed deal between SPAT and Picasso regarding the sale of "Eric Cartmen" posters in Connecticut.

Award damages of $75,000 for tortious interference with contractual relations.
