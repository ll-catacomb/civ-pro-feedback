## Assignment 2, Question

The North Carolina Rules of Civil Procedure ("NCRCP"), which govern proceedings in North Carolina state court, provide as follows:

**Rule 65: Installment Contracts, Pretrial Seizure and Return of Goods Not Realty**

(a) This section provides the right of a seller regarding pretrial seizure and possession of goods or tangible items of property, not realty (hereinafter called the "Goods"), who sells to a buyer on a written, signed installment contract.

(b) A seller of Goods in a contract providing for periodic installment payments by a buyer may move the court for a pretrial seizure, and for possession pending completion of the litigation, of those Goods, as follows.

> (1) The seller shall file with the court a complaint for an unpaid balance due on the installment payments, which complaint shall describe the facts and circumstances of the sales contract and the nature of the Goods, and shall describe the payments made and missing on the installment contract. The seller/plaintiff shall NOT serve the buyer/defendant at this time.
>
> (2) The seller shall file with the complaint a certified copy of the contract as well as of proof of all payments received on the contract.
>
> (3) The seller shall file one or more affidavits, written by persons with personal knowledge of the events described, and sworn under penalty of perjury, attesting to the non-payment of one or more installments due, to the expiration of any applicable cure period, to the notification by the seller to the buyer of the past due

<!-- page: 3 -->

> amounts, and to the continued refusal by the Buyer to make the relevant payments or to return the Goods to the Seller.
>
> (4) The seller shall file with the court a motion and accompanying arguments thoroughly discussing any applicable law. Such motion shall request an ex parte[^1] hearing from the Court at its earliest convenience.

(c) The Court, acting through the first available judge, upon receiving a motion as described in section (b)(4), above, shall at its earliest convenience schedule an ex parte hearing on the motion, at which time it will review the papers filed by the Seller. If the Court finds that the affidavits, certified copies, and other evidence presented by the Seller, establish that the Seller is likely to succeed on the merits of its complaint for non-payment of installment payments duly owed, it shall issue a writ directing the Sheriff of the Court to

> (1) seize, using force if necessary, the Goods from the Buyer and to return them to the possession of the Seller, and
>
> (2) to serve upon the Buyer, in person (or in the case of an incorporeal entity, upon a high-level official of that entity) all of the papers described in section (b), above, along with a summons to appear before the Court.

(d) Litigation shall then ensue on the Complaint in the ordinary course of business.

[^1]: An ex parte hearing is one in which only one party (here, the seller), shows up to speak to the court.

North Carolina has a long-arm statute that allows its state courts to exercise personal jurisdiction in any circumstance "permitted by the United States Constitution."

Slughorn, Inc. was a small corporation incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Charlotte, NC. Slughorn, Inc.'s sole product is Felix Felicis, a nearly-magical catalyst essential to an improved chemical process to manufacture a highly popular and profitable chocolate frog candy. Slughorn's small (three-person) manufacturing plant is located in Charlotte, NC. Slughorn's sole method of advertising is the Internet. Its webpage shows a beautiful aerial, nighttime view of the Charlotte skyline, and each of the subsequent pages shows a similar view of a North Carolina City. The customer order page shows a photograph of happy beachgoers around a sign that says, "Outer Banks, NC." Customers place orders by sending an email to the company's email account, slughorn@gmail.com. Customers with questions about any aspect of dealing with the company are similarly instructed to send the company an email at its business email address. The website states that payments to Slughorn are accepted via electronic transfer to an account in the Local Savings and Loan Bank of Charlotte, NC,

which is a small bank with two locations, both in Charlotte. No other means of contact (including a business address) is on the company's website, although its Charlotte, NC address can be found in the company's articles of incorporation, which are available online at the website of the Secretary of State of Delaware. Note that because Felix Felicis is a catalyst, it is not consumed or destroyed in the manufacturing process.

Honeydukes, Inc., is a small candy company incorporated in South Carolina with its principal place of business in Columbia, SC. Honeydukes makes chocolate frogs. On February 4, 2014, Honeydukes sent an email from its business email account placing an order for 10 ounces of Felix Felicis, with a purchase price of $300,000.00. Slughorn responded by emailing Honeydukes its standard business contract, by which delivery would be made immediately by special courier, with Honeydukes to pay $30,000 each month, payments due at the beginning of each month, first payment due on March 1, 2014. Honeydukes signed the contract. Delivery of the Felix Felicis was made to Honeydukes' office by a courier on February 19, 2014.

Honeydukes made the March 1, April 1, and May 1 payments via wire transfer to Slughorn's bank account at the Local Savings and Loan Bank of Charlotte, NC. On May 29, however, it emailed Slughorn that it would not be making further payments, stating that the Felix Felicis it had received was impure and of low quality, and that as a result, its manufacturing operation of chocolate frogs was operating at half speed. Honeydukes offered to pay $60,000 more (for a total of $150,000) in final settlement of all debts owed Slughorn. Slughorn responded with an email stating that it did not believe Honeydukes' assertion of a bad product, and demanding that payments be made on schedule. Honeydukes responded that it would make no further payments until its complaints about the quality of the Felix Felicis were resolved. There was no further communication between the parties, and no further payments were made.

On September 17, Slughorn filed the following documents in the United States District Court for the Western District of North Carolina:

- A complaint for breach of contract reciting that Honeydukes had agreed via email contract to pay $300,000 in $30,000 monthly payments for 10 ounces of Felix Felicis; that delivery of the product had been made on February 19, 2014, that the product delivered was of high quality, and was still in Honeydukes' manufacturing plant in South Carolina; that Honeydukes had made the first three payments but had missed the payments due in June, July, August, and September; that Slughorn had sent an email demand for the payments due; that Slughorn had refused payment. The complaint sought payment of $120,000.00 in damages for the missed payments.
- Certified copies of the email contract and all emails exchanged between the parties.
- Sworn affidavits by Slughorn employees with knowledge of the events above describing them in full.

- Certified copies of bank statements from the Local Savings and Loan Bank of Charlotte, NC, showing that Honeydukes had made the first three payments due on the contract but none since.
- A motion requesting the court to issue a writ of possession under North Carolina Rule of Civil Procedure 65, such that the sheriff would seize the Felix Felicis from Honeydukes and return it to Slughorn, pending litigation. The motion was accompanied by a brief discussing the law and a request for a rapid ex parte hearing.

On September 30, 2014, the North Carolina federal court held a hearing. The judge reviewed the papers Slughorn had filed and issued an order directing the federal sheriff to repossess the Felix Felicis. On October 1, 2014, the sheriff drove to Honeydukes' headquarters in South Carolina, served all of Slughorn's court papers on the Honeydukes' Chief Executive Officer, and demanded possession of the Felix Felicis. Later that afternoon, Honeydukes placed a vial containing the substance into the hands of the sheriff, who drove it back to Slughorn's offices in Charlotte.

You are an associate in the law office that represents Honeydukes. The partner you are working for says that Honeydukes has told her that it REALLY needs the Felix Felicis to continue its manufacturing operation. Even at half efficiency, it is making a lot of money on the chocolate frogs, and it cannot manufacture chocolate frogs without the substance. The partner asks you to describe any and all arguments your client might make against the process Slughorn used to obtain repossession of the substance, or otherwise against the lawsuit. The partner tells you NOT to discuss the issue of whether the Felix Felicis that Slughorn provided was in fact faulty; she's still investigating the facts on that issue.

What arguments could your client use?
