## Substantive Assignment

Assume the following (some of which is legally inaccurate, and all of which is factually fictional), for the purposes of this assignment.

Historically, an action for negligence encompassed four elements: (i) that the defendant owed a duty to the plaintiff, (ii) that the defendant breached that duty by failing to exercise due care, (iii) that the breach of due care caused the plaintiff's injury, and (iv) that the plaintiff suffered damages (including pain and suffering) as a result. Negligence was historically an action at law.

Historically, an action for breach of a duty owed to a third-party beneficiary to a contract, also known as a "third-party breach" lawsuit, encompassed the following elements: (i) that the defendant engaged in a contract, (ii) that the primary purpose of the contract was to benefit a third party named in that contract, or a class of individuals named in that contract, (iii) that the defendant breached the contract, (iv) that the plaintiff suffered damages as a result (including pain and suffering). The remedy for a third-party breach action was traditionally an order from the chancellor to the defendant to perform the contract or, if that were impossible, for disgorgement to the plaintiff of the amount of money the defendant had received in payments for the contract. Third-party breach was historically an action in equity.

Assume the following background: Invoking its powers under U.S. Const. amends. XIV–XV, in January of 2018, Congress passed the Election Improvement Act of 2018 (the "EIA"), Section 1 of which created the United States Election Agency (the "USEA"). Congress designed the USEA to administer certain issues relating to voting rights. Relevant portions of the EIA read as follows:

### Section 2: Remedies

Any person or entity who alleges that a US government agency, a state agency, or a private individual acted in violation of applicable law relating to the administration of an election may bring a petition before the USEA. If an administrative law judge within the USEA finds a violation of law, the agency shall order anyone responsible, including a federal or state or local governmental entity, to take such action as the USEA deems necessary to prevent violations of law. Moreover, the USEA may direct the person or entity who acted in violation of applicable law to provide such monetary relief as the USEA shall, in its discretion, deem appropriate.

### Section 3: Review

Any party aggrieved by a decision, order, or other action of the USEA may seek a declaration of its invalidity by suing the agency in the federal district court having jurisdiction over the situs of the events giving rise to the controversy. The district court shall review all

### Section 4: Procedures

The USEA shall adjudicate all disputes with the utmost expedition. In keeping with the extraordinary requirement of speedy resolution of election disputes, the USEA shall make its findings of fact and conclusions of law based solely on a written record submitted by the parties. In no event, however, shall the parties to a USEA dispute be entitled to take discovery, nor shall the USEA hear live testimony, nor shall the USEA allow any action filed within it to expand beyond the parties and claims in the original complaint. In all other matters, the USEA may use such procedures as it deems appropriate, including any principles or rules used in federal courts, such as the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

No federal or state court, nor any state administrative agency, nor any federal agency other than the USEA, shall have jurisdiction over any lawsuit described in Section 2, other than as specified in Section 3.

In the November 3, 2020 election, voters in some municipalities around the State of Massachusetts complained of long waits at polling booths. In a November 4, 2020 interview with *The Boston Globe*, Secretary of State William Galvin claimed that his office was not responsible for any delays that might have occurred. According to Secretary Galvin, any problems that might have existed (and the Secretary did not admit anything had gone amiss) were the fault of the Walter White Printing Services, Inc. ("WW"), a Massachusetts corporation with its principal place of business in Boston, MA. WW was the printing company the Secretary's office had hired to print the Massachusetts ballots. Secretary Galvin stated that he had originally ordered 3,000,000 paper ballots to be printed, but that one month before the election, he had orally increased the order to 5,500,000. In fact, according to Secretary Galvin, WW had provided only 2,000,000 ballots.

On November 14, Jane Margolis, a domiciliary of Springfield, MA, filed a handwritten petition before the USEA against WW. In her petition, Margolis stated that she had arrived at Boston Polling Place 157 at 9 am, but had had to wait until 5 pm to cast her vote. Margolis also stated that she was disabled due to a muscular and skeletal problem with her back and legs, and the long wait (most of which she had to spend standing, due to a lack of seats and pandemic-related social distancing requirements) had caused her excruciating pain. Her petition stated, "I wasn't going to quit. Too many people died to give me the right to vote." She asked the USEA to order WW to pay her $10,000 for her pain and suffering. The last three sentences of her petition were as follows: "I know very little about the law, and I want the government to give me a lawyer to help me with this case, as it is too complicated for me to handle on my own. I don't know how or what I'm supposed to tell you. I swear that everything I wrote here is true, under penalty of perjury." Margolis signed her petition. She also attached to it a copy of the *Boston Globe* article cited above. The USEA gave WW notice of the action and invited a response.

On December 6, 2020, WW filed an answer denying Margolis' allegations. It submitted an affidavit of its CEO, Mr. Walter White, stating (i) that its written contract with the Commonwealth of Massachusetts required it to print and deliver only 3,000,000 ballots, something it had done in a timely manner, (ii) that it had received no communication, oral or written, from Secretary Galvin or from any other Massachusetts official asking it to provide more ballots. White's affidavit attached a copy of the WW contract with Massachusetts, which did require WW to print 3,000,000 ballots. The contract also stated that it was intended to provide ballots in a timely manner for the benefit of each and every Massachusetts voter. Finally, the contract also stated that Secretary Galvin "could, via written or oral communication, increase the number of ballots requested by so communicating to WW, the charges for which will be" as specified in a later clause in the contract. White signed his affidavit under penalty of perjury. WW's lawyers also filed a short legal brief which, among other things, demanded a jury trial before concluding, "WW stands ready to provide such further evidence in support of its contentions as the USEA might require, if the USEA will only notify us of what might be persuasive or useful."

On December 20, 2020, the USEA issued a Decision And Order, which read in its entirety as follows: "We find that Petitioner Margolis has demonstrated that WW acted wrongfully in not providing the required number of ballots in a timely fashion, but we find that she has not proven her pain and suffering damages with sufficient specificity. Therefore, we award her $100."
