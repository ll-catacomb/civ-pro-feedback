# Assignment 1: Model Answer (TA Draft)

Civil Procedure Fall 2010
Professor Greiner
669 words

---

Gringotts can get to court before Hufflepuff, and choose the forum, by bringing a declaratory judgment in South Carolina federal court. Since Gringotts is suing for a declaratory judgment, subject matter jurisdiction is based on whether there would be SMJ over the "coercive action" that would be filed by the party who "should" be the plaintiff (Greiner imaginary lawsuit rule). A federal defense is not sufficient. *Mottley*. In this case, the actual plaintiff is Hufflepuff, who would bring a state law breach of contract claim. This presents a problem because of the well-pleaded complaint rule, since in this case the "real" complaint would be based on state law. Accordingly, some could argue that we could not bring this declaratory judgment in federal court.

However, federal arising under jurisdiction may also lie over state-law claims that implicate significant federal issues. See *Smith*. It could be argued that *Smith* only applies if the federal issue arises as part of the plaintiff's cause of action, which would not be the case here since the plaintiff's claim is based on state law. However, *Grable* established a two-factor test for determining whether there should be federal jurisdiction. First, the courts will consider whether there is a substantial federal interest. We will argue that this is established because Hufflepuff must first prove that EHMA, a federal statute, is unconstitutional before reaching her state law claim. The text of Section 1 of EHMA states that the financial crisis is "catastrophic to the United States economy, and, thus, to our national security," indicating that this statute is responding to a significant federal issue. Next, the *Grable* test considers whether adjudicating this lawsuit in federal court will upset the division of labor between federal and state courts. Federal courts will only exercise their jurisdiction if that balance will not be disturbed. The question presented here is purely legal, like the one in *Grable*, so it is not likely to upset this balance. Once a federal court has resolved this issue, similar litigation can move forward in state courts.

Even if arising under jurisdiction is not present, we may be able to get into federal court under diversity jurisdiction. Since Hufflepuff lost her down payment of $80,000, she meets the $75,000 amount-in-controversy requirement. To establish diversity, Hufflepuff and Gringotts must be domiciled in different states. Hufflepuff's domicile is likely not North Carolina. Although she was born there and lived there with her parents through high school, after her parents died, she subsequently bought a house and established residency in South Carolina. Since she has not expressed any intention of returning to North Carolina, it is not likely that she will be considered domiciled there. Hufflepuff may be considered domiciled in South Carolina based on her residency and home ownership there. However, if she intends to leave the state immediately after college and move to New York, she may not be considered domiciled in South Carolina because she is lacking intent to stay there. Gringotts is incorporated in Delaware, so will necessarily be considered domiciled in that state. A company is also domiciled in the state in which its principal place of business is. Based on the Supreme Court's recent ruling in *Hertz*, the principal place of business should be determined based on the nerve center test. Gringotts' operational headquarters are in North Carolina, probably establishing its nerve center in that state. Therefore, it will likely be considered domiciled there for the purposes of diversity. While it's not totally clear where Hufflepuff will be considered domiciled, since it probably will not be in North Carolina, and certainly will not be in Delaware, it is fairly likely that diversity will be established.

Finally, we must ensure that a federal court in South Carolina would have personal jurisdiction over Hufflepuff. Since Hufflepuff is living in South Carolina, she has established minimum contacts for the courts in that state to have personal jurisdiction over her. Since we're filing in South Carolina, we will have subjected ourselves to the personal jurisdiction of the courts there.
