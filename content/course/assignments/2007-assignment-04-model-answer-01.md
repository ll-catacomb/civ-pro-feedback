## Question 1

The Watertown voters would try to employ offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel against Charles Townsend to preclude the company from litigating whether the company breached its contract with the State and whether this failure delayed voting. Drawing on *Parklane*, the Watertown voters could argue that Charles Townsend's breach of a statewide contract made similar future actions foreseeable and gave the company every reason to vigorously contest the Boston Five's allegations. Since the company lost its efforts to contest the issues, the Watertown voters should not suffer the expenses of re-litigation as a penalty, particularly because EIA Section 4 prohibited the USEA panel from allowing the Watertown voters to join the Boston Five action.

However, Charles Townsend would argue that the issue of whether its breach of the ballot contract caused delays for Watertown voters was neither actually litigated nor necessary to a judgment that a ballot shortage caused delays for voters in Boston. Though evidence of Boston might show a contractual breach, it would be insufficient to prove that the shortage caused delays outside of Boston; perhaps the State had enough ballots for Watertown and the delays there were caused by some other mishap. Even assuming a court would consider a theory of statewide causation, Charles Townsend could argue that the trifling damages requested by the Boston Five reduced its incentive for aggressive litigation. The company could also argue based on Restatement § 83 and *Parklane* that the USEA handicapped its defense because 1) the USEA rules prohibited live testimony often critical to a contract dispute and 2) the panel denied a motion to compel its contractual partner even to be deposed. Since such procedures are available in federal court and might have enabled Charles Townsend to win a better result, it would be unfair to take them away by allowing preclusion.

## Question 2

Charles Townsend would challenge the USEA decision as a violation of its Seventh Amendment right to a jury trial. Under *Tull*, a court would 1) compare the Boston Five's claim for relief to causes of action existing at the time of the ratification of the Amendment in 1791 and 2) examine whether the remedy sought sounds more in law or in equity. The first inquiry would be fruitless because notions of a widespread right to vote were unknown to pre-revolutionary English law. In any event, *Tull* made clear that the legal or equitable nature of the remedy is more important. Though the Boston Five sought both damages and an injunction, the mere co-existence of legal and equitable claims does not guarantee the attachment of Seventh Amendment rights. The Boston Five could nevertheless claim that the unique voting rights thread running through both their claims for relief distinguishes those claims from common law tort or contract actions. Moreover, drawing on *Atlas Roofing*, they could argue that the Seventh Amendment does not prevent Congress from creating statutory causes of action to vindicate public rights without the delays and expense of jury trials. Indeed, Congress noted in the EIA that election disputes garner an "extraordinary requirement of speedy resolution."

In response, Charles Townsend could distinguish *Atlas Roofing* because the OSHA proceeding upheld there involved a suit by the United States as sovereign. In contrast, the Boston Five brought the USEA action as private parties. Though the EIA allows the government to commence actions, a judge might be reluctant to conclude that Congress can vitiate the Seventh Amendment merely by creating coincident private and public remedies.

Moreover, Charles Townsend could argue that the Boston Five made claims based on private rather than public rights. While the Boston Five might have been willing to incur delays because they valued voting, their damages could have just as easily been caused by highway construction delaying their commute. Thus, their claim really amounts to tortious interference with contract. In the alternative, the Boston Five's complaint also seems like a claim for contractual breach since Charles Townsend surely contemplated that voters stood as third-party beneficiaries to its ballot contract.

In addition, for the purposes of the second *Tull* inquiry, these tort and contract claims reside at the core of the common law. Under this theory, the Boston Five's equitable complaint seems like a cover to avoid a jury trial, particularly because an order to avoid creating illegal obstacles to voting adds little to existing statutory and constitutional obligations. Though Congress can enact causes of action to vindicate public rights, it cannot eviscerate Seventh Amendment rights in any case remotely related to the exercise of such rights.
