## Question

Assume the following background: Invoking its powers under U.S. Const. amends. XIV–XV, on January 2, 2006, Congress passed the Election Improvement Act of 2006 (the "EIA"), Title 1 of which created the United States Election Agency (the "USEA"). Congress designed the USEA to administer certain issues relating to voting rights. Relevant portions of Title 1 of the EIA read as follows:

---

**Section 2: Remedies**

If the USEA finds a violation of state or federal election law, or that some person or entity has breached a contract or committed a tort relating to elections, the agency may direct the responsible person or entity to provide such monetary relief to anyone damaged thereby as the USEA shall deem appropriate. The USEA may also provide appropriate equitable relief.

**Section 3: Procedures**

The USEA shall adjudicate all disputes with the utmost expedition. In keeping with this directive re speed, the USEA shall make findings of fact and conclusions of law based on a written record submitted by the parties. The record may include affidavits, documents and other exhibits, interrogatory responses, and deposition transcripts. In no event, however, shall the USEA hear live testimony, nor shall the USEA have the power to compel persons who would rather not do so to testify before it. In all other matters, the USEA may use such procedures as it deems appropriate, including the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. There shall, however, be no appeal from USEA rulings to any court (state or federal).

**Section 4: Option To Sue in Federal Court Without First Proceeding to USEA**

Any person or party injured by a violation of state or federal election law, or by a breach of a contract or the commission of a tort, with respect to the conduct or administration of elections may (without first proceeding in the USEA) sue the person or entity who caused the injury in a federal district court of competent jurisdiction. The court may award any and all relief, equitable or legal, as it deems appropriate.

---

In the November 7, 2006 election, voters in various municipalities around the State of Massachusetts complained of long waits at polling booths. In a November 8, 2006 interview with The Boston Globe, Secretary of State William Galvin claimed that his office was not responsible for any delays that might have occurred. According to Secretary Galvin, any problems that might have existed were the fault of the Charles Townsend Printing Services, Inc., the printing company the Secretary's office had hired to print the Massachusetts paper ballots. Charles Townsend is incorporated in Massachusetts, where it also has its principal place of business. Secretary Galvin stated that he had originally ordered 3,100,000 paper ballots to be printed, but that one month before the election, he had orally increased the order to 4,000,000. In fact, according to Secretary Galvin, Charles Townsend had provided only 3,100,000 ballots.

On November 14, five citizens of the City of Boston, each an associate of the prestigious Boston law firm of Garrett, Monroe, & Duncan, LLC, initiated a proceeding in the USEA against Charles Townsend. In subsequent affidavits and deposition testimony, the "Boston Five," as they became known, stated that they had arrived at Boston Polling Place 157 at 9 am, but had had to wait until 5 pm to cast their votes. The Boston Five further averred and testified that because they had been absent from work the whole of November 7, Garrett, Monroe, & Duncan had charged each of them a day against allotted vacation leave time. As calculated from their salaries, the value of a full day of vacation for each was $750.00. The Boston Five's legal theory was that Charles Townsend had breached its contract with the State of Massachusetts to print enough ballots, causing them the monetary damage as detailed above. The Boston Five also submitted a copy of The Boston Globe article containing Secretary Galvin's interview, but provided no other evidence.

On November 21, after receiving appropriate notice of the action, Charles Townsend filed an answer in the USEA denying the Boston Five's allegations and moved for an order compelling the Secretary of State to give a deposition regarding the alleged oral contract modification. Charles Townsend further provided affidavits from its officials to the effect that it had received a contract to print 3,100,000 ballots for the 2006 election, that it had complied with that contract, and that it had never received any modification request (oral or written) from Secretary Galvin. On November 23, the USEA denied the motion to compel Secretary Galvin's deposition (citing Section 3, above), then announced that it would rule in about a month.

On November 28, Charles Townsend sued the Boston Five in the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts, seeking a declaratory judgment that it was not liable in any way whatsoever to the Boston Five. Charles Townsend requested that the District Court expedite the case. On December 6, the Boston Five moved to stay the District Court litigation, citing the fact that the USEA was already adjudicating the dispute. On December 8, the Massachusetts District Court granted the Boston Five's motion for a stay, observing, "I expect the USEA to resolve this dispute, whereupon I can simply enter judgment on preclusion grounds."

On December 11, Charles Townsend filed a notice of appeal from the District Court's stay order. Later that same day, it filed an emergency motion in the United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit requesting that its appeal be expedited. The First Circuit granted the motion to expedite.

You are a law clerk for one of the First Circuit judges assigned to the case. Your judge asks you for a memorandum addressing the following questions:

1. Is there federal court subject matter jurisdiction over Charles Townsend's lawsuit?

2. Is there appellate jurisdiction over Charles Townsend's appeal?

3. Assuming a "yes" to questions (1) and (2), is the District Court's stay appropriate? Focus your answer on whether the District Court could appropriately give preclusive effect to an eventual USEA decision.
