# Assignment 1 Model Answer

Florean can probably sue Bathilda, Tonks, and Chang in the same lawsuit in federal court. Florean will make a standard breach of contract claim against Bathilda and Tonks, and a negligent (depending on HCOL) interference claim against Chang.

---

## SMJ

Since these claims all fall under state law, there is no arising-under jurisdiction. Jurisdiction is based on complete diversity (*Strawbridge*). Florean is domiciled in OR (plaintiff); Bathilda a citizen of DE (inc) and CA (PPoB) (defendant); Tonks in DE (inc) and CA (PPoB) (defendant); and Chang in DE (inc) and WA (PPoB) (defendant). Each individual claim would meet the AIC requirement because Tonks/Bathilda's claims are $112.5k each (> $75k), and the claim against Chang for damages would be $225k (> $75k).

---

## PJ and Venue

4(k)(1)(a) long-arm allows federal court to exercise jurisdiction over state-law claims, and all states here have long-arm to limits of constitution.

**DE** — Florean can sue all three in DE because all domiciled there = general jurisdiction. Venue would be proper because all incorporated there — § 1391(b)(1).

**OR** — Could possibly get specific jurisdiction over everyone in OR. Bathilda passes the minimum contacts test because Bathilda contracted directly with Florean to purchase OR corn and negotiated purchase at Florean's farm in OR, thus purposefully availing itself of business in OR. Jurisdiction would be fair/reasonable because of this business relationship.

- Specific jurisdiction over Chang because Chang contracted to build the OR bridge, also availing itself of OR business and establishing minimal contacts to exercise jurisdiction over Chang in OR. OR has an interest in regulating the conduct that occurred in state (bridge repair/closure), which impacted an OR farmer, so it is fair to hail Chang to OR.
- Specific jurisdiction over Tonks in OR may be a roadblock to suit here. Contracts were signed over the internet, which is difficult to locate (*Griffis*), and Tonks's contract isn't even with Florean — Tonks bought corn from Bathilda, a CA/DE company. A court could say Tonks knew it bought corn coming from OR, thus purposefully availing itself of corn business in OR, but this isn't a surefire win. It is not clear that Tonks's business to purchase Florean's corn from Bathilda establishes minimal contacts with OR or that it would be fair to hail Tonks into court in a state in which it has not done business directly.
- If IPJ established over all, bridge closure = § 1391(b)(2) venue in OR (only one district).

**ID** — Likely out — only have general jurisdiction there over Tonks and others don't have contacts there (except perhaps Bathilda has contacts through the phone call doing business, which is easier to locate than internet activity). Definitely not over Chang.

**CA** — Chang likely isn't subject to jurisdiction there, as there are no contacts relating CA to Chang; have general jurisdiction over Bathilda and likely specific jurisdiction over Tonks for their business with Bathilda. If could sue in CA, venue would be proper in Eastern District where Bathilda's HQ located.

**WA** — No. Chang at home, but Bathilda and Tonks have no relevant contacts.

---

## Choice of Law (COL)

Under *Klaxon*, a federal court sitting in diversity will apply the HCOL analysis of the state in which it sits.

For 1st Restatement states, *lex loci delicti* will mean applying CA law to the breach claim where final act of breach was made, which also equals the final act for the negligent contract interference claim against Chang.

For 2nd Restatement states, CA law will probably also apply to the breach of contract claim (place where corn delivered); unless we say that the center of the parties' relationship is OR where the business began and where the bridge blockage occurred that caused the damage — when including Chang in the claim, this center makes more sense than CA. 2nd Restatement will also allow using different law for the Chang claim besides the last act of the breach, so alternatively, court might apply CA law to the breach claim and OR law to the negligence claim against Chang.

**Choice:** OR/WA negligent interference is likely the only successful claim against Chang. No evidence of specific intent to hurt farmers — just didn't follow orders and take them adequately into account. So Florean will want to choose a jurisdiction that will apply OR/WA law to that claim in case his claim against Bathilda/Tonks is not successful. DE seems like the best choice because it definitely has jurisdiction over all defendants and is a 2nd Restatement state. It will apply the same substantive law to the breach claim because all relevant states have this same law. Regarding the tort claim against Chang, likely the relationship of the two parties for the interference claim is centered in OR, OR substantive law will be applied, and Florean will only have to prove negligence, rather than specific intent. OR has interest in regulating the legality of conduct that occurred in OR, and OR has interest in protecting OR resident (*Babcock*).

CA requires intentionality for a tortious interference claim, and the facts probably do not support the theory that Chang intentionally interfered with the Florean-Bathilda/Tonks contracts. Edgecombe showed negligent indifference to the damage she could cause farmers by not scheduling the bridge repairs in the winter at a time with no harvest, but did not specifically intend to cause the harm. If CA law applies, Florean almost certainly loses. Best chance is DE court applying OR law.
