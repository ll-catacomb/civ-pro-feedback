# Assignment 2: Model Answer

There is diversity SMJ (Slughorn is a citizen in NC and DE, Honeydukes in SC, and amount in controversy exceeds $75K). There is venue because a substantial portion of events are centered in NC. There are four remaining arguments Honeydukes can make against Slughorn's lawsuit and seizure of the Felix Felicis (FF).

## 1) NC Court Has No IPJ

First, IPJ requires a long-arm statute and a constitutional basis. NC's long arm statute goes to the limits of the Constitution, and under 4(k)(1)(a), the NC federal court can piggyback on that state statute.

Second, IPJ requires either general or specific jurisdiction. Honeydukes can argue there is no general jurisdiction because it does not do business in NC, and there was no tag (*Pennoyer* and *Burns*). Moreover, Honeydukes does not have minimal contact with NC to establish specific jurisdiction (*Int'l Shoe* and *Shaffer*). Looking to the contacts among the defendant, forum and nature of the litigation, Honeydukes can argue that it did not purposefully avail itself to NC law because it was not even aware that Slughorn was in NC (*McGee*). While Honeydukes wired money to a bank in NC, that is likely insufficient to establish minimum contacts. This is analogous to *Asahi*, where simply putting a product, or in this case money, into a "stream of commerce," or in this case a bank, does not satisfy purposeful availment to that state. Moreover, much like the company in *McIntyre*, Honeydukes did not target any action towards NC. Even under a loosely interpreted effects test, Honeydukes can argue that unlike *Calder*, they did not know effects would have been in NC.

## 2) NC Violated Procedural Due Process

Honeydukes can argue that it did not have an opportunity to be heard before its property was taken away. Because there is no alternative process to compare, *Matthews* is less helpful here. Instead, we look to *Vitek* and the Restatement of Judgments (2d) § 83[^1] to compare whether the NC scheme is constitutionally viable.

While the NC scheme had some safeguards such as sworn affidavits, an independent arbitrator, a specific complaint, a detailed brief, and evidentiary requirements, these are unlikely to overcome the inability for Honeydukes to call witnesses and answer questions at the hearing (*Vitek*). First, there is no justification for why the FF had to be seized so urgently. There was no need, presented by Slughorn or the state, to immediately seize the potion. Second, while the nature of proof is document-based (a contract), the dispute about product quality likely requires more witness testimony and information from the other side. Lastly, because the potion is old property, the lack of a bond requirement further supports a due process violation. Slughorn was not required to post a bond, and Honeydukes was never given an opportunity to post a counterbond to regain possession of the potion.

## 3) The Federal Court Incorrectly Applied State Law (*Erie*)

Honeydukes can argue that the NC court should have used federal law instead of NCRCP 65. Instead, they should have applied FRCP 65 governing preliminary injunctions (or some other analogous pretrial seizure device) because the seizure of the potion was a procedural issue.

Under the *York* test, a court should use state law if the issue would affect the outcome of the case. Slughorn would have been unlikely to seize FF with a preliminary injunction. Not only would he have a low likelihood of success given the lack of IPJ, there is no irreparable harm (the potion wouldn't be destroyed in manufacturing and damages later is an adequate remedy). Furthermore, in balancing the equities, Honeydukes is likely harmed more because they need the potion to survive. Lastly, the court could decide that the possibility of a company taking back a critical component of manufacturing is adverse to the public interest. Because Slughorn would have had a different outcome under the federal rule, *York* would say to use state law.

However, the Harlan concurrence in *Hanna* test dictates the use of federal law. If a rule affects primary conduct, then it is a substantive issue. However, in this case, the usage of state or federal law would be unlikely to factor into Slughorn and Honeyduke's actions as they were negotiating the contract. Therefore, it does not affect people's pre-litigation actions and is a procedural issue. The Harlan concurrence in *Hanna* test would apply federal law.

The Hanna-Stewart test agrees. First, there is a valid and applicable federal rule of civil procedure, FRCP 65. Because FRCP 65 is governed by the Rules Enabling Act, it is valid, and it does not abridge on any substantive state right. Moreover, it is applicable because it is a pretrial method of ordering a certain action. Because a valid and applicable federal rule exists, the Hanna-Stewart test deems the use of federal law.

## 4) NC Federal Court Did Not Have Jurisdiction Over the FF to Seize It

Honeydukes can argue that the seizure of the FF is analogous to a Quasi in rem case where the potion was seized to be potentially used as later relief after the plaintiff proves his case. However, in such seizures, the issuing court can only seize property that is physically present within its jurisdiction. The FF was in SC at the time of the writ's issuance, and therefore, the NC federal court did not have jurisdiction to seize it.
