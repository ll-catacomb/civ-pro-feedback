## CivPro Assignment 2 — Sample Answer

The federal court in Virginia will likely dismiss the suit as time-barred. The court must consider which law applies — an inquiry with both a vertical component (between the federal statute and state law) and a horizontal component (between South Carolina and Virginia state law). The only limitations period that would allow the suit to proceed is South Carolina's. Because its four-year limitations period will likely not apply — as per the analysis below — the suit will likely be dismissed.

### Jurisdiction and Venue

The suit was originally filed in South Carolina federal court. We begin by asking whether jurisdiction is proper. Subject matter jurisdiction is, given that the parties are diverse and the amount in controversy exceeds the statutory minimum (1332). There is personal jurisdiction on two grounds: general personal jurisdiction, because of the ubiquity of Megashop stores in the state, and specific personal jurisdiction, because by selling South Carolina-related tourist memorabilia the company evinced an intent to avail itself of the South Carolina market (*Int'l Shoe*, *McGee*).[^1] Under 4(k)(1)(a), the federal court piggybacks on the long-arm statute of the state in which it sits, and South Carolina's personal jurisdiction statute goes to the full extent of the constitution. Venue is proper for two reasons. First, the venue inquiry collapses to the jurisdictional inquiry (1391(c)), and we established above that the defendant is subject to personal jurisdiction in South Carolina. Second, a transfer is always permitted when consented to by both parties (1404(a)), which is the case here.

### Horizontal Choice of Law

Given that jurisdiction in South Carolina is proper, the federal court in Virginia will use South Carolina's horizontal choice of law principles. Because statutes of limitations are procedural for horizontal choice of law purposes, if Virginia applies any state limitations period (as opposed to the federal one) it will use South Carolina's limitations period (*Ferens*).

### Vertical Choice of Law

There is now a collision between federal law (a two year limitations period under which the suit would be time-barred) and the applicable state law (South Carolina's, in which the limitations period is four years and the suit could continue). To determine whether to apply the state or federal limitations period, we turn to our three *Erie* tests.[^2]

**York.** Under *York*, the difference between state and federal law is clearly outcome determinative, since the suit lives under state law but not under federal law. As a result, we should use the **state limitations period**.

It bears mention that the facts in this hypothetical are extremely similar to those in *York*, but with one key difference: in *York* there was no federal limitations period, so the choice was between no limitations period and the state limitations period, whereas here the choice is between two different limitations periods. It remains unclear whether the *York* court would have reached a different conclusion if given the facts in our hypothetical — the answer depends on which of the different rationales for *Erie* was driving the *York* court's decision. If the *Erie* doctrine rests on federalism, then Congress has no power to declare substantive rules in certain areas, and this area (statutes of limitations on tort actions) is a traditional state law domain with which Congress should not interfere.[^3] If *Erie* was a separation of powers decision, concerned predominately with federal courts overstepping their bounds, this case does not raise concerns, given that there is a statute (passed by Congress and signed by the president) setting out the federal statute of limitations. Closely related is the prudential rationale for *Erie*, which maintains courts should not make federal general common law without congressional consent. It similarly does not apply here because we are dealing with a statute rather than common law. Finally, if the *Erie* doctrine rests on Equal Protection (fear of discrimination against in-state plaintiffs, see *Swift*), it is irrelevant here because the parties are diverse, thereby obviating the potential for the discrimination that existed in *Swift*.

**Hanna majority.** Under Step 1 of the *Hanna* majority's test, the federal limitations period is valid and applicable. It is valid because Congress has the power to create lower federal courts (Art. III, Sect. 2), which includes the power to regulate the goings-on in those courts (Rules Enabling Act, Art. I, Sect. 8, cl. 18). It is applicable because it speaks directly to the case at hand — we can't really read the statute so as to not apply here. As a result, the Supremacy Clause dictates that the **federal limitations period** applies, and the case is dismissed.[^4]

**Harlan's concurrence.** The same result occurs under the test that Justice Harlan sets out in his *Hanna* concurrence. Differing limitations periods almost certainly won't affect parties' primary conduct, so there's no need to try to reconcile state and federal limitations periods. Instead, the **federal limitations period** applies, and the case is dismissed.

[^1]: Even though this particular lamp was sold in NC, not SC, the presence of SC images, and our assumption that Megashop stores in SC carried the same product, indicates that the company purposefully availed itself of the SC market.
[^2]: NB: Later in the semester you will learn a fourth *Erie* test, but for now there are only three.
[^3]: Given the expansion of federal power in the decades since *Erie*, this rationale is likely dead.
[^4]: NB: Given that the issue is resolved in Step 1, there is no need to do a Step 2 analysis.
