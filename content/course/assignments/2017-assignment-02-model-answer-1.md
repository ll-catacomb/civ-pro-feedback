## Assignment 2 — Civil Procedure 2, 2017

### Question 1(a): Transfer Motion — QQS v. SSS

The transfer motion should be granted. This case is properly in federal court because there is complete diversity between the parties and there is only one claim, with damages requested above the $75,000 amount-in-controversy requirement.

Pursuant to the Supreme Court's opinion in *Stewart*, a federal court looks to the transfer statutes to decide the transfer motion despite a state prohibition on forum selection clauses — i.e., under an *Erie* analysis, the transfer statutes are valid and applicable. In doing a transfer statute analysis under *Atlantic Marine*, no weight is given to private party interests when there is a forum selection clause, since the plaintiff already expressed a private interest in the transferee forum by signing the contract. No strong public policy considerations weigh against the transfer; it is a dispute between two private parties based on a contract negotiated only with one another. The court should approve the transfer.

### Question 1(b): Damages Cap After Transfer

QQS's recovery will probably be limited to $100,000.00. Under *Atlantic Marine*, after transferring to a location selected by a forum selection clause, we conduct the HCOL analysis from the perspective of the transferee court (here, SDNY in New York). The HCOL rules of New York apply (*Ferens*). According to the prompt, the validity of recovery-limiting clauses is procedural for HCOL purposes, so it does not matter which restatement New York uses or which state's substantive laws will ultimately govern the merits; New York's procedural law governs this issue. New York law and federal common law both enforce contract clauses limiting damages. Therefore, the court will likely apply New York law and enforce the clause limiting damages. QQS's recovery will probably be limited to $100,000.00.

### Question 2: Forum Non Conveniens — QQS v. HH

There is no Supreme Court case directly on point, so we do an *Erie* analysis to decide whether to follow state law or federal law. Forum non conveniens is a federal common law doctrine, so under *Hanna* (any version), we consider the twin aims of *Erie*.

Enforcing state law might encourage plaintiffs who have signed forum selection clauses to file in states that do not enforce them, but applying federal law would encourage parties to choose a federal forum simply because it will override state law. This suggests using state law. Moreover, since contracts signed in Alabama would be subject to different laws depending on whether the case is in state or federal court, there would be inequitable administration of laws if federal law is applied — which again suggests using state law under the *Hanna* majority test.

Under *Harlan in Hanna*, the court asks whether the rule affects parties' primary conduct — i.e., their pre-litigation behavior. Prior to litigation, parties might choose in which state to negotiate and sign their contracts hoping that certain clauses are or are not enforced. This suggests the rule is substantive, pointing toward state law.

Under the *York* test, this affects the outcome of whether the motion would be approved, and since granting a dismissal might mean the case is filed in Ontario — which might have different procedures and laws — it could affect the ultimate outcome. Under *York*'s permissive test, this is probably substantive, suggesting state law.

The court should apply Alabama law and find the forum selection clause invalid for forum non conveniens purposes. With the forum selection clause invalid, there is little reason to dismiss the case. The other FNC factors do not weigh in favor of dismissal; QQS filed in Alabama, has witnesses in Alabama, and the contract was negotiated in Alabama. This suit should remain in the Alabama federal court. If the court determines that the issue of forum selection clauses in a forum non conveniens motion is procedural for VCOL purposes, then under federal common law it should enforce the forum selection clause and dismiss the case on forum non conveniens grounds.
