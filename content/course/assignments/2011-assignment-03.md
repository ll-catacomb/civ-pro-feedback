## Question

### Statutory Background

Assume the following background: Invoking its powers under U.S. Const. amends. xiv–xv, on January 2, 2006, Congress passed the Election Improvement Act of 2006 (the "EIA"), Title 1 of which created the United States Election Agency (the "USEA"). Congress designed the USEA to administer certain issues relating to voting rights. Relevant portions of Title 1 of the EIA read as follows:

---

**Section 2: Remedies**

If the USEA finds a violation of state or federal election law, or that some person or entity has breached a contract or committed a tort relating to elections, the agency may direct the responsible person or entity to provide such monetary relief to anyone damaged thereby. The USEA may also order appropriate equitable relief.

---

**Section 3: Procedures**

The USEA shall adjudicate all disputes with the utmost expedition. All decisions will be made by USEA administrative law judges. In keeping with this directive regarding speed, the USEA shall schedule and hold any evidentiary hearing (trial) within 20 days of a complaint being filed. In addition, the USEA may consider any other submissions of the parties it deems appropriate, including affidavits, documents, and other exhibits. No form of discovery shall, however, be permitted in USEA adjudications, nor shall the USEA have the power to compel persons who would rather not do so to testify before it. A plaintiff initiating a proceeding within the USEA shall serve a summons and complaint upon the defendant by mailing via regular mail to the defendant's last known address, which mailing must be posted within three days of the initiation of the complaint.

<!-- page: 3 -->

**Section 4: Review of a USEA Decision**

Any person aggrieved by a USEA decision or order may appeal that order to the United States Court of Appeals of the circuit with geographic jurisdiction over the area in which the events leading to the proceeding took place. The Court of Appeals' review shall be limited to the procedures used to adjudicate the USEA decision. The Court of Appeals may not review whether the evidence the USEA considered was sufficient to support the USEA's decision.

---

### Facts

In the November 2, 2010 election, voters in various municipalities around the State of Massachusetts complained of long waits at polling booths. In a November 8, 2010 interview, published on The Boston Globe's website, Secretary of State William Galvin claimed that his office was not responsible for any delays that might have occurred. According to Secretary Galvin, any problems that might have existed were the fault of the Charles Townsend Printing Services, Inc., the printing company the Secretary's office had hired to print the Massachusetts paper ballots. Charles Townsend is incorporated in Massachusetts, where it also has its principal place of business. In the Boston Globe interview, Secretary Galvin stated that under the contract, the decision of how many ballots to print was left to Charles Townsend's discretion. Originally, Charles Townsend announced that it would print 3,100,000 ballots. According to Secretary Galvin, one month before the election, he had sent Charles Townsend a letter stating that 3,100,000 ballots would not be enough, and urging the company to print 4,000,000. In fact, according to Secretary Galvin, Charles Townsend had printed only 3,100,000 ballots, despite early indications that the turnout on Election Day would be high. Galvin told the Boston Globe reporter that this decision was "rank negligence."

On November 15, five citizens of the City of Boston, each an associate of the prestigious Boston law firm of Garrett, Monroe, & Duncan, LLC, filed a complaint in the USEA against Charles Townsend. In their complaint, the "Boston Five," as these five plaintiffs became known in the media, alleged that they had arrived at Boston Polling Place 157 at 9 am, but had had to wait until 5 pm to cast their votes. Each alleged that as a result, they had been unable to fulfill their primary function for their law firm, reviewing documents, on that day. As a result, the law firm had charged them a vacation day. Each associate sought $900.00 in damages, as well as an injunction requiring Charles Townsend to take out a newspaper advertisement apologizing for the fact that its negligence had caused inconvenience to voters. The Boston Five's complaint made clear that they were relying on a negligence theory (a common law tort tried to the King's Bench in the olden days). The USEA immediately issued a scheduling order setting the trial for 9 am on December 3, 2010, and stating that it would issue its final decision as soon as possible thereafter.

On November 16, the Boston Five placed a copy of their complaint, a USEA summons, and the USEA's scheduling order in the mail addressed to Charles Townsend, Inc., Number Four Privet Drive, Boston, MA. The address came from the (book) version of the 2010 Yellow Pages found in the offices of Garrett, Monroe, & Duncan. On November 20, one of the Boston Five called the number listed underneath the address in the 2010 Yellow Pages for Charles Townsend to discuss possible settlement; the call did not go through because the number had been disconnected.

<!-- page: 4 -->

On December 3, the USEA convened its trial on the Boston Five complaint, at 9 am. At 9:30 am, a harried-looking lawyer named Josephina Bosley, from Charles Townsend's office of general counsel, arrived. Bosley stated that the first she or anyone else at Charles Townsend had heard about the USEA proceeding was when she read an article about the trial that morning in the online edition of The Boston Globe. Bosley explained that in March of 2010, Charles Townsend had moved its office location and, correspondingly, changed its telephone number. The company's new address and telephone number had been available on the company's website since mid-April, 2010. Bosley requested a 30-day delay in the trial to allow her to prepare her company's defense. She also demanded a trial by jury. Finally, she objected to all aspects of the proceedings on due process grounds. The USEA administrative law judge denied the requests, stating that he had no ability under Section 3 of Title 1 of the USEA (quoted above) to grant any of Bosley's motions.

The trial commenced. Each of the Boston Five testified that he/she had lost a day's work waiting to vote. Over Bosley's objection, a copy of the Boston Globe article in which Secretary Galvin had stated that the election day delays were due to Charles Townsend's failure to print enough ballots was admitted into evidence. Although Bosley cross-examined the Boston Five, she was unable to shake their testimony. Bosley moved the judge to issue an order requiring Secretary Galvin to testify before the judge in person; the judge denied the motion, citing Title 1, Section 3 of the USEA (quoted above). Bosley sought an order requiring the Boston Five to produce a copy of the letter Secretary Galvin had allegedly sent to Charles Townsend requesting that more ballots be printed; that order was denied. Bosley renewed her previous objections and motions; the objections were overruled, and the motions denied. After that, Bosley introduced no evidence and called no witnesses.

At the close of the evidence, the USEA administrative law judge ordered a 90-minute recess. At the end of the 90 minutes, the administrative law judge stated that he saw no reason to deliberate any further, and handed out a ruling that he had written during the recess. The opinion found for the Boston Five, ordered Charles Townsend to pay each of them $900.00, and issued an injunction requiring Charles Townsend to take out an advertisement in The Boston Globe apologizing for its negligent conduct and the inconvenience it had caused to all voters.
