## Background

Assume the following background: Invoking its powers under U.S. Const. amends. xiv-xv, on January 2, 2006, Congress passed the Election Improvement Act of 2006 (the "EIA"), Section 1 of which created the United States Election Agency (the "USEA"). Congress designed the USEA to administer certain issues relating to voting rights. Relevant portions of the EIA read as follows:

**Section 2: Remedies**
If the USEA finds a violation of federal election law, the agency shall order anyone responsible, including a state or local governmental entity, to take such action as the USEA deems necessary to prevent future violations of law. In addition, the USEA may direct the responsible person or entity to provide such monetary relief as the USEA shall, in its discretion, deem appropriate.

**Section 3: Review**
Any party aggrieved by a decision of the USEA may seek a declaration of its invalidity by suing the agency in the federal district court having jurisdiction over the situs of the events giving rise to the controversy. The district court shall review all matters of law de novo, but it shall hear no new evidence to decide the dispute, and shall upset agency findings of fact only if such are clearly erroneous.

**Section 4: Procedures**
The USEA shall adjudicate all disputes with the utmost expedition. In keeping with the extraordinary requirement of speedy resolution of election disputes, the USEA shall make its findings of fact and conclusions of law based on a written record submitted by the parties. The record may include affidavits, authenticated documents and other exhibits, interrogatory responses, and deposition transcripts. In no event, however, shall the USEA hear live testimony, nor shall the USEA allow any action filed within it to expand beyond the parties and claims in the original complaint. In all other matters, the USEA may use such procedures as it deems appropriate, including any principles or rules used in federal courts, such as the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

**Section 5: Option To Bypass the Agency**
A person injured by a violation of federal election law may file an action in the USEA against any person or entity responsible, as provided in Sections 1-4. In the alternative, the injured person may seek redress by filing an action in federal district court without first proceeding to the USEA. In the latter event, the court shall have the remedial powers articulated in Section 2, above.

---

In the November 7, 2006 election, voters in various municipalities around the State of Massachusetts complained of long waits at polling booths. In a November 8, 2006 interview with The Boston Globe, Secretary of State William Galvin claimed that his office was not responsible for any delays that might have occurred. According to Secretary Galvin, any problems that might have existed (and the Secretary did not admit anything had gone amiss) were the fault of the Charles Townsend Printing Services, Inc., the printing company the Secretary's office had hired to print the Massachusetts paper ballots. Secretary Galvin stated that he had originally ordered 3,100,000 paper ballots to be printed, but that one month before the election, he had orally increased the order to 4,000,000. In fact, according to Secretary Galvin, Charles Townsend had provided only 3,100,000 ballots. Finally, Secretary Galvin told the Globe reporter that he had already taken steps to reduce the future waiting times, steps including hiring additional poll workers, instituting additional polling stations, and expanding the time during which each polling station would remain open.

On November 14, five citizens of the City of Boston, each an associate of the prestigious Boston law firm of Garrett, Monroe, & Duncan, LLC, filed an action in the USEA against Secretary Galvin (in his official capacity) and Charles Townsend. In subsequent affidavits and deposition testimony, the "Boston Five," as they became known, stated that they had arrived at Polling Place 157 at 9 am, but had had to wait until 5 pm to cast their votes. The Boston Five further averred and testified that because they had been absent from work the whole of November 7, Garrett, Monroe, & Duncan had charged each of them a half day against allotted vacation leave time. As calculated from their salaries, the value of a full day of vacation was $500.00. The Boston Five also submitted a copy of The Boston Globe article containing Secretary Galvin's interview, but provided no other evidence. In terms of relief, the Boston Five requested that the USEA issue an order against Secretary Galvin and Charles Townsend requiring them (i) to assure that no polling place delays occurred in future elections, and (ii) to pay the Boston Five the value of their docked vacation time.

After receiving notice of the action, Secretary of State Galvin filed an answer denying the Boston Five's allegations. The only evidence he submitted, however, was an affidavit from a member of his staff, which stated that she had personally observed operations at Polling Station 157 from 8 am to 6 pm and had seen waits of "30 minutes to two hours but no longer." The Secretary refused to make himself or any members of his staff available for deposition.

After receiving notice of the action, Charles Townsend Printing Services, Inc. filed an answer denying the Boston Five's allegations, demanded a jury trial, and moved for an order compelling the Secretary of State to give a deposition. It further provided affidavits and deposition testimony to the effect that it had received a contract to print 3,100,000 ballots for the 2006 election, that it had complied with that contact, and that it had never received any modification request (oral or written) from Secretary Galvin.

The USEA struck the jury trial demand and denied the motion to compel Secretary Galvin's testimony. On the merits, the USEA found that Charles Townsend had failed to comply with a modified contract to print 4,000,000 ballots, and that this failure had caused the Boston Five to have to wait for eight hours to vote. The USEA held that this delay constituted an undue burden on the constitutionally protected right to vote. The USEA further found that Secretary Galvin was implementing steps to prevent delays on future election days, so it denied the Boston Five's request for an order against future delays. It did, however, order Charles Townsend to pay the Boston Five $250 each as compensation for their docked vacation time. The USEA issued a final judgment to this effect on January 3, 2007. Charles Townsend immediately announced that it would shortly file an action in the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts challenging the USEA's decision.

---

The date is January 5, 2007. You are sole practitioner with an office in Watertown, MA. Two voters there heard about the Boston Five's victory in the USEA. They tell you that they experienced substantial delays when attempting to vote in the November, 2006 election. One voter, a father, says that he had to pay for after-school day care services so as to stay in line at his polling place. The second voter says that her employer docked vacation time from her because of her long absence from work as she waited to vote. Believing that any dispute is likely to end up in federal district court eventually, the two voters want to sue Charles Townsend in district court under Section 5 of the EIA. Accordingly, they voters ask you to address two questions:

1. If they (the two Watertown voters) sued Charles Townsend in federal district court seeking compensation for their losses, what use (if any) could they make of the USEA Boston Five decision?

2. Is Charles Townsend likely to succeed in its own federal district court action against the USEA seeking to overturn the Boston Five decision?
