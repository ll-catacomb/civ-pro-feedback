## Assignment 1 — Civil Procedure 2, 2017

### Question 1: Remand

The federal judge should not grant the motion to remand.

While Hermione filed for removal more than 30 days after the removable event, Malfoy filed for remand more than 30 days after Hermione's removal motion. As a result of Malfoy's late remand filing, the judge can only remand for lack of SMJ, and SMJ is most likely satisfied because the parties are completely diverse (*Strawbridge*, § 1332). Hermione has moved around a lot, and while she does not intend to return to New Jersey, she has not lived anywhere else with an intent to stay (*Mas*). She does not intend to stay in Oregon. So Hermione is a New Jersey domiciliary, Malfoy is a citizen of Delaware and Oregon, and the AIC ($135k) is sufficient. If Malfoy had filed for remand on time (within 30 days of removal), then the judge should grant the motion because Hermione breached the 30-day window and is a New Jersey resident.

### Question 2: Transfer

The judge should grant the transfer motion.

Hermione's transfer motion can only be granted if the lawsuit could have been brought in the District of Oregon at the time of the original filing (*Hoffman*). Suit could have been filed in Oregon: SMJ via diversity (see Question 1); venue is proper per § 1391(b)(2) because the car was purchased and the accident occurred in Oregon; and Oregon can exercise specific IPJ (FRCP 4(k)(1)(A)) because of purposeful availment and minimum contacts (*International Shoe*).

Public and private interest factors support the transfer. The NJ federal court would use NJ state choice-of-law rules (*Klaxon*). The accident happened in Oregon and the contract was signed in Oregon (the blood is there), so the HCOL analysis would require NJ to use Oregon substantive laws. It is better to have an Oregon court apply Oregon state laws. There is also a public interest in allowing Oregon jurors to assess the reasonableness of the warranty promise in the contract, to enforce Oregon state laws, and to evaluate the testimony of Oregon witnesses. Oregon has a greater interest in the dispute (Malfoy's customers are there). Private interest factors support the transfer as well. The transfer is not inconvenient to Malfoy (it chose NJ but has its PPOB in OR) and Oregon is Hermione's home state. Evidence is in Oregon, witnesses are in Oregon, and both parties own property in Oregon so the judgment can be enforced.

### Question 3: Supplemental Jurisdiction over Farley

Based on the cases and statutes we have read, a federal district court should not have subject matter jurisdiction over Farley's claim, joined with Hermione's, against Malfoy.

<!-- page: 2 -->

Farley brought state law claims, so diversity is the only source of SMJ. Farley and Malfoy are diverse parties (Farley = NJ, Malfoy = DE & OR, see Question 1), but Farley's claim does not have a sufficient AIC (only $50k). While the Constitution allows for minimal diversity and imposes no AIC requirement, § 1332 requires complete diversity (*Strawbridge*). It would make little sense to strictly require complete diversity — based on state citizenship — but allow one party to have insufficient AIC. A judicial system that allowed a federal district court to have SMJ over Farley's claim could incentivize gamesmanship. For example, a group of plaintiffs with small claims would be incentivized to find one plaintiff with one claim over $75k, just to get into federal court. The text of § 1332 supports that the federal court should not have SMJ: § 1332 requires that the "matter in controversy" exceed $75k and Farley's claim does not.

The text of § 1367 and judicial efficiency, however, do support that the court could hear claims brought by Farley and Hermione together. For efficiency, hearing the two claims together would require just one trial, one judge, one time that witnesses are in court, one jury, etc. And § 1367 states that the district courts have jurisdiction over related claims — here the claims brought by Farley and Hermione derive from the same contract and the same car accident.

However, efficiency is not an end in itself. Given that diversity jurisdiction is less relevant today than it was pre-Civil War (when we really worried about judicial bias against out-of-state parties), the FRCP should be read to limit — rather than expand — diversity jurisdiction.
