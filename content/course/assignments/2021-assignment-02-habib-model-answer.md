## 1

Cannot dismiss based on lack of SMJ. Canadian plaintiff and ND defendant mean complete diversity, and $300,000 AIC satisfy diversity jurisdiction requirements. IPJ exists because defendant corporation is a citizen of ND. Venue is proper because IPJ exists (§ 1391(c)(2)).

### FNC

Defendant can move to dismiss under FNC.

Under *York*, court will use ND FNC rule and dismiss because the choice is outcome determinative. Under ND FNC, because events of the lawsuit occurred outside ND (shipment from SD, imbibing in Canada, harm in MT), the case will be dismissed. Under Federal Common Law FNC analysis, court may or may not dismiss, depending on its analysis of the private and public interests at stake, but there is no obviously superior forum.

Under *Hanna* majority (Scalia or Ginsburg), because law at issue is FCL, Court will apply twin aims of *Erie*. Difference in law may encourage forum shopping, as foreign plaintiffs looking to sue in ND will bring claims in federal court if they can meet the AIC requirement to avoid dismissal. Difference in law would cause inequitable administration of laws, as foreign plaintiffs who can't meet AIC cannot avail themselves of a forum in ND. Court likely to use ND FNC law, dismiss case.

Under Harlan/*Hanna*, court assesses whether applying federal law would alter primary conduct of the parties. Applying Federal FNC to ND corporations opens them up to suits from foreign plaintiffs, which could dissuade corporations who ship overseas from incorporating in ND, altering primary conduct. Court likely to dismiss under ND FNC.

### Pleading

Defendant can move to dismiss under ND statute because plaintiff failed to attach medical bills. ND statute clashes with FRCP 8(a)(2), which only requires short plain statement of the claim.

Under *York*, Court will use ND statute because choice is outcome determinative. ND statute mandates dismissal, while FRCP allows the case to proceed.

Under *Hanna*/Scalia, Court will only use FRCP if it is both valid and applicable. FRCP is valid if it doesn't abridge, enlarge, or modify a state substantive right, which it never does. The FRCP is valid because it "really regulates procedure" (*Sibbach*). Though narrow construction is possible (*Walker*), Scalia likely to find FRCP applicable, and use FRCP. Case proceeds.

Under *Shady Grove*/Ginsburg, Court likely to interpret FRCP narrowly to as not to apply and thus not clash with state law, arguing FRCP only sets a floor for pleading requirements, states can require more. State law can apply, case dismissed.

Under Harlan/*Hanna*, Court is likely to find pleading rules would not alter primary conduct of litigants, because filing procedure would concern lawyers. Court would use federal law, and case would proceed.

### SoL

Defendant can move to dismiss under ND 2-year SoL, arguing that harm occurred 26 months before lawsuit was filed, with imbibing of medicine constituting the action by the defendant alleged to have caused injury. Conflicts with Federal law, which would start limitations period with plaintiff's onset of illness, occurring 22 months before filing.

Under *York*, choice of law is outcome determinative. Court would use ND law and dismiss.

Under *Hanna*/Scalia, court will use Federal statute if valid and applicable. Valid given constitutional grant of power to Congress to create lower courts and thus to administer their procedure. Applicable because the statute speaks directly to the SoL issue, and court is unlikely to construe Congressional statute narrowly. Federal law prevails, case proceeds.

Under *Shady Grove*/Ginsburg, it is hard to see how a court could construe this Congressional statute narrowly, and it would be reluctant to do so with a statute (unlike it would a FRCP). Federal law will govern under supremacy clause, case proceeds.

Under Harlan/*Hanna*, when SoL begins running won't alter primary conduct of parties, as it's largely a procedural concern for lawyers. Federal law applies, case proceeds.

## 2

Each basis relies on a different level of law. The FNC issue is FCL, the pleading issue concerns FRCP, and the SoL issue concerns a statute, each warranting a different level of deference and scrutiny under the "valid and applicable" assessment from *Hanna*.

## 3

Varying combinations of the state statute on the length of a SoL and the federal statute on when the SoL period begins to run can produce an incoherent system of law. For example, given facts analogous to those present, a plaintiff could bring a personal injury claim for injuries sustained after consuming expired medicine 10 years after the purchase and/or production of the medicine, despite having a 2-year SoL. This could undermine the legislative intent behind the state 2-year SoL. Solution is to use only state initiation doctrine with the state SoLs.

## 4

Under Harlan/*Hanna* and Harlan/*Scalia* analyses of the pleading rules, court would allow case to proceed despite failure of plaintiff to attach medical bills to complaint. This undermines some justifications of *Erie*: it overrides the ND state legislature's decision to require medical bills at the filing stage of a personal injury suit (federalism), and also puts in-state plaintiffs at a disadvantage, because they cannot avail themselves of more lenient Federal pleading rules (equal protection). Diverse plaintiffs' ability to do so may in turn encourage forum shopping. Conversely, this outcome is in line with *Erie* doctrine's interest in preventing the creation of FCL
