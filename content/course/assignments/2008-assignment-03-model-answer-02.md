# Assignment 3 – Model Answer 02

## Question 1: Federal Subject Matter Jurisdiction

Yes, there is arising-under subject matter jurisdiction over Townsend's lawsuit. For a court to find subject matter jurisdiction in a declaratory judgment, the party who would file the "coercive action" under the facts of the case must have subject matter jurisdiction; a defense under federal law is insufficient. Because the Boston Five could have sued in a federal district court under § 4 of the EIA, this creates arising-under jurisdiction. Therefore, Townsend can sue for declaratory judgment in federal court.

## Question 2: Appellate Jurisdiction

Yes, there is probably appellate jurisdiction pursuant to the collateral order doctrine. The district court's order to stay the litigation because of a parallel proceeding is similar to that in *Colorado River*. These stays are appealable under the *Cohen* exception to interlocutory appeals. The collateral order doctrine requires that for an order to be appealable it must: (1) conclusively determine the disputed issue (i.e., be final); (2) resolve an important issue collateral to the merits; and (3) be effectively unreviewable if immediate appeal were not allowed.

Here, (1) the stay would conclusively determine the issue of whether the case will go forward; (2) the stay is collateral to the merits of the case (which is a contract dispute); and (3) the stay is effectively unreviewable on appeal from a final judgment because, assuming the USEA's decision merits preclusive effect, Townsend's federal suit would be dismissed for claim preclusion and so it would not matter to the First Circuit whether the stay was erroneous or not.

Any other basis for appellate jurisdiction would probably fail: (a) a stay is not otherwise a final order (the case is not dismissed or otherwise formally disposed of); (b) the stay could not be appealed under Rule 54 because there is only one claim being made by the parties (whether the order for ballots was increased); (c) there were no findings from the judge to trigger § 1292(b) (the judge did not say that the order involves a controlling question of law, or that there is substantial ground for difference of opinion, and that immediate appeal of the order may materially advance the ultimate termination of litigation); (d) there was no injunction to trigger § 1292(a)(1); and (e) issuing a writ of mandamus is only appropriate in extraordinary instances and this is not such an instance.

## Question 3: Appropriateness of the Stay

The district court's stay is not appropriate because an eventual dismissal on preclusion grounds in reliance on the USEA proceedings would probably be in error. This case is vastly different from *Colorado River*, where the federal court stayed its proceedings pending the resolution of concurrent state court proceedings involving the same matter; here, the concurrent litigation is taking place in the USEA — a federal agency.

An agency's procedures must be of sufficient quality to merit issue or claim preclusion, and the USEA's procedures appear insufficient. Two of the purposes of the doctrine of res judicata are to prevent the waste of scarce judicial resources and courts reaching inconsistent results, but these benefits do not take precedence over giving litigants a fair trial with procedural protections. To balance these competing interests, the Second Restatement of Judgments states that agency determinations are given preclusive effect only if the proceedings entail essential adjudication elements including: (1) adequate notice; (2) the right to present evidence and legal arguments, and to rebut evidence and arguments; (3) formulation of issues of law and fact; (4) a rule of finality; and (5) other procedural safeguards. These factors are necessary to ensure that preclusive effect is given only to agency determinations that mirror the due process safeguards available in federal litigation.

In this case, the USEA is resolving a factual dispute over whether the order for ballots was increased, based solely on a newspaper article and a written record without any live testimony to assess the parties' relative credibility. Moreover, the USEA may not compel testimony of any witness, no matter how critical to the litigation, nor does the USEA allow appeals of its decisions. Because of the USEA's stark procedural inadequacies, the district court should not give preclusive effect to the agency's findings; therefore, a stay pending the litigation of the parallel suit would be inappropriate.
