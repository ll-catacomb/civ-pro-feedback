## Assignment 2 — Civil Procedure 2, 2016

### Question 1: Motion to Dismiss

The EDVA is a federal court sitting in Virginia, a diversity case. Under *Klaxon Co. v. Stentor*, federal courts in diversity apply the choice of law rules of the state in which they sit. Virginia applies the First Restatement. Under the First Restatement, tort claims are governed by the law of the place where the wrong occurred — i.e., "where the blood is" (*Alabama Great Southern RR v. Carroll*). The drone crashed in Roanoke, Virginia, so Virginia law governs all three counts.

Virginia requires recklessness — intentionally taking an action highly likely to cause injury — for drone crash personal injury claims. West Virginia requires only negligence.

#### Count 1 — Negligent Operation Against Oliver Wood

Count 1 should be dismissed. Virginia law applies, and Virginia requires recklessness for drone crash claims. Negligent operation is legally insufficient under Virginia law regardless of how plausibly the facts are alleged. The court should dismiss Count 1 for failure to state a claim under Rule 12(b)(6). However, the court should grant leave to amend so Kingsley can plead reckless operation if the facts support it. Whether Wood's continuing to operate a drone he had personally reported as defective constitutes recklessness is a question worth exploring in a recklessness-based amendment.

#### Count 2 — Negligent Maintenance Against Chudley Cannons

Count 2 should also be dismissed for the same reason. Virginia requires recklessness; the negligent maintenance count is insufficient as a matter of law under Virginia's standard.

#### Count 3 — Reckless Maintenance Against Chudley Cannons

Count 3 should survive the motion to dismiss. Applying Rule 12(b)(6) under *Twombly/Iqbal*, the court accepts all well-pleaded facts as true and asks whether the claim is plausible. The facts alleged — that the Tornado 360 had a known steering malfunction reported by three previous operators, including Wood himself, and that the Cannons did nothing to fix it — plausibly support recklessness. The Cannons had actual knowledge of a defect likely to cause injury and deliberately failed to address it. This satisfies Virginia's definition of recklessness (intentional action/inaction highly likely to cause injury). Count 3 states a plausible claim.

### Question 2: Motion to Transfer

#### 2(a) — Transfer Analysis

<!-- page: 2 -->

Under 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a), transfer is available to any district where the case could have been brought, for the convenience of parties and witnesses, in the interest of justice. The case could have been brought in SDWV: the Chudley Cannons are incorporated in West Virginia with their principal place of business there, establishing proper venue and personal jurisdiction.

Private interest factors: The Cannons' maintenance records and most witnesses are in West Virginia. The maintenance workshop relevant to the Tornado 360's defect is in Wheeling, WV. Oliver Wood is a West Virginia citizen. These factors favor transfer to SDWV.

Public interest factors: Virginia has an interest in compensating its injured citizen (Kingsley is a Virginia citizen) and in regulating drone activity within its borders. West Virginia has an interest in the conduct of its businesses and citizens. These factors are roughly neutral.

The defendant has the burden of showing that transfer is warranted. Given the location of the maintenance records, the defect history, and most of the witnesses relevant to the maintenance claims, transfer to SDWV is appropriate.

#### 2(b) — Choice of Law After Transfer

Under *Van Dusen v. Barrack*, a § 1404(a) transfer initiated by the defendant does not change the applicable law. The receiving court (SDWV) applies the choice of law rules of the transferring court (EDVA, sitting in Virginia). EDVA applies Virginia's First Restatement. Under the First Restatement, the blood is in Virginia (the crash occurred there), so Virginia substantive law applies. The SDWV would therefore apply Virginia's recklessness standard despite being located in a Second Restatement state.

This result is consistent with the purposes of *Van Dusen*: defendants should not be permitted to use § 1404(a) transfer as a mechanism to obtain more favorable substantive law.
