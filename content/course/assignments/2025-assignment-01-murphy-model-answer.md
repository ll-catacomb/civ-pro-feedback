## SMJ

The court likely lacks SMJ. For a declaratory judgment request, we look at the imaginary lawsuit to determine SMJ. Here, the imaginary lawsuit would be a negligence lawsuit under Colorado state law, with Connor suing Skynet and/or Reese.

**AUJ:** The imaginary lawsuit does not involve a federal cause of action (T-800 does not create a cause of action), and likely does not raise an implied federal question per *Smith-Grable*. In particular, the issue is not embedded nor substantial, because negligence *per se* is only one manner of demonstrating Reese's negligence. The dispute is not over federal law, either, but instead over the actual fact of Reese's drunkenness.

The balancing factors point in the same direction: (1) If we allowed this case to be litigated in federal court, many other similar cases could follow, as there are federal rules and regulations controlling many aspects of pilot conduct and airplane safety, any of which might be implicated in a typical negligence claim. (2) If Congress wanted this to be litigated in federal court, it could have created a cause of action within T-800. (3) There is no unique federal interest at stake here beyond a generalized concern about safety.

**DJ:** We begin by examining the citizenship of each party to the lawsuit:

- Reese is domiciled in Arizona. When he relocated there from North Dakota, he purchased a house, and had no date at which he was planning on reconsidering his residence in the state.
- Skynet is a citizen of Mexico (i.e. "outside of America"), as it was incorporated there and has a PPOB there.
- Connor is likely domiciled in Arizona. She was born there, and though she went to college in Colorado, she had plans to relocate elsewhere (pilot's school) and had a definite date of reconsideration (graduation). *Mas*. Hence, she likely is *not* a citizen of Colorado.

Diversity jurisdiction requires complete diversity. *Strawbridge*. Therefore, because Reese and Connor are both citizens of Arizona, the court may not exercise diversity jurisdiction.

The lawsuit is also likely barred due to an insufficient amount-in-controversy ($60,000 is less than $75,000)—though, theoretically, Reese and Skynet could be exposed to more damages than Connor originally contemplated, if she revises her estimated damages for pain and suffering.

## IPJ

<!-- page: 2 -->

Connor may assert that the court cannot exercise personal jurisdiction over her.

**GPJ:** Connor is not a citizen of Colorado, as discussed above. If *Daimler* applies to natural persons, then she may be subject to general jurisdiction as she currently spends a substantial majority of her time in Colorado for college. Otherwise, the court may not exercise personal jurisdiction over her.

**SPJ:** Per FRCP 4(k)(1)(A), we look to the Colorado long-arm statute to determine the permissible jurisdiction for the court to exercise. Colorado's long-arm goes to the limits of the Constitution.

We look to see whether there are sufficient contacts between Colorado, Connor, and the subject matter of the lawsuit such that exercising jurisdiction would not offend traditional notions of fair play and justice. *International Shoe*, *Volkswagen*. Here, there likely are not: The incidents giving rise to the lawsuit occurred entirely within Arizona. Connor's typical residence in Colorado is unrelated to the subject matter of the litigation, and hence cannot qualify as a contact. However, Connor did reserve the trip from Colorado, and the plane's flight path originally would have taken it over Colorado. On balance, however, this likely is not enough: The alleged negligent behavior and the injury occurred entirely outside of Colorado airspace, and a single outbound phone call is less substantial than a single transaction (*McGee*).

In the alternative, it likely would not be fair + reasonable: Though Connor can travel easily to Colorado, plaintiff's convenience is not high as both Reese and Skynet are located elsewhere. Colorado does have an abstract interest in maintaining air safety standards, but since the allegedly unsafe conduct occurred outside of Colorado airspace, that interest is generic insofar as it would justify exercising jurisdiction over any drunken pilot who could have theoretically flown a plane over Colorado. There is (some) evidence located in Colorado: Ventura, the other passenger, has likely returned to her college residence and hence could conveniently testify. On balance, however, this does not cross the threshold for fair and reasonable.

## Statute of Limitations

Federal courts apply the HCOL rules of the state in which they sit. *Klaxon*. Since statutes of limitation are procedural for the purposes of horizontal choice of law, the district court will apply the forum state's law. Therefore, the statute of limitations for lawsuits in Colorado is one year, and if the court is able to establish jurisdiction, it should rule in favor of Reese and Skynet on declaratory judgment.
