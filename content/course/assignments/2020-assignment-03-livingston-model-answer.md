Both WW and Margolis have grounds to challenge the procedures used by the USEA. WW will likely prevail on their jury trial claim. Either party may succeed in a general PDP challenge. Margolis will likely lose her claim for an attorney.

## 1. WW's Right to a Jury Trial

WW was probably entitled to a jury trial, after one was timely requested. Jury trials are only available at law, not equity. Since 7A "preserves" the right to a jury trial, we need historical analysis. *Terry*.

Our first prong of the *Terry* test — the historical analysis — is unclear (especially since neither complaint nor decision specified a COA), but suggests equity. The COA that fits best with the complaint and order is 3rd party breach, because the only reason WW would have a "duty" to Margolis, as required by negligence, would be as a 3rd party beneficiary. (Remember, the ballot contract was "for the benefit of MA voters.")

BUT we know from *Terry* that prong 2 — type of remedy requested — is more important. Though the remedies EIA envisions *sound* equitable, this is still probably a suit at law — Margolis is asking for damages, NOT disgorgement ("give me the money you made from the ballot contract") or specific performance ("print extra ballots").

Even if this suit is "mixed law/equity," we have chucked the clean-up rule, *Terry*, and federal courts favor jury trials. WW likely prevails.

### a) Public Rights Exception Doesn't Apply

This case was heard in an agency, not a federal court. Because there is no historical corollary to the administrative state, we need to test whether the public rights exception allows the agency to avoid sitting a jury. We need an *Atlas Roofing* test!

But because the USEA is not acting as a regulator here (it's not even a party), there's no public rights exception. *Granfinanciera*. Notably, it's not even really adjudicating statutorily created public rights, since it's enforcing garden-variety election law.

## 2. Margolis' Right to an Attorney

A *Mathews* analysis suggests Margolis has no right to an attorney at government expense. To begin, we presume that because Margolis' physical liberty is not at stake, she has no right to an attorney. The law will view her private interests as minimal-to-moderate. This is a deprivation of "property" (her damages), not liberty, nor her means to live, *Goldberg*. *Matthews* suggests property rights carry less weight.

Though WW ostensibly has an interest in fair adversarial processes, they also have an interest in efficient case resolution, weighing against a state-funded attorney for Margolis.

The court will likewise view the risk of erroneous deprivation as acceptable, given the government's interests in conserving already-scarce attorney resources. Margolis did say she couldn't understand the proceedings, and her opponent did have an attorney, but while an attorney would probably have made a positive difference, the actual question of her damages isn't terribly complex, and state resources are already taxed to the max by providing counsel in liberty cases.

## 3. General Insufficiency of Procedural Scheme

When evaluating the general sufficiency of a procedure, *Vitek* provides a guide. While there was an independent decision-maker (not a clerk like in *Sniadach*), several other elements mentioned in *Vitek* are missing, particularly the opportunity to present evidence and question witnesses.

### A) Written Notice

We have little information about the notice WW was given. While it's clear they had actual notice, the agency may not have provided the constitutional *Mullane* minimum.

### B) Opportunity to Present Evidence / Question Witnesses

Here, the parties were "heard" by way of written documents — so this isn't a case with *no* pre-deprivation hearing (like *Doehr*), just one with an imperfect pre-deprivation hearing.

Because this is a fact-intensive case turning on conflicting accounts (Galvin said he requested more ballots, WW said he didn't), rather than a cut-and-dry case of, say, medical evidence, live testimony seems necessary. *Mathews*. Plus, written submissions are likely inadequate, as indicated by both parties' uncertainty about what to include. *Kelly*.

The parties' ability to question the witnesses is also lacking. The ALJ clearly relied on the *Boston Globe* article, and therefore unsworn "testimony" by Galvin, but there was no procedure by which WW could question him. While adjudicating cases "on the papers" increases efficiency and decreases cost, it decreases accuracy, which should outweigh the benefits here.

### C) Written Decision

While the written decision was given, it was vague, and didn't cleanly state the grounds for the decision — making appeal difficult. The written decision here also highlights how badly we need a more robust ability to give evidence — since it said Margolis had not proven her damages and that Margolis had proven the breach of duty by WW, without providing the parties a robust opportunity to contest or supplement either factual question or indicating what evidence it relied on.
