# Assignment 3 Model Answer

## Question 1

No. I support the preserved right to a jury afforded by the Seventh Amendment. Traditionally, suits in law carried a right to a jury trial, while suits in equity were tried before a judge. There is also the law/fact divide. Our system supposes that judges are better when addressing issues of law and that juries are better when dealing with issues of fact. This is the traditional touchstone of allocating decision-making between judges and juries. The federal court system believes that the strengths of a judge are four-fold: interpreting law (statutes, administrative regulations, and case law); analyzing complex written documents (like patents); handling long-standing, complicated disputes; and ensuring that there is stare decisis and uniformity in the law. However, the system also finds strengths in juries, particularly the evaluation of the credibility of witness testimony. In addition, juries provide a common person perspective to issues such as negligence and provide demographic diversity and a better reflection of "community standards." Juries thus help democratize our judicial system. To cheat the system of that by eliminating the jury system in favor of efficiency would be a mistake.

## Question 2

**SMJ:** There is no AUJ because this is a state cause of action for battery (*First Federal*). There is DJ because the AIC is $100,000 and parties are diverse (Rafael = NJ, Splinter = NY) (*Strawbridge*).

**IPJ:** Although it probably doesn't matter here, there is general jurisdiction in the SDNY over Splinter (domicile, 4(k)(1)(A)). For IPJ issues related to Rafael and April, D.N.J. can exercise general IPJ (domiciles, 4(k)(1)(A)).

**Venue:** Although it probably doesn't matter here, venue in the SDNY is proper under § 1391(b)(1) (Splinter's domicile) and under § 1391(b)(2) (beating occurred there). Venue vis-à-vis Rafael and April would be proper in D.N.J. under § 1391(b)(1) (domiciles).

### 2(a)

Yes, Rafael could assert claim preclusion as an affirmative defense. Regardless of who won, Splinter is claim precluded (or precluded by FRCP 13) because Splinter should have brought a compulsory counterclaim against Rafael. Splinter's counterclaim here is compulsory because it arises from the same T/O. Remember that as here in the declaratory judgment realm, Splinter needed to counterclaim on the issue (Rafael's battery liability) that he would have sued on in the "imaginary case." Splinter is claim precluded because (1) the prior suit was final and on the merits (the court entered judgment on a jury verdict), (2) the present suit arises out of the same claim and surrounds the same T/O (because a suit about the justification defense and battery cover the same incident of Rafael and April beating up Splinter) and (3) the parties are the same in both suits (Rafael and Splinter).

### 2(b)

This is slightly less certain. For the reasons just stated, the SDNY case was final/on the merits and the present suit arises out of the same claim and T/O. For April to be claim precluded, she would also have to have been in privity with Rafael. She is not a successor in interest, but April exercised substantial (actually, complete) control over Rafael's litigation decisions. However, while she did not have a legal ability to compel Rafael, Rafael delegated control to April. That is likely enough for privity, so April will likely be claim precluded, and Splinter can use that defense.

### 2(c)

There is an argument to be made for either side here in relation to whether Bebop's claim arises out of the same T/O as Splinter's. Arguably, the two batteries were separate and occurred in separate cities, but one could also argue that the justification defense being used in both arises out of the same anger triggered by the same class. In terms of administrability and overlapping evidence, the cases are similar. Ultimately, however, Bebop *can* sue here because he was not a party to L1 and there is no privity here, as there was no substantial control or successor in interest. Bebop can assert diversity jurisdiction, see above re venue and IPJ.

## Question 3

No, it's a bad rule suggestion for several reasons. Proponents will point to a few advantages, namely efficiency in the form of lower litigation expense, fewer cases for overcrowded courts, and uniform outcomes across similar claims. However, there are distinct disadvantages to the interweaving of claim preclusion and intervention. First, significant access to justice issues will make the lives of ordinary civil plaintiffs seeking recourse much more difficult. While it will help limit claims, this rule would also bar many claims simply because a prospective plaintiff failed to intervene. Second, there are significant issues with not requiring notice. Notice and Procedural Due Process jurisprudence reflects a policy that would-be litigants shouldn't lose their rights to pursue claims and that they maintain a constitutional right to proper notice (Due Process under *Mullane*). This rule would violate that policy by forgoing a notice requirement like that statutorily prescribed by FRCP 4, which is meant to protect parties. While I am sympathetic to rule changes aimed at making our overcrowded and overcomplicated justice system more efficient, the negative implications of this rule are too great to warrant instituting it.
