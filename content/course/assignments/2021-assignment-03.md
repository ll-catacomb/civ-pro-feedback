## Virginia Law

In 2018, responding to a well-funded lobbying campaign from the Virginia Chamber of Commerce, the Virginia General Assembly passed the Rupert Giles Tort Reform Act (the "Giles" Act). The Act includes the following provision.

> **Section 6.7: Heightened Summary Judgment Standard**
> (a) The General Assembly finds that lawsuits may deter investors from starting or investing in industries in the Commonwealth of Virginia.
> (b) Virginia Rule of Civil Procedure 56 is hereby amended to add a new subsection (i), as follows: "In any action alleging negligence against any manufacturer or distributor of a commercial or consumer product, if the plaintiff seeks damages for pain and suffering, the plaintiff shall before the close of discovery provide an affidavit from a licensed psychiatrist detailing the nature and extent of the plaintiff's mental distress. Upon a motion for summary judgment by a defendant alleging a plaintiff's failure to comply with this subsection, the court shall forthwith dismiss the action with prejudice. Any motion for summary judgment on the issue of liability or damages by a plaintiff who fails to comply with this subsection shall be denied."

In all other ways relevant to this exam, the Virginia Rules of Civil Procedure are identical to the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

## Law of Negligence

All states relevant to this assignment have a cause of action called "negligence." The elements of the negligence cause of action are that (i) the defendant owed a duty of care to the plaintiff, (ii) the defendant breached that duty by failing to act with due care, (iii) the defendant's breach caused (iv) harm to the plaintiff.

- For the purposes of this assignment, you may assume that all persons (including incorporeal entities) owe a duty to act with due care to all other persons (or corporations or incorporeal entities) with whom they interact or anyone affected by their actions. In other words, you can assume that a plaintiff need not prove element (i) of negligence, regardless of whether the case is brought in state or federal court.
- With respect to element (ii), a plaintiff ordinarily proves this element by alleging or providing evidence that the defendant failed to exercise the ordinary care that a reasonable person would exercise under the circumstances.

<!-- page: 3 -->

- With respect to element (iii), for the purposes of this assignment, a plaintiff proves causation by providing evidence that absent the action constituting the breach of duty, the plaintiff's injury would not have occurred.[^1]
- Injuries are proved through any relevant proof.

## Stipulated Facts

The parties to this lawsuit stipulated to the following facts (meaning that the parties agreed that they were true without the need for proof).

Xander Harris, Inc. ("XH") is a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in Baltimore, Maryland. It manufactures self-driving trucks of all sizes, from light pickups all the way up to 18 wheelers. All of its manufacturing occurs in a massive plant outside of Baltimore, MD. Each vehicle that XH produces bears a distinctive script "XH" symbol on its front fender. XH conducts no sales activity to consumers or business, nor does it advertise. Instead, it sells its products exclusively in an enormous purchase and sale clearinghouse market facility located in Alexandria, VA called "The Spike." At The Spike, sellers and buyers of self-driving vehicles meet, conduct inspections, negotiate sales terms, transfer money, and take delivery of vehicles. XH sells to various distributors at The Spike. XH insists that any company to whom it sells one of its vehicles must subsequently sell the vehicle directly to a consumer.

Faith, Inc. is a North Carolina corporation with its principal place of business in Columbia, South Carolina. Faith is a large distributor (to consumers) of self-driving vehicles. It advertises and sells self-driving vehicles in a great many states in the eastern half of the United States, including Pennsylvania, Delaware, Maryland, Virginia, North Carolina, and South Carolina. Faith purchases self-driving trucks from, among other sources, manufacturers who patronize the clearinghouse market at The Spike in Alexandria, VA. In early 2020, Faith began advertising a set of XH self-driving trucks throughout its multi-state service area, with television ads running on local TV stations in different cities in Pennsylvania as well as all of the states mentioned immediately above. Faith also ran ads in local newspapers in Pennsylvania cities, including Philadelphia; similar ads ran in local newspapers of cities in other states. The ads all directed those interested in self-driving pickups to Faith's website, where potential buyers could see complete information, including photographs, of the trucks for sale. People interested were instructed to contact a Faith employee named Cordelia Chase. The ads that ran in Philadelphia specified that Faith had 10 XH self-driving trucks waiting in a rented parking facility in the area, ready for immediate inspection and delivery.

In early February of 2020, Chase received an Internet message from Buffy Summers, a Pennsylvania domiciliary living in Philadelphia. Buffy inquired how soon she could inspect one of the XH light vehicles she'd seen advertised on her local TV station, and how much it would cost. A series of email exchanges followed, in which Buffy indicated an interest in purchasing one of the trucks. Buffy was delighted to learn that she could buy the truck as soon as she liked after inspecting it, and that she could inspect it right outside her house, because Chase could instruct the truck to drive itself to her house and park there. Buffy was also excited that her sister, Dawn Summers, visiting from her home in Charleston, SC,

At 10:45am, Buffy and Dawn bundled up and went to the sidewalk in front of Buffy's house, located at 1630 Sunnydale Drive, in Philadelphia. Buffy suffered a concussion, a broken leg, and several cuts; she required care in a nearby Philadelphia hospital for 25 days and underwent extensive physical and mental therapy before eventually making her recovery.

Buffy demanded $300,000 to compensate her medical and rehabilitative expenses, as well as her pain and suffering.

No party took discovery, and the parties began to prepare for summary judgment motions.
