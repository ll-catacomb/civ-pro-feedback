## File Motion to Remand

The partner should take a 3-week vacation so she can file a timely motion to remand (by June 6, 30 days after May 7 removal), which must be granted because Defendant's removal was procedurally defective (removed more than 30 days after the removable event: service on April 1). An extra incentive to remand is that a PA state court will apply PaRCP 8(f)(2) (*infra*).

## File PaRCP 8(f)(2) Motion (if partner does not file a timely motion to remand)

Because Defendant failed to attach the requisite affidavit to its motion to dismiss, the partner should file a motion to fix damages, which will succeed if PaRCP 8(f)(2) applies. The EDPA will apply PA HCoL rules, *Klaxon*, and anything arguably procedural is procedural for HCoL, so 8(f)(2) is procedural for HCoL. Whether that rule applies to Defendant's motion to dismiss in PA federal court, given FRCP 8's existence, is an *Erie* problem.

Under *York*, because guaranteeing significant damages without further investigation of Plaintiff's injuries may well "substantially affect" the "outcome" of the case, the state's law should apply here. Under *Hanna* (Ginsburg and Scalia), every FRCP is valid because it "really regulates procedure" (*Sibbach*). Under Scalia's interpretation, FRCP 8 is applicable because it concerns pleading requirements, so it — not PaRCP 8(f)(2) — applies here. Ginsburg, though, may have cast the FRCP as a "floor" that allows PaRCP 8(f)(2) to add a requirement that does not directly conflict with the FRCP — apply state law. Under Harlan, because the state legislature enacted PaRCP 8(f) to address "primary conduct" — willingness of doctors to practice in PA — apply state law.

## Predicted Ruling on Defendant's 12(b)(6) Motion to Dismiss

Likely denied. Applying *Iqbal*, the complaint is factually specific everywhere except parts of paragraph 14, which contains some conclusory statements. The non-conclusory alleged facts left over obviously may well constitute "damages" (paragraph 12), "breach" (paragraphs 10-11), and "causation" (paragraphs 11-12) if true. With "duty" imputed by law, the complaint therefore makes out a plausible (not merely possible) case for negligence under PA law.

Although Defendant's motion to dismiss was limited to 12(b)(6), the court has an independent duty to independently (*sua sponte*) assure itself of its own SMJ before ruling on any motion. Here, 1332 diversity jurisdiction exists, as Plaintiff (SC) and Defendant (Incorp.-DE, PPoB-MD) are completely diverse (*Mas*; *Strawbridge*), and the AIC ($200,000) exceeds $75,000.

## Predicted Ruling on Motion to Transfer

Apply JG's "Happy Court Rule."

### Must Court Transfer/Dismiss?

No, because the PA federal court is likely happy with proper venue and PJ over Defendant. Venue is automatically proper in removed cases. There is no GIPJ over Defendant (DE, MD, *see supra*), who is not a citizen of PA, and is not "really at home" under *Daimler*, as there is no evidence more than 50% of its business is in PA. But SIPJ likely exists. FRCP 4(k)(1)(A) and

PA's long-arm statute combine to extend SIPJ to constitutional limits. Minimal contacts consist of the facts that Defendant has an office in Harrisburg, PA and partially conducted the sale at issue in Philadelphia. It is likely also "fair and reasonable" to extend SIPJ over Defendant, because D has extensive contacts with PA (*supra*), its office in the state makes litigating there convenient, PA has an interest in adjudicating torts that occur (at least partially) within its borders, and the key witness (salesman who assured plaintiff that the plant was not an orchid) works at the Harrisburg, PA office.

### May the Court Transfer?

Maybe. Venue is proper in SC under 1391(b)(2) because the injury (allergic reaction) occurred in SC. PJ is less likely. There is no GIPJ over Defendant (*supra*). Regarding SIPJ, FRCP 4(k)(1)(A) applies SC's long-arm, which is to constitutional limits. There are minimal contacts, because Defendant shipped directly into South Carolina without an intermediary (distinguishing *McIntyre*), with a single such event sufficient (*McGee*). And as in *McGee*, South Carolina has a "manifest interest in providing effective means of redress when" businesses selling products into the state hurt their residents, but that the crucial witness is in PA (*see supra*), the Plaintiff can travel there as he has made a full recovery and has already been to PA counsel against extending SIPJ over Defendant in SC. That said, SC (place of the injury) law will likely govern substantive tort issues. This could go either way.

### Should the Court Transfer, if it may or may not?

With no forum-selection clause, apply *Gulf Oil*'s standard. The plaintiff's choice of forum is presumptively correct. And, because of the location of the key witness (PA), the office from where the sale was made (PA), and the local interests in adjudicating a tort in the jurisdiction where the misfeasance of the Defendant occurred (PA), that presumption is not unsettled.

Additionally, it is not "in the interest of justice" (*Hoffman*) to transfer a removed case in which a motion to remand would obviously succeed.
