Declaratory judgment will likely fail.

## SMJ of the Declaratory Judgment

To examine SMJ of a declaratory judgment suit, look at the "imaginary suit" it is trying to anticipate (i.e., Connor's suit).

### AUJ

There is no AUJ. T-800 creates no cause of action available to private citizens (*Mottley*).

Is there *Smith-Grable*? Likely no. The issue is not substantial (the case does not necessarily turn on Reese having violated the IPA; Connor can prove negligence in another way), even though it is disputed. Even still, the dispute here is factual — whether or not Reese was actually drunk — more so than it is legal, pointing to this being an issue for state courts. Congress also likely intended for this kind of issue *not* to go to federal court, since it stated expressly that private citizens could not so. Similarly, there is no *Smith-Grable* because of a situation similar to *Merrill-Dow*. We do not want to cause a flood of cases into the federal system because of a violation of a federal duty/regulation (aviation rules would affect many people) (*Merrill*), nor do we want to interpret a state claim that looks at a duty imposed by federal statute to create a cognizable federal claim (*Moore*).

### DJ

There is no DJ (while Connor only wished to sue Skynet, the "imaginary" suit here envisions Connor suing both Skynet and Reese) because there is no complete diversity. To do this, we need to see where each one of these "characters" are "citizens."

Skynet: is incorporated and has PPOB in Mexico. For DJ purposes, this is a 51st state.

Connor: domiciled in Arizona. Born and raised there, has license and parents there, and only went to college in Colorado with no intent to stay indefinitely.

Reese: domiciled in Arizona. While born and raised in North Dakota, he moved to Arizona, bought house, and intends to live there "for the rest of his life."

Because Reese and Connor are both from Arizona, there is no complete diversity.

The suit also fails to meet the AIC requirement of DJ. $60K in damages is less than the $75K requirement for DJ.

## IPJ

Connor may also claim that the court has no personal jurisdiction over her.

### GIPJ

There is likely no general personal jurisdiction because Connor is domiciled in Arizona. However, while the *Daimler* test has never been applied to human beings, it could be argued that Connor, as a college student in the state who has spent well over half of each of the past three

years in CO, is essentially at home there. Similarly, the record does not indicate whether she was tagged, so we can't use *Burnham* reasoning.

### SIPJ

Colorado long-arm. 4K1A. The court likely does not have SIPJ over Connor, either. While Connor did call Skynet over the phone from her Colorado apartment (which might be a sufficient contact actually linking the defendant to *the subject matter of litigation*, unlike the fact that she just went to school there), and she later bought a ticket for a trip she thought would fly over CO, the plane never ended up flying in Colorado and she ultimately signed the contract in Mexico (*McGee*). Similarly, the action here is a tort suit that is unrelated to any claim that could be made on the contract.

Is it F+R? Connor's relevant contacts to Colorado appear limited; CO is convenient for her because she lives there (assuming she hasn't moved out yet after graduation); CO is convenient for the plaintiffs Skynet and Reese because they regularly operate/fly flights there; CO doesn't have any specific strong interests in this subject; location of witnesses/evidence is mixed (other passenger is likely in CO, but Reese in AZ and plane presumably in Mexico).

### Colorado Stay?

Connor cannot initiate a Colorado Stay because there is no active litigation occurring at the state level; it was only threatened.

## HCOL?

Putting all these to the side, and assuming, for the sake of argument, that the Court has both SMJ and PJ, then the declaratory judgment would likely come out in Skynet and Reese's favor. A federal court uses the HCOL of the state it sits. Statute of limitations is a procedural issue, so we use the law of the forum state as well. Colorado's SOL on a negligence claim is 1 year. Because the events that led to the suit occurred in June of 2022, and September 2023 is more than one year from there, the statute of limitations has expired.
