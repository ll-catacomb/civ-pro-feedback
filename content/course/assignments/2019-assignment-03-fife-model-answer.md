## Question 1

### Hypothetical one: Federal law.

*York* outcome-determinative test favors state law because only the state signature requirement, and not the federal, warrants dismissal.

Under *Hanna* majority, both Scalia and Ginsburg would apply FRCP as valid and applicable. FRCPs are valid under the REA if they "really regulate procedure," *Sibbach*, and do not abridge or modify a substantive right. A signature requirement doesn't affect a substantive right and clearly regulates procedures of the court.

Here, both Scalia and Ginsburg agree FRCP is applicable. Scalia looks at the text of the FRCP and it's explicit on signature requirement. Ginsburg wants to look at the state's interest and interpret the FRCP narrowly. Here, state may have interest in ensuring parties carefully review court filings. She could say SCRCP is compatible as FRCP sets a floor, not a ceiling. Without more facts or legislative record, the state interest doesn't seem strong enough to warrant this strained interpretation.

Harlan will look at how the rule affects pre-litigation conduct. A signature requirement wouldn't affect a party's behavior unless they planned on sloppy lawyering. Apply federal law.

### Hypothetical two: State law.

*York* likely permits the FRCP — a preliminary injunction is a preliminary ruling before the court fully adjudicates the claim, so it's likely not outcome determinative under *York* where *York* involved a statute of limitations dismissal preventing the entire claim from going forward.

Under both Scalia and Ginsburg interpretations the law is valid because it "really regulates procedure" and does not modify, abridge, or enlarge a substantive right.

Ginsburg will apply TRCP by interpreting the FRCP to accommodate Texas' strong desire to preserve judicial discretion without overly formalistic constraints. She will apply *Walker*-esque analysis and say FRCP is just a default and bend the FRCP beyond recognition to accommodate state concerns. TRCP applies.

Scalia will just look at federal law and not worry about the state law at all. FRCP speaks directly to injunctions, so therefore apply FRCP.

Harlan will apply state law. FRCP discourages preliminary injunctions, which makes corporations and policymakers bolder because it diminishes the effectiveness of a valuable litigation tool from parties trying to stop a corporate merger or new government policy. Similar to *Cohen*, the FRCP affects pre-litigation behavior.

### Hypothetical three: Federal law.

<!-- page: 2 -->

If faced with a motion to dismiss, *York* applies state law because the suit is time-barred under state law, but not under federal statute.

*Hanna* majority both apply federal law. They'll agree it's valid as necessary and proper to Congress' creation of the federal courts, passed through bicameralism and presentment. Congress intended to reach the issue — the statute specifically mentions diversity jurisdiction and there's no language permitting exceptions.

Scalia applies statute — Congress validly spoke directly to the matter at hand. For Ginsburg, the state interest is hard to see — perhaps efficient dispute resolution or ensuring availability of evidence, but a statute validly enacted by Congress provides less flexibility to accommodate state interests, especially when they're minimal. Statute, therefore, applies.

Harlan will apply statute — statute of limitations mostly affects behavior when considering litigation, not pre-litigation primary conduct.

## Question 2

None of the tests capture the diversity of interests and the magnitude to which they are implicated across the hypos. The first hypo minimally implicates federalism and equal protection. Federal signature rule hardly infringes on a meaningful state interest and forum shopping would only occur if a plaintiff intended to do bad lawyering.

The second hypo more greatly affects equal protection/forum shopping because a litigant will have different remedies depending on where they file, which gives preference to out of state litigants seeking injunctions. Moreover, the hypo strongly implicates federalism because the standards of preliminary injunctions involve serious policy decisions about whether we want to provide judges discretion or if we want to be really certain before issuing an injunction.

The final hypo speaks to the nature of the law — when Congress legislates, the ball is out of the judiciary's court and unless Congress surpassed its authority, the federal bench must listen. So, when Congress acts in accordance with an enumerated power, the federal courts can feel comfortable applying federal law because acting according to statute diminishes separation of power concerns and the supremacy clause justifies deference to federal law.

Each of the tests are overly focused on a single *Erie* interest and therefore too inflexible to capture all of these interests and their varying degrees of magnitude. Scalia and *York* are far too formalistic. *York* focuses too myopically on forum shopping, while Scalia's deference to text only accounts for separation of powers. Ginsburg, by contrast, focuses predominantly on federalism without much regard for litigation gamesmanship and likely contravenes separation of powers by morphing FRCP to accommodate state law. Moreover, Harlan's test thinks only about preventing litigation gamesmanship, not separation of powers or the nature of the law.
