Assignment 3 Model Answer — TA

**1.** "Law vs. equity" is a historically-based allocation of power, linked to the Chancellor's role in finding facts for "equity" matters and the King's Bench law courts, which utilized a jury. Today, despite a "step one" historical analysis, the distinction turns largely on form of remedy sought by the plaintiff (e.g. compensatory or punitive damages are "law" and so produce a right to jury trial; specific performance and injunction are "equity" matters for judicial determination). Disadvantages include that a constitutional protection turns on the interpretation of similar remedies — e.g. ejectment at law and disgorgement at equity. An advantage is consistency with the phrasing of the 7th Amendment's "preserv[ation]" of a right to trial by jury; absent a historically-based doctrine, interpretation of that word would require reinterpretation.

"Fact vs. law" allocates findings of fact to the jury and findings of law to the judge, as both a function of historical practice and for practical considerations regarding comparative decisionmaking capacity. An advantage is that juries can provide a community standard on disputed matters of fact, or important non-fact-based standards (e.g. obscenity or negligence), insulating the judge from making both "filtering" and substantive decisions about standards, while reserving the more technical function of statutory interpretation to the judge. Disadvantages include (i) that judicial fact-finding at pre-trial stages introduces inconsistencies in pursuit of the purported value — incorporating community standards, (ii) the increasing complexity of civil litigation, and (iii) that complicated disputes in a modern economy place outcomes in the hands of underprepared jurors.

**2.** These changes, one affecting the right to jury trial and the other providing guidance to judges regarding live testimony, must be more fully defined in order to assess their impact on "gatekeeping" debates. The proposed repeal will need to identify whether it prohibits jury trial in all civil cases or whether jury trial is simply no longer a protected right. More relevant to "gatekeeping" are the lack of clarity on the proposed standard of "wherever possible" and whether oral argument will still be standard regarding the application of law to facts. If judges have significant discretion to allow witness testimony and if oral argument is permitted, the impact will likely be limited.

Assuming limited discretion regarding "possible" and restrictions on oral argument, these changes implicate the "gatekeeping" functions of pre-trial stages, a delicate balance between individuals' ability to seek justice and the system's need to preserve efficiency and protect parties from baseless suits with price tags that necessitate settlement. The proposals' impact will depend on whether you see cost reduction as primarily needed at discovery stage or at trial stage. If cost control is needed at the discovery stage (the view motivating *Iqbal* and *Twombly*), this proposed change counsels for strict(er) pleading standards since it elevates the record's determinative status — *increasing* incentives to spend heavily on discovery. If, by contrast, the cost of litigation is thought to lie in conducting trials, this change could support a shift in the federal system away from *Twombly* and *Iqbal*, back to notice pleading, since the dreaded litigation costs had been curtailed.

These changes could mean significant savings on overall litigation costs by restricting costs subsequent to record-creation. However, the likelihood of seeing an increase in already-striking discovery costs could reinforce the federal system's shift to code pleading. If legislators agree the proposal could generate cost savings, they should clarify a notice pleading standard to ensure the courts do not close the gates too aggressively as a reaction to these reforms.

A motion for summary judgment should only be granted where there are no disputed issues of material fact, i.e. where it is irrelevant whether determinations of fact are made by the judge or jury, based on transcribed or oral testimony. Consequently, this change *should* make limited difference regarding debates about or standards for motions for summary judgment. In practice, if oral arguments are eliminated, final adjudication would occur largely on the basis of the written record. This collapses the decision on summary judgment and final judgment. It is unclear whether a judge, facing no jury to decide matters of fact and no oral testimony that may sway, would rule on motion for summary judgment or would simply decline to rule and immediately issue a final judgment.

The two proposals act in concert to increase judges' power over cases and to isolate consideration of evidence within the judge's head. This increase and isolation increases the chance for error or abuse, counseling broader appellate review of district court decisions (note that at least one traditional basis for limiting appellate review, that the trial judge heard the witnesses testify live, would no longer be extant). It is not clear, however, that error would be more likely to occur at summary judgment stage or that, indeed, the motion would continue to be a useful instrument. To the extent that oral argument may be eliminated and discretion to hear testimony constrained, efficiency and consistency may be served by combining summary judgment and final decision into one ruling, allowing "grant of summary judgment" in those cases where there are no issues of material fact, thus focusing the judge's decision on the matters of law. Potential for mistake and error could be avoided (and any increased demand on appellate courts' mitigated) by requiring judges who are deciding issues of fact to articulate fully the basis on which they decided (see FRCP 52).
