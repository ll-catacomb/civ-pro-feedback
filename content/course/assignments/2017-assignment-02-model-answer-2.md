## Assignment 2 — Civil Procedure 2, 2017

### Question 1(a): Transfer Motion — QQS v. SSS

The transfer should be granted. The court will find no reason to sua sponte remand. SMJ is established by diversity (the AIC is easily sufficient).

The federal transfer statutes govern the district court's disposition of forum selection clauses, not state law (*Stewart*). Despite the apparent vertical conflict of laws, the court should apply the transfer statutes. Further, federal courts will enforce valid forum selection clauses except in exceptional cases (*Atlantic Marine*). Courts will consider the public interest factors, but these are rarely enough to invalidate a clause. The understanding is that the plaintiff has already waived private interest factors by entering into a contract with the clause. There is nothing exceptional about the public interest factors in this case.

As venue and personal jurisdiction exist in SDNY, the original lawsuit could have been brought in the transferee court and therefore the transfer is granted.

### Question 1(b): Damages Cap After Transfer

Yes, the recovery will likely be limited. Following the transfer, the NY federal court will apply NY law (*Klaxon*). Instead of having to use the happy court rule, the forum selection clause results as if the lawsuit was originally brought in the NY court (*Atlantic Marine*). The prompt states that damages-limiting clauses are substantive for VCOL purposes, so the SDNY uses the law of some state. Within a HCOL analysis, procedural issues fall under the law of the forum. NY law considers damage-limiting clauses as procedural for HCOL and does not invalidate contract clauses. Applying NY law will likely result in limited recovery.

### Question 2: Forum Non Conveniens — QQS v. HH

No. The motion to dismiss should be denied.

While the federal transfer statutes do not apply to non-federal court venues, forum non conveniens still exists within federal courts (*Atlantic Marine*). Through federal common law, *Piper Aircraft Co. v. Reyno* — analyzing private interests of litigants, enforceability of judgment, fair trial, public interest — a court could decide to grant the FNC motion to dismiss. However, under state law a court does not necessarily need to grant the motion. State law prohibits forum selection clauses, the Alabama court has proper jurisdiction to hear the case, and can decide what to do with the motion. Since there is a conflict of state and federal law, an *Erie* analysis is necessary.

Following the *York* test: if the outcome of a suit would differ under federal versus state law, use state law. Here, the outcome would be different under federal law (grant FNC motion to dismiss) as opposed to state law (deny FNC motion to dismiss). Therefore, apply state law and deny the motion to dismiss.

*(Note: York did not specify whether the test pertains to deciding an issue or the case. The previous analysis was for deciding an issue — to grant the FNC motion. You could argue that the case might ultimately get dismissed under the application of state law for a different reason, and therefore the final outcome would be the same. If the outcome is the same, then federal law would apply.)*

Under the *Hanna* majority: since there is no valid and applicable on-point federal statute (*Atlantic Marine*), the court looks to federal common law (FNC and *Piper*) and considers the twin aims of *Erie*. If the federal law is against the twin aims (prevent forum shopping and prevent inequitable administration of laws), apply state law. Here, the difference between federal and Alabama law could easily cause an out-of-state plaintiff to choose state or federal court, depending on whether the plaintiff wants the forum selection clause to apply. This leads to forum shopping. Therefore, apply state law and deny the motion to dismiss.

Under *Harlan in Hanna*: Does federal law change the parties' pre-litigation conduct? Here, HH might not have entered into the contract if it knew that Alabama law would apply and invalidate the forum selection clause. HH might have been motivated to act because of federal common law, thinking it could move for a FNC motion to dismiss if litigation began in Alabama. Since it appears that federal law influenced the party's pre-litigation conduct, apply state law and deny the motion to dismiss.
