**(i) Actions**

**We should file a motion to remand to state court immediately.** Defendant's removal petition was filed >30 days after service and therefore is procedurally defective. As for your holiday, we have 30 days from the May 7th erroneous motion for removal to move for remand. After a 3-week trip we would still be able to file, but if a 5-week vacation is taken we will be procedurally ineligible to motion for remand and could be stuck in federal court!

**We should file a PRCP 8(f)(2) motion.** The Defendant failed to file the requisite affidavit such that under PRCP 8, they are barred from disputing the damages we demand for pain and suffering ($100,000). The EDPA will apply PA HCoL rules, *Klaxon*, and anything arguably procedural is procedural for HCoL, so 8(f)(2) is procedural for HCoL. We would be able to bind Daario in PA state court, but PRCP 8's applicability in federal court is less certain. An *Erie* inquiry is necessary. Under *York*'s "outcome determinative" test, the rule is determinative and thus substantive in that is fixes the amount of P&S damages, therefore it applies. Under both Scalia and Ginsberg in *Hanna* FRCP 8 is valid as it "really regulates procedure" (*Sibbach*). Under Scalia's test in the *Hanna* Majority, FRCP 8 is applicable and controls as it conflicts with PRCP 8, because both directly set response requirements. Under the Ginsberg test in *Hanna*, FRCP 8 is not necessarily applicable because looking to uphold the policy goals of PA, the court would interpret FRCP 8 narrowly, as setting a minimum requirement to which PRCP 8 merely adds. State law controls. Under the *Harlan* majority test, PRCP 8 is substantive because it affects pre-litigation "primary conduct" (attracting qualified physicians to PA), therefore the state law applies.

**(ii) Motion Analysis**

**The MTD will likely be denied.**

Under *Twombley/Iqbal* we remove ¶ 14 as legally conclusory and find the remaining factual allegations nudge the complaint from possible to plausible. Specific facts support all elements, duty (legally prescribed), breach ¶10-11, causation ¶11, and damages ¶12.

The Court will *sua sponte* review the complaint for SMJ, which is present under § 1332. Complete diversity exists (*Strawbridge*): Podrick is a citizen of S.C., Daario's POI is DE and PPOB is MD; and the AIC ($200,000) exceeds $75,000.

**The motion to transfer will also likely be denied.**

*Must the court transfer? No.*

EDPA has proper venue and PJ over Daario and is likely a "happy court". Venue is automatically proper in all removed cases. There is not GIPJ over D. Daario's POI is DE and PPOB is in MD and there is no evidence that 50+% of its revenues come from PA. (*Daimler*) However, SIPJ is likely present. There is a PA state long arm statute reaching constitutional limits and FRCP 4(k)(1)(A) authorizing jurisdiction over an out of state D. There are minimal contacts (*Int'l Shoe*) as Daario maintained an office in Harrisburg. Plaintiff was in Philadelphia when he learned of Daario and called the Harrisburg office to order his flower, which in turn led to the potential negligence claim. Extending SIPJ over D is also fair and

reasonable as D's brick and mortar office in PA makes litigating there convenient and likely expected; PA has an interest in torts that originate within its borders and a key witness, the salesman who assured plaintiff that the flower was not an orchid works, in Harrisburg.

*May the court transfer? Potentially.*

The transfer statutes allow transfer from any district to any "happy court". Venue is proper in DSC under § 1391(b)(2) because the injury occurred in DSC. There is likely no GIPJ over Daario in SC (see above). SIPJ would rely on FRCP 4(k)(1)(A) and long arm. Here, there are potentially minimum contacts, because Daario purposefully availed itself of SC by doing business there, shipping their floral products directly into the state without a distributor. (*McGee*, contra *McIntyre*). It could be fair and reasonable because HCOL analysis likely points to applying SC law, SC has an interest in torts "blooming" within its borders, and evidence from the injury and hospital stay, to the plant itself, is in S.C.

*Should the court transfer? Probably not.*

Since EDPA is a "happy court", we apply the *Gulf Oil* factors. Plaintiff's choice of venue was EDPA, which presumptively should be honored. While much evidence is in S.C. (see supra), a key witness is in PA (see supra). Juries in both districts would be concerned about negligence (PA) and injury (SC) in their jurisdictions. The factors coming out in both directions is likely not enough to overcome the presumption.
