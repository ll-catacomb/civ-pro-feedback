## Question 1

#### Part a

Gringotts would need to seek a declaratory judgment on Helga's slip and fall (S&F) claim (28 U.S.C. § 2201). Courts have SMJ over a declaratory judgment lawsuit if the court would have SMJ over a suit filed by Helga (Imaginary Lawsuit Rule). Because an S&F claim is a state law claim, Gringotts lacks FQJ and can only sue Helga in SC federal court if there is complete diversity (*Strawbridge*). Gringotts is domiciled in NC because its nerve center is in Durham (*Hertz*) and DE because it's incorporated there. Helga's current living situation is in flux between NC and SC, but her domicile doesn't seem to have changed since she was born in NC. Although Helga intends to stay in SC, she hasn't yet taken up permanent residence there (*Mas*). Because living in a car is likely too transitory to establish residency, Helga is likely domiciled in NC. Even if Gringotts successfully argued that parking her car every night in SC established Helga's residency and intent to stay in SC, the suit is AIC deficient since Helga's medical bills only amount to $20,000. Therefore Gringotts likely can't sue Helga in SC federal court in a lawsuit concerning only the S&F claim.

#### Part b

Because SC federal court likely doesn't have diversity jurisdiction (Part a) and the suit would be AIC deficient regardless (the house is only worth $65,000), Gringotts can only proceed in its quiet title action over #4 Privet Drive (4PD) in SC federal court if the court has FQJ. A quiet title action is a state law claim, but there may be FQJ under the *Smith-Grable* exception because the EHMA is a federal issue that's embedded in the quiet title action. First, the quiet title action depends on the court's interpretation of the EHMA, meaning the dispute is primarily an issue of law — the constitutionality of the EHMA — not fact. Second, the EHMA is a substantial federal interest — its provisions are "perhaps the most important to have been passed by Congress" — and warrants a federal forum because it's in the government's interest for its legislation to be resolved in a federal court. Treating the claim as "arising under" might upset the division of labor between state and federal courts because it could sweep many quiet title actions that contest the constitutionality of the EHMA into federal court. However, a court will likely hold that the *Smith-Grable* exception applies given the centrality of the EHMA in the claim and importance of the statute. Therefore Gringotts can likely sue Helga in SC federal court in a lawsuit concerning 4PD.

#### Part c

If the court found that Helga is domiciled in SC, then SC federal court would have diversity jurisdiction over a lawsuit contesting both claims because compounding the value of 4PD and the medical bills surpasses the required AIC. However, since diversity jurisdiction is unlikely and SC federal court likely doesn't have SMJ over the S&F claim, Gringotts could only bring both claims together in a single lawsuit if the court had supplemental jurisdiction. A federal court can accept a state claim tacked onto a federal anchoring claim if both claims arise out of the same transaction or occurrence (t/o) with a common nucleus of operative fact (*Gibbs*). The S&F occurred when Helga met with Voldemort to discuss the deal, but the only common facts are the location, plaintiff, and defendant. The court is unlikely to find that the S&F, which centers on the maintenance of Gringotts' facility and only tangentially coincided with the controversy over the title of 4PD, stems from the same case or controversy. Therefore, Gringotts likely cannot bring both of the claims together in a single lawsuit.

### Question 2

Gringotts can only prevail in any litigation over the S&F if SC law applies since the puddle resulted from the manager's negligence. Because S&F tort law is substantive, determining the appropriate standard of care requires a horizontal choice of law analysis.

If the case is in SC court, the court will use the 1st restatement for HCOL. Because Helga slipped in Gringotts' SC office, the blood is in SC (*Alabama Great Southern RR*). Therefore the recklessness standard for S&F tort accidents would apply and Gringotts would likely prevail because the manager didn't act recklessly.

If the case is in NC court, the court will use the 2nd restatement for HCOL. Therefore NC will consider state interests that apply to the issue and look for the "center of gravity" of the transaction (*Babcock*). Helga would likely argue that NC law should apply because NC has an interest in protecting its residents. Gringotts will likely argue that SC law should apply because the S&F took place in SC and SC has an interest in regulating the maintenance of business facilities. However, since NC law will allow for more adequate protection of its citizens, a NC court is more likely to apply a negligence standard for S&F tort accidents. Therefore, Gringotts would likely lose in NC court because the manager acted negligently.
