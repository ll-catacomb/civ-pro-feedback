## Question 1

### Hypothetical one:

Under *York*, state law applies if the difference in rules is outcome-determinative. Here, the state rule would be applied because the lawsuit is dismissed under SCRCP but not under FRCP (attorney signed only the last page of the complaint, which is allowed under FRCP 11, but requires dismissal under SRCP 11).

Under the *Hanna* majority (Scalia) valid and applicable test, the FRCP would be applied. FRCP 11 is valid because REA § 2072 empowers the Supreme Court to promulgate the rule and it does not abridge, enlarge, or modify substantive rights — it "really regulates procedure" under *Sibbach*. Under a broad reading of FRCP 11, the Rule is applicable because it controls the application of signatures on a complaint.

The narrow *Hanna* test (Ginsburg in *Shady Grove*) favors the SCRCP. It's not clear how important the state interest is in its signature rule, but a narrow reading of its applicability is possible — because FRCP 11 requires a signature but does not address how many signatures are required, it can be construed to not apply to the issue at hand.

Under Harlan's *Hanna* concurrence, we examine whether the rule would affect the parties' pre-litigation conduct. The number of signatures required on the complaint would not affect the pre-litigation conduct — it would only affect the actions of the lawyers in the litigation. Thus, the rule is procedural and FRCP 11 would apply.

### Hypothetical two:

Unlike in hypothetical one, this is a rare case where the *York* test is going to favor the federal rule. Since any ruling on the injunction will merely be preliminary, the suit will go forward either way and the choice of rule is not outcome-determinative.

Under the *Hanna* majority (Scalia) valid and applicable test, the result is the same as in hypo one: the FRCP would be applied. However, it's a bit of a closer call on validity. The application of factors in a preliminary injunction comes closer to modifying substantive rights than the signature rule. However, the FRCP is always going to be found to be procedural per *Sibbach*'s "really regulates procedure" test. Because the issue is a preliminary injunction, FRCP 65 is clearly applicable.

The result is the same under the narrow *Hanna* (Ginsburg) test. Though the state presumably has an interest in providing preliminary injunctions to plaintiffs who satisfy the Rule 65 factors and can develop persuasive merits arguments (though who might not have the requisite evidence right away), it is difficult to construe the federal law narrowly given that FRCP 65 directly applies to preliminary injunctions.

Under Harlan's *Hanna* concurrence, the choice of rule is not going to affect the parties' pre-litigation behavior: FRCP 65 applies.

### Hypothetical three:

As above, the *York* test is outcome-determinative, so the state rule would apply in this situation.

Under the *Hanna* majority (Scalia) test, we go with the federal statute if it is valid and applicable. The federal statute is valid because it was passed through bicameralism and presentment pursuant to Congress's power under the Necessary and Proper Clause. It applies because the issue is whether the suit was filed within the limitations period.

Under the narrow *Hanna* (Ginsburg) test, the result is the same. The federal statute is valid and applicable. Because it's a federal statute there is probably less of an inclination to construe it narrowly (but see *Gasperini*); regardless, it is difficult to say that the federal limitations period does not apply to a question about whether the suit was filed within the limitations period.

Harlan in *Hanna* probably favors the federal statute because statutes of limitation tend to not affect a party's pre-litigation conduct. However, if the parties are sophisticated corporate entities, the applicable limitations period could affect pre-litigation conduct (such as insurance) based on the perceived litigation risk.

## Question 2

The *Erie* tests applied in Question 1 are not sufficiently flexible to address the variation in the importance of state and federal interests implicated. The *York* test is totally inflexible because it will nearly always choose the state law no matter how compelling the federal interest is (and if it chooses the federal law it will be because there's no difference in outcome of the case, not because of the strength of the interests involved).

The two *Hanna* tests are also inflexible because the inquiry is based on the source of the law — statutory, regulatory, or common law — rather than the policy rationales underlying the laws. Thus, in hypotheticals one and two, the *Hanna* majority test chooses the federal rule, even though the state interest is stronger in regulating whether plaintiffs can receive preliminary injunctions than regulating the number of signatures on a pleading. And the narrow *Hanna* Ginsburg test actually reaches the counterintuitive result: it chooses the state signature rule in hypothetical one due to narrow construction of applicability to accommodate state interests — but the interest is stronger in hypothetical two.

Harlan's *Hanna* concurrence seems to provide the most coherent test to distinguish between substantive and procedural law and thus is probably best able to address the variation in importance of state and federal interests. Of course, Harlan's test made no difference vis-à-vis the varied interests in hypotheticals one and two, but in general it will be more likely to accommodate state interests as the primary regulators of behavior in cases where the rules affect pre-litigation conduct.
