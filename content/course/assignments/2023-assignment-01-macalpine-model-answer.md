# Assignment 1 Model Answer

## A) Grounds to Dismiss/Remand

Defendants may move to dismiss/remand for lack of SMJ, which will fail.

There is diversity jurisdiction (*Strawbridge*) — Groucho is domiciled in TX, Zeppo is domiciled in DE, Sylvania has incorporation in DE and PPoB in OK. Both claims are AIC sufficient (the claim against Sylvania is $150,000; the claim against Zeppo is $250,000. Both claims exceed $75,000).

### IPJ

Defendants will move to dismiss for lack of PJ. That will fail against Zeppo, but might succeed against Sylvania.

We have a long arm to constitutional limits; under FRCP 4(k)(1)(a) the TX federal court can "piggy-back" off of it.

There is no general in personam jurisdiction (IPJ) since neither defendant is "at home." Neither is from TX, neither does >50% of business in TX.

*Shoe* factors/minimal contacts analysis:

**Zeppo:** There would likely be specific IPJ because Zeppo purposefully reached into TX (he drove into the state, first saw DiS there, visited a friend, and as a result later stole something from someone in TX). These key events relate Zeppo, Texas, and the subject matter of the litigation.

**Sylvania:** Sylvania does not solicit business in TX. Sylvania "reached into" the state to have Groucho as a customer, but didn't advertise to/target TX and has few US-based customers. *McGee* says that Sylvania purposefully availed itself of TX customers, but it wasn't all that much as there were few customers. Further, Groucho created the account while he was outside Texas and then later returned to TX (possibly like *Hanson*), and he paid with a credit card, so Sylvania may not have even known that he was a Texas resident.

Fair and reasonable factors: Texas has an interest in regulating conduct within its borders and in protecting Groucho from harm, Texas is convenient for Groucho, not too inconvenient for Zeppo (he went to the state before), less convenient for Sylvania (who has one customer there but no real contacts otherwise), witnesses/evidence may be in TX (Chico testimony, Groucho's computer), but the internet evidence is not clearly located anywhere, Texas law could possibly apply (see below) and TX best at applying TX law.

## B) HCOL

The question says that the Texas federal court will use Texas HCoL rules. Texas uses 2R — it will favor *lex loci delicti* but will also consider section 6 factors. The negligence tort occurred where DiS was stolen — the theft occurred on the internet. Where was the "item located at the time of the theft?" Policy should play into this discussion.

**MA/SC:** One could argue that Duck in Soup (DiS) was located on Sylvania's servers in MA/SC when it was stolen. But there is no way to determine which state held the most data and today, data gets stored all over the place, and often gets moved.

**CA:** We could also say that DiS was "stolen" when it allegedly entered Zeppo's possession/hard drive in CA, but even so, there's little argument that it would make sense that DiS was in California before or when it was stolen, which is what matters.

**DE/OK:** Sylvania considers itself to be in these states, and negligence causes of action could be directed at company PPoB or incorporation locations. The decisions that gave rise to the negligence were made in Oklahoma, and thus the evidence about those decisions is there. Groucho knew, or could have known, that he was doing business with a DE/OK company, so one could argue that either would meet the parties' expectations.

**TX:** This is the last place Groucho viewed and owned DiS, so a court may find that the "blood spilled" here with respect to the theft. This would comport with Groucho's conception, but perhaps not the conception of Sylvania, which may not have known where Groucho was. TX may also want to apply TX law for judicial efficiency and the case is largely grounded in TX.

If a court determines DiS was located in MA/SC/CA, it may choose one of them as the location of a substantial portion of the events for HCoL purposes. This seems unlikely, as Texas is a clearer location for the theft and Oklahoma for the negligence. If the court uses TX law, contributory negligence is available. However, for the negligence suit, the court may use OK law, because that is where the decisions on security were likely made. Thus, comparative negligence would be available.
