**(a)** Patil can argue that York County, under color of state law, deprived her of property without due process of law. Specific necessary procedure are found in the mini-code of civil procedure in *Vitek*.

**Notice.** Notice must be of such nature as would reasonably convey the necessary information. *Mullane*. In other words, the plaintiff should take the actions that someone who actually cares about the defendant receiving notice would. Actual notice is not required; nor are "extraordinary" efforts by the plaintiff. This is a vague standard, but Patil can argue that the bank's post on facebook did not satisfy the requirement of *Jones v. Flowers*. Because Patil posted her away status on facebook, the bank employee should have known that the attempt to notify her has failed. Once the initial reasonable effort to give notice fails, *Flowers* requires additional reasonable measures. Here, the bank already knew that mail is a reliable method of communicating with Patil because of their previous correspondence. Hence, the bank ought to have at least attempted to mail a copy of the summons and complaint to her.

The bank would likely attempt to distinguish *Flowers*. In that case, the mailed notice was returned unclaimed, which provides an affirmative signal that the initial attempt had failed. Here, the bank employee would have had to be paying some attention to know that his method of service has failed. Seeing the message and processing it are two different things: the employee may be tasked with writing many of these posts each day. The bank can also argue that defendants may not opt out of notice-giving methods provided for by statute just by posting an away status. Because Patil might have checked her Facebook by, say, borrowing someone else's phone, the notice had not obviously and certainly failed. Here, the key factual question will be whether the bank employee knew or should have known that posting on Facebook would not actually give Patil notice.

**Proceedings.** In addition to challenging the facts in this case, Patil can attack the statutory scheme broadly. Several features of the scheme potentially threaten the core due process values from *Vitek*.

- *Insufficient time to answer*. By giving defendants only 14 days to answer, the scheme may deprive them of a reasonable opportunity to respond. By comparison, FRCP 12(a)(1)(A)(i) allows 21 days.
- *Inability to adequately prepare for trial*. Because trial must take place within 14 days, defendants may not have time to prepare all necessary evidence and legal theories. Further, courts have no discretion to extend this time in more complicated cases. There is also no discovery, which is not constitutionally required generally, but nonetheless may be necessary for the defendant to vindicate her rights.
- *No jury*. The Seventh Amendment does not apply to the states, so there is no constitutional jury requirement, but if a case turns on credibility assessments, then a federal appellate court might weigh the lack of a jury against the constitutionality of the scheme.
- *Independent decisionmaker*. The Housing Court is allowed to adopt as part of the written judgment proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law submitted on the first day of trial. This suggests that the judge may not weigh the evidence independently, but will improperly rely on the proposals. By contrast, FRCP 52(a) effectively discourages this practice.
- *No appeal*. Appeals are not constitutionally required generally, but judges are only human. If the Housing Court makes a mistake, this scheme has no means of undoing the error.

Of these potential due process violations, the most promising challenge is to target the inability to adequately prepare for trial. Patil can propose a clear alternative: give the judge discretion to allow limited discovery and extensions of time. Under *Mathews v. Eldridge*, the private interest is possession of one's real estate and potentially one's home — arguably quite significant. The government's interest is stated in VBHA §1 — to allow banks to foreclose promptly, and thus ease the "unprecedented liquidity crisis" which "poses a grave threat to the public interest." This also seems quite significant. The risk of error will be the key inquiry.

The state would urge that, like in *Mathews*, the evidence here will be almost entirely documentary, and because the defendant should have access to all documents related to her own property, discovery is unnecessary to reduce the risk of error. Similarly, additional time is unnecessary because she or her lawyer only needs to read the documents. Patil could counter that the cost is tiny. Precisely because documents predominate, discovery will be cheap and could turn up relevant evidence. The only cost is delaying foreclosure, but because most defendants with meritless defenses will not bother to answer, this cost will be small. And the judge's discretion can keep costs low.

**Counsel.** In civil proceedings, there is no guarantee to counsel. *Lassiter* denied counsel in a case involving parental rights, and it's hard to believe that the *Mathews* test would come out differently here.

**(b)** To avoid a jury trial, Patil should ask for declaratory and/or injunctive relief (equitable remedies) only. The Seventh Amendment would allow a jury trial if legal relief, such as damages, are involved.
