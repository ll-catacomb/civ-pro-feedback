### PJ

No state has general jurisdiction over both XH and Faith.

Faith's truly at home in NC, SC, but not essentially at home in any of the others, since no evidence suggests any one of the states they sell in has the majority or a large fraction of their business (*Daimler*).

XH's truly at home in DE, MD, and maybe essentially at home in VA (*Daimler*) since they do all their sales there — substantial contacts — and there's a long arm.

**Specific Jurisdiction:**
SC/NC/DE/MD/VA/PA all have long-arm statutes to the limits of the Constitution, so any of the federal district courts in those states could piggyback with 4(k)(1)(a).

SC/NC/DE/MD non-starters for SJ: Faith has no contacts with DE/MD, XH has no contacts with NC/SC.

Probably no SJ in PA for XH, but OK for Faith.

For XH, the exercise of jurisdiction would be inconsistent with due process because insufficient contacts between XH, forum, and subject matter of the litigation (*Int'l Shoe*). If minimum contacts were okay, then it'd probably be fair/reasonable to exercise jurisdiction in PA: XH nearby, most witnesses in PA, PA has interest in regulating, HCOL probably favors PA at a minimum on some issues (see below). But because minimal contacts are insufficient, PA likely doesn't have IPJ over XH.

### HCOL

The key difference in state law is whether the violation of regulation 5X5 can serve as the basis of Buffy's negligence claim. Because the issue's substantive (question about the nature of the claim, rather than procedural issue like time limits on filings), we need HCOL analysis.

In first restatement states (PA, NC, SC), the place where the harm happened governs. Harm happened in PA, so PA law would govern in any of these states, which likely precludes theory's per se/failure to inspect theory of negligence.

A second restatement state (VA/DE) would be better because they begin with the first restatement, but then consider the section six factors to see if strong considerations lead to a different result. Center of gravity could favor PA or VA (harm in PA, but most of the failures to inspect in VA plus sales there). VA has a strong state interest in regulating faulty sales within their borders, but PA has competing strong interest in protecting litigants who are their own citizens. The second restatement favors cutting up the case into issues, however, so perhaps the relevance of a failure to inspect could be governed by VA law, where the failure happened. If Buffy sues in VA and the § 6 factors end up somewhat balanced, a VA court may weigh in favor of selecting VA law because of the needs of the interstate judicial system — VA more familiar with their own law — and Buffy could use the per se/failure to inspect theory.
