## 1a.

No jurisdiction issues. This is a straightforward in rem case where ownership of seized property is central to the lawsuit's subject (unlike quasi in-rem, where D's property is seized to secure satisfaction of an unrelated personal claim, like seizing a corporation's shares in DE for a harm committed in OR, *Schaffer*).

Establishing PJ in in-rem suit, is *International Shoe*'s minimum contacts required? *Schaffer* says yes, but doesn't provide clear answer if holding is restricted to its facts or if *International Shoe* applies to in-rem and quasi in-rem. Regardless, still no jurisdiction issues. If minimum contacts required, no problem, because Picard is a MA domiciliary and there is a clear relationship between Picard's house, MA, and the lawsuits subject. If not, then in-rem suits only require that the property be within the court's geographic jurisdiction; this is also true.

## 1b.

Yes, can likely bring a suit claiming the County violated his right to a hearing. To determine, *Doehr* allows for *Mathews v. Eldridge* test to evaluate an existing procedural scheme against a new one (i.e. new scheme = Picard having a hearing before his house was seized). Here Picard's private interests will likely outweigh the average risk of erroneous determination and competing public interest.

**Private interests:** Picard's personal interests are massive. He literally no longer has a place to live; he couldn't even pack up his things. Property ownership is vaunted and needed for survival (*Doehr*) and he put his life savings into the down payment. A basic need is more urgent than the bank's private interest of getting cash; it can look at other liquidable assets while Picard has only his "cows, chickens, and tribbles." (*Doehr*).

**Risk of erroneous determination:** Low risk, at least not as high as *Doehr* where there was a question to the extent of the plaintiff's injuries resulting from assault and battery. Here there isn't any uncertainty on if Picard missed his payments or not; the evidence is straightforward and it's not a huge risk that a clerk issued the seizure (*Sniadach*, *Doehr*). That said, Borg isn't required to file a bond, so there isn't a market filter preventing banks from light-speeding shaky foreclosures to unexpecting and impoverished debtors (*Fuentes*).

**Public interest:** High interest because of the MA statute that explicitly passed law to allow banks to quickly foreclose. That said, the envisioned liquidity crisis is probably not a farmer's $80k loan, but rather big biz that abuse speculation with multimillion/billion dollar transactions. Also, a hearing before the foreclosure could be expedited to days or weeks; which on balance would not push the crisis further.

## 2.

Yes, county likely violated Picard's right to notice. *Mullane* requires reasonably calculated attempt at notice. That said, if the county learns it failed its first attempt at notice, it has a continuing obligation to try again. (*Flowers*) Here, the county sheriff knew the email bounced back and forwarded it to Q, who deleted it. This is particularly damning given that MBPA § 5(d)(ii) specifies that email must be sent. Picard's address is easily known to the sheriff/county so it would not be a "heroic effort" (*Dusenberry*) to mail the notice. While sheriff did leave a voicemail, *Greene* suggests even tacking a notice on the door is insufficient for eviction cases, so this is still insufficient (statutorily via MBPA § 5(d)(ii) and through case law, *Greene*).

## 3a.

Yes, Picard can likely bring both claims together.

**PJ:** All parties are Massachusetts residents so Massachusetts can exercise general PJ (G-IPJ) over parties. Federal court can piggyback off of state G-IPJ via 4(k)(1)(a).

**Venue:** § 1391(b)(1) venue is proper in district where all D's reside. Here, all D's reside in MA and there is only one federal court. Also property in question is in Massachusetts so venue also proper under § 1391(b)(2).

**SMJ:** For claim against Worf County, there is AU jurisdiction because COA based on violation of federal statute 42 U.S.C. § 1983. This is the anchoring claim that can allow state theft COA to be added under supplemental jurisdiction § 1367(a) because the two claims share the "same case or controversy" (that the seizure of Picard's property was unlawful). 1367(b) is not relevant because the anchoring claim is AU. State won't use its discretion under § 1367(c) to decline jurisdiction because the theft COA is not a novel issue of state law, doesn't dominate the anchor claim, and the anchor claim has not been dismissed. Additionally Picard can use a permissive joinder to sue both Borg and the County under Rule 20(a)(2) because both claims arise out of the same transaction/occurrence — the legality of the property seizure.

## 3b.

Picard likely needs to drop the theft charge against Borg. *Terry* counsels a 2-prong test to determine if there is a right to a jury trial. The first prong considers the historical context and analogizing as necessary. We don't have any historical analogs, so we move to step 2, which examines the remedies sought. Against Worf County, Picard is seeking an injunction which is an equitable remedy determined by a bench trial/judge; he's okay there. But the suit against Borg seeks compensatory damages which is remedy of law determined by a jury. Drop the theft charge to avoid a jury trial.
