**Question 1:**

First, Patil can claim she did not receive constitutional notice. In *Greene*, the Court held flyers on an apartment door to constitute insufficient notice. A facebook post may similarly be insufficient — it is easily missed on a wall covered in a myriad of other posts. The fact, then, that the Act permits notice through facebook post may per se render the Act unconstitutional, even when (unlike in this case), the specific party *did* receive actual notice (see *Wuchter*).

Even if facebook notice might *sometimes* be permissible, in Patil's case it was not. First, the bank knew there was a superior method of notice: they had been successfully corresponding with Patil by mail for weeks. Second, Patil's facebook away message may have alerted the bank that its notification had failed, requiring, under *Jones*, additional effort from the bank. This argument is not a slam dunk: in *Jones*, the notifying party received evidence of non-receipt by registered mail. Here, the away message was not specifically targeted at the bank; furthermore, there may be privacy-based policy reasons to not require a bank employee to read a mortgagee's facebook posts. Patil can also argue that the bank's sudden and unanticipated switch from one form of notice to another was per se unreasonable: a pattern of use may provoke reliance behavior in a party (here, Patil), and the sudden switch may suggest bad faith on the part of the bank. The bank may respond that Patil negligently failed to receive notice, as she should have been checking her facebook page. The reliance argument may counter this attack.

Beyond notice, Patil can point out four potential infractions of due process in the Housing Court: the lack of appealability, discovery, or court-funded representation, and the brief and rigid time frames. The first three elements won't render the system unconstitutional by themselves: *Vitek* suggests that appealability and discovery are not constitutionally required, and *Lassiter* closes off a right to counsel (Patil's potential injury — losing a home — is less severe than the injury the Court addressed in either case — being committed or losing custody of a child, respectively). Nevertheless, though the lack of these elements does not per se render the system unconstitutional, their absence removes significant safeguards which might otherwise mitigate the burdensome timeframe.

The Court has three extremely brief time frames, none of which may be extended for any reason. First, a party has only two weeks during which to receive notice and file an answer. There is no suggestion anywhere in the Act that any circumstance would merit an extension — not illness, the death of a loved one, nor an unexpected absence. The 14 days is thus a blunt instrument, inflexible and insensitive to the exigencies of human existence.

Second, the trial begins within two weeks of a party's answer, and that party must be prepared, on the first day of trial, to submit proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law and to navigate the complex VA rules of evidence. The lack of representation and discovery make this brief time frame especially burdensome. With no right to discovery, a party must gather all relevant evidence herself — within only two weeks. With no state-funded lawyer, she must understand the VA rules of evidence with no assistance. Indeed, Patil didn't understand the legal fine print of her mortgage, and it is likely that evicted mortgagees, as a class, often suffer from this legal confusion: such confusion suggests they especially need more time to prepare for a trial with complex legal rules. Evicted mortgagees are also likely to be poor; taking time off work to prepare for trial would thus be especially burdensome.

Third, a party has only seven days to vacate the premises of her home upon losing at trial. Again, the defining characteristics of the class in question render the rigidity of this timeframe especially onerous. This provision virtually guarantees that numerous poor Virginians will end up homeless. Taken together, these three brief, rigid time frames may make it impossible for someone like Patil to truly be heard.

A court will weigh Virginia's interests against Patil's. The state is facing a liquidity crisis that is in part caused by banks' inability to foreclose "promptly." Virginia thus has a clear interest in promptness which parallels the government's interest in *Matthews*. Nevertheless, a *Matthews* test helps here: comparing extendable time frames with those herein would reveal that a slightly more flexible timing scheme might meet the state's interests without burdening the mortgagee and sporting a high risk of error.

As an additional final factor, no jury is available. It isn't required as a matter of federal constitutional law (the 7th Amendment doesn't apply to the states), but if a case happened to turn on a credibility determination, a federal court might think the absence of a jury potentially significant.
