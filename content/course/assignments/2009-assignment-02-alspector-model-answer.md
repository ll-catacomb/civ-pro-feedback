# Assignment 2 Model Answer

## Part A

Hufflepuff should be able to sue York County in federal court. She can assert that York County has deprived her of her Fourteenth Amendment right to procedural due process under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, which provides a cause of action against a state actor who limits any federally protected rights. Because her claim arises under a federal statute, suing in federal court should not be an issue. Hufflepuff can use a few theories.

First, Hufflepuff can attack the notice provisions of the SCBHA. Under *Mullane*, the government is required to make a reasonably calculated effort to serve notice, and this should appear as if the government actually wants to give notice. Leaving messages on cell phones, generally, might be insufficient because people change cell numbers often because of loss or theft. Low-income mortgagors might have infrequent computer access, might not know how to read PDFs because they are unfamiliar with the format, or the email might not be received because of spam filters. In Hufflepuff's case specifically, the government knew that the email was never received, so they did not even fulfill their own level of sufficiency of notice, and they did not seem like they were making a reasonable effort. Under *Jones*, the government is required to follow up if it knows that the notice wasn't received. However, Hufflepuff was warned that she would be contacted via cell about the foreclosure, the bank confirmed her number, and she did indicate that she understood email by including it in her mortgage materials. The court might see Hufflepuff's disappearance as purposeful avoidance of the foreclosure. Nonetheless, the court will probably find that the county should stick with traditional notice, like certified mail and notice in hand, as required in § 5(a).

Second, SCBHA § 8 also poses a due process issue under *Mathews*. The *Mathews* factors are: (1) risk of erroneous deprivation, (2) claimant's interest in the property, and (3) the State's interest. The risk of error is large because the mortgagor might have some way to defend the suit, and the default judgment would stick despite the small 10-day window to file an answer (which might not even be enough time to find a lawyer). Like in *Doehr*, the private interest is high because it affects a person's property interest and, even more extreme than *Doehr*, § 8 would deprive them completely of shelter. This affects both the mortgagor and their family, which makes the private interest even larger. The bank's interest is low, because the benefit they receive from quickly obtaining title to the building is low. The bank also does not have to post a bond as a security for an unlawful eviction. However, the government presumably has a high interest, since the legislature called the crisis a "grave threat to the public interest" (§ 1), which weighs against Hufflepuff. The appeals process outlined in § 8(c) also provides due process difficulties — a person being foreclosed upon is unlikely to have enough money to pay five months of mortgage payments. Balancing the above, the court would most likely find § 8 to be a violation of due process.

Additionally, Hufflepuff could complain that she should be entitled to a court-appointed attorney (*Lassiter*). However, the court is unlikely to find this persuasive, as her liberty is not at stake. And as noted above, because of the small 10-day window between service and answer, an attorney might not have helped at all.

## Part B

In order to guarantee a bench trial, Hufflepuff should drop any claims for damages and only seek equitable remedies. Under the first element of the *Terry* test, it is unlikely that York could ask for a jury trial because there is no real historical analogy for attacking procedural due process. Therefore, turning to the second, and more heavily weighted, part of the *Terry* test, Hufflepuff's requested relief should be purely equitable.
