We can dismiss the complaint possibly for lack of SMJ and more likely for failure to state a claim.

## I. SMJ

### 1. AUJ

Per well-pleaded complaint rule, cause of action must be created by federal law (*Mottley*). Simple negligence is a state law cause of action; no AUJ over Count I.

Possible *Smith-Grable* exception for Count II, but unlikely. Negligence per se requires showing violation of the CPSC regulation, a federal law. If proven, the violation proves negligence; Buddy would certainly dispute it. Therefore, a substantial and disputed federal issue is embedded within state law cause of action. However, allowing S-G exception sweeps too many state-law cases into federal courts, especially given Congress's finding about increased drone use. Moreover, proving violation turns on factual issues such as alcohol level and drone identification (*Empire*). District court will probably not exercise discretion to extend AUJ.

### 2. DJ

DJ might exist over the case, depending on Bob's citizenship. AIC = $100k > $75K.

Buddy = RI, having lived there his whole life. For Bob's citizenship, start with his place of birth and see if domicile changed from RI, i.e. whether he established residence elsewhere without intention to leave at a particular time. While going to college usually does not change domicile, Bob moved into a condo in MA; driver's license, voting registration, marriage & cohabitation with MA citizen all signal intention to stay long-term. If so, then Bob = MA and DJ exists. However, the plan to move to Florida arguably disproves intention to stay, although its lack of specificity in time discounts its relevance.

## II. IPJ

IPJ exists. D.MA has no general jurisdiction over Buddy (= RI), but has specific jurisdiction. MA has long-arm statute going to limits of Constitution. Per DPC, Buddy operated the drone and caused the accident in MA, so minimum contacts link him to the forum (*International Shoe*). Visiting his friend supports defendant's convenience in going to forum; MA has interest in providing redress to victims of accidents in its territory, and its law will likely apply — so fair and reasonable. Per 4k1A, a federal court could exercise IPJ.

## III. Failure to State a Claim (FRCP 12(b)(6))

Under federal law, Bob's complaint will be dismissed. FRCP 8(a) represents plausibility pleading. Under *Iqbal*, ¶4 recites the elements of simple negligence that D breached duty of care and that his negligence caused harm. Likewise, ¶5 merely asserts D's violation of federal regulation. So we ignore those. The remaining allegations — D's operation of Syndrome drone and the same drone striking P — do not render it plausible that D was negligent, mostly due to their failure to mention D's drinking/alcohol level.

However, there is a VCOL issue here due to conflicting MA rule, which implements notice pleading, i.e., P need only put D on notice of basic events giving rise to litigation (see,

Conducting the Erie tests shows that complaint will likely be dismissed.

### 1. York

As explained above, FRCP vs. MA RCP is outcome determinative. Thus, follow MA rule; complaint dismissed.

### 2. Hanna Majority

We follow FRCP if it's valid and applicable. Federal rule is valid if it does not abridge/modify substantive right (REA). Under *Sibbach*, a rule is procedural if it really regulates procedure. Since FRCP 8 regulates process of writing complaint, it's arguably procedural and therefore valid.

**A. Scalia**

For applicability, we read rule in its plain meaning. FRCP 8 plainly applies to Bob's complaint, so no need to look at state statute. Follow federal rule; complaint dismissed.

**B. Ginsburg**

Consider policy behind state rule and see if federal rule can be narrowly construed to accommodate state policy. MA rule reflects plaintiff-friendly policy of facilitating access to courts, especially for unsophisticated litigants. However, the plausibility pleading of FRCP 8 implements a stricter standard than the notice pleading of MA rule, so it's difficult to construe FRCP 8 to defer to MA rule (i.e. FRCP is already narrower than MA rule). Even under Ginsburg, we probably follow federal rule; complaint dismissed.

### 3. Harlan Concurrence

State rule is substantive if it drives parties' primary pre-litigation conduct. Perhaps a lax pleading standard leads drone operators to be more careful and would-be plaintiffs to be more risk-tolerant, but it seems implausible that ordinary people consider pleading standards to calibrate their risk tolerance. The issue of what a complaint must include is likely procedural. Follow federal rule; case dismissed.
