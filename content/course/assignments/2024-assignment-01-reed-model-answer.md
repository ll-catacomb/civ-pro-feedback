## QUESTION ONE

### Arising-Under Jurisdiction

There is no arising-under jurisdiction. Breach of contract is a state law cause of action (*Mottley*), and there is no federal issue, so no *Smith-Grable*.

### Diversity Jurisdiction

Whether a court will have diversity jurisdiction depends on where the plaintiff and defendants are domiciled.

Clear cases:
- Perrin is domiciled in TX.
- Ishamael is domiciled in NV.

Less clear:

Tuon was originally a NY domiciliary. The question is whether her domicile has changed to CA now that she has rented a lot there and constructed her BYYKWB on it. *Mas* suggests not: We have no indication that Tuon intends to stay in CA indefinitely. BYYKWB is a pop-up-style bumper boats, and no other evidence suggests that Tuon plans to stay in CA.

Damodred was originally a TX domiciliary. The question is whether moving to NY, obtaining a job there, inquiring about buying an apartment, and looking at exclusively NY schools for her graduate education is enough to change her domicile to NY. If she has manifested an intent to stay in NY (*Mas*), then she is a NY domiciliary now. This is close.

Ishamael: There is complete diversity (*Strawbridge*).

AIC > 75k? Yes, 100k (§ 1332), for all defendants. So, there is DJ for Ishamael.

If Damodred is domiciled in TX, then there is no complete diversity. If Damodred is domiciled in NY, then the answer is the same as Ishamael and Tuon.

### Supplemental Jurisdiction

§ 1367(b) prevents the use of a DJ anchoring claim when the result will be a *Strawbridge* conflict (*Owen v. Kroger*), which again depends on Damodred's domicile, so § 1367(b) is not available here.

## QUESTION TWO

Assume no defendants consent to IPJ, nor a tag, in any jurisdiction. No defendant, except Damodred regarding NY, has anything close to systematic and continuous contacts (*Daimler*) with states other than their domiciles. For all analyses, federal courts piggy-back via 4k1a. All states have long-arms to constitutional limits.

### California?

**Specific IPJ (Tuon/Damodred)?**

Minimum contacts?
It is likely that Tuon and Damodred have sufficient contacts with CA for that court to exert PJ over them. Contacts relating subject matter, defendant, and the forum (*Kulko*) are as follows: the accident took place in CA, the state to which both of these defendants temporarily moved to (i.e., purposefully availed themselves of, *Hanson*) to make money from the BYYKWB business/job.

Fair/reasonable? (*McGee*)
Tuon is operating his business there, Damodred is working there and presumably living there for the job, so it cannot be too inconvenient a forum. It is convenient for plaintiff; though he's from TX, he does visit CA. Witnesses/evidence are in CA where the accident took place. As the situs of the accident, CA law will likely apply.

**Specific IPJ (Ishmael)?**

Minimum contacts?
This is almost certainly a "no." There is no evidence that Ishmael has ever been to CA or even knew that Tuon would take the BYYKWB to CA. However, maybe as in *McGee* Ishamel "reached in" to CA to sell his goods? But unlike that case, the money here was exchanged in NV, delivery was in NV, and it was Tuon who took the BYYKWB across state lines.

Fair/reasonable? (*McGee*)
Ishamael has no contacts with CA. That said, NV is not that far from CA. Also, it might be convenient for Perrin, for the reasons stated above. Witnesses/evidence are all in CA. And, the court will likely apply CA law.

### Nevada?

Damodred has no contacts at all there, and thus no minimal contacts, so no.

## QUESTION THREE

A federal court sitting in diversity will apply the HCOL rules of state they are in (*Klaxon*). Whether the defendants can argue contributory negligence concerns the standard of care people owe each other in day-to-day life, so it is clearly substantive for HCOL purposes.

### California/Texas (First Restatement)

We apply *lex loci delicti*. Since the injury took place in CA, apply CA law (*Great Alabama Railroad*). Yes, contributory negligence defense is available.

### New York (Second Restatement)

We begin with a *lex* presumption, which means presuming that we will apply CA law (*Babcock*). The second step is to consider factors.

- The evidence is in CA.
- Convenience is a wash, since the court can easily apply either law.
- Contributory negligence (CA law) is more protective of NY resident (defendant) in this case than NY law, so there is really no policy reason for a NY district court to depart from applying CA law.

There is nothing here that overcomes the presumption, so apply CA law. Yes, contributory negligence defense is available.
