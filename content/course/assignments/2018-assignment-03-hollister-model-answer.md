## Question 1

Weasley lives in New Hampshire, and it looks like she intends to stay there. She is a NH domiciliary. Aberforth is a Maine domiciliary. Assuming Aberforth sues for the full amount of his medical and related bills, AIC > $75k and diversity is met. NH has general IPJ over Weasley because she is a NH domiciliary, and Maine has specific IPJ because the suit arises out of conduct in Maine (*Kane*): Maine has a long-arm statute that goes to the limits of the constitution. Weasley established minimum contacts when she purposely availed herself of Maine when she drove through Portland, hitting Aberforth. She has minimum contacts and specific IPJ in Maine is fair and reasonable. That makes state and federal court IPJ OK under FRCP 4(k)(1)(A). Venue is proper in NH through § 1391(b)(1), and in Maine through § 1391(b)(2). So, there are four possible systems in which Aberforth could file suit: NH state court, Maine state court, and federal court in NH or Maine. Maine federal and state courts, and NH federal court, require plausibility pleading (*Twiqbal*, *Malfoy v. Patil*), which creates a higher burden for plaintiffs to meet on pleading. NH state court uses notice pleading (*Longbottom v. Parkinson*), which requires only that Aberforth show "discernable grounds for relief" sufficient to lead to discovery (*Dioguardi*). As there is a valid and applicable federal law governing pleadings (FRCP 8(a), via the Rules Enabling Act), NH fed court will apply federal procedural law (*Hanna*), which in this case is plausibility pleading (*Twiqbal*). Or, at least, that is likely enough to be a serious cause of concern. NH state court is therefore the least burdensome system for Aberforth. As an out-of-state plaintiff, Aberforth might be worried about home cooking, and prefer federal court. But the heightened burden of plausibility pleading is probably more limiting than home cooking. I recommend filing in NH state court.

## Question 2

1. Plaintiff Aberforth Dumbledore is a domiciliary of Maine. Defendant Ginny Weasley is a domiciliary of New Hampshire. The amount in controversy, $150,000, exceeds the value specified by 28 U.S.C. § 1332. Jurisdiction is proper under 28 U.S.C. § 1332(a)(1).
2. Dumbledore is entitled to relief for Weasley's negligence related to a car accident that occurred on March 15, 2018.
3. At 10:30 pm on March 15, 2018, plaintiff Dumbledore was knocked to the ground by a car while crossing the street in front of 12 Grimmauld Place, Portland, Maine.
4. At the time, Dumbledore was wearing a brightly-colored reflective shirt.
5. Immediately before being struck, Dumbledore heard a screeching of tires.
6. Immediately after he was struck, Dumbledore observed defendant Weasley emerging from the driver's side of the vehicle that hit him.
7. When Weasley's companion, Harry Potter, approached Dumbledore, Dumbledore detected a smell of alcohol.
8. In April of 2018, Dumbledore was diagnosed with degeneration of his hip vasculature.
9. Dumbledore's physician has advised that this degeneration was most likely caused by trauma to the area.

10. Trauma from a car accident is the type of trauma that causes degeneration.
11. Dumbledore's physician identified the degeneration as life-threatening.
12. Following immediate surgery and related treatment of the injury, Dumbledore incurred $150,000 in medical, physical therapy, and medication bills.

## Question 3

The First Circuit should grant Aberforth's motion to dismiss. § 1291 grants Courts of Appeals jurisdiction over appeals from final decisions of district courts, but decisions aren't final until relief has been granted (*Liberty Mutual*). As Ginny filed immediately, there has been no relief and the District Court's judgment isn't final. This is a suit for damages only, so § 1292(a)(1) doesn't apply, and there is no indication of any difficult issue that calls for double discretion, so § 1292(b) most likely doesn't apply. The litigation was over a single claim so no Rule 54(b) jurisdiction. Likewise, there are no collateral orders so no *Cohen* exception. This is not a class action so Rule 23(f) doesn't apply. Finally, there is no reason to believe that the District Court judge has exhibited a pattern of bad behavior meriting a § 1651 writ of mandamus. There is no appellate jurisdiction, and the First Circuit should grant the motion.
