## Assignment 2 — Civil Procedure 2, 2016

### Question 1: Motions to Dismiss

As a threshold matter, the EDVA must apply Virginia's choice of law rules (*Klaxon*). Virginia uses the First Restatement, so the applicable substantive law is determined by "where the blood is" — i.e., the location of the tort. The drone crash occurred in Roanoke, Virginia, so Virginia substantive law governs all three counts.

#### Count 1 — Negligent Operation Against Oliver Wood

Virginia requires recklessness, not negligence, for drone crash personal injury claims. Count 1 pleads negligent operation, which is legally insufficient under Virginia law. The EDVA should grant the motion to dismiss Count 1. The facts as pleaded — that Wood operated a drone he himself had previously reported as defective — may support a recklessness theory, so Kingsley should be given leave to amend to reframe the claim as reckless operation.

Under *Iqbal/Twombly*, a complaint must plead factual content that makes a claim plausible, not merely possible. Even assuming the court were to construe Count 1 liberally, "negligent operation" does not state a cognizable claim under Virginia law regardless of how well-pleaded the facts are. Dismissal with leave to amend is appropriate.

#### Count 2 — Negligent Maintenance Against Chudley Cannons

Same analysis as Count 1. Virginia's recklessness requirement means negligent maintenance is insufficient. Count 2 should be dismissed. The underlying facts may support a recklessness claim, but negligence alone does not state a claim under Virginia law.

#### Count 3 — Reckless Maintenance Against Chudley Cannons

Count 3 survives. Recklessness under Virginia law means intentionally taking an action highly likely to cause injury. The Cannons received reports of the steering malfunction from three operators, including Wood, and took no corrective action. Continuing to deploy a drone with a known, repeatedly-reported defect that had caused crashes constitutes intentional inaction in the face of near-certain injury. Under *Twombly/Iqbal*, Count 3 is plausible on its face and states a viable claim. Motion to dismiss Count 3 should be denied.

### Question 2: Motion to Transfer

#### 2(a) — Whether to Transfer

<!-- page: 2 -->

Under § 1404(a), the court weighs (1) whether the case could have been filed in the transferee district, (2) the convenience of parties and witnesses, and (3) the interest of justice.

The Chudley Cannons are a West Virginia corporation headquartered in Wheeling, WV. SDWV has personal jurisdiction over the Cannons and venue is proper there. The case could have been filed in SDWV. Oliver Wood is a West Virginia citizen; SDWV would also have personal jurisdiction over him.

Convenience factors favoring transfer: the Cannons' main operations, employees, and maintenance records are in West Virginia; most documentary evidence of the defective Tornado 360 and the reports about the steering malfunction would be located there. Convenience factors against transfer: Kingsley is a Virginia citizen; the crash occurred in Virginia; local Virginia witnesses (medical providers, other spectators) are in Virginia.

On balance, because the core issue is the Cannons' knowledge of the defect and their failure to act — a matter centered on their West Virginia operations — transfer to SDWV is likely appropriate.

#### 2(b) — Law Applied After Transfer

Under *Van Dusen v. Barrack*, when a defendant moves for transfer under § 1404(a), the transferee court applies the choice of law rules of the transferor court. The transferor is EDVA (Virginia), which uses the First Restatement. Under the First Restatement, Virginia law applies because the crash occurred there. The SDWV therefore applies Virginia substantive law — the recklessness standard — to Kingsley's claims.

The rationale for *Van Dusen* is that transfer should not change the law applicable to the merits. A defendant should not be able to forum-shop for favorable substantive law by moving for a convenience transfer.
