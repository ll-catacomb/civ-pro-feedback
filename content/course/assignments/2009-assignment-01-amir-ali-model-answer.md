## Question (a)

The best strategy for getting the case to MD federal court would be to remove the case based on diversity jurisdiction. Since the amount in controversy requirement is satisfied and no defendant is domiciled in MD, we can remove to federal court if we can establish complete diversity. To get complete diversity, we need to establish that Pettigrew and Diggle are domiciled in different states (Weasely is not an issue, since she is domiciled in D.C. and Pettigrew is definitely not domiciled in D.C.). Our best case is to argue that Pettigrew is domiciled in VA and that Diggle is domiciled in SC or Barbados. We would point to the fact that Pettigrew has lived in VA for four years, purchased a condo, registered to vote, and received a VA driver's license – all of which makes it look like he intends to stay in VA. On the other hand, Diggle only went to VA for college and nothing indicates that he ever intended to stay there; therefore, we would contend that he is still domiciled in SC, where he was born and raised, or that he is now domiciled in Barbados. There are also other possible arguments; for example, it would also be possible to argue that Pettigrew is domiciled in SC and Diggle is domiciled in VA or Barbados.

Alternatively, to make the case for complete diversity an easy one, we could get Diggle to move for dismissal based on lack of IPJ. None of the facts suggest that Diggle has the pervasive contacts required to establish general jurisdiction, and Diggle probably does not have sufficient minimum contacts in MD for specific jurisdiction. *Int'l Shoe*. None of the facts suggest that Diggle has ever been to MD and Diggle had no idea that Weasely would take his car into MD, so there was no "purposeful availment" here. *Cf. Kulko*. If we can get Diggle out of the suit, it would be easy to remove to MD federal court because there is no question that Pettigrew and Weasely are domiciled in different states.

## Question (b)

We probably could not get the court to transfer this case to Virginia Federal Court under 1404(a) or 1406. These provisions only allow a district court to transfer a case to another district where that case might/could have been brought. Presumably SMJ would be fine in VA federal court, given that we are already in MD federal court. However, it is not absolutely clear that VA could have exercised IPJ over Weasely. Presumably Weasely has some contacts in VA, given her relationships with [at least] two people in the state. Weasely also availed herself to VA law by picking Diggle up, but this may not be sufficient to say that Pettigrew's negligence suit arises out of Weasely's contacts with VA. Finally, Pettigrew probably could not have brought this case in Virginia Federal Court because he could not have laid venue under § 1391(a). *See Hoffman v. Blasky*. We could try to argue that Pettigrew could have laid venue under § 1391(a)(2) because a substantial part of the events occurred in VA. Perhaps if almost all of the driving took place in VA we would have an argument; otherwise, it appears as though we're sunk under § 1391.

## Question (c)

We could definitely argue FNC. Our strongest argument would be that D.C. is a more convenient forum. Specifically, we would point to the fact that key evidence (the car) and key witnesses (Ernie & hospital staff) are located in D.C. We could also argue that, although the *crash* took place in MD, the negligence at issue actually took place in D.C.

Additionally, at least one of the defendants is a domicile of D.C.; whereas, none of the litigants are domiciled in MD. The MD court may still choose to hold onto the case, given that MD law will likely be applied because the last event necessary, the crash, took place in MD. *See First Restatement*.
