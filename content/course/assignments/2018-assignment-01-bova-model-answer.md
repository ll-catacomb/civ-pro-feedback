## Question 1

Probably can't dismiss on SMJ. There may be diversity: AIC is $150K (>$75K) and parties are citizens of different states (§ 1332); Gringotts is a Delaware and NY citizen, and Alicia could be domiciled in Texas based on going to college, substantial assets, job offer, shopping for condo, asking boyfriend to move there, and saying she'll never leave. However Alicia may still be domiciled in Delaware if she's not considered permanently moved (doesn't yet own the condo, hasn't started the job), in which case complete diversity does not exist. But there's still FQ-SMJ under *Smith*-*Grable*: it's a state cause of action, but there's a federal issue regarding the written contract and specification of bond rating levels under TBCIAA. This is a substantial issue invoking serious federal interests — Congress wants to have the power to address investment problems contributing to the Great Recession and better regulate investments. It's disputed because Gringotts will fight Alicia's version of the story to avoid paying damages. It won't upset the federal-state balance of work — many such cases could end up in federal court anyway under diversity jurisdictions due to corporations' citizenship, and fraudulent investments of this type aren't so common they'd overwhelm federal court system (compared e.g. to divorce). Though the federal issue isn't purely legal, the interest is sufficiently substantial to override this.

Can't dismiss on IPJ. Texas can't exercise general IPJ over Gringotts — Gringotts isn't a Texas citizen (it's incorporated in Delaware and PPOB is in NY). There's no evidence a large proportion of Gringotts' business occurs in Texas (only one branch there), so no general IPJ under *Daimler*'s "at home" principle either. However there's specific IPJ because Texas' long-arm goes to limits of Constitution, and there are minimum contacts linking Alicia, Gringotts, and Texas (Gringotts has a Texas branch, Alicia signed contract and opened account there). *International Shoe*. There are no problems of fairness/reasonableness because Gringotts opted to do business in Texas and thus "purposefully availed" itself of Texas law. *Hanson v. Deckla*.

Gringotts CAN move to transfer based on improper venue in NDTX. There's no venue under § 1391(b)(1) because NDTX courthouse lacks IPJ over Gringotts (no branch there, no contacts at all); nor under § 1391(b)(2) because no events happened in NDTX (no minimum contacts); nor § 1391(b)(3) because it could've been brought in another court that has both IPJ and venue (see next paragraphs). NDNY would fit easily because it's Gringotts' PPOB.

Gringotts could move to transfer to SDTX because IPJ is okay anywhere in Texas (see analysis above) and venue is proper under § 1391(b)(1) or § 1391(b)(2) — there are minimum contacts linking Gringotts to that district, and most of the events happened there. This transfer would likely be granted because witnesses like Bellatrix are there, and it's where Alicia lives so it's convenient for her.

Gringotts could move to transfer to EDLA because IPJ is okay (minimum contacts linking Gringotts, suit, and state since Gringotts has a branch there and thus "purposefully availed" itself of LA law, Alicia deposited money there, and Alicia received Gringotts' email there), and venue is okay under § 1391(b)(1) (again minimum contacts). This transfer would likely be granted because some of the events happened there, some witnesses are there (e.g. person who helped Alicia deposit the $150,000), and it's not too inconvenient for Alicia (it borders Texas, she's already traveled there).

Gringotts could move to transfer to Delaware or NDNY because they have general IPJ over Gringotts, and venue is proper under § 1391(b)(1) (Gringotts has residency in both districts). However transfer to either of these courts is NOT likely to be granted — they're inconvenient for Alicia (she has no links to either state), no events happened there, no witnesses there, and those courts would have to apply a different state's law (TX or LA).

## Question 2

Yes punitive damages if Louisiana law applies, no if Texas law applies. HCOL analysis is required. Under *Klaxon* federal courts normally apply COL rules of state where they're sitting, which leads to Louisiana COL rules. Under *Van Dusen* a transfer should be nothing more than a change of courts, assuming venue was proper in the transferor court (happy court rule). In this case, the transferor court (NDTX) was NOT happy because venue was improper (see above). Therefore the *Van Dusen* rule is not applicable here, and the EDLA court should apply the COL rules of state where it's sitting — Louisiana. Louisiana uses First Restatement, so we need to find "where the blood is" — where the injury actually occurred. Alicia learned of the fraud in Louisiana (that's where she received the email saying she had $0), so Louisiana law applies. Since Alicia did in fact discover that she suffered the loss/harm (that's what the email told her), the last element of fraud in the inducement in Louisiana could be proven. Thus Alicia can get punitive damages assuming she wins the suit.
