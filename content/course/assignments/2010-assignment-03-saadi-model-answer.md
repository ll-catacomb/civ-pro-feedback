# Assignment 3: Model Answer

---

According to *Semtek*, the source of law is federal common law. Pansy (PP) might try to employ nonmutual offensive collateral estoppel (NOCE) against Weasley & Weasley (WW); however, it is uncertain whether she could have joined in Luna's (LL) suit, which implicates policy concerns courts have expressed in their hesitation to apply NOCE.

Issue preclusion probably does not apply. The issues in PP's case are the same: tortious interference with contractual obligations and WW's claim regarding the constitutionality of the EIA. Given that LL's case concerned only these issues, they were probably necessary for the decision orally to grant LL's but deny WW's motion for summary judgment. (*Rios*.) However, LL's case probably was not actually and fully litigated. (*Cromwell*.) Although the judge issued an oral statement of her decision, the case was settled before final judgment was entered. Not only does the lack of an actual decision render this case inappropriate for issue preclusion, but policy considerations also weigh against it. Even though applying issue preclusion might serve judicial efficiency, precluding WW from reasserting its claims on the unconstitutionality of the EIA would greatly disadvantage WW, especially if WW has no other possible defenses, as it would automatically trigger liability. Furthermore, other potential plaintiffs probably exist from the election incident. If WW is issue precluded, they can keep suing and asserting NOCE against WW until an inconsistent judgment arises—10 or 300 suits from PP's—which is unfair to WW. Thus, issue preclusion probably should not apply.

Given that the "same party" requirement has been relaxed, *Blonder-Tongue*, if issue preclusion were applicable, PP might attempt to invoke NOCE, thereby barring WW from raising the defenses of unconstitutionality it employed in LL's suit in PP's subsequent suit. (*Parklane*.) Most of the *Parklane* factors are satisfied. There are no prior inconsistent judgments. WW also had sufficient incentive to litigate in LL's suit given the high punitive damages at stake. (However, if WW believed it unlikely that punitive damages would have been awarded, whether WW had sufficient incentive might be more uncertain.) While it is possible that some procedural elements may differ from federal to state court, there are no major procedural advantages available to WW in state court that were not available to WW in federal court. Finally, PP, as a Massachusetts resident, probably could not have joined in the first suit. Given that LL likely is a NH resident and WW is a Massachusetts corporation, if PP had joined in the first suit, she would have destroyed complete diversity under § 1332. (*Strawbridge*.) However, if a § 1331 arising-under claim is available PP could have joined the first suit. PP's claims probably arise out of a common nucleus of operative facts and are part of the same case or controversy as LL's claims; therefore, it might seem as if she could have joined via supplemental jurisdiction. (*Gibbs*.) Yet the EIA did not provide a federal cause of action. Thus, § 1367(b) would have barred PP from joining, because PP would have been a party seeking to intervene as a plaintiff under Rule 24, thereby destroying complete diversity. The only other possibility is if LL's case fulfills the *Smith* factors, in which a case may arise under federal law even though the state law creates the cause of action. Here, LL's claim of tortious interference with contractual relations was indeed a state law cause of action that depends upon interpretation of the federal EIA and its constitutionality. Additionally, the *Smith* test requires that the issue is a legal issue (as interpretation of a statute is), and both important and seriously disputable (which this case might not be). Thus, PP might have tried to join LL's former suit.

Regardless of whether PP can invoke NOCE, courts have discretion in determining whether plaintiffs generally may. (*Parklane*.) If PP could have joined in the first suit, it seems unfair to allow her to invoke NOCE now. First of all, if WW did not have sufficient incentive to litigate (see above) or if it did not foresee the possibility of future lawsuits, allowing the use of NOCE would be unfair to WW. Furthermore, PP intentionally might have waited for LL to litigate the issue, so that if LL lost, PP would know what strategies to avoid, and if LL won, PP could simply assert NOCE against WW.

If PP could not have joined in LL's suit, however, she might be allowed to use NOCE now, as the concerns of PP gaming the system and unfairness to WW are greatly minimized. WW would not suffer from procedural disadvantages that would render the use of nonmutual offensive collateral estoppel unfair in this case (there is no obvious change in the rules that put WW at a disadvantage by being in state court) and could still raise a different defense, as WW would only be barred from raising unconstitutionality of the EIA. (*Cromwell*.)
