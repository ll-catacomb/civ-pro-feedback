## Question 1

The *Matthews v. Eldridge* analysis to determine PDP demands for Buffy's access to counsel weighs against right to counsel. Buffy's private interest — damages — does not outweigh government interest in reducing costs, and *Lassiter* suggests a presumption against counsel unless physical liberty is at stake.

## Question 2

The balance of the four F.R.C.P. 65 PI factors weighs against granting the motion, mostly based on the lack of irreparable harm:

(1) Buffy is likely to prevail on the merits. The duty element of her negligence claim is assumed satisfied. Breach is also likely satisfied: based on the visible dents in the car that did not result from its collision with Buffy and the fact that the car went over the curb to hit Buffy and Dawn, Chase probably did not take ordinary care to inspect the car before sending it Buffy's way. Same for XH, but might be more tenuous because they gave cars to Faith. But-for cause is satisfied. Without the car going over the curb, Buffy would not be injured. Injury is satisfied.

(2) There is no irreparable harm absent relief because Buffy's injury already occurred. The court can compensate with damages. Money (that can be awarded/reimbursed later) can pay for her transportation.

(3) Balance of the equities does not tip in Buffy's favor. The harm resulting if PI issued to XH & Faith is monetary; the harm resulting if PI not issued to Buffy is also monetary.

(4) Public interest would not be served by granting PI. If there is a regulatory scheme governing self-driving cars, would damage that scheme (*Amgen*). If no regulatory scheme, PI could chill innovation of self-driving cars.

## Question 3

Applying F.R.C.P. 56, Buffy's motion for summary judgment would be granted. Buffy must show no genuine issue of material fact and that she is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. Using Brennan's framework in *Celotex*, all facts are reasonably viewed in the light most favorable to Faith and XH, the nonmoving party. Buffy, the moving party, bears the burden of persuasion for liability. She must provide evidence that if unrebutted would require a rational trier of fact to find in her favor. Buffy did do so through submitting the Maclay and Angel affidavits, which, together, show that there was a lack of due care in inspecting the trucks after obvious evidence that they were malfunctioning, and that this breach was causally connected with Buffy's injury by the specific truck.

The burden of production shifts to XH and Faith. They might have produced evidence of a genuine issue of material fact based on the Chase affidavit, which by averring adequate testing conflicts with a finding of a lack of due care based on Buffy's attached affidavits. However, an analogy to *Scott v. Harris* should lead to a court to grant Buffy's motion. Based on the photos of

If VA's heightened summary judgment standard applies (Section 6.7, including amended V.R.C.P. 56), Buffy's motion for summary judgment will be denied. She did not provide the required affidavit from a psychiatrist. *Erie*:

Under *York*, the difference between state and federal law is outcome determinative of summary judgment. Use state law and deny Buffy's motion for summary judgment.

**Harlan:** difference in V.R.C.P. 56 and federal law likely would not impact Buffy's pre-litigation conduct, only the acquisition of affidavits in preparation for litigation. However, the heightened standard's impact on investors might compel companies to move to VA and alter their conduct before litigation. **Scalia:** F.R.C.P. 56 validity measured by REA (doesn't abridge, enlarge or modify a substantive right, as it "really regulates" procedure (*Sibbach*)). Applicability: governs the exact issue of what is required for summary judgment motion. Apply F.R.C.P. and grant summary judgment.

**Ginsburg:** Agrees with Scalia on validity. For applicability, construe federal rule with sensitivity to important state interests. Express state interest articulated in "Giles" Act against deterring investors from self-driving vehicle businesses in VA. Might say that F.R.C.P. and V.R.C.P. don't conflict because F.R.C.P. is merely silent on the issue of affidavit requirement. Apply V.R.C.P. and deny Buffy's summary judgment motion.
