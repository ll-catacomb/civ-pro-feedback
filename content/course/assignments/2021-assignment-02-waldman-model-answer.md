## Q1

No SMJ/IPJ/Venue/Process arguments. FRCP 12(b)(1–5)). Defendant has three arguments that plaintiff failed to state claim. FRCP 12(b)(6). FDC is sitting in diversity — it'll conduct VCOL analysis to determine whether to apply federal or ND law for each issue.

Application of ND law on any issue will lead to dismissal (out-of-state plaintiffs, no attached medical bills, and a lawsuit filed in month 26, not within 24 months, of the misconduct),[^1] while application of federal law means suit will proceed. Under *York*, FDC would dismiss under any of these arguments. This is true even for FNC because there are public interest factors favoring proceedings, and because the alleged misconduct, drug ingestion, and sickness all occurred in different states, leaving no obviously superior forum. *Piper*.

### Argument 1: FNC

**Hanna/Scalia:** Likely substantive/state law applies. Federal FNC is FCL. *Piper*. So the RDA controls. *Erie*'s twin aims favor dismissal here. Applying federal law would mean different results in state court (dismissal) vs. FDC (no dismissal), resulting in an incentive to forum shop if a lawyer wants to keep the litigation in ND.

**Hanna/Ginsberg:** Same as *Hanna* (Ginsberg/SG = *Hanna* Majority for FCL).

**Harlan:** Likely substantive/state law applies. Assuming the company ships outside the US often, ND's unwillingness to hear cases emerging from inevitable harm occurring from the company's products would inform the company's primary conduct. (If ND FDC hears these cases, then company might cease selling to non-US customers, or take more safety precautions as it does so.) However, if the company is small/negligence is rare, then applying FCL would *not* alter primary conduct.

### Argument 2: Pleading-Req

ND statute requires dismissal because plaintiff didn't attach their medical bills to their complaint. The ND requirement is absent in FRCP 8(a)(2), which doesn't require supporting evidence with a complaint (or speak to the issue).

**Scalia/Hanna:** Likely procedural/federal — no dismissal. FRCP 8 is valid (FRCP derived from REA, it "really regulates procedure," *Sibbach*, because what's required in a complaint concerns the manner and means of litigation). FRCP 8 is likely applicable, since it gives uniform pleading standards — it governs what's required in a complaint, and there's no requirement of medical bills (or any other attachment).

**Ginsburg/SG:** Substantive/state, favors dismissal. FRCP still valid (see Scalia argument), but not applicable to the question of whether medical bills are required — FDC will likely construe FRCP narrowly

### Argument 3: SOL

**Hanna/Scalia:** Likely Procedural/Federal law. This is a statute, so FDC is unlikely to construe federal law narrowly.

## Q2

Each argument hinged on a different source of federal law: Argument 1/FNC was based on a state conflict with FCL; Argument 2/Pleading Requirements was based on conflict with FRCP; Argument 3/SOL was based on federal statute. These bring about different Erie tests, particularly under the two Hanna majority frameworks.

## Q3

Pleading issue presents incoherence concerns. If the FDC applies SG/Ginsberg, plaintiffs would have to ensure their claim complied with state *and* federal pleading requirements. This would be burdensome on pro-se/low-resource litigants. FRCP can be modified via SCOTUS-overseen process (REA). FRCP 8 could *clearly state* that state pleading requirements are the *exclusive* requirements in diversity cases. While this might create forum-shopping (litigants looking for states with favorable pleading requirements), it'll help litigants know what they have to file *before* filing. Confusing pleading requirements don't advance *Erie*'s policy goals, federalism, or federal supremacy — they instead create unnecessary confusion.

SOL issue portends incoherence. A FDC could apply *length* of SOL from state law while applying the federal standard for *when* SOL begins to run (possibly different from state's). This threatens incoherence — legislatures determine SOL *length* based on what causes them to run.

FDC unlikely to dismiss on pleading basis — this doesn't further *Erie*'s goals. First, this solution resembles the "general law" *Erie* disparaged — it's a judge-made compromise (judge-made raises separation of powers concerns) that contorts federal law (it's not clear that there's a better solution, since Ginsberg's approach would constitute judicial contortion to minimize federal law). Second, it usurps state power — ND clearly desires to limit the volume of litigation in its courts, and this upsets that. Usurpation of state power is a federalism concern.

But allowing claim without medical paperwork does advance equal protection principles. There's equal protection in requiring non-ND domiciles to meet abnormal pleading requirements beyond that which exist in other states. *Cf. Swift*.
