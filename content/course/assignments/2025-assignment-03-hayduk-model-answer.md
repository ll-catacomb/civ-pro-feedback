## Question 1

### IRJ

Because the seizure took place in July and the suit wasn't filed until September, that was not a "reasonable time" after the repossession per the USPL. Though the parties agreed notice was reasonable, the USPL doesn't apply because the in rem lawsuit was faulty.

Under the narrow view of *Shaffer*, IRJ is lacking because the NFT isn't in the forum state, Maine. Because the NFT is incorporeal, it's unclear where the NFT is. Maine doesn't make sense from a policy perspective because as Lurch's PPoB, it is moveable and Pugsley may not know that's where Lurch hosts its public-facing website. Pugsley could argue it was in ND because the NFT was in the Fester environment there and everything necessary to host it was there (e.g., programmers, servers, Fester code / data), though only Lurch would know where it stores data and because data may be stored in multiple places, the NFT might exist in multiple places. The NFT could be in MA (*Balk*), because the NFT codes and information necessary for ownership transferred to Pugsley. Based on administration ease, it could make sense for the NFT to be in DE, since it's easy to find that Lurch is incorporated in DE and places of incorporation don't move.

Under the broad view of *Shaffer*, with no argument for GIPJ, we need SIPJ. We have statutory authorization (provided by the USPL) and the Maine long-arm statute that goes to the limits of the Constitution. However, *International Shoe* demands minimal contacts, which are lacking. Though this case is like *McGee* because the dispute is about a contract with a ME citizen without an intermediary, Pugsley did not purposefully avail himself of Maine's laws (*Hanson*) just because he bought the NFT from Lurch whose PPoB was in Maine and its public-facing internet website/presence was administered from servers in Maine. Pugsley purchased the NFT at his home in MA and he had no notice he might be hauled into court in Maine (*WW Volkswagon*). F&R factors (*McGee*) also disfavor ME because ME is inconvenient for Pugsley and the evidence of Lurch's backdoor in the Fester environment is in ND, though some evidence may exist in ME (e.g., public-facing servers).

### PDP

Like the pre-judgment seizure in *Doehr*, this seizure likely violates the *Mathews* test. 1. The private interests of Pugsley and Lurch are relatively balanced as they both assert a property interest in the NFT. The NFT implicates subsistence for neither Pugsley nor Lurch (*Mathews*), though trading this type of property was Pugsley's main source of income. 2. There is a high risk of erroneous deprivation compared to alternatives. Through Lurch's backdoor, the seizure takes effect instantly and requires no presentation of evidence prior to the deprivation. To reduce risk of erroneous determination, the court could require Lurch to post bond, allow Pugsley to counter-bond to resist seizure, and/or hold a hearing prior to ordering the seizure. 3. The government also has an interest in accuracy and low adjudication costs.

## Question 2

The 14th amendment and FRCP 4 address what is necessary to assert IPJ so as not to deprive a person of due process. Though the 14th amendment broadly requires due process, Rule 4(k)(1)(A) ensures that a federal court can have IPJ over a defendant only if the state in

<!-- page: 2 -->

which the court is located would have jurisdiction. For a suit filed in federal court where the court does not have GIPJ over a defendant, to establish SIPJ over a defendant, the court requires a long-arm statute, minimal contacts linking the defendant, the subject matter of the litigation, and the forum, and that the forum is fair and reasonable, per *McGee*.

Though the details in FRCP 4 ensures that a defendant is only subject to litigation where a plaintiff has followed every step determined by the Supreme Court via the Rules Enabling Act, the system can make it difficult for a plaintiff to sue a large foreign corporation in a court that's accessible to the plaintiff, such as in *McIntyre*. The *McIntyre* plurality justified the ruling that SIPJ did not exist based on state sovereignty and respecting the defendant's rights. However, if Congress passed a long-arm statute that deputized the federal courts to exercise SIPJ where the corporation maintains minimal contacts with the US, instead of just with NJ, the *McIntyre* case may have been ruled differently. Congress and the federal courts may worry this would overwhelm the US court system with additional litigation and discourage foreign companies from doing business in the US to avoid creating minimal contacts. However, I believe this would be an improvement in holding foreign companies who do harm accountable and allow recovery for aggrieved plaintiffs. As Justice Ginsburg noted in her dissent, for a large corporation like *McIntyre* it should have foreseen being haled into court in NJ, or at least somewhere in the US. What is fair and reasonable for a giant corporation that sells its products worldwide is different from what is fair and reasonable for an individual who can be subject to SIPJ by visiting a forum temporarily, such as in *Burnham*.
