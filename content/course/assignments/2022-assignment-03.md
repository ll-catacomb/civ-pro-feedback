Jean-Luc Picard, a farm-hand, was born in western Massachusetts and lived his life there. He worked on Kahn Farm in Worf County, Massachusetts, toiling daily caring for cows, chickens, and tribbles.

Borg Bank, Inc. was incorporated in and had its principal place of business in Boston, Massachusetts. Massachusetts has only one federal district court, the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts. Massachusetts has a long-arm statute providing that its courts may exercise any type of jurisdiction so long as doing so is consistent with the limits of the U.S. Constitution.

Picard liked the countryside. In January of 2022, Picard signed a mortgage contract with Borg Bank so as to buy a one-bedroom house on a five-acre plot of land in the woods of Worf County, Massachusetts. The purchase price for the property was $100,000. Picard paid $20,000 (his life savings) in cash as down payment to the seller, and he borrowed $80,000 from Borg to pay the remainder. The contract between Picard and Borg specified that the Bank would retain legal title to the property, but that the Worf County Property Registry would show both the Bank's and Picard's ownership interests in it. The contract obligated Picard to pay $2000 per month in principal and interest until the loan was repaid. Picard provided Borg with his cell phone number and his only email address, picard@kahnfarm.com, an email that the Kahn Farm provided for him.

Picard made the February–May payments. In the middle of May, however, Kahn Farm went bankrupt, shut down all of its systems, fired all of its employees, and shuttered its doors. Picard was devastated. Lacking a paycheck, he missed the payment due to Borg Bank on June 1, 2022. On June 10, 2022, Borg Bank Officer Uhura called Picard on his cell phone to ask him about the missed payment. Picard told Uhura that he had lost his job, and that he wasn't sure what he would do next as there were very few openings for uneducated farm-hands in the area. Uhura wished Picard good luck but told him that the Borg Bank's policy was to move quickly to foreclose on delinquent loans.

On June 14, 2022, a dear friend named Spock told Picard some good news. First, a federal program provided assistance to farm-hands hit by hard times: cash payments of $3000/month were available for as long as 12 months. Second, another friend of Spock's named Scottie was going to leave his job at the nearby Enterprise Farm at the end of September. Spock knew the captain of Scotie's farm well. According to Spock, that captain would likely hire Picard for the job.

Spock took Picard to a local federal government office and helped him apply for the federal farm-hand program. The federal officer said that the paperwork typically took about six weeks to process, but that it was highly likely that Picard would qualify. Next, Spock took Picard to meet Captain Kirk of the Enterprise Farm. Kirk liked Picard, and said that he could have Scottie's job at the end of September.

Picard was jubilant. He thoroughly thanked Spock (who responded only by raising his eyebrow), and thought for a moment how to celebrate his good fortune. He decided to go on a four-week camping trip in a national park adjacent to his five acres. About ten miles away from his property was a cave where Picard liked to spend time during the summer studying the bats who lived there. He departed for the cave on June 27, 2022. He intended to keep his cell phone with him at all times, but found on the 27th while hiking to the cave that it lost reception about two miles away from his home, a fact for which he was grateful given that he hoped to spend the four weeks exclusively focusing on bats. He decided to turn it off until he returned. Every week, Picard hiked back to his house to pick up his mail.

Meanwhile, Borg Bank, always anxious to consume, had been busy. It had heard nothing from Picard about the mortgage, and after July 1 came and went with no news of either the June or the July payment, it sprang into action. Its loan officer, Q, ordered Uhura to initiate foreclosure proceedings under the Massachusetts Bank Protection Act (the "MBPA"), adopted in 2021. Relevant portions of the MBPA read as follows:

**Section 1: Statement of Purpose**

The Legislature finds that banks in this state and across the nation face an unprecedented liquidity crisis due to bad mortgage loans, and that this liquidity crisis poses a grave threat to the public interest due to the citizenry's great reliance on banks. Among the causes of this crisis are:

-- an inability of banks holding mortgages to foreclose and to evict delinquent homeowners promptly, which prevents banks from quickly seizing and selling the property and removing bad loans from their books . . . .

**Section 5: In rem and Summary Eviction Lawsuit, Notice**

Any bank holding a mortgage on a property located in Massachusetts, when the mortgagor misses a payment of the loan, may institute an in rem lawsuit seeking summary foreclosure of the property and eviction of the homeowner from that property. The bank shall file the complaint in a Housing Court of the relevant county. Upon filing of such a complaint, the sheriff of the county in which the property lies shall immediately

give notice of the action to the mortgagor/homeowner. Notice of the action may be given to the mortgagor in any of the following ways:

- (a) via hand delivery/personal service on the mortgagor;
- (b) via posting of notice on the relevant property;
- (c) via regular mail sent to the mortgagor; OR
- (d) via electronic notice, if the mortgagor has provided a cell phone number and email address to the bank on the mortgage application. Electronic notice under this subsection shall consist of both of the following:
    - (i) a telephone call to a cell phone number previously provided by the mortgagor to the bank; AND
    - (ii) an email attaching the complaint and summons in .pdf form to an email address previously provided by the mortgagor to the bank.

The sheriff of the county Housing Court is deemed to have complied with subsection (d)(i) of this section if they leave a message on a voicemail system associated with the cell number notifying the mortgagor of the in rem and Summary Eviction action, the Housing Court Case number, and the address of the Housing Court.

**Section 6: Temporary Eviction: Seizure During Pendency of Proceedings**

When a bank files a complaint in a Housing Court, it may also request from the clerk of the court an order entitling it to take immediate possession of the property so as to make repairs needed to prepare the property for sale and to prevent the homeowner from damaging the property. The bank shall file an affidavit describing the facts underlying the delinquency of the loan and verifying its intent to put the property up for sale forthwith. Upon filing of a suitable affidavit, the clerk of the court shall issue an order of possession to the bank, which may then execute such an order by evicting the mortgagor (with the assistance of the Housing Court Sheriff, if necessary).

**Section 8: Summary Eviction: Proceedings**

- (a) If, in ten days after the Housing Court sheriff has given notice to the mortgagor of proceedings under Section 5, the mortgagor fails to file a response to the in rem and Summary Eviction lawsuit, the mortgagor is deemed to have consented to foreclosure and eviction, and the bank may proceed forthwith to claim title to the property and sell it to satisfy the amount of the outstanding loan, returning any excess in the purchase price to the mortgagor. A Housing Court judgment to this effect shall be issued immediately and is final. No motions to reconsider or for relief from judgment shall be entertained, and no appeal will lie.
- (b) . . . [this section, omitted, provides procedures to be followed when the mortgagor does respond to the lawsuit]

At 9 am sharp on July 12, 2022, at Q's orders, Borg filed an in rem lawsuit under Section 5 of the MBPA. That same morning, Borg provided the Sheriff of Worf County with electronic copies of the Summons and the Complaint, along with Picard's email address and cell phone number. At 10:30 am, the Sheriff called Picard's cell phone number, only to be sent to voicemail. The Sheriff left a message telling Picard that an in rem and Summary Eviction action had been filed against him for non-payment of his mortgage, providing the number of the lawsuit

and the address of the Worf County Housing Court. The Sheriff also emailed a copy of the Summons and Complaint in .pdf form to picard@kahnfarm.com, but received a bounce-back email notice that no such email existed. The Sheriff responded by forwarding a copy of that bounce-back email notice to Q at Borg. Q deleted the Sheriff's forwarded email.

Also on the morning of July 12, 2022, Borg filed a request under Section 5 for an order requesting immediate possession of the five-acre property. Accompanying that filing was an affidavit from Q, sworn under penalty of perjury, (i) providing a copy of the mortgage documents along with receipts showing Picard's February–May payments, (ii) swearing under penalty of perjury that no payments had been made in June or July, and (iii) affirming Borg's intention to sell the property immediately to recover the cost of the loan. The Housing Court Clerk, finding Borg's papers proper, issued an Order of Temporary Eviction awarding immediate possession and the right to make repairs to Borg. On the afternoon of July 12, 2022, a Borg-hired locksmith, accompanied by Uhura and an officer from the Worf County Sheriff's Office, visited the property and changed the lock on the front (and only) door. They noticed that a tree branch had fallen, inflicting minor damage to the roof. On July 13, a Borg-hired contractor removed the fallen branch and fixed the roof.

On July 18, Picard visited the property. He checked the external mailbox, and finding nothing of interest there, he did not try to enter the house. He walked around the exterior to satisfy himself that nothing was amiss. Finding nothing wrong, he began his long hike back to the cave.

On the morning of July 25, 2022, the Worf County Housing Court declared Borg to be the owner of the five-acre property and ordered the Worf County Property Registry to so record in its records ("the judgment"). Q, having lined up a purchaser the previous week, sold the property the next day to Ms. Diana Troi for $110,000. Troi moved in on July 27, 2022.

Subsequently, Q decided that, after accounting for interest and fees, the $110,000 purchase price was exactly the amount that Picard still owed Borg on the loan. Borg therefore returned nothing to Picard, and Picard lost his $20,000 down payment.

When Picard returned home on the morning of July 29, 2022, he found that he had no place to live. He immediately hiked to your office. You are a lawyer in Worf County, and a friend of Picard's. Picard asked you the following questions, and requested a brief explanation for each of your answers:

1. Did the July 12, 2022, pre-judgment seizure of the property under MBPA Sections 5–6 violate
    a. Picard's rights related to jurisdictional doctrines?
    b. any other of Picard's constitutional rights?
2. Did the July 25, 2022 Worf County court judgment and subsequent sale of the property violate Picard's constitutional rights?
3. Suppose the answer to question #2 was "yes," that is, that the judgment transferring ownership of the property to Borg Bank, with the subsequent sale, was unlawful. Suppose further that Picard then decided he wanted to sue Worf County and Borg Bank simultaneously in Massachusetts federal district court. His theory against Worf County

would be violation of his constitutional rights under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, and he would seek a declaration that its procedures violated his constitutional rights and an injunction requiring the Worf County Property Registry to record him as the proper owner of the five-acre property. Against Borg, he would seek $20,000 in damages, under a state-law tort cause of action of theft, corresponding to his lost down payment. He had the following questions:
    a. If he tried to sue the two defendants in a single federal court lawsuit, could the federal court hear the case? (Note: You do not need to discuss whether either the 1983 action nor the theft action would succeed on the merits, just whether the federal court can hear them together in a single lawsuit.)[^1]
    b. Suppose that, due to a prior criminal record causing him to fear that a jury would be biased against him, Picard wanted to avoid a jury trial at all costs in his federal court lawsuit. Would he have to give up one of his causes of action to assure that neither defendant could demand a jury trial?

[^1]: In case you'd like further information: Because Worf County is a County, all of its actions are taken "under color of state law" for section 1983 purposes. Also, assume that there is no 11th Amendment or sovereign immunity defense to this suit. Finally, some of you may have heard or read that at one time, a plaintiff could not use section 1983 to sue a county (because a county was not a "person" within the meaning of that statute). That was the law at one time, but it is no longer the law. You aren't supposed to know any of this, and you don't need the information in this footnote. Just focus on whether a federal district court can hear the two causes of action together.
