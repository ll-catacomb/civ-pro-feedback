## Assignment 3 Draft Answer

**1A) Law vs. Equity.** This doctrine allocates cases to *juries* that are historically parallel to suits at law (or suits asking for compensatory damages), while allocating cases to *judges* that are historically parallel to suits at equity (or asking for specific performance). (*Chauffeurs*). The advantage of this doctrine is its fidelity to the text of the 7th Amendment, which does not create but rather "preserves" the right to a jury trial. Yet, the jury trial has been called "the sword in the bed of the merger of law and equity" because now that courts of law and courts of equity have merged, this test is not easily applied in practice. Indeed, the two prongs of the *Chauffeurs* test often point in opposite directions; because the second prong (the relief sought) is more important than the first, this begs the question of whether prong one (concerning historical parallels) is relevant to current lawsuits.

**1B) Fact vs. Law.** This doctrine allocates questions to *juries* that require fact-based determinations, such as questions of "reasonableness" or "negligence," while allocating questions of law to *judges*. This doctrine is justified on the basis that juries are a "cross-section of society," and are therefore better at applying community standards to fact-bound questions. Judges, on the other hand, have expertise in construing written instruments, and they can provide more uniformity in their rulings. One downside is that the law-fact distinction may be murky in certain types of litigation — i.e. patents (*Markman*) — and therefore this distinction may not provide an appropriate guide for whether a judge or jury should rule on a given issue.

**2)** Granger's proposed changes (G'sPC) would likely affect "gatekeeping" functions of pre-trial litigation in a number of ways. Concerning motion for failure to state a claim, a major question here is the extent to which G'sPC could shape the current movement from notice pleading towards a "plausibility" standard (as embodied by *Bell Atlantic*/*Iqbal*). If the key concern in the Court's embrace of the *Iqbal* standard is to control for the costs of discovery, then G'sPC's extra emphasis on the production of transcripts may bolster the argument for tighter "gatekeeping" functions: a tighter plausibility standard would mean that fewer lawsuits would make it to discovery (which could become ever more expensive under G'sPC). However, if the costs of litigation are mainly associated with impaneling a jury and lengthening the length of the trial, then perhaps notice pleading would be desirable under G'sPC: more lawsuits could go to discovery, where information would be revealed and parties would have the opportunity to settle, thereby saving the cost of continued litigation (which could easily overwhelm the system if too many cases made it to trial). Therefore, notice pleading, paired with opportunities to settle and to have the case be dismissed at the summary judgment phase, could serve the legal system well by creating multiple avenues for resolving (or dismissing) the case before the trial begins.

At the summary judgment phase, the impact of G'sPC becomes more complicated. Here, the judge functions not only as a "gatekeeper," by keeping certain cases out of litigation, but he/she also plays a key role in deciding which questions present "genuine issues of material fact" — and therefore, which questions merit further examination at the trial phase. Under G'sPC, it is unclear what the fate would be for cases that were deemed to present genuine issues of material fact. Without a jury, and with only testimony based on the record produced at discovery, what additional tools would a judge have at his/her disposal to resolve these "live" questions during the trial stage? Not only would the decision-maker be the same, but also there is little reason to believe that the record would change at trial.

While G'sPC understandably seek to address the shortcomings of the jury trial, taken together they pose a threat to the various checks and balances that have developed to accept and then filter relevant information before it reaches a decision-maker. Given the extent to which the production of depositions and other evidence occurs largely without the oversight of the judge, there is potential for manipulation of depositions by the parties. At this stage, real cross-examination is not possible

because the parties are attempting to explore the nature of their cases and thus cannot focus on a clean presentation of evidence in their favor, and so there is less opportunity for one party to challenge the evidence presented by the other. (*Celotex* also says that the evidence doesn't have to be admissible at this stage — you just have to show that you could prove these things in trial). Normally, during the summary judgment stage, the judge does not make credibility determinations about the evidence (*Scott v. Harris*). Rather, this would happen at trial: evidence would be presented to the judge and jury, and each party's counsel would challenge this evidence. If G'sPC were adopted, it is unclear how the evidence would be presented to the judge at trial (would it remain unchanged from the pre-trial record?), and whether the judge could make important determinations about validity and credibility without the participation of parties and/or witnesses. It would therefore be difficult to support G'sPC as an appropriate remedy to some of the failures of the modern jury trial.
