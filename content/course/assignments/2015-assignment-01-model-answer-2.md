LupinBank should sue for a declaratory judgment that it is not liable for negligence per se. Lupin could possibly sue in state and federal courts in NY and NJ. NY and NJ both have declaratory judgment statutes, and we can use § 2201 as well. Lupin wants to sue in a court where there's a chance NJ negligence law will be applied, which would eliminate its liability for punitive damages. That court is most likely NY state court. (If it sues in a NY federal court, and the court applies NJ law, the court might dismiss for lack of SMJ. See below.)

**IPJ:** Penelope is only in NY to attend college and it doesn't seem like she has any expressed intention to stay, so it's likely she's still a NJ domiciliary. Therefore, NJ state court has general jurisdiction over Penelope. And with Rule 4(k)(1)(A), NJ federal courts have general jurisdiction over her as well.

NY courts likely have specific jurisdiction over Penelope for this transaction or occurrence. It's likely an NY court will find Penelope has substantial contacts with NY since she purposefully availed herself of NY protections. She (1) visited Lupin's website, (2) maintained a NY bank account, and (3) obtained an NY credit card. Alternatively, Lupin could tag Penelope in NY. Either way, if NY state court has jurisdiction over Penelope, NY federal courts do as well through Rule 4(k)(1)(A) and NY's long-arm statute.

**SMJ:** We use the Imaginary Lawsuit Rule to determine federal arising-under SMJ. Although negligence is a state cause of action, because Penelope's claim depends on an interpretation of federal law (CFPB regulation § 5633), there may be federal question jurisdiction in NY and NJ federal courts (*Smith*/*Grable*). The commentary to § 5633 articulates a federal interest in protecting consumers. However, FQJ on this basis may upset the balance of labor between state and federal courts. Because the violation of § 5633 is fact-bound, and adjudication would open the door to a flood of negligence per se suits in federal courts (FQJ any time there is a violation of a federal statute or regulation in a suit by a person within the zone of protection), a federal court may hold that the *Smith*/*Grable* exception does not apply.

So SMJ in NY and NJ federal courts would likely need to be based on diversity. Penelope is a NJ citizen. NY is Lupin's nerve center, so even though 90% of its employees work in NJ, it's a NY citizen. *Hertz*. Federal courts in both states would apply state law regarding punitive damages, since they're substantive (*Erie*). But the horizontal choice-of-law (HCoL) between NY and NJ determines whether Lupin can meet the AIC requirement for diversity: AIC depends on whether the $60k in punitive damages is available to count as part of § 1332's $75k requirement. For vertical choice-of-law (VCoL), punitive damages are substantive. That means that in NJ, *lex loci* applies since it's a First Restatement state. Since the tort happened in NY, NJ state and federal courts would apply NY law and allow unlimited punitive damages. This means NJ federal court has SMJ. This also means NJ courts are a bad option for Lupin.

An NY suit may be a better option for Lupin. We know NY is a Second Restatement state (*Babcock*), and as such NY would consider a number of factors to inform its HCoL analysis. Some of the most relevant factors include: (1) the place of the tort; (2) the forum's interest in governing conduct in state territory; (3) protection of state residents; (4) convenience and efficiency of litigation; and (5) the locus of the parties' relationship. It seems like the balance of these factors weighs in favor of applying NY law and finding SMJ in NY federal court. But Lupin would argue that NJ law should apply, perhaps because NY doesn't have a strong interest in protecting a NJ citizen. If the analysis led to the application of NJ law, AIC would not be met and a NY federal court could not hear this case in diversity (there's no AIC requirement for FQJ). Since at least there's a shot at barring punitive damages here, Lupin would prefer to bring suit in a NY court, probably a NY state court.

**Venue:** Lupin can likely lay venue in both NY and NJ federal courts. Since Penelope is a NJ domiciliary, venue is properly laid there through § 1391(b)(1). Venue is also probably proper in Manhattan federal court through § 1391(b)(2) since a substantial portion of the transaction or occurrence occurred there.
