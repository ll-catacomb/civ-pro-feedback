# Assignment 1: Model Answer

---

To seize the forum, Gringotts should file a declaratory judgment action in SC federal court. However, because the DJ statute (28 U.S.C. § 2201(a)) does not itself create a grant of SMJ (*First Federal*), Gringotts must show that the federal court either has jurisdiction in diversity or as a federal question. In determining this question, the federal court will look to the "imaginary lawsuit" or "coercive action"—the lawsuit HH would have filed against Gringotts if she had been the one originating the action.

Under federal question (arising-under) jurisdiction, a federal court must have both a constitutional and statutory grant of power. Article III gives federal courts a very broad mandate (*Osborn*), so that isn't a problem. However, § 1331 has been interpreted much more narrowly, requiring that the plaintiff assert a claim that arises under federal law in his well-pleaded complaint (*Mottley*). For purposes of this rule, anticipated defenses do not matter. Thus, since the court will be looking at HH's imaginary complaint, her breach of contract (state law cause of action) claim will fail the well-pleaded complaint rule and preclude federal jurisdiction on this theory.

Nevertheless, federal question jurisdiction can exist in a few very rare situations when state law creates the cause of action but the plaintiff's right to relief depends on federal law (*Smith*). Under the multi-factor analysis set out in *Grable*, a federal court may hear this type of claim when the state action implicates a contested and substantial federal issue and when federal adjudication of the matter will not upset the federal-state balance of judicial responsibilities. On the first prong, Gringotts can argue that this case certainly implicates an important question of federal law, since in order for HH to obtain relief, she needs to prove the constitutionality of the EHMA. Furthermore, Congress has acknowledged that this statute is especially important in Section 1 of EHMA. A trickier argument is on the second prong. Since HH's mortgage modification was one of several issued by the SC czar to Gringotts (to put this in perspective, several modifications in the first four months of the Act's existence to one of probably one hundred banks in one of fifty states), there may be a significant problem in the disruption of federal-state balance.

Alternatively, Gringotts can argue that SMJ exists by way of diversity (§ 1332). Diversity jurisdiction is proper when there is complete diversity of citizenship (*Strawbridge*) and when there is $75,000 in controversy. The amount-in-controversy requirement here is easily met—HH lost her $80,000 down payment. Diversity of citizenship is more difficult. Citizenship for persons is determined by domicile (*Mas*), which itself is defined by residence in a state and indefinite leaving time. Gringotts can argue that HH is a SC citizen because she has residency in SC and lacks a definite plan to leave (after all, she bought a house there and isn't just staying in dorms or renting). However, HH might counter that she is still a citizen of NC (where she was born) because she is only in SC for college and has (albeit indefinite) plans to leave the state some day for a career in NY.

If the court decides that HH is a NC citizen, this would defeat complete diversity because Gringotts is also a citizen of NC. Corporations are citizens of two states—the state in which they are incorporated and the state in which they have their principal place of business (defined in *Hertz* to be their "nerve center" where the headquarters are). In this case, Gringotts is both a citizen of Delaware (incorporation) and of NC (nerve center). In the end, diversity jurisdiction probably will be established because of HH's indefinite plans to leave SC.

If a lawsuit is filed in SC federal court and SMJ is found, HH still might argue that the court lacks IPJ over her. According to *International Shoe*, a court may only exercise IPJ if the defendant has sufficient minimum contacts and the exercise of jurisdiction comports with notions of fair play. In this case, considering that HH bought a house in SC, has lived there for a good amount of time, has paid taxes there, etc., the court will most likely find that it has IPJ.
