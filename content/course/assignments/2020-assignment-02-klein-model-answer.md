## I. Motion to Transfer

Does DRI have SMJ over the lawsuit? There is no AUJ since the case does not involve a federal issue. DRI has DJ only if CD and AIC are met. AIC is met ($500K > $75K), and CD is met — MM Corp. is a NJ resident (incorporated in NJ and PPOB in NJ), and Shrek is a RI resident.

Per *Hoffman*, a transferor court may transfer to another happy court (or a court to which all parties agree). Is DMA a happy court? DMA almost certainly has IPJ over Shrek through MA's long arm statute and 4k1a — Shrek participated in creating the deepfake in Springfield, MA, and all negotiations re the purchase of the stock were conducted in Boston, MA. This should qualify as minimal contacts. DMA's exercise of jurisdiction would be fair and reasonable since Shrek purposefully availed himself of the benefits of doing business in MA, and MA has an interest in regulating the conduct of business and torts committed within its state. Venue is also proper under § 1391(b)(2), since Shrek's participation in MA in creation of the deepfake is an event "giving rise to the claim."

Finally, consider the public interest factors under *Gilbert*. Per *Atlantic Marine*, there is a presumption in favor of a transfer pursuant to a FSC. Thus, the court should transfer to DMA absent compelling public interest factors to the contrary, since the FSC operationalizes the private interest. Public interest factors appear to point toward a transfer, because the central role that MA landmarks and citizens play in Mulan's product endorsements ("MA loves you…," etc.) suggests that MA has a strong state interest in the lawsuit. Additionally, as explained below, it is likely that MA law applies.

If DMA is a happy court, DRI should transfer despite RI's UFSCOLPA statute — per *Stewart*, the transfer statutes are valid and applicable procedural rules that supersede state law regarding forum selection clauses.

## II. Choice of Law Analysis

Per *Atlantic Marine*, when transferring pursuant to a FSC, use the COL rules of transferee state — in this case, MA. But the contract says to use NJ substantive law. Federal common law would uphold the contractual COL provision, while MA's UFSCOLPA would find the COL clause invalid. What do the *Erie* tests say?

(1) *York*. The difference between state and federal law in this case would likely be outcome-determinative, because the public access defense is likely available under a MA COL analysis, but is not available under NJ law, and because the public access defense likely would result in Shrek's prevailing in the lawsuit. Use state law.

(2) *Hanna* (both Scalia and Ginsburg versions). Use *Erie*'s twin aims for federal common law. Disregarding the state statute can easily lead to forum shopping, because out-of-state plaintiffs seeking to enforce a COL clause will file in federal court, while those seeking to avoid an unfavorable COL clause will file in state court. This also results in inequitable administration since out-of-state defendants sued in state court can remove to federal court to ensure the enforcement of a COL clause, while in-state defendants would not have the option. Use state law.

(3) Harlan in *Hanna*. The state statute regulates primary conduct by mandating that a state's COL laws be applied to lawsuits heard in the state, which ultimately effects which substantive law applies. It is safe to

assume that contractual COL provisions are drawn up by sophisticated parties whose conduct clearly contemplates the effect of COL rules on their exposure to litigation. Use state law.

All *Erie* tests point toward the use of state law. If state law applies, DMA should disregard the contractual COL provision and conduct a MA COL analysis. MA is a 2nd restatement state, so MA's public access defense is potentially available even if the place of wrong was not in MA. The analysis begins with a presumption in favor of NJ law because the injury (the drop in stock price) occurred in NJ. However, MA appears to have a strong state interest in this case because the tort affected the reputability of MA-centric product endorsements; because the parties' business relationship was centered in MA; and because the tort was actually committed in MA — even though the harm was felt in NJ. Thus, it appears that it would be reasonable for DMA to apply MA law and allow Shrek to invoke the public access defense.

However, if federal common law applies, use NJ substantive law. NJ law does not recognize a public access defense, so the defense would be unavailable to Shrek.
