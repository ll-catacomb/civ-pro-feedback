## Assignment 2 — Civil Procedure 2, 2016

### Question 1: Motions to Dismiss

The EDVA applies Virginia's choice of law rules (*Klaxon*). Virginia uses the First Restatement, meaning the substantive law of the place of the wrong governs. The tort occurred in Roanoke, Virginia — that is "where the blood is" — so Virginia law governs all three counts.

Virginia requires recklessness (intentionally taking an action highly likely to cause injury) for drone crash personal injury claims. West Virginia requires only negligence.

#### Count 1 — Negligent Operation Against Oliver Wood

The EDVA should grant the motion to dismiss Count 1. Virginia law governs, and Virginia requires recklessness, not negligence. Regardless of how well-pleaded the negligence facts are, they fail to state a claim cognizable under Virginia law. Wood should be aware that amendment to allege recklessness is possible given the underlying facts (Wood had himself reported the Tornado 360's malfunction), but Count 1 as pleaded must be dismissed.

#### Count 2 — Negligent Maintenance Against Chudley Cannons

The EDVA should grant the motion to dismiss Count 2 for the same reason. Virginia's recklessness requirement precludes a standalone negligence-based drone crash claim. Count 2 fails as a matter of law.

#### Count 3 — Reckless Maintenance Against Chudley Cannons

The EDVA should deny the motion to dismiss Count 3. Under *Twombly/Iqbal*, the court accepts well-pleaded facts as true and determines whether the claim is plausible. The Cannons had received reports of the Tornado 360's steering malfunction from three operators; they did nothing to repair it. This amounts to intentionally ignoring a known, serious defect — squarely within Virginia's definition of recklessness. Count 3 is plausibly pleaded and states a claim.

### Question 2: Motion to Transfer

#### 2(a) — Transfer

<!-- page: 2 -->

Under 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a), the EDVA may transfer to any district where the action could have been brought, for convenience of parties and witnesses and in the interest of justice.

The Chudley Cannons are a West Virginia corporation with their principal place of business in Wheeling, WV. Venue would be proper in SDWV and the court would have jurisdiction over the Cannons there. Oliver Wood is a West Virginia citizen and would be subject to personal jurisdiction in SDWV. The case could have been brought there.

Convenience: The key evidence on the maintenance claims — records of the steering malfunction reports, the Tornado 360's maintenance history, the Cannons' internal communications — is in West Virginia. The Cannons' employees most knowledgeable about the defect are in West Virginia. These witnesses and documents would be more conveniently accessed in SDWV.

Against transfer: The crash site is in Virginia; Virginia witnesses (Kingsley's treating physicians, other spectators) are in Virginia; and Kingsley, the plaintiff, chose Virginia as his forum. Courts generally give some weight to a plaintiff's forum choice.

On balance, the maintenance-focused nature of the surviving claims (Count 3) and the location of relevant evidence in West Virginia support transfer.

#### 2(b) — Applicable Law After Transfer

Under *Van Dusen v. Barrack*, transfer under § 1404(a) at the defendant's behest does not alter the applicable substantive law. The SDWV applies the choice of law rules of the transferor court — EDVA, applying Virginia's First Restatement. Under the First Restatement, the wrong occurred in Virginia, so Virginia law governs. Accordingly, SDWV would apply Virginia's recklessness standard even though West Virginia itself uses the Second Restatement and applies a negligence standard.

This outcome reflects the Erie/choice-of-law principle that defendants cannot gain a substantive advantage through § 1404(a) transfer. As the Supreme Court recognized in *Black & White Taxicab*, allowing parties to manipulate applicable law through forum selection — whether by reincorporating in another state or by seeking transfer — would undermine the uniformity that *Erie* and *Van Dusen* seek to preserve.
