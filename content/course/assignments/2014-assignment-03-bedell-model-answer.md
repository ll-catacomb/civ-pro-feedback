# Assignment 3: Model Answer

## Question 1

At least in the absence of any possible federal SOL, SOL is substantive for VCOL purposes (*York*), so we will use state law; the question is which state.

If NH were a happy court, you will use NH's laws (including procedural rules). Is NH a happy court? Venue is likely appropriate in NH because that is where the accident took place (substantial event). However, NH is unlikely to have IPJ over Olivanders because there are no minimum contacts. In federal court, 4(k)(1)(a), NH long-arm goes to the limits of the Constitution, so issue is specific IPJ under the DPC. Olivanders would argue that it did not purposefully avail itself of NH's laws or otherwise do business in the state (*McGee*); this was an unintentional tort where the liability was not foreseeable (*WWVolkswagon*), and because Olivanders used a distributor, it is unlikely to be liable (*Nicastro*). However, Pansy Parkinson would argue that Olivander could foresee being haled into court because Mr. Olivander encouraged Luna Lovegood to sell there and thus sought to access the NH market. Olivanders seems likely to win on IPJ, in which case the shorter MA SOL would apply and the suit would be dismissed. However, if NH could exercise IPJ, NH law would apply, and Parkinson would win the SOL question.

For claim preclusion (only if case lives under NH SOL): This is clearly the same claim and the same parties, so it's a question of whether the dismissal is on the merits. NH would have to give at least as much preclusive effect as MA, but in MA, an SOL dismissal is not on the merits. The case could proceed. However, under *Hart*, a state may be able to give more preclusive effect, in which case NH could use its own policy and dismiss.

For issue preclusion (again, only if under NH law), Olivanders's claim would fail because though the issue of whether the claim was filed too late under MA law was litigated, the issue of whether the claim was filed too late under NH law has not been litigated. Because the laws have different substantive provisions, it is not the same issue, and there is no issue preclusion.

## Question 2

This case should be dismissed. Parkinson had the opportunity to file this case anywhere she wanted, but on her first try she irresponsibly picked a court where she was not able to make a valid claim. Olivanders had to hire lawyers and go to court to respond to this poorly-planned lawsuit, and it is unfair to him that he should then have to do the same thing again after Parkinson realized her mistake. This is also a clear example of forum shopping, which is something we have seen the courts try to discourage in other contexts (e.g., *Erie*). Finally, this is an inefficient use of judicial resources, and by allowing the second lawsuit to continue, the court removes the incentive for Parkinson to get her lawsuit right the first time around.

Moreover, this case raises problematic federalism concerns, as the first case was dismissed in state court, but could now potentially be heard in federal court right down the road. Moreover, the potential for bizarre combinations of different states' laws means

that it's possible the court could apply Massachusetts SOL, but NH "on the merits" rules, leading to non-sensical legal combinations that no legislature actually would have enacted.

However, though the result in this particular case seems to make little sense, it would be difficult to craft a rule that properly resolves the problem. Many of the principles and theories underlying the analysis make sense as applied in most cases and further values we want to promote. For example, we do want a state to respect another state's laws by recognizing that other state's judgments on preclusive effect; we also want to ensure that if a case is transferred for convenience purposes, the plaintiff still has the opportunity to use the laws of the jurisdiction where he originally filed. If rules are sensible the vast majority of the time, it doesn't necessarily serve larger goals of efficiency, fairness, and proper power distribution to rewrite those rules completely just because they produce strange results at the margins. The only way to "fix" a situation like we have here may be to give judges latitude to decide cases "against" the rules where the system is so clearly being abused — but even this introduction of discretion raises questions about equal application of laws and foreseeability. So while an outcome that allows this case to continue may initially seem like a perversion of justice, it may in fact be a small price to pay for a generally more consistent and predictable system.
