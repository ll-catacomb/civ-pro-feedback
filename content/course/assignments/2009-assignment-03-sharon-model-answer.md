# Assignment 3 Model Answer

When a state court adjudicates a federal cause of action, federal common law as set forth in *Parklane Hosiery* should govern the preclusive effect of the resulting judgment.

While *Semtek* borrowed from state preclusion law to define the content of federal common law, *Semtek*'s concerns about federalism and *Erie*'s twin aims require a different result when the rendering court is adjudicating a federal cause of action rather than sitting in diversity. *Erie* suggested that federal courts sitting in diversity should apply state law on substantive issues so as to protect the autonomy of states in making important judicial determinations. *Semtek*'s holding was likely influenced by these concerns, deferring to states' decisions about the proper preclusive effect to give to prior judgments. However, when a state court adjudicates a federal claim, this calculus is altered. Because federal courts sitting in diversity have no "ownership" over the state claims being litigated (merely serving as a neutral forum to protect out-of-state defendants) it makes sense to defer to state preclusion law. Similarly, because state courts adjudicating federal causes of action are not experts in the underlying federal claims, they should defer to federal common law regarding preclusion. Thus, *Semtek*'s deference to state law to define the content of federal common law is less persuasive in the context of federal claims than in diversity.

*Semtek*'s concerns about *Erie*'s twin aims also weigh in favor of a different result when adjudicating federal claims. *Erie* sought to avoid "substantial variations [in outcomes] between state and federal litigation" which would "likely . . . influence the choice of a forum." *Hanna*, 402. To advance this aim, state court judgments on federal causes of action should be afforded the same preclusive effect as federal court judgments on similar claims. Otherwise, parties would face two different preclusion regimes based on where the plaintiff brought the lawsuit initially and whether the defendant could remove to federal court — a result *Erie* aimed to prevent. In Segundus' suit, for example, if the state reviewing court were to apply New York's unlimited offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel (ONMCE) regime to Primus' judgment, Devil would almost surely be found liable again. Yet, if Primus had initially brought the case in federal court or if Angel had consented to removal, the reviewing court would have applied the *Parklane* rule, likely preventing ONMCE and allowing Devil to re-litigate liability. Thus, if a state reviewing court did not apply *Parklane*, *Erie*'s goal of preventing substantial variations in outcomes between federal and state litigation would be obstructed.

These divergent outcomes encourage vertical forum shopping, as defendants have an incentive to remove the initial lawsuit to federal court to benefit from the *Parklane* rule. *Semtek* sought to avoid these incentives, concerned that defendants would systematically remove to take advantage of more favorable preclusion rules. *Semtek* also suggests that the need for a uniform preclusion rule governing all federal question judgments is greater than for diversity judgments, where it was acceptable for states to apply different preclusion rules.

*Hart* offers a different methodology than *Semtek* for determining the content of preclusion law: rather than borrowing state preclusion law, the court substitutes its own policy determinations about the desirability of ONMCE. Yet, even following *Hart*'s policy-driven methodology, *Parklane*'s rule is superior for securing objectives of efficiency and fairness. First, unlimited ONMCE undermines efficiency by encouraging plaintiffs to "wait and see" rather than joining litigation early, resulting in multiple lawsuits which impede judicial economy and create the kind of piecemeal litigation that *Colorado River* sought to avoid. *Hart* is correct that when there are already multiple lawsuits, ONMCE can promote efficiency by preventing the expenditure of resources inherent in re-litigating liability and diminishing the risk of inconsistent verdicts. Yet, *Parklane* seeks to prevent multiple lawsuits, structuring incentives so that plaintiffs cannot reap benefits from sitting on the sidelines when they could have joined early on. Second, *Parklane* promotes fairness by ensuring that defendants are only bound by unfavorable verdicts when they had the incentive and opportunity to litigate the first suit vigorously, thus guarding against unfair results which could arise if the first suit was frivolous or did not afford sufficient procedural guarantees. Thus, while *Hart* assumes that a defendant "has had his day in court," *Parklane* seeks to ensure that this opportunity was meaningful before determining that the defendant "should not be permitted to litigate the question anew." *Hart*, 1334. Finally, *Parklane* promotes repose, preventing defendants from being surprised by offensive use of a prior judgment when future lawsuits were not foreseeable.
