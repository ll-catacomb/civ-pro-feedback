## 1. Assessing the Seizures

### a. Assessing Constitutionality

**i. The Illinois Seizure**

The IL seizure is likely unconstitutional. Notice must be reasonably certain to reach the relevant party (*Mullane*) and supplementary notice is required if the initial attempt fails (*Flowers*). Here, WeBuyDebts is aware that Hermione did not receive notice and did not follow up. Additionally, it definitely could have. Its crack investigative team could have found another method of contacting her through its research. So they failed to use the best method practicable (*Schroeder*). Further, there was no pre-seizure hearing and the property seized is hugely significant to Hermione, as in *Connecticut v. Doehr*.

**ii. The North Carolina Seizure**

The NC seizure is constitutionally stronger: (a) decision maker is a judge, (b) competent documentary evidence has been submitted to support seizure, (c) WeBuyDebts has a clear pre-seizure interest in the property, (d) there is some notice + consent within the purchase agreement of the possibility of this kind of seizure.

### b. Assessing Jurisdiction

**i. Quasi-in-rem (Illinois)**

Per *Shaffer*, it's difficult to entirely separate a party's interest in their property (Hermione's bank account in IL) from the party itself. And, the case for jurisdiction over Hermione herself in Illinois is very questionable — Hermione is a Missouri citizen, who accumulated debts in Missouri. It's hard to make a specific jurisdiction case that related the forum, the subject matter of the litigation, and the defendant. While Hermione purposefully availed herself of IL as a forum, her bank account is not closely related to the subject matter of the litigation (the credit card debt).

**ii. In rem (North Carolina)**

This is less problematic because the broom was located in NC at the time of the seizure and NC presumably has an interest in enforcing the law within its territory. But, Fenrir and Hermione did not agree to buy the broom in NC, did not purchase the broom from WeBuyDebts in NC, and did not advertise the broom for sale in NC. Further, Hermione did not purposefully avail herself of NC or have any knowledge that the broom was brought to NC (nothing suggests she even knew of Fenrir's driving route).

## 2. Attacking the Constitutionality of the Statutory Scheme

### a. Procedural Due Process

To attack the constitutionality of the statutory scheme, I would assess:

**i. Who is the decision maker?** If the decision-maker is a judge, as in the NC seizure, the confiscation is more likely to survive a due process challenge. However, if the decision-maker is a clerk, its constitutionality is more suspect.

**ii. Does the party seeking relief have a pre-existing interest in the property being confiscated?** This factor weighs heavily against the scheme in the quasi-in-rem context of the IL seizure, since there's no interest in the property. In the NC context, WeBuyDebts retained a property interest in the broom until the full price was paid. So, it had a preexisting interest in the property being confiscated.

**iii. Is the seizure purely to establish jurisdiction or is it for security purposes?** Here, the IL bank account seizure is purely to establish jurisdiction so it is relatively less compelling. However, WeBuyDebts has a valid argument that the NC broom seizure is required to prevent debtors from absconding with the property. On the other hand, there seems to be a limited risk of destroying the property, since that would place the debtor in an even more financially imperiled position. This cuts against the IL seizure's constitutionality and in favor of the NC seizure's constitutionality.

**iv. Does the applicant for the seizure have to show probable cause or meet a lesser burden of proof?** In the NC context, WeBuyDebts has at least shown something more than conclusory allegations. However in IL, there is little information. The former is constitutionally stronger than the latter.

**v. Can the dispute be resolved by uncontested documentary evidence?** If not, there should be a hearing. This weighs in favor of the constitutionality of the NC seizure because it's a case that can be resolved on the papers. They've provided documentation of the purchase agreement and the missed payment. The other seizure is more difficult to determine. In any event, there was not a lot of supportive documentation provided.

**vi. Is a bond and counterbond procedure available?** If not, this would weaken the scheme's constitutionality. No information is provided on this.

**vii. Is a hearing available either pre- or post-seizure?** A pre-seizure hearing would be best. But, an immediately post-seizure hearing would also strengthen the scheme. If no hearing is available, I would attack on this basis.

### b. To Attack Jurisdiction

**i. Quasi-in-rem:** The fact that the statute allows for seizure in quasi-in-rem context may be constitutionally suspect per *Shaffer*.

**ii. In rem:** Less problematic. Might argue that seizing mobile items (like racing brooms or cars) could lead to litigating in a forum that has no connection to the suit. The NC case is a good example of that.
