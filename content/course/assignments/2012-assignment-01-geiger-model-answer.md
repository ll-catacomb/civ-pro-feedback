Arthur Weasley ("AW") has two objectives: receive a judgment for the value of the car and avoid federal court. For the first goal, AW must sue at least Crabbe Corporation ("CC") because Ludo Bagman ("LB") has no assets (his house is in foreclosure) and is therefore judgment-proof. He must also sue in a state that will apply a set of state laws that includes respondeat superior so CC is liable for LB's negligence. For his second goal of avoiding federal court, AW must sue in a state court from which CC cannot remove; one way to do so would be to assure that there is no diversity jurisdiction over the parties. Arising under jurisdiction is inapplicable here because the cause of action derives from state common-law.

Of the relevant states, GA, VA, and DE apply respondeat superior. Assuming the matter stays in state court, AW must choose a jurisdiction that will apply the substantive law of one of these states. For horizontal choice of law purposes, a state following the First Restatement doctrine of *lex loci delictus* applies the substantive law of the state in which the accident occurred, in this case Georgia. *See Alabama RR*. A state following the Second Restatement will look to the forum state with the most significant contacts in the case. *Babcock*. In this matter NC is the center of gravity, as both the plaintiff and the person accused of negligence are NC citizens, the alleged negligence took place in NC, and the site of the accident was completely fortuitous. GA law is much more favorable to AW than NC law, so he will want to bring suit in a state applying the First Restatement (NC or SC).

To avoid permanent removal to federal court in NC or SC, AW must avoid completely diversity of the parties (the amount-in-controversy requirement is easily fulfilled). *Strawbridge*; 28 U.S.C. § 1332. AW and LB are both domiciles of NC; while LB may move in the future and thus currently may not have the intention to remain in NC indefinitely, *Mas*, until he becomes domiciled in a new state he remains a NC resident for diversity purposes. AW's true target, CC, is domiciled in DE and VA. *Hertz*. A defendant cannot remove for diversity purposes if she is sued in her own domicile. AW can sue CC and stay in state court if he (1) sues LB and CC, thereby destroying complete diversity, or (2) sues CC in DE or VA. AW cannot sue in DE or VA for choice of law purposes (see above), so he must sue both LB and CC in either NC or SC, where lack of complete diversity will keep the matter in state court.

The remaining question is, in which of these states (NC and SC) can the court exercise personal jurisdiction over the defendants? Neither defendant has contact with SC and an SC court's judgment would be unenforceable. *Int'l Shoe*. NC has general IPJ over LB as a NC citizen. NC can also exercise IPJ over CC because it operates all of its repair shops in NC and (allegedly) committed the negligence there, creating the minimum contacts necessary under *International Shoe*. AW's best plan is to sue both LB and CC in NC state court: (1) the court will apply GA's substantive law of respondeat superior, thereby holding CC liable for the alleged negligence; (2) the defendants cannot permanently remove to federal court because there is not complete diversity; and (3) NC can exercise IPJ over both defendants.

[N.B.: One might argue that GA has IPJ even under *Volkswagen* because LB specifically knew AW was taking the car there and could reasonably anticipate that an accident caused by his negligence could lead to action against him. It is unlikely, even if one accepts that argument, that LB meets *Calder*'s three-step test, and *Nicastro*'s plurality opinion also runs against GA exercising IPJ.]
