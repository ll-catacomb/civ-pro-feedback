## 1. *Peverell v. DMC*

DMC, as a corporation, is domiciled in state of incorporation (GA) and of its principal place of business (SC). As such, there would have been no federal SMJ had DMC joined the lawsuit against Delacour: medical malpractice is a state law claim, and both Peverell and DMC are SC domiciliaries, so there's no diversity jurisdiction. Rule X does not create SMJ; this claim could not have been brought initially. The best interpretation of Rule X would not require defendants to attempt to assert claims without SMJ (which would be highly inefficient), and by analogy to the rule in *Marrese*, preclusion could not attach; the Peverell v. DMC suit should therefore go forward.

## *Peverell v. Derwent*

Here, there would have been diversity jurisdiction had Derwent been joined in the *Peverell v. Delacour* lawsuit: Derwent is domiciled in GA, Peverell is domiciled in SC, and the amount in controversy seems likely to exceed the statutory minimum. Under Rule X, Peverell was required to assert this claim in his earlier lawsuit. The best interpretation of Rule X would preclude plaintiffs from bringing such claims in later lawsuits (as with Rule 13(a) compulsory counterclaims and the rule in *Marrese*); the Peverell v. Derwent suit should therefore not go forward.

## 2.

Rule X should not be adopted. It would have some benefits, improving efficiency in some ways — by requiring plaintiffs to assert all claims against all defendants involved in the same transaction or occurrence, fewer judicial resources would be involved than if the facts of that transaction or occurrence had to be litigated in multiple separate trials. Single trials might also allow more effective determinations of the relative liability of each potentially responsible defendant, in cases like Mr. Peverell's.

However, Rule X is also likely to promote inefficiencies, including unnecessarily aggressive litigation — plaintiffs (and cross-claimants, etc.) are likely to sue everyone tangentially responsible for an occurrence, to preserve their future litigation options should they fail in a suit against the more obvious targets. The inherent ambiguity of Rule X might contribute to such risk aversion — it is unclear what likelihood of success constitutes "knowledge" of a claim for relief. Such litigation could be expensive, stressful, and inconvenient to these marginally involved defendants, and (unlike Rule 13's compulsory counterclaims) would drag many additional parties into court who would otherwise have escaped these costs. In addition, for complicated claims spanning activities and defendants spread across various states, multiple lawsuits in multiple jurisdictions might be more efficient for the parties, witnesses, or gathering of evidence.

Finally, the complexity and expense of a trial may scale non-linearly with the number of parties involved. Given the currently broad interpretation of "the same transaction or occurrence," as in e.g. *Heyward Robinson*, the number of litigants in a trial could snowball very quickly under Rule X. Rule 42's discretionary powers might mitigate this problem, but the inefficiencies caused by a higher percentage of massive multi-party litigations seem likely to outweigh any benefits of fewer trials.

In addition, Rule X (depending on its interpretation) might create bad incentives for plaintiffs to avoid research — according to the plain language of Rule X (which requires actual knowledge), a plaintiff could avoid triggering the rule by suing each defendant as soon as it becomes a plausible target. It also raises the same fairness and venue-shopping concerns as with
