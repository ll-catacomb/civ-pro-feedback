## Assignment 2 — Civil Procedure 2, 2017

### Question 1(a): Transfer Motion — QQS v. SSS

The motion to transfer presents a conflict between Alabama law, which would prohibit the NDAL from enforcing the forum selection clause and transferring the lawsuit to the SDNY, and the federal transfer statutes, which would compel the transfer unless "extraordinary circumstances unrelated to convenience of the parties" clearly disfavor granting the motion (*Atlantic Marine*). Under *Stewart*, Alabama law will not govern, so the forum selection clause should be enforced and the transfer motion granted unless extraordinary circumstances involving public interest factors defeat the transfer motion. There are no extraordinary circumstances from the facts as given, and, regardless, public interest factors rarely defeat a transfer motion. The transfer motion should be granted.

### Question 1(b): Damages Cap After Transfer

Absent a forum selection clause, state law applicable in NDAL would also apply in SDNY under the happy court rule, because NDAL is a happy court (personal jurisdiction and venue exist due to contract negotiation there), per *Klaxon*. But because the parties are bound by a forum selection clause, the SDNY will not use the transferor court's law. *Atlantic Marine*.

Damage-limiting clauses are procedural for HCOL purposes, so the SDNY district court should use the forum's law. Since New York is the forum and New York law allows damage-limiting clauses, SDNY will enforce the clause and limit recovery to $100,000.

There are no vertical choice of law (VCOL) issues in this case, as federal common law and New York state law do not differ with respect to the validity and enforcement of damage-limiting clauses. Regardless, since damage-limiting clauses are a substantive issue for VCOL purposes, SDNY will use state law.

### Question 2: Forum Non Conveniens — QQS v. HH

A vertical choice of law analysis is required.

The federal transfer statutes only govern transfers within the federal court system. In cases in which a forum selection clause points to a foreign forum outside of the federal court system, the doctrine of forum non conveniens applies. *Atlantic Marine*. The QQS-HH contract's forum selection clause points to Ontario, Canada, so forum non conveniens applies.

However, the NDAL must decide whether Alabama state law or federal common law applies, since Alabama state law would prohibit the enforcement of the forum selection clause while federal common law would allow it. Looking to the *Erie* doctrine, under *York*: if the outcome of the suit would be determined differently based on the use of federal or state law, the court must use state law. As it pertains to the lawsuit itself, there would be different outcomes if state or federal law is used (i.e., whether the suit is dismissed or not), so the court should use state law. However, arguably, the outcome of the dispute itself (i.e., the breach of contract claim) would not be determined at this stage since QQS is still able to file the suit in a court in Ontario, Canada, which may be a reason for the court to use federal common law.

Under the *Hanna* majority (any interpretation of it): for conflicts between state law and federal common law, the court must look to the twin aims of *Erie* (preventing forum shopping and the inequitable administration of law), and if the use of federal common law would go against these aims, apply state law. The difference between federal and Alabama law would incentivize choosing state court over federal court and vice versa at the time of filing depending on the goals of the parties, leading to forum shopping. Additionally, inequitable administration of laws is likely to occur given the difference between the use of federal or state law. Accordingly, the court should use state law.

Under the *Harlan* concurrence in *Hanna*: the court asks if the use of state or federal law will cause parties to change their "primary conduct," i.e., their general behavior regarding their affairs. Arguably, parties may draft their contracts to include forum selection clauses requiring litigation in foreign forums if the court applies state law, or may forgo such clauses entirely if the court applies federal law. This test also suggests using state law.
