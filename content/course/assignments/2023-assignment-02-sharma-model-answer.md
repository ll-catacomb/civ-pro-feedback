# Assignment 2 Model Answer

## Q1

### 12(b)(1): SMJ

§ 1331 against Leto: The RRA is a federal CoA, so there is § 1331 jurisdiction for the Leto claim. *Mottley*.

The claim against Duncan is state law with no embedded federal issue (no § 1331), and Duncan and Chani are both citizens of PA (no § 1332). But NDNY can exercise supplemental jurisdiction over the state-law negligence claim against Duncan. State and federal claims arise from the same CNOF. *Gibbs*; § 1367(a). § 1367(b) is inapplicable because the court is exercising AUJ over the RRA claim. None of the discretionary factors listed in § 1367(c) are present.

### 12(b)(2) PJ

**Leto:** NDNY can exercise general PJ over Leto, a NY corporation.

**Duncan:** NDNY cannot exercise general PJ over Duncan because Duncan is a PA domiciliary. However, NDNY can likely exercise specific PJ over Duncan. 4K(1)(A). The NY long-arm statute goes to the limits of the Constitution. There are minimal contacts linking Duncan, the subject matter of the litigation, and NY. The train ticket was sold in NY. The failure to inspect the train also happened in NY. Regarding fair and reasonable factors, Duncan has at least some contacts in NY, and it must be reasonably convenient for Duncan to get to NY, because he works there in part. NY has an interest in ensuring that trains leaving its stations are safe. Evidence regarding the negligence is likely to be in NY because the negligent omission occurred there. If any state law applies (see below), it may well be NY. A NY federal court will apply NY HCOL rules (*Klaxon*). NY is a R2 state, so we begin with the presumption that PA negligence law applies under *lex loci delicti*. Section 6 factors might be sufficient to dislodge that presumption and render NY law applicable. The center of gravity of the parties' relationship is NY because that is where the ticket was sold and the wrongful omission took place. *Babcock*. NY federal court is going to be better at applying NY law relative to PA law.

### 12(b)(3)

Venue is improper in NDNY because Leto is a PA & NY resident and Duncan is a PA resident (so no § 1391(b)(1)), and nothing substantial occurred in NDNY (so no § 1391(b)(2)). The train ticket sale and the negligent omission took place in SDNY, and the accident in EDPA. § 1391(b)(3) venue is inapplicable because there are at least two judicial districts in the U.S. where this dispute could be resolved (see Q2 transfer analysis below). Therefore, a motion to dismiss or transfer filed under 12(b)(3) is highly likely to succeed.

### 12(b)(6)

All parties agree that Duncan's actions were negligent, but not reckless. If PA law governs the dispute, then Chani's claim can be dismissed for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. By contrast, if NY law governs the dispute, then the lawsuit can proceed. *Erie* determines whether choice-of-law clause is valid.

- *York*: Use state law because it is outcome determinative. Choice-of-law clause is invalid.
- *Hanna* Majority (Scalia + Ginsburg): Twin aims of *Erie*. Using the federal common law upholding COL clauses would promote forum shopping and inequitable administration of the laws. State law governs and the choice-of-law clause is invalid.
- *Hanna* Concurrence (Harlan): Choice-of-law clause may affect pre-litigation conduct because they change how aggressively businesses sell products. Leto sold train tickets under the assumption that PA law would govern any dispute. Perhaps employees would have been trained further if Leto thought NY's more pro-plaintiff law would control.

Choice-of-law clause is substantive; state law governs, so the choice-of-law clause is invalid.

In case *Erie* tests indicate the choice-of-law clause is invalid, we proceed with HCOL analysis. See NY HCOL analysis in PJ analysis in 12(b)(2) section above. If Section 6 factors overcome *lex loci delicti* presumption, lawsuit can proceed. If *lex loci delicti* governs, then the blood was spilled in PA, so PA law governs, and the lawsuit will be dismissed.

## Q2

Because venue is improper, NDNY is an unhappy court. Must transfer to happy court where case could have been originally filed. *Hoffman*. For venue, § 1391(b)(3) is unavailable, see above.

- **WDPA/MDPA/EDNY/WDNY:** Venue is improper because a substantial part of the events giving rise to the lawsuit did not occur in any of these districts, § 1391(b)(2). These are unhappy courts.
- **EDPA:** Venue is proper here § 1391(b)(2) because the train accident occurred in Philadelphia. It is also proper under § 1391(b)(1), and EDPA can exercise general PJ over Duncan and Leto, because Duncan is a Philadelphia domiciliary and Leto's PPOB is in Philadelphia.
- **SDNY:** Venue is proper under § 1391(b)(2) because the negligent omission that caused the accident occurred in Manhattan. SDNY can exercise PJ over both defendants for same reason NDNY can, see above.

Transferor court was unhappy, so we use transferee court HCOL rules. *Cf. Ferens*. If we transfer to EDPA, PA law governs because PA is a R1 state, and the blood was spilled in Philadelphia. If we transfer to SDNY, NY law might govern because New York is a R2 state, and the Section 6 factors might overcome the *lex loci delicti* presumption. Transfer to EDPA is better for Duncan's lawyer so recklessness standard is used, and case can be dismissed under 12(b)(6).
