## Assignment 1 — Civil Procedure 2, 2017

### Question 1: Remand

No. The 30-and-30 rule was violated in both instances. Hermione filed to remove outside the 30-day allotted window, so normally the motion to remand would be granted. But Malfoy moved to remand more than 30 days after Hermione's removal motion, so no remand. The federal court could still remand for lack of SMJ. No AUJ exists — this is a state law cause of action. Looking to diversity jurisdiction: AIC is over $75k. There is complete diversity — Malfoy's PPOB is in Oregon and Hermione is domiciled in NJ — despite not having lived there since high school, she has never moved to another location with the intent to stay there. SMJ is proper under § 1332.

### Question 2: Transfer

Federal district court transfers are governed by the transfer statutes.

**Step 1 — Could the case have been brought in Oregon?** Per *Hoffman*, the statutes are limited by whether the plaintiff could have brought the case in the transferee court in the first place, so we must determine whether the court would have IPJ and whether venue could be properly laid.

For IPJ, a federal court piggy-backs on the long-arm statute of the state in which it sits. Oregon's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the Constitution. There is no general IPJ over the defendant because Hermione is not domiciled in Oregon. For specific IPJ, there must be minimum contacts between the defendant, the forum, and the subject matter of the litigation. Here, the defendant purposefully availed herself of the resources of Oregon by purchasing the car there and the accident occurred there, so minimum contacts are likely sufficient. The plaintiff could have brought the case against Malfoy in Oregon.

Turning to venue — venue is properly laid under § 1391(b)(2) since Oregon is the district in which a substantial part of events giving rise to the claim occurred: the car was bought there and the accident occurred there.

The lawsuit *could* have been brought in Oregon.

**Step 2 — Should the case be transferred?** Using a § 1404(a) analysis, going through public and private factors. The presumption of plaintiff's choice of forum can only be overcome by a clear outweighing of factors.

Public factors: hearing the case in NJ would add a jury duty burden for NJ residents for an accident that occurred in Oregon due to a sale in Oregon — weighs in favor of transfer; Oregon has a local interest in ensuring that cars being sold in the state are not defective and dangerous to consumers — weighs in favor of transfer; possible docket congestion for either court, but the prompt does not indicate whether either court is particularly congested, so no bearing on this decision.

Private factors: more access to witnesses in Oregon since that is where the accident and sale occurred — weighs in favor of transfer; less cost to defendants for travel and out-of-state attorneys, more convenient for Malfoy since its PPOB is in Oregon — weighs in favor of transfer; possibility to view the accident scene/wreckage located there — weighs in favor of transfer.

**Answer:** The case could have been brought in Oregon and should be transferred there since local interests, convenience of evidence, witnesses, and cost all weigh against the presumption of plaintiff's forum.

### Question 3: Supplemental Jurisdiction over Farley

The district court should not have SMJ to hear Farley's claim against Malfoy alone because, although there would be complete diversity, the AIC requirement for diversity jurisdiction is not met. The court must therefore consider whether it can exercise supplemental jurisdiction under § 1367 to hear Farley's claim together with Hermione's.

Section 1367(a) says that all claims that form the same case or controversy are permissible, but § 1367(b) narrows that to disallow claims that would frustrate the jurisdictional requirements of § 1332. Section 1332 requires both complete diversity and AIC. It is clear that diversity is maintained if Farley's claim is allowed, but AIC is less clear. On its own, Farley's claim is below the $75k AIC amount at $50k. The question is therefore whether a supplemental claim under a diversity action must meet both the complete diversity and AIC requirements of § 1332, or whether it is sufficient that both claims together meet the requirements.

The purpose of diversity jurisdiction is to prevent home cooking, and the AIC requirement is merely a way of weeding out complaints that are relatively trivial, preserving the state/federal division of labor and preventing federal courts from being overcrowded. Since the claim would be supplemental to a claim that already meets diversity jurisdiction requirements, it would not greatly increase the work of the court for a merely trivial amount alone. The total of both Farley's and Hermione's claims exceeds the AIC requirement, preserving the § 1332 requirements if both plaintiffs are considered together. Furthermore, it would frustrate efficiency to have a state court hear the entire case again to decide Farley's claim, and inconsistent verdicts in state and federal court risk judicial inequity. Therefore, the claim should be allowed.
