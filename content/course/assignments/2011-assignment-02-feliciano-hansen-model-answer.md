## Part 1

**(a)** Whether the Durmstrang Federal Court (the "DFC") will apply state or federal law depends on which test is applied.

Under Harlan's inquiry, we must determine whether the application of a federal rule would affect the primary conduct of the parties involved in litigation. As is made clear in the opinion, the DFC believes that it will. Under Harlan's concurrence, the state rule should control.

Under *Hanna* we must ask whether the federal rule is valid and on point. Then, we must decide whether the federal and state rules clash, which depends on how broadly or narrowly one reads the rule. A FRCP is valid if it comports with the REA (does not "abridge enlarge or modify substantive rights") and Congress had the constitutional power to pass the law and did so validly — though it's questionable after *Iqbal* and *Twombly*, 8(a)(2) and 12(b)(6) still meet this criteria. Moreover, given that the language of the rules are identical, it is clear that the federal rule addresses the same exact issue and clashes. The federal pleading standard should control.

*York* counsels that the line between substance and procedure is determined by whether it affects the outcome of the litigation — if it affects the outcome then it is, by definition, substantive. Pleading standards are the first available tools for killing a lawsuit. If the DFC were to religiously enforce the federal standard, cases that might have otherwise survived a 12(b)(6) motion in state court, would be thrown out. Under *York* the state rule should apply.

**(b)** The RDA states that the *laws* of the several states are the rules of decision. *Erie* clarifies that "law" is not some 'transcendental' form independent of a particular judge or jurisdiction — in fact, the law is what judges say it means. Because *Erie* defines law in this way, it is clear that the state interpretation of 8(a)(2) and 12(b)(6) is state law.

**(c)** Removal limitations should be a discretionary factor in a judge's analysis on whether the federal or state pleading standard should apply depending on how a case got to federal court. Removal limitations prompt a more case-by-case consideration of fairness to litigants and litigation strategy, particularly in regards to Durmstrang defendants ("DDs").

Removal limits DDs access to a federal forum. DDs are unable to remove if sued in state court as they are in their home state — *i.e.*, "home cooking" isn't a relevant issue. Plaintiffs who did not want a DD to benefit from the higher federal pleading standards could just sue that defendant in state court. Out-of-state defendants in a diversity action would always be able to remove.

If the parties have arrived at the DFC, as opposed to a state court, we should ask how and why they got there. If a DD is in state court, it is likely because the plaintiff saw a benefit to trapping him there; in contrast, if a DD is in the DFC, it is only because the plaintiff has allowed it, either by filing in the DFC initially or by not contesting the defendant's removal action. But, why would a plaintiff allow the case to be heard in a federal forum? There could be lots of reasons, but, clearly, guarantee of a more lenient pleading standard wasn't one of them. Since the defendant never, in some sense, had a right to the federal standard, and the federal standard did not matter that much to the plaintiff, you might say that it doesn't matter whether the state or federal rule is applied — that is, unless there's a separate compelling reason to favor one over the other.

What about the out-of-state defendant? He always had a right to the federal forum. In that case, does it make sense to favor the plaintiff as the plaintiff never had the ability to trap the out-of-state defendant in a state court which applied the more relaxed state standard? Could we even say that the plaintiff has, in some sense, the *right* to trap? Should the out-of-state defendant have a right to a different pleading standard just by virtue of diversity when the relevant horizontal choice of law rules result in the application of Durmstrang substantive law to the case? Really, how concerned are we about prejudice at this point?

## Part 2

The state courts should probably have an obligation to the federal courts the way the federal courts do to the state courts. Admittedly, underlying the *Erie* doctrine is a strong current of federalism that just isn't present here, not to mention there is no statutory basis for a reverse *Erie* doctrine (*i.e.*, there is no corresponding § 1652). On the other hand, ensuring that the federal laws are being applied in a consistent manner throughout the states seems to be very much in line with the original spirit of the *Erie* opinion (discouragement of forum shopping and prevention of inequitable administration of laws). One of *Erie*'s chief policy concerns was that parties should not be able to achieve different results depending on whether or not they file in state or federal court. When federal courts are "adjudicating a state-created right solely because of the diversity of citizenship of the parties" they should, in essence, act like "another court of the state" (*York*). Why shouldn't the state courts behave in the same fashion?
