## Question 1

Yes, the court should grant the motion to dismiss. First, the complaint does not pass the plausibility pleading test articulated in recent U.S. case law (*Iqbal*). We must cross out any conclusory statements and accept only those leftover pieces of the complaint as true before deciding whether it plausibly states a claim. Here, both paragraphs 2 and 3 of the complaint are conclusory (they merely state that the defendant drove negligently and that such negligence caused plaintiff's injury). The elimination of paragraph 2 alone renders the complaint insufficient, as it only states an amount in controversy and establishes the court's SMJ over the action.

The court could also find that the complaint is time-barred, depending on which *Erie* test it adopts. The SoL argument depends upon two questions: whether a one-, two-, or three-year SoL governs, and whether the governing SoL began to run in March 2014 (misconduct) or April 2015 (discovery). Because this is a diversity case, under *Erie*, the Nebraska District Court must apply federal procedural rules, but state substantive rules. The relevant state law here is Nebraska's because the Nebraska District Court was a happy transferor (it had SMJ under diversity, PJ and venue because Ginny is domiciled in Nebraska) [*Van Dusen*], and because limitations periods are procedural for purposes of HCOL. Depending on whether the limitations period and limitations triggers are considered procedural or substantive for VCOL purposes, then, four combinations of rules are possible at the outset: 1yr + discovery; 1yr + misconduct; 2yr + discovery; 2yr + misconduct.

The holding of *York* (that limitations periods are substantive for VCOL purposes) is not dispositive here, because that case involved only federal laches (whereas here, Congress passed a 1 year statute of limitations). Under the reasoning of *York*, which is concerned with vertical uniformity of outcomes, the complaint should not be time-barred, because a Nebraska state court would allow the suit to proceed (under the state's 2 yr limitations period beginning at the time of misconduct).

Under the *Hanna* majority test, the suit might be time-barred because the court would apply the federal limitations period but the state limitations trigger. Because the federal 1 year limitations period is a statutory provision, then so long as it is constitutionally valid and applicable, it trumps the state SoL (*Stewart*). The FLPA is directly applicable, and is likely permissible under Congress' constitutional authority to create the lower federal courts. Because the discovery rule is federal common law, a *Hanna*-minded court might perform a modified outcome-determinative test (probably under the guise of the twin aims of *Erie*, which amounts to the same thing) to determine whether federal or state law ought to apply. Application of the federal and state rules here would certainly lead to different outcomes, so the outcome-determinative test would suggest using the state rule for when the limitations clock starts ticking. See below on whether this makes any sense.

Under Harlan's *Hanna* opinion, the question is whether choosing the federal over the state law would affect the primary conduct of parties outside of court, which is meant to be left to state regulation. It does not seem likely that a 1 or 2 year statute of limitations would alter a tortfeasor's degree of care or a tort victim's conduct beyond their choice of when to sue. However, it is definitely plausible that application of a discovery rule vs. a misconduct rule would impact the way the victim of a tort might respond to the kind of accident at issue here. For

example, had Dumbledore known that a statute of limitations might be triggered at the moment of misconduct, he might have scheduled a doctor's appointment immediately following the accident, instead of waiting for any symptoms to present. Under *Hanna* 2, then, we might also get the absurd result that the federal limitations period but state limitations trigger would apply.

## Question 2

No. The complaint should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim or for being outside the statute of limitations. First, it is almost an exact copy of Form 11, which is provided as part of the FRCP (see Rule 84) as an example of a permissible complaint for negligence. Arguably, the plausibility pleading standard articulated in recent Supreme Court case law is inconsistent with the rules. From an access to justice perspective, it seems especially important that a pro se plaintiff be able to model their papers off officially provided forms, rather than needing specialized legal knowledge to file a complaint.

Second, practically speaking, dismissal of a complaint that meets the demands of Form 11 would pose an insurmountable bar to many potential negligence plaintiffs. After all, the complaint can only allege information that a plaintiff can gather without the tools of discovery. How is a potential plaintiff like Dumbledore to gather more information about the alleged tortfeasor without access to the court system?

Third, on the SoL question, strict application of the various *Erie* tests to the two VCOL questions separately can yield the absurd result that a federal court sitting in diversity would apply the federal limitations period and state limitations trigger. This would make the suit time barred when brought as a diversity case in federal court even though both under an exclusively federal set of rules and under an exclusively state set of rules, the suit would be allowed to proceed. This result seems directly at odds with the rationales of *Erie*, as it manufactures a forum-shopping problem where there need not be one at all.
