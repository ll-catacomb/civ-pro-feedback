# Assignment 2: Model Answer

---

Jones probably can only sue Clinton in Arkansas state court, Arkansas federal court, NY state court, and NY federal court because these are the courts where she can get IPJ. There is IPJ over Clinton in NY because there is general jurisdiction in the state where the defendant resides. It should be fairly easy to establish IPJ in Arkansas because Clinton had minimum contacts there (all of his acts giving rise to the complaint occurred in Arkansas, *International Shoe*, and as the former governor of Arkansas, it is reasonably foreseeable that he would be haled into Arkansas court for his conduct as governor, *World-Wide Volkswagen*). We probably could not get IPJ in CA since Clinton has not had minimum contacts there. Since there is IPJ in Arkansas and NY state courts, there is also IPJ in Arkansas and NY federal courts. 4(k)(1)(A).

There is SMJ in Arkansas and NY federal courts because the parties are diverse and the amount in controversy exceeds $75,000. Article III § 2; 28 U.S.C. § 1332.

We can lay venue in NY federal court since Clinton resides there. § 1391(a)(1). We can lay venue in Arkansas federal court because Arkansas is the locus of the events at issue. § 1391(a)(2).

We want to litigate where our complaint will be subject to notice pleading standards because our complaint would probably survive a 12(b)(6) motion under notice pleading. Under *Iqbal* pleading standards, however, our complaint would probably be dismissed. The complaint doesn't include well-pleaded factual allegations that adverse employment consequences were orchestrated by Clinton because of Jones's refusal to engage in a sexual relationship. Rather, the complaint at most includes legal conclusions in regard to element three of the Arkansas antidiscrimination statute. This is not enough to nudge our claim across the line from conceivable to probable.

If we sue in NY or Arkansas state court and the case stays there then notice pleading standards would be used. While both courts will probably apply Arkansas substantive law (Arkansas uses the First Restatement so the law of the state where the injury took place governs; NY uses Second Restatement and under an interests analysis Arkansas law would probably govern), each court will use their own procedural law. The standard for the motion to dismiss is likely to be considered procedural for horizontal choice of law purposes and state courts always use their own procedural law. Since both courts use notice pleading standards, we would be in good shape if the case stayed in either state court.

I recommend that we sue in NY state court because Clinton is likely to remove to federal court if we sue in Arkansas and then the *Iqbal* standards might apply. If we sue in NY, however, Clinton will be stuck with state notice pleading standards because he cannot remove from his home state court to federal court on the basis of diversity. § 1441(b).

We want to avoid suing in federal court to begin with because then the *Iqbal* pleading standards might apply. Figuring that out requires an *Erie* analysis. We could try to argue that the state notice pleading standards should apply since they don't directly conflict with FRCP 8 and 12 (a *Walker*-like move). Even though the state RCP are identical to FRCP 8 and 12, it will still be hard to establish there is not a direct conflict since the state court and SCOTUS have interpreted the rules to call for very different standards. We could argue that *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal* should be read narrowly to only apply to potentially high cost discovery suits, but this would be a hard sell since our case arguably could be a high cost discovery suit. In addition, *Iqbal* seems inconsistent with this narrow interpretation since the court could have tried to contain discovery if that was the main concern. Therefore, the Court will probably find a direct conflict.

Under *Hanna*, if an on-point FRCP directly conflicts with state law, the FRCP applies as long as it is valid. The Court will probably consider FRCP 8 and 12 valid because pleading standards are rationally capable of classification as procedural regulations. Also, the Court will probably find that FRCP 8 and 12 do not abridge, enlarge, or modify a substantive right under the REA because pleading standards do not have a substantial impact on state policy unrelated to litigation.

We could try to argue that the *Iqbal* judicial interpretation of FRCP 8 and 12 is not the FRCP itself. Rather, we'd argue that the judicial interpretation qualifies as a federal judicial practice and thus is subject to the outcome determinative test as modified by *Hanna*, which calls for consideration of the twin aims of *Erie*. We'd argue that the vertical choice of law would affect forum shopping and the equal administration of law at the time of filing (since vertical choice of law would impact whether a case is dismissed) and at the outcome stage (if the complaint is dismissed in federal court but allowed in state court, the state suit might end successfully). Clinton might push back that the vertical choice of law won't impact primary conduct (sexual harassers probably aren't considering pleading standards during their misconduct) but the Court will probably still find that state law applies since the primary conduct test (based on *Hanna* concurrence) is secondary to the time of filing test (based on *Hanna* majority).

Since we can't be sure whether the federal court will apply the state or federal pleading standards, we are best off avoiding the whole mess by suing in NY state court.
