## Law regarding self-driving trucks and front proximity detectors

### Federal Law

In 2016, Congress passed the Willow Rosenberg Self-Driving Trucks Protection Act (the "Willow Act"). Named after a woman hit by a self-driving truck as she was standing on a sidewalk casting a spell, the Willow Act included the following provisions:

> **Section 201: Findings And Purpose**
> The Congress finds that self-driving trucks pose hazards to pedestrians almost as great as human-driven trucks. In particular, the Congress finds that front proximity detectors[^1] have proven to be a particular technological challenge for manufacturers of self-driving trucks. One purpose of this Act is therefore to empower the Secretary of Transportation (the "Secretary") to issue regulations promoting the safety and well-being of pedestrians through appropriate regulation, with particular attention to front proximity detectors.

> **Section 202: Department of Transportation ("DoT") Duties**
> (a) To promote the safety and well-being of pedestrians, after appropriate notice and comment as required by the Administrative Procedure Act, the Secretary shall issue regulations concerning the design, manufacture, sale, purchase, and maintenance of self-driving trucks, with special attention to the issue of front proximity detection.
> (b) . . .

In 2017, acting pursuant to the Willow Act, DoT Secretary Riley Finn promulgated the following regulations.

> **49 C.F.R. § 5X5: Front Proximity Detectors**
> (a) Any corporation or other business that manufactures or sells self-driving trucks shall, before completing the sale of any self-driving truck to any other person or entity, conduct an appropriate test of the front proximity detector and corresponding braking mechanism (such as causing the truck to drive slowly toward an obstacle or barrier to see if it stops on its own) in the presence of the buyer to discover whether the front proximity detector on the vehicle is in good working order.

> (b) No corporation or other business shall purchase a self-driving truck without witnessing the test of the front proximity detector described in subsection (a).
> (c) . . .

No other provision of the Willow Act nor of Secretary Finn's regulations has any language relevant to this exam.

### Law relating to personal jurisdiction, choice of law, respondeat superior, and civil procedure

All states relevant to this exam have long-arm statutes allowing the exercise of personal jurisdiction whenever the Constitution so allows.

Virginia and Delaware use the Second Restatement for horizontal choice of law. Pennsylvania, North Carolina, and South Carolina use the First Restatement.

### States and districts

Pennsylvania has three federal district courts; Philadelphia is located in the Eastern District of Pennsylvania. Virginia has two federal district courts; Alexandria, VA is located in the Eastern District of Virginia. South Carolina, Maryland, and Delaware each have a single federal district court. North Carolina has three districts; if a lawsuit relevant to this exam were to occur in North Carolina, it would occur in the Eastern District of North Carolina.

### Facts

It manufactures self-driving trucks of all sizes, from light pickups all the way up to 18 wheelers. XH conducts no sales activity to consumers or business, nor does it advertise. Instead, it sells its products exclusively in an enormous purchase and sale clearinghouse market facility located in Alexandria, VA called "The Spike." At The Spike, sellers and buyers of self-driving vehicles meet, conduct inspections, negotiate sales terms, transfer money, and take delivery of vehicles. XH sells to various distributors at The Spike. XH insists that any company to whom it sells one of its vehicles must subsequently sell the vehicle directly to a consumer. The XH employee in charge of many sales transactions was named Tara Maclay.

Faith, Inc. is a North Carolina corporation with its principal place of business in Columbia, South Carolina. Faith is a large distributor (to consumers) of self-driving vehicles. [^1]: Front proximity detectors are devices that detect objects or obstacles in front of a vehicle. [^2]: You may assume for the purposes of this exam that Buffy may sue XH and Faith for the negligence of its employees (if she can sue anyone at all). In other words: All states relevant to this exam use the doctrine of respondeat superior, meaning that a company is legally responsible (and thus can be sued) for any action taken, or any failure to take an action, within the scope of an employee's employment duties. For example, if a restaurant delivery-person drives too fast on their way to drop off a pizza at a customer's house and, as a result, hits and injuries a pedestrian, then the pedestrian may sue the restaurant for damages as vicariously liable under the doctrine of respondeat superior.
