## Substantive Assignment

All states relevant to this assignment have long-arm statutes providing that their state courts may exercise jurisdiction to the extent permissible under the U.S. Constitution.

All states relevant to this assignment have a tort cause of action for "conversion," also known as theft. A defendant commits the tort of conversion if they exercise wrongful dominion or control of the plaintiff's property, assets, or money to the detriment of the rights of the actual owner. "Property" in this context encompasses digital assets. The last event necessary for the completion of this tort is the removal of the item converted/stolen from the control of the plaintiff, so the tort occurs where the item was located at the time of the theft.

All states relevant to this assignment have a tort cause of action for negligence. The elements of this cause of action are that the defendant owes a duty of care to the plaintiff (everyone owes each other a duty to exercise ordinary care), that the defendant breached this duty by failing to act with ordinary care under the circumstances, and that the breach caused quantifiable harm to the plaintiff. In all states relevant to this assignment, a plaintiff can sue a defendant for negligence if the defendant failed to exercise ordinary care in guarding an item (including a digital item) that the plaintiff entrusted to the defendant's keeping, where such failure caused the loss (due to theft or otherwise) of that item. In such cases, the last event necessary for the completion of the tort is the theft/conversion of the item from the possession/control from the defendant such that the plaintiff no longer has access to the item. In other words, in this context, the tort occurs where the item was located at the time of the theft.

In South Carolina, South Dakota, and Texas, a defendant may assert the defense of contributory negligence. All other states relevant to this assignment use a comparative negligence system.[^1]

Texas, Oklahoma, and South Dakota follow the Second Restatement for horizontal choice of law. Massachusetts, South Carolina, and Delaware follow the First Restatement for horizontal choice of law.

Sylvania, Inc. was incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in Oklahoma City, OK. Its programmers all work in a massive facility in Oklahoma City, OK. The company specialized in selling cheap and un-hackable data storage. Sylvania was the first company to use quantum computing to encrypt its information on its servers, so anyone hacking into its systems except by way of a user account would obtain only gobbledygook. Users of Sylvania's data storage could access their data from anywhere in the world. They did so through a username and a password. Unlike most data storage companies, Sylvania did not require users to choose passwords of any particular length, nor did passwords have to include a particular mix of characters (e.g., numbers, letters, and special characters).

Also unlike most data storage companies, Sylvania allowed, but did not require, users to opt to enable two-factor authentication ("2FA") to log into their data storage accounts. In 2FA, a user must provide a username and a password to initiate the login process; the system then sends a code to the user's cell phone, which the user enters into the system to complete the login process. Thus, with 2FA, a hacker attempting to gain access to a user's account would require both knowledge of the user's username and password as well as possession of the user's cell phone (or some other method of acquiring and entering the system-generated code).

Sylvania's storage methods were as follows. When a user uploaded data to one of its accounts, Sylvania used patented technology to split the information into two pieces, neither of which could be used without the other. It stored the two pieces separately on its servers located in Boston, MA and in Columbia, SC.

Sylvania did no advertising. It depended entirely on word of mouth and recommendations from its users. Users created accounts on its website, sylvanianotfreedonia.com. Users paid in advance via credit card or cryptocurrency through an interface on sylvanianotfreedonia.com. Sylvania was especially popular among artists who created NFTs and other forms of digital crafts, and its customer base spanned the globe with no particular concentration in one country, but was overwhelmingly European and Asian. [^1]: As a reminder of our discussion in class:
- **Contributory negligence:** If the defendant persuades the trier of fact (the judge, or the jury) that the plaintiff was in any way at fault for the plaintiff's injury, no matter how small the plaintiff's contribution to the loss, the defendant is relieved of all liability. So, for example, if the plaintiff was 10% responsible for a $1000 loss, and the defendant 90% responsible, then the defendant owes the plaintiff $0.00.
- **Comparative negligence:** If the defendant persuades the trier of fact (the judge, or the jury) that the plaintiff was in any way at fault for the plaintiff's injury, the trier of fact apportions responsibility for the loss according to the fraction of the parties' fault. So, for example, if the plaintiff was 10% responsible for a $1000 loss, and the defendant 90% responsible, then the defendant owes the plaintiff $900.00.
