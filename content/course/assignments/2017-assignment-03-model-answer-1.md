## Assignment 3 — Civil Procedure 2, 2017

### Question 1: Motion to Dismiss

The complaint should not be dismissed.

**Preliminaries**

The Nebraska district court (DCNE) has SMJ via diversity: Ginny = NE, Aberforth = ME, AIC = $150,000 > $75,000. There is no HCOL issue presented by this fact pattern: NE and ME both use (i) the *Twiqbal* pleading standard, (ii) the misconduct rule for SOL initiation, and (iii) limitations periods of two years or longer. IPJ and venue were proper in DCNE (general IPJ, FRCP 4(k)(1)(A), § 1391(b)(1)). But had there been an IPJ or venue problem, the Maine district court (DCME) is a happy court. ME's long-arm statute goes to the limits of the Constitution. Ginny has minimum contacts with ME. She purposely availed herself of Maine by driving there and enjoying the state's protections. She also sampled local brews and attended a beer festival in ME, both connected to the accident leading to Aberforth's claim. It is also fair and reasonable to allow IPJ: it is convenient for Aberforth; ME has an interest in promoting road safety; the accident happened in ME so evidence and witnesses are there. Venue is also proper because the accident happened in ME (§ 1391(b)(2)).

**Failure to State a Claim**

The complaint probably meets the plausibility pleading standard. First, conclusory statements should be disregarded. Here, "negligently drove a car" is conclusory. Nonetheless, the complaint presents enough factual content to plausibly show all elements required to state a tort claim: Ginny drove the car and hit Aberforth while she was drunk and shouldn't have been driving (duty and breach); Aberforth was injured (harm); and plausibly the injury was caused by the car accident (causation). Thus, DCME should not dismiss the complaint on Rule 12(b)(6) grounds.

**Statute of Limitations**

DCME must choose between the misconduct/discovery rule and the applicable SOL. Under the discovery rule, the filing is within both federal and state SOLs. Under the misconduct rule, the timing is beyond the federal 1-year SOL but within both state SOLs (NE = 2 years, ME = 3 years). Because DCME is sitting in diversity and federal law differs on both choices, there are two *Erie* issues: the misconduct/discovery rule and the applicable SOL length.

**State Misconduct Rule vs. Federal Discovery Rule**

*York*: If DCME uses the federal SOL (1 year from filing), the choice between misconduct and discovery rules is outcome-determinative — if the misconduct rule is used, the case would be dismissed; if the discovery rule is used, the case would survive. So DCME should use the misconduct rule under *York*. If DCME uses the NE state SOL (2 years from misconduct), the choice is not outcome-determinative, and DCME can apply the discovery rule.

*Hanna*: The discovery rule is federal common law. The court consults the twin aims of *Erie*. Because the discovery rule is generally more lenient to victims, the twin aims likely suggest using state law (the misconduct rule) to discourage forum shopping and avoid inequitable administration of laws.

*Harlan* probably suggests using the misconduct rule, because the state misconduct rule may affect people's primary conduct: a misconduct rule may reduce a tort victim's incentive to discover harms and file lawsuits early. It would likely also affect the business strategies of car insurance companies.

**If DCME applies the federal discovery rule**, SOL is not an issue, and DCME should not dismiss the complaint.

**If DCME applies the misconduct rule**, an *Erie* analysis is required to determine the applicable SOL length. The time between misconduct (3/15/2016) and filing (8/12/2017) is approximately 17 months — beyond the federal 1-year SOL but within NE's 2-year SOL.

*York*: Using the state SOL (either NE or ME) is outcome-determinative when compared to the federal 1-year SOL, so state law should be used.

*Hanna* (statutory track): The FLPA is likely valid — Article III and the Necessary and Proper Clause likely give Congress authority to enact it. The FLPA is also applicable: its plain text states that when the plaintiff alleges "negligent operation" of a car, the SOL "shall be" 1 year. The FLPA is directly on point and should be applied. *Harlan* probably would apply the federal SOL, as a choice of SOL length alone rarely affects people's primary conduct.

**Overall conclusion**: Only by mixing the state misconduct rule (for start date) and the federal 1-year SOL would the complaint be dismissed — a combination that no rational court should adopt. The misconduct/discovery rules are intimately connected to SOL length, so it makes little sense to create a hybrid by combining state and federal laws. The court will likely choose to apply either federal law or state law to both issues. Under either coherent combination, Aberforth filed the lawsuit within the applicable limitations period. The complaint should not be dismissed.

### Question 2: Issue Preclusion Against Harry

Aberforth cannot invoke issue preclusion against Harry.

Harry and Ginny are different parties. Because Harry did not have an opportunity to appear in court to defend himself in Lawsuit 1 (the Ginny case), Due Process forbids invoking issue preclusion to deprive him of his day in court. Additionally, Ginny is not in privity with Harry. There is no suggestion that Harry controlled Ginny or was otherwise identified with her. Although Ginny voluntarily agreed to lie, thus "assuming" Harry's potential liability, this is not the same as stepping into the "legal shoes" of Harry as required to establish privity.

The answer remains the same regardless of what law is applied. The focal point is not about preclusion doctrine per se — it is that Due Process requires a defendant to have a meaningful opportunity to be heard.
