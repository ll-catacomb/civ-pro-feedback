If Snow can transfer to NDTX or WDTX, he may be able to use TRCP 8(a)(4).

Snow can't get SDCA to dismiss for lack of SMJ because there is DJ (Snow's incorporated in DE with its principal place of business in TX; Peeta is domiciled in CA; AIC = $400k > $75k).

Maybe Snow can force a transfer by objecting to IPJ. CA's long-arm goes to constitutional limits and explicitly welcomes these suits; per FRCP 4(k)(1)(A), federal jurisdiction piggybacks off CA's long-arm.

i. **General:** Snow is only actually at home in DE/TX. And Snow is not essentially at home (*Daimler*) in CA: Snow lacks 'substantial, continuous, and systemic contacts' with CA; it doesn't do anything close to >50% of its business there.

ii. **Specific:** Because Snow used an intermediary and didn't target any conduct at CA (e.g., via ads/website targeting), it's unlikely to qualify under the *McIntyre* plurality. Under Breyer's concurrence, a single sale is insufficient, but perhaps 10 would be sufficient. It'll be hard to find minimal contacts (*International Shoe*) due to lack of targeting and the use of a distributor. Fairness and reasonableness factors (*McGee*) are fairly pro-SDCA (the plaintiff is in SDCA, though the evidence is somewhat evenly split between the scene of the accident in SDCA and the manufacturing site in WDTX; CA substantive law will apply (CA is an R1 HCOL state (as is TX)). But ultimately, SIPJ is probably not satisfied due to lack of minimal contacts.

Snow probably can't force a transfer via a venue objection. Per § 1391(b)(2), venue is proper in the district where a substantial part of the events giving rise to the claim occurred — here, that's SDCA, where the accident happened (or maybe WDTX, where the set was manufactured).

So, it's not clear that SDCA is happy (venue + IPJ). If not, SDCA must transfer — likely to NDTX, where GIPJ exists and venue is proper under § 1391(b)(1) as Snow's PPOB. [Or perhaps WDTX, where venue is arguably proper under § 1391(b)(2), see above, and GIPJ is equally satisfied. It could theoretically transfer to DE, where venue is proper per § 1391(b)(1) as Snow's incorporation state and GIPJ exists for the same reason, but none of the *Gulf Oil* factors point there, so that's highly unlikely.]

Even if SDCA is happy, it may transfer anyway if doing so is in the interest of justice (*Gulf Oil/Piper*). So, presume plaintiff's choice (SDCA) and consider public/private interest factors, which here net out or lean slightly toward CA, so transfer is unlikely: For private interest, see *McGee* fair and reasonableness analysis above, pointing to SDCA; for public interest, CA and TX courts are similarly congested, though CA has demonstrated an interest in adjudicating these types of cases.

**If Snow effectuates a transfer, can he take advantage of TRCP 8(a)(4)?**

If SDCA was *happy*, its COL rules follow to NDTX or WDTX and the TX court will use CA procedural law (*Ferens*). If SDCA was *unhappy*, WDTX/NDTX would apply their own COL rules, and would apply TX procedural law.

<!-- page: 2 -->

TRCP 8(a)(4) is probably procedural, since most things arguably procedural are treated as procedural for HCOL. So, if SDCA was unhappy and TRCP 8(a)(4) is indeed procedural, TX courts will apply it. This sets up a conflict with federal law → *Erie*!

i. Under the *York* 'outcome determinative' test, Peeta's case will be dismissed if it doesn't comply with TRCP 8(a)(4). So, the outcome hinges on the application of TRCP 8(a)(4). Use **state** law.

ii. Under the *Hanna* majority test (Scalia version), FRCP 8 is valid under the Rules Enabling Act. (Per *Sibbach*: something is valid if it really regulates procedure, which Federal Rules do.) It's also applicable: The plain language of FRCP 8 speaks to pleading standards, which are at issue. Use **federal** law.

iii. Under *Hanna* majority test (Ginsburg version), FRCP 8 is valid (per the same analysis as Scalia). But it's arguably inapplicable. FRCP 8 sets a floor; TRCP 8(a)(4) adds more requirement atop the floor. FRCP 8 is silent as to whether products liability suits require heightened pleading standards, so it's arguably not applicable. Use **state** law.

iv. Under the Harlan *Hanna* concurrence 'primary conduct' test, applying TRCP 8(a)(4) might result in companies altering products manufacturing on the front end. Texas clearly thinks it does, since it's the stated purpose of the rule. Use **state** law.

Snow should request a transfer to NDTX or, in the alternative, WDTX.
