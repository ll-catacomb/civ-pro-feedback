## Question 1

To determine whether the suit is timely, we first have to determine which tolling period rule to apply. From an HCOL perspective, both Restatements characterize limitations rules as procedural, thus we apply the forum's rules. Which forum? Aberforth first sued in Nebraska, which had both IPJ and venue over Ginny, since it's her home district (*Hoffman*). Thus, Nebraska is a 'happy court' and its rules follow the transfer (*Ferens*). Thus, we apply the misconduct rule, if applying state law. This raises an *Erie* issue, since federal common law uses the discovery rule.

Under the *York* test, this choice might affect the outcome (depending on the limitations period, as per below), and thus we apply state law. Applying *Hanna* majority, the discovery rule is common law. *Hanna* suggests we should look at the twin aims of *Erie* (to prevent forum-shopping, and inequitable administration of the laws). Applying the federal rule here would lead to both, since the federal rule is different from the state rule, and the plaintiff can choose a forum based on which rule he wants. Similarly, an out-of-state defendant might have to face a different rule in a diversity action than an in-state defendant. Thus, we would apply state law. Applying Harlan, however, we would use federal law, since it seems unlikely that the tolling period would affect pre-litigation behavior. It has nothing to do with the actual standards of conduct for negligence, but only deals with when the limitations period starts to tick. Because the tests point in different directions, we can't know for sure which rule will apply. If the discovery rule applies, then Ginny's motion to dismiss on limitations grounds should be dismissed regardless of the length of the limitations period. So I'll assume for now that the Nebraska misconduct rule applies.

The next determination is the length of the limitations period. For the same reasons as above, this is another *Erie* question between Nebraska (2 years), and federal law (1 year). The *York* decision on limitations periods is not controlling here, since *York* was decided in the absence of a controlling federal statute. Nonetheless, *York* still suggests we use state law, since applying federal law would cause this suit to be untimely, whereas state law would allow it to go forward. The *Hanna* test, however, leads to a different result here. Since the limitations period comes from a statute, the question is whether the rule is valid and applicable. Congress can rely on Article III to pass this statute, since it governs what happens in a federal court, and thus it is valid. It is also so specific and on-point that it's hard to argue it's not applicable to this case. Thus, *Hanna* suggests we apply federal law. Harlan, like above, suggests we apply federal law since the limitations period does not affect primary conduct.

If we apply the federal period, this suit is out of time, since the case was filed more than a year after the misconduct occurred. If we apply the state period, the suit is timely. If we believe that the *Hanna* majority is controlling, then the suit is out of time. (But see below.)

In addition, Ginny can also get the suit dismissed under FRCP 12(b)(6) on the grounds that it fails to state a claim. Under the *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal* standards, the complaint needs to allege enough facts to raise a reasonable expectation that discovery will reveal evidence, and the plaintiff must allege a plausible claim for relief. The complaint merely conclusively states that the "defendant negligently drove a car against the plaintiff," without alleging the elements of the claim of negligence, or any facts suggesting Ginny's actions meet these elements. Taking out this conclusory statement (*Bell Atlantic*) leaves the complaint failing to state a cause of action. Thus, Ginny's motion to dismiss should be granted. (But again, see below.)

## Question 2

I do not think the complaint should be dismissed on these grounds, because the law as applied to this case results in unfair outcomes.

Limitations periods in general serve efficiency and fairness purposes, by incentivizing plaintiffs to bring cases early, while the evidence is still fresh. In this case, however, applying state law to one aspect of the limitations period, and federal law to another, as the *Hanna* majority arguably requires, doesn't make any sense. Applying all of Nebraska law, or all of federal law, to this case would result in the case being timely. It seems ridiculous to expect plaintiffs to file within a year of misconduct happening, when it might take them a while (as in this case) to realize that an injury occurred. This is especially ridiculous when proof of injury is a required element of a tort claim — the plaintiff cannot file a negligence suit pre-emptively, in the expectation that they may be injured.

In addition, applying the *Twiqbal* standards to this case seems unnecessary. In *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal*, the defendant was facing a costly, time-consuming, and (in the *Iqbal* case), potentially national-security-related discovery process, if the claim was allowed to proceed. In these situations, it made sense to require a higher standard of plausibility before proceeding to discovery. It makes less sense in this case, where the claim is fairly simple, discovery is unlikely to be very extensive, and the plaintiff cannot possibly know, without discovery, whether Ginny was drinking (and therefore breached the standard of care etc.). In addition, this complaint mirrors Form 11, which FRCP 84 says is sufficient under the rules.
