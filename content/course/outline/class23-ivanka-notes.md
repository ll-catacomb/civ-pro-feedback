## Background Cases, Statutes and References

### Cases (Common Law)

- ***Beacon Theatres, Inc. v. Westover*, 359 U.S. 500 (SCOTUS)(1959)**
  In the same lawsuit involving both equity and law (and law/fact) issues, when scheduling adjudications, they must be scheduled in a way to preserve the party's right to a jury trial.

- ***Wal-mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes*, 564 U.S. 338 (2011)(SCOTUS)** *(establishes the commonality element of class action litigation)*
  Class certification under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(a) is improper if there is no common injury that may be resolved across the entire class.

- ***Phillips Petroleum Co. v. Shutts*, 472 U.S. 797 (1985)(SCOTUS)**
  (1) A state may exercise jurisdiction over the claim of an absent class-action plaintiff, even if the plaintiff lacks minimum contacts with the state, so long as the plaintiff is provided with minimal procedural-due-process protection.
  (2) In a class action, the forum state must have a significant contact or aggregation of contacts creating a state interest in the claims asserted by each plaintiff class member to ensure that the choice of the forum state's law is not arbitrary or unfair.

- ***Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Court*, 137 S.Ct. 1773 (2017)(SCOTUS)**
  For a state court to assert specific jurisdiction, there must be an affiliation between the forum state and the specific claim at issue.

- ***Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur v. Cauble*, 255 U.S. 356 (1921)(SCOTUS)**
  A suit brought on behalf of all members of a class is cognizable by the federal district court where diversity of citizenship exists between the parties complainant (named representatives) and defendant, and the decree will bind all members.

## Federal Sources of Law

### Federal Rules of Civil Procedure

**Fed. R. Civ. P. 23 – Class Actions**

**(a) Prerequisites.** One or more members of a class may sue or be sued as representative parties on behalf of all members only if:

(1) the class is so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable;

(2) there are questions of law or fact common to the class;

(3) the claims or defenses of the representative parties are typical of the claims or defenses of the class; and

(4) the representative parties will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class.

**(b) Types of Class Actions.** A class action may be maintained if Rule 23(a) is satisfied and if:

(1) prosecuting separate actions by or against individual class members would create a risk of:

> (A) inconsistent or varying adjudications with respect to individual class members that would establish incompatible standards of conduct for the party opposing the class; or
>
> (B) adjudications with respect to individual class members that, as a practical matter, would be dispositive of the interests of the other members not parties to the individual adjudications or would substantially impair or impede their ability to protect their interests;

(2) the party opposing the class has acted or refused to act on grounds that apply generally to the class, so that final injunctive relief or corresponding declaratory relief is appropriate respecting the class as a whole; or

(3) the court finds that the questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members, and that a class action is superior to other available methods for fairly and efficiently adjudicating the controversy. The matters pertinent to these findings include:

<!-- page: 3 -->

> (A) the class members' interests in individually controlling the prosecution or defense of separate actions;
>
> (B) the extent and nature of any litigation concerning the controversy already begun by or against class members;
>
> (C) the desirability or undesirability of concentrating the litigation of the claims in the particular forum; and
>
> (D) the likely difficulties in managing a class action.

**(c) Certification Order; Notice to Class Members; Judgment; Issues Classes; Subclasses.**

(1) Certification Order.

> (A) Time to Issue. At an early practicable time after a person sues or is sued as a class representative, the court must determine by order whether to certify the action as a class action.
>
> (B) Defining the Class; Appointing Class Counsel. An order that certifies a class action must define the class and the class claims, issues, or defenses, and must appoint class counsel under Rule 23(g).
>
> (C) Altering or Amending the Order. An order that grants or denies class certification may be altered or amended before final judgment.

(2) Notice.

> (A) For (b)(1) or (b)(2) Classes. For any class certified under Rule 23(b)(1) or (b)(2), the court may direct appropriate notice to the class.
>
> (B) For (b)(3) Classes. For any class certified under Rule 23(b)(3)—or upon ordering notice under Rule 23(e)(1) to a class proposed to be certified for purposes of settlement under Rule 23(b)(3)—the court must direct to class members the best notice that is practicable under the circumstances, including individual notice to all members who can be identified through reasonable effort. The notice may be by United States mail, electronic means, or other appropriate means. The notice must clearly and concisely state in plain, easily understood language:
>
> (i) the nature of the action;

<!-- page: 4 -->

> (ii) the definition of the class certified;
> (iii) the class claims, issues, or defenses;
> (iv) that a class member may enter an appearance through an attorney if the member so desires;
> (v) that the court will exclude from the class any member who requests exclusion;
> (vi) the time and manner for requesting exclusion; and
> (vii) the binding effect of a class judgment on members under Rule 23(c)(3).

(3) Judgment. Whether or not favorable to the class, the judgment in a class action must:

> (A) for any class certified under Rule 23(b)(1) or (b)(2), include and describe those whom the court finds to be class members; and
>
> (B) for any class certified under Rule 23(b)(3), include and specify or describe those to whom the Rule 23(c)(2) notice was directed, who have not requested exclusion, and whom the court finds to be class members.

(4) Particular Issues. When appropriate, an action may be brought or maintained as a class action with respect to particular issues.

(5) Subclasses. When appropriate, a class may be divided into subclasses that are each treated as a class under this rule.

**(d) Conducting the Action.**

(1) In General. In conducting an action under this rule, the court may issue orders that:

> (A) determine the course of proceedings or prescribe measures to prevent undue repetition or complication in presenting evidence or argument;
>
> (B) require—to protect class members and fairly conduct the action—giving appropriate notice to some or all class members of:
>
> (i) any step in the action;

<!-- page: 5 -->

> (ii) the proposed extent of the judgment; or
> (iii) the members' opportunity to signify whether they consider the representation fair and adequate, to intervene and present claims or defenses, or to otherwise come into the action;
>
> (C) impose conditions on the representative parties or on intervenors;
>
> (D) require that the pleadings be amended to eliminate allegations about representation of absent persons and that the action proceed accordingly; or
>
> (E) deal with similar procedural matters.

(2) Combining and Amending Orders. An order under Rule 23(d)(1) may be altered or amended from time to time and may be combined with an order under Rule 16.

**(e) Settlement, Voluntary Dismissal, or Compromise.** The claims, issues, or defenses of a certified class—or a class proposed to be certified for purposes of settlement—may be settled, voluntarily dismissed, or compromised only with the court's approval. The following procedures apply to a proposed settlement, voluntary dismissal, or compromise:

(1) Notice to the Class.

> (A) Information That Parties Must Provide to the Court. The parties must provide the court with information sufficient to enable it to determine whether to give notice of the proposal to the class.
>
> (B) Grounds for a Decision to Give Notice. The court must direct notice in a reasonable manner to all class members who would be bound by the proposal if giving notice is justified by the parties' showing that the court will likely be able to:
>
> (i) approve the proposal under Rule 23(e)(2); and
> (ii) certify the class for purposes of judgment on the proposal.

<!-- page: 6 -->

(2) Approval of the Proposal. If the proposal would bind class members, the court may approve it only after a hearing and only on finding that it is fair, reasonable, and adequate after considering whether:

> (A) the class representatives and class counsel have adequately represented the class;
> (B) the proposal was negotiated at arm's length;
> (C) the relief provided for the class is adequate, taking into account:
> > (i) the costs, risks, and delay of trial and appeal;
> > (ii) the effectiveness of any proposed method of distributing relief to the class, including the method of processing class-member claims;
> > (iii) the terms of any proposed award of attorney's fees, including timing of payment; and
> > (iv) any agreement required to be identified under Rule 23(e)(3); and
>
> (D) the proposal treats class members equitably relative to each other.

(3) Identifying Agreements. The parties seeking approval must file a statement identifying any agreement made in connection with the proposal.

(4) New Opportunity to Be Excluded. If the class action was previously certified under Rule 23(b)(3), the court may refuse to approve a settlement unless it affords a new opportunity to request exclusion to individual class members who had an earlier opportunity to request exclusion but did not do so.

(5) Class-Member Objections.

> (A) In General. Any class member may object to the proposal if it requires court approval under this subdivision (e). The objection must state whether it applies only to the objector, to a specific subset of the class, or to the entire class, and also state with specificity the grounds for the objection.

<!-- page: 7 -->

> (B) Court Approval Required for Payment in Connection with an Objection. Unless approved by the court after a hearing, no payment or other consideration may be provided in connection with:
> (i) forgoing or withdrawing an objection, or
> (ii) forgoing, dismissing, or abandoning an appeal from a judgment approving the proposal.
>
> (C) Procedure for Approval After an Appeal. If approval under Rule 23(e)(5)(B) has not been obtained before an appeal is docketed in the court of appeals, the procedure of Rule 62.1 applies while the appeal remains pending.

**(f) Appeals.** A court of appeals may permit an appeal from an order granting or denying class-action certification under this rule, but not from an order under Rule 23(e)(1). A party must file a petition for permission to appeal with the circuit clerk within 14 days after the order is entered or within 45 days after the order is entered if any party is the United States, a United States agency, or a United States officer or employee sued for an act or omission occurring in connection with duties performed on the United States' behalf. An appeal does not stay proceedings in the district court unless the district judge or the court of appeals so orders.

**(g) Class Counsel.**

(1) Appointing Class Counsel. Unless a statute provides otherwise, a court that certifies a class must appoint class counsel. In appointing class counsel, the court:

> (A) must consider:
> (i) the work counsel has done in identifying or investigating potential claims in the action;
> (ii) counsel's experience in handling class actions, other complex litigation, and the types of claims asserted in the action;
> (iii) counsel's knowledge of the applicable law; and
> (iv) the resources that counsel will commit to representing the class;

<!-- page: 8 -->

> (B) may consider any other matter pertinent to counsel's ability to fairly and adequately represent the interests of the class;
> (C) may order potential class counsel to provide information on any subject pertinent to the appointment and to propose terms for attorney's fees and nontaxable costs;
> (D) may include in the appointing order provisions about the award of attorney's fees or nontaxable costs under Rule 23(h); and
> (E) may make further orders in connection with the appointment.

(2) Standard for Appointing Class Counsel. When one applicant seeks appointment as class counsel, the court may appoint that applicant only if the applicant is adequate under Rule 23(g)(1) and (4). If more than one adequate applicant seeks appointment, the court must appoint the applicant best able to represent the interests of the class.

(3) Interim Counsel. The court may designate interim counsel to act on behalf of a putative class before determining whether to certify the action as a class action.

(4) Duty of Class Counsel. Class counsel must fairly and adequately represent the interests of the class.

**(h) Attorney's Fees and Nontaxable Costs.** In a certified class action, the court may award reasonable attorney's fees and nontaxable costs that are authorized by law or by the parties' agreement. The following procedures apply:

(1) A claim for an award must be made by motion under Rule 54(d)(2), subject to the provisions of this subdivision (h), at a time the court sets. Notice of the motion must be served on all parties and, for motions by class counsel, directed to class members in a reasonable manner.

(2) A class member, or a party from whom payment is sought, may object to the motion.

(3) The court may hold a hearing and must find the facts and state its legal conclusions under Rule 52(a).

(4) The court may refer issues related to the amount of the award to a special master or a magistrate judge, as provided in Rule 54(d)(2)(D).

## Lecture Recap for Day 22

**Four types of mutuality element of issue preclusion** (jurisdiction-dependent):

1. Requires parties be the same and in the same procedural posture (strictest):
   - Plaintiff in L1 must be the plaintiff in L2
   - Defendant in L1 must be the defendant in L2

2. Requires parties be the same but can be in different procedural postures:
   - Plaintiff in L1 can be the plaintiff or defendant in L2
   - Defendant in L1 can be the defendant or plaintiff in L2

3. Distinction between **offensive** and **defensive** nonmutual collateral estoppel (Federal Common Law Rule — *Parklane Hosiery*):
   - Defensive nonmutual ordinarily **allowed** (removes incentive for P to sue defendants one at a time; incentivizes joining all defendants in one suit)
   - Offensive nonmutual ordinarily **not allowed**, with exception: if P2 could not have joined, participated in, or intervened in L1 (*Parklane*)

4. No mutuality requirement at all

**Intersystem Preclusion:**

Full faith and credit obligation is always required when a judgment comes from a different jurisdiction. It means giving "at least as much preclusive effect" as the rendering court's jurisdiction would give.

**Full Faith and Credit Governing Obligations:**

| Rendering Court | Governing Source |
|---|---|
| State court | § 1738 (regardless of subsequent court) |
| Federal "arising under" | Uniform federal common law (*Parklane Hosiery*) |
| Federal "diversity" | State preclusion law of the state where the federal court sits (*Semtek*) |

<!-- page: 10 -->

**Semtek rationale:** Horizontal uniformity among federal courts must give way to vertical uniformity (same result in fed court as in state court when sitting in diversity).

- If state preclusion law where the federal diversity court sat would preclude → subsequent court (state or federal) **must also preclude**
- If state preclusion law would NOT preclude → subsequent court **might or might not** preclude (*Migra*, *Semtek*)

***Hart*** recap:
- Rendering court: federal district court in Texas (diversity)
- Texas required mutuality; different plaintiff → Texas would NOT preclude
- No full faith and credit obligation on the Hart (NY) court to preclude
- NY court decided to preclude anyway on efficiency grounds (analogous to *forum non conveniens*, abstention doctrine)

**Agency preclusion:**
- Most jurisdictions give preclusive effect (claim or issue) to agency adjudications if the agency meets procedural due process requirements
- Restatement of Judgments § 83 lists requirements (non-exhaustive)
- ***Marresse* note:** When a rendering agency lacks the ability to adjudicate certain causes of action, those will **not** be precluded in subsequent litigation (important given the narrow scope of agency jurisdiction)

## Law, Equity and Scheduling

### *Beacon Theatres, Inc. v. Westover*, 359 U.S. 500 (SCOTUS)(1959)

In the same lawsuit involving both equity and law (and law/fact) issues, when scheduling adjudications, they must be scheduled in a way to preserve the party's right to a jury trial.

**Facts:**
- After Beacon Theaters, Inc. (plaintiff) built a drive-in movie theater in California, it notified Fox West Coast Theatres, Inc. (Fox) that Fox's practice of forming "first run" clearance contracts — contracts giving certain theaters exclusive rights to show new movies — was violating antitrust laws.
- Before Beacon could file suit, Fox filed a Complaint for Declaratory Relief, asking the District Court for a declaratory judgment that Fox's clearance contracts were not in violation of antitrust laws and requesting a preliminary injunction to prevent Beacon from filing suit.
- Beacon filed a counterclaim alleging that Fox was violating the Sherman Antitrust Act, and demanded a jury trial.
- Judge Westover (defendant) found that the issues were basically equitable, and directed that the issues be tried in front of the district court before being submitted to a jury. Court of appeals upheld. SCOTUS granted certiorari.

**Holding and Reasoning (Black, J.):**
- When legal and equitable issues exist in the same trial, the **legal issues must be first resolved by a jury** before the equitable issues may be resolved by the judge.
- The Declaratory Judgment Act and the FRCP act in concert with the Seventh Amendment to preserve the distinction between jury and nonjury issues while protecting litigants' right to a jury trial.
- The district judge abused his discretion when he granted equitable relief preventing Beacon from having its claims heard before a jury.

<!-- page: 12 -->

**Simplified:**
- Old Movie Theater (OMT) negotiated exclusive "first run" rights with movie distributors.
- New Movie Theatre (NMT) wanted to show the same newly released movies; distributors were unsure whether they could contract with NMT without violating OMT's exclusivity.
- NMT threatened to sue OMT for antitrust violation (unreasonable restraint of trade).
- OMT pre-emptively filed for declaratory judgment (equity) that its clearance contracts were lawful, plus sought an anti-lawsuit injunction.

<!-- page: 13 -->

- NMT filed a counterclaim for antitrust damages and demanded a jury trial.
  - NMT (as consumers/public) wanted a jury because consumers would be the triers of fact and likely sympathetic.
- Judge tried to bifurcate: hear the equitable injunction first, then the damages case.

**The problem:** Both claims share the same "transaction or occurrence" and case or controversy — what is at work here is **"law of the case"** (analogous to, but not the same as, issue preclusion):
- *Issue preclusion* requires a final judgment; *law of the case* does not — it says once something has been decided within a case, it shall not be redecided in that same case.
- If the judge adjudicates the equitable claim first and finds (e.g.) that the theaters are in the same geographic market, that finding could preclude NMT from having the jury decide that same factual issue.

**Solution:** Hold the **jury trial first** → the jury decides the common factual issues → judge can then still issue the injunction. OMT loses nothing (there is no right to a bench trial); NMT preserves its jury trial right (which is a constitutional entitlement when damages are at stake).

<!-- page: 14 -->

**SCOTUS:** It's unfair to deny NMT its Seventh Amendment jury trial right.

This relates to *Parklane Hosiery*: in *Parklane*, the SEC's equity trial did in fact preclude the jury trial that private plaintiffs demanded — but that was in **separate lawsuits**. In the **same lawsuit**, scheduling cannot be used to defeat the jury trial right.

**BLL:** In the same lawsuit, when scheduling adjudications that involve claims at law and claims at equity, they must be scheduled in a way to preserve the right to a jury trial.

<!-- page: 15 -->

General policy principle: if we can have jury trials without too much inconvenience, we will have them. Scheduling is not a good reason to deny a jury trial. *(See also* Chauffeurs v. Terry*)*

## Class Actions

### Types

**1. Prejudice Class Actions** — Authorizes a class from which members cannot exit in order to avoid prejudice from individual lawsuits. Two subparts:

**FRCP 23(b)(1)(A):**
- Risk that individual actions would create "incompatible standards of conduct" for the party opposing the class
- Only appropriate when the class primarily seeks injunctive or declaratory relief
- A series of individual lawsuits would place defendant in a position of uncertainty not knowing how to treat other class members
- Not appropriate when plaintiffs primarily seek monetary damages

**FRCP 23(b)(1)(B):** *(Bankruptcy Rationale — not covered in this class)*
- Deals with potential prejudice to absentees
- Authorizes certification when individual actions "would be dispositive of the interests" or "substantially impair or impede" the ability of nonparties to protect their interests
- Example: multiple litigants to a limited fund (insurance proceeds)

**2. Injunctive/Declaratory Relief Class Actions** — Class seeking injunctive or declaratory relief to change defendant's conduct prospectively rather than compensate for past harms

**FRCP 23(b)(2):**
- Defendant's conduct needs to apply generally to the class (perfect harmony not required)
- Applies only when a **single injunction or declaratory judgment** would provide relief to each member of the class
- Not authorized when each member would be entitled to a different injunction or declaratory judgment
- Not authorized when each class member would be entitled to individualized amounts of damages

<!-- page: 16 -->

**3. Damage Class Actions** — Class suits seeking damages for injuries caused by the same defendant.

**FRCP 23(b)(3):**
- Requires claims of class members to share questions of law or fact that **predominate** over any questions affecting only individual members
- Class treatment must be shown to be a **superior** mode of adjudication
- Four factors to establish predominance and superiority:
  - (A) the class members' interests in individually controlling the prosecution or defense of separate actions
  - (B) the extent and nature of any litigation concerning the controversy already begun by or against class members
  - (C) the desirability or undesirability of concentrating the litigation of the claims in the particular forum
  - (D) the likely difficulties in managing a class action
- Members are **permitted to opt out** of this action
- When one or more central issues are common to the class and can be said to predominate, the action may be certified under 23(b)(3) even though other matters (e.g., damages or individual affirmative defenses) will have to be tried separately

---

### Requirements for Class Certification — FRCP 23(a)

Four main requirements:

**1. NUMEROSITY:**
- Quantitative: class of 20 members is likely too small; class of 40+ likely sufficient
- Qualitative: joinder does not have to be impossible — only difficult and inconvenient

**2. COMMONALITY:** Class members must have suffered the **same injury**, not just a violation of the same law. (*Wal-Mart*)
- Class-wide uniform question of law or fact to resolve all class members' claims
- Case must be able to **generate common answers apt to drive the litigation's resolution**

**3. TYPICALITY:**
- Some relationship between commonality and typicality (*Wal-Mart* dicta)
- Looks to whether the class representatives are **sufficiently similar** to the rest of the class (in terms of legal claims, factual circumstances, and stake in the litigation) so that certifying those individuals would not be unfair to the rest of the proposed class

**4. REPRESENTATIVENESS:**
- The named plaintiff must "**fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class**"

## Fells Wargo Hypo — Class Action Mechanics

**Facts:**
- Bank — **Fells Wargo** is programming computer systems in **Austin, Texas (Western District)**
- Bank has hundreds of thousands of individual bank accounts, including Plaintiff (**Southall's**) account
- Mid-level employee (**O'Quinn**) goes to his supervisor (**Zhang**) with a scheme that would make the bank $150M in profits in exchange for a promotion
- **Scheme:**
  - Take $1 from each consumer account per month, put it in a bank-controlled account, for 2 years ($24 per consumer account total)
  - No one is likely to sue over $24 individually (filing fee alone exceeds $24)
- Zhang approves the scheme; O'Quinn (from Austin) creates a computer program to implement it

<!-- page: 18 -->

**NOTE:** Corporations which serve consumers can frequently be in a position to hurt a large number of people just by taking a very tiny little bit of money.

**Plaintiffs' Attorneys: Law Firm of Brigantti-Abraham & Gupta, LLP ("B-A & G")**
- File lawsuits on behalf of plaintiffs on a contingency basis
- In class lawsuits, attorneys take ~10–30% off the top of any settlement; the rest is divided among plaintiffs
- Attorneys front all filing fees, deposition transcripts, witness fees — paid back from settlement
- Attorney needs a signed consent to representation (retainer) from each client
- Attorneys investigate potential wrongdoing: some reporter interviewed **Southall** (a bank consumer experiencing weird charges)
- Attorneys investigate, approach bank, get stonewalled
- Paralegal **Sao** calls O'Quinn about the charges; O'Quinn is unresponsive

**Defendant (Bank's) Attorneys: Law Firm of Minion & Fallah, LLP**
- O'Quinn and Zhang explain the phone call from B-A & G paralegal
- Attorneys instruct O'Quinn and Zhang to say nothing to anyone; refer all calls to attorneys' office
- Send bank-wide notice directing all inquiries about the charges to the attorneys' office

<!-- page: 19 -->

**Plaintiffs' firm begins seeking representation contracts:**
- **Southall** (domiciled in Delaware) agrees to be represented by B-A & G
  - Best-case scenario: she might get $80 (possibly with punitive/treble damages)
  - Must invest many hours in depositions, interrogatories
- **Collective Action Problem:** Getting each plaintiff to join via individual retainer agreements would be impractical and extremely costly — can only represent all plaintiffs if there is a **court order certifying the class**

The most important actors in a class action are the **plaintiffs' attorneys:**
- They found a theory
- They found a client
- They fronted money
- They filed a lawsuit

**Policy:**
As a society, we want bank-scheme behavior to not happen again. Ways to prevent it:
- Common law litigation (requires the litigation)
- Congressional legislation
- Creating regulatory agencies with remedies
- Empowering government lawyers (AGs) to sue on behalf of consumers
- Private Attorneys General (law firms empowered to sue on behalf of consumers)
- Class lawsuits

But large corporations lobby against such legislation, so class litigation becomes the practical remedy.

## Mechanics of Class Action Certification — Fells Wargo Hypo

**Finding Parties:**
- Plaintiff attorneys sue the **bank** (not O'Quinn individually), because the bank has money.
- **Plaintiff (Southall):** domiciled in Delaware
- **Defendant (Fells Wargo):** incorporated in NY; principal place of business (PPOB) in NJ

**Personal Jurisdiction:**

*General jurisdiction (objecting out-of-state defendant):*
- New York (state of incorporation)
- New Jersey (PPOB)

*Specific jurisdiction (objecting out-of-state defendant):*
- Texas (Western District):
  - Long-Arm Statute: suppose TX has one
  - Minimal contacts:
    - Defendant: O'Quinn created the program while sitting in Austin; the program was used to steal money from individual customers
    - Forum: Texas
    - Subject matter of litigation: the theft scheme originated in Texas
  - Fair and reasonable:
    - Defendant situated in Texas
    - Evidence at the bank's computer in Austin
    - Bank can get attorneys to Texas easily
    - Texas has state interest in regulating banking conduct within its borders
- Delaware: may exercise specific jurisdiction, but only for the Delaware plaintiffs; likely insufficient to cover the nationwide class (*Bristol-Myers Squibb*)

<!-- page: 21 -->

Note: ***Bristol-Myers Squibb*** was not a class action — it was a joinder of individual lawsuits. But depending on how a court views class actions, it may not grant jurisdiction over non-Delaware plaintiffs when there is an objecting out-of-state defendant.

### *Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Court*, 137 S.Ct. 1773 (2017)(SCOTUS)

For a state court to assert specific jurisdiction, there must be an affiliation between the forum state and the specific claim at issue.

**Facts:**
- Plaintiffs, residents of California and 33 other states, brought suit against Bristol-Myers Squibb (BMS) (defendant) in California Superior Court alleging that Plavix (a drug BMS manufactured) injured them.
- BMS: Delaware corporation with PPOB in New York.
- Plavix was not designed, developed, or manufactured in California.
- Non-California plaintiffs did not buy or take Plavix in California.
- BMS moved to quash service of summons on non-California residents.
- Superior court denied; CA Court of Appeal held CA had specific jurisdiction over nonresidents (because their claims were identical to CA residents' claims). CA Supreme Court affirmed. SCOTUS granted certiorari.

**Holding and Reasoning (Alito, J.):**
- For specific jurisdiction, there must be an **affiliation between the forum state and the specific claim at issue**.
- It is not sufficient for the defendant to have other contacts with the forum state; contacts must be **related to the claim at issue**.
- If the defendant's conduct giving rise to the claim and the plaintiff's injury both occur outside the state, the court likely cannot assert specific jurisdiction.
- The fact that the claims and injuries of resident plaintiffs are identical to those of nonresident plaintiffs is **immaterial** to the question of specific jurisdiction.
- BMS did not design, develop, or manufacture Plavix in California; nonresident plaintiffs did not buy or take Plavix in California. Both the conduct and the injuries occurred outside California.
- California courts do not have specific jurisdiction over the nonresidents' claims.

<!-- page: 22 -->

***Shutts*** says that we would not have to worry about the plaintiff-side of *Bristol-Myers Squibb*: a Delaware court could have jurisdiction over the class action plaintiffs as long as they receive adequate notice and the opportunity to opt out.

In *Shutts*, the court was exercising personal jurisdiction **over Plaintiffs**. *Bristol-Myers Squibb* discusses whether a court can get personal jurisdiction **over the objecting out-of-state defendant** — and there is still an open question about whether *BMS* even applies to class actions.

### *Phillips Petroleum Co. v. Shutts*, 472 U.S. 797 (1985)(SCOTUS)

(1) A state may exercise jurisdiction over the claim of an absent class-action plaintiff, even if the plaintiff lacks minimum contacts with the state, so long as the plaintiff is provided with minimal procedural-due-process protection.

(2) In a class action, the forum state must have a significant contact or aggregation of contacts creating a state interest in the claims asserted by each plaintiff class member to ensure that the choice of the forum state's law is not arbitrary or unfair.

**Facts:**
- Phillips Petroleum Co. (defendant), a producer and seller of natural gas, extracted gas from leased real property in 11 states.
- Shutts (plaintiff), a royalty owner, brought a class-action suit in Kansas state court, alleging that class members were owed interest on royalty payments that Phillips had delayed.
- Trial court certified a class of royalty owners from all 50 states, Washington D.C., and foreign countries.
- Shutts sent first-class mail notice to each class member — advising that they could appear in person or by counsel, would otherwise be represented by the named plaintiffs, and could opt out by returning a "request for exclusion."
- Final class: 28,100 members; ~97% of class members and over 99% of the leases had no connection to Kansas beyond the lawsuit.
- After trial, Kansas court found Phillips liable under Kansas law for interest on delayed royalty payments.
- Phillips appealed: (1) no personal jurisdiction over absent class members; (2) opt-out notice was insufficient; (3) trial court erred in applying Kansas law to all claims.
- Kansas Supreme Court affirmed. SCOTUS granted certiorari.

<!-- page: 23 -->

**Issues:**
1. May a state exercise jurisdiction over the claim of an absent class-action plaintiff, even if that plaintiff lacks minimum contacts with the state, so long as the plaintiff is provided with minimal procedural-due-process protection?
2. In a class action, must the forum state have a significant contact or aggregation of contacts creating a state interest in the claims asserted by each plaintiff class member?

**Holding and Reasoning (Rehnquist, J.):**

*(1) Yes.*
- The minimum-contacts requirement (*International Shoe*, *Shaffer v. Heitner*, *World-Wide Volkswagen*) involved the exercise of jurisdiction over **out-of-state defendants**, not plaintiffs.
- The burden placed on an out-of-state defendant (hire counsel, travel to forum, participate in discovery, pay damages) is far greater than on an out-of-state plaintiff.
- The out-of-state plaintiffs here were not forced to travel anywhere, did not need to hire counsel, and were not subject to liabilities for fees or costs.
- Because out-of-state plaintiffs face less burden, states may exercise personal jurisdiction over their claims even though they lack the minimum contacts needed for defendant personal jurisdiction.
- However, the forum state must still provide **minimum procedural-due-process protection**:
  - Plaintiffs must receive notice and opportunity to participate
  - Absent plaintiff must be given the opportunity to remove himself from the class
  - Absent class members must be adequately represented at all times by the named plaintiff
- Class members in this case received appropriate due-process protection → Kansas court appropriately exercised jurisdiction.

*(2) Yes.*
- To meet Due Process and Full Faith and Credit requirements, a forum state's substantive law may be applied only if the state has a **significant contact or aggregation of contacts**, creating a state interest, such that the choice of the forum's law is not arbitrary or fundamentally unfair.
- There is no greater leeway given to a court presiding over a nationwide class action.
- Jurisdiction over plaintiffs and choice-of-law are **entirely separate inquiries**.
- Kansas has an interest in protecting its Kansas class members and in regulating Phillips's conduct given Phillips's presence in Kansas.
- However, Kansas has no "common fund" in Kansas and no interest in claims unrelated to the state.
- Conflicts exist between Kansas law and the laws of Texas and Oklahoma (e.g., statutory interest rates in TX and OK are much lower than the rate applied under Kansas law).
- Application of Kansas law to every claim was arbitrary and unfair — reversed on this issue and remanded.

### *Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes*, 564 U.S. 338 (2011)(SCOTUS)

Class certification under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(a) is improper if there is no common injury that may be resolved across the entire class.

**Facts:**
- Dukes and two other current or former female Wal-Mart employees were three named plaintiffs in a proposed class action that included approximately 1,500,000 current and former female Wal-Mart employees.
- Plaintiffs alleged that Wal-Mart engaged in a corporate culture of discrimination against female employees in violation of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964.
- Plaintiffs alleged not a violation of an express corporate policy but rather that local Wal-Mart managers' **subjective discretion** over pay and promotions was exercised disproportionately in favor of men.
- Plaintiffs sought injunctive and declaratory relief as well as back pay.
- District court approved certification; court of appeals affirmed. SCOTUS granted certiorari.

<!-- page: 28 -->

**Holding and Reasoning (Scalia, J.):**
- Under FRCP 23(a)(2), plaintiffs must show "there are questions of law or fact common to the class."
- **Commonality requires** the plaintiffs to demonstrate that **all class members have suffered the same injury** — it is not sufficient simply to allege violation of the same statute.
- Claims must depend upon **a common contention that may be resolved across the entire class**.
- *General Telephone Co. of the Southwest v. Falcon* (1982): probing beyond the pleadings is sometimes necessary; certification is proper only if "rigorous analysis" reveals FRCP 23(a)'s prerequisites have been met.
- With respect to FRCP 23(b)(2): claims for monetary relief may not be certified under this provision if the monetary relief is not incidental to injunctive or declaratory relief. 23(b)(2) applies only when **a single injunction or declaratory judgment** would provide relief to each member of the class. Individualized damages claims belong in FRCP 23(b)(3).
- In this case, there is no significant proof that Wal-Mart operated under a **policy** of discrimination. Conversely, Wal-Mart has an express policy **forbidding** workplace discrimination.
- The lower court improperly certified the class under FRCP 23(a).
- The lower court also improperly certified plaintiffs' back-pay claims under FRCP 23(b)(2) — back pay claims constitute individualized monetary damages, not incidental to injunctive relief.
