## Background Cases, Statutes and References

### Cases (Common Law)

- ***Zahn v. International Paper Co.*, 414 U.S. 291 (1973) (SCOTUS)**
  Not only the named representative plaintiff, but each and every unnamed class member is required to meet the amount-in-controversy requirement. (Pre-dates and was superseded by 28 USC § 1367 – Supplemental Jurisdiction statute.)

- ***Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur v. Cauble*, 255 U.S. 356 (1921) (SCOTUS)**
  A diversity suit brought by some in behalf of all of the members of a class is cognizable by the federal district court where diversity of citizenship exists between the parties complainant (named representatives) and defendant, and the decree will bind all members.

- ***Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah*, 545 U.S. 546 (2005) (SCOTUS)**
  Where other elements of diversity jurisdiction are present and at least one named plaintiff satisfies the amount-in-controversy requirement, the court may exercise jurisdiction over other plaintiffs who might otherwise be properly joined but who do not allege damages which reach the jurisdictional amount. Also held that 28 USC § 1367 – Supplemental Jurisdiction statute invalidated *Zahn*.

- ***Shady Grove Orthopedic Associates v. Allstate Insurance Company*, 120 S. Ct. 1431 (2010) (SCOTUS)**
  A state law that prevents certain types of damages from being pursued via class action may not limit federal class actions that satisfy the requirements of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23.

- ***Gideon v. Wainwright*, 372 U.S. 335 (1963) (SCOTUS)**
  The Fourteenth Amendment incorporates the Sixth Amendment right to counsel to the states.

- ***Turner v. Rogers*, 564 U.S. 431 (2011) (SCOTUS)**
  The Due Process Clause does not require a right to counsel for an indigent individual at a civil contempt proceeding when there is a prospect of incarceration, but the court must have alternative procedural safeguards in place to determine whether counsel should be appointed.

## Federal Sources of Law

### Statutes

#### 28 USC § 1367 – Supplemental Jurisdiction

**(a)** Except as provided in subsections (b) and (c) or as expressly provided otherwise by Federal statute, in any civil action of which the district courts have original jurisdiction, the district courts shall have supplemental jurisdiction over all other claims that are so related to claims in the action within such original jurisdiction that they form part of the same case or controversy under Article III of the United States Constitution. Such supplemental jurisdiction shall include claims that involve the joinder or intervention of additional parties.

**(b)** In any civil action of which the district courts have original jurisdiction founded solely on section 1332 of this title, the district courts shall not have supplemental jurisdiction under subsection (a) over claims by plaintiffs against persons made parties under Rule 14, 19, 20, or 24 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, or over claims by persons proposed to be joined as plaintiffs under Rule 19 of such rules, or seeking to intervene as plaintiffs under Rule 24 of such rules, when exercising supplemental jurisdiction over such claims would be inconsistent with the jurisdictional requirements of section 1332.

**(c)** The district courts may decline to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over a claim under subsection (a) if—
1. the claim raises a novel or complex issue of State law,
2. the claim substantially predominates over the claim or claims over which the district court has original jurisdiction,
3. the district court has dismissed all claims over which it has original jurisdiction, or
4. in exceptional circumstances, there are other compelling reasons for declining jurisdiction.

**(d)** The period of limitations for any claim asserted under subsection (a), and for any other claim in the same action that is voluntarily dismissed at the same time as or after the dismissal of the claim under subsection (a), shall be tolled while the claim is pending and for a period of 30 days after it is dismissed unless State law provides for a longer tolling period.

**(e)** As used in this section, the term "State" includes the District of Columbia, the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico, and any territory or possession of the United States.

**The attorney cannot represent the client unless the client agrees, or unless the class has been certified and thus relieves the lawyer of the obligation to find a formal agreement to represent each client.**

A policy concern is the "**negative expected value claims**" – lawsuits that, if filed, are expected to result in a loss of money for the plaintiff. Certainly, if one has a small claim and has to pay a filing fee, he/she can expect to be in the negative. ### Four Required Elements for Class Certification (FRCP 23(a))

- **Numerosity** – Have a large number such that joinder would be impractical
  - 40+ plaintiffs automatically meets this requirement
  - 20 or fewer: courts like to deal with them by joinder
  - Between 20–40: courts look to geographic dispersion to make decision (more dispersion → more likelihood of finding numerosity)

- **Commonality** – Class members must have suffered the *same injury*, not just a violation of the same law (*Walmart*)
  - Links class members' claims to each other

<!-- page: 4 -->

- **Typicality** – Class representatives must be sufficiently similar to the rest of the class so that certifying those individuals to represent the class would not be unfair to the rest of the proposed class.
  - Links class members' claims to the named plaintiff

- **Representativeness** – Worried about conflicts of interest between named representative and class members
  - Want the representative to not have a conflict of interest with the rest of the class members

### Most Common Type: Damage Class Actions (FRCP 23(b)(3))

Class suits seeking damages for injuries caused by the same defendant.

**FRCP 23(b)(3):**
- Requires **claims of class members to share questions of law or fact that predominate over any questions affecting only individual members**
- Class treatment must be shown to be a **superior mode of adjudication**
- Sets forth four factors to establish predominance and superiority:
  - **(A)** the class members' interests in individually controlling the prosecution or defense of separate actions
  - **(B)** the extent and nature of any litigation concerning the controversy already begun by or against class members
  - **(C)** the desirability or undesirability of concentrating the litigation of the claims in the particular forum
  - **(D)** the likely difficulties in managing a class action
- Members are permitted to opt-out of this action
- **When one or more of the central issues in the action are common to the class and can be said to predominate**, the action may be considered appropriate to certify under Rule 23(b)(3) even though other important matters will have to be tried separately, such as damages or some affirmative defenses peculiar to some individual class members

### Subject Matter Jurisdiction

- We need an independent basis of subject matter jurisdiction for the named plaintiff's claim:
  - Arising under; or
  - Diversity

- Then we need to figure out how can we get subject matter jurisdiction with respect to the unnamed plaintiffs' claims:
  - Typically, we may want to use **Supplemental Jurisdiction**:
    - **Diversity (28 USC § 1367(a))** –
      - If we have an anchoring claim for the named class member (which is what we need), then we only need to look to the diversity of the named Plaintiff (*Supreme Tribe*)
      - That named plaintiff will need to meet the **AIC ($75K)** to be the anchoring claim for Supplemental Jurisdiction
      - If a **named plaintiff meets the AIC and has complete diversity**, then **Supplemental Jurisdiction** can be exercised with respect to the unnamed class members who individually do not meet the AIC (*Exxon*)

> ***The rule in *Exxon* is the same when there is a plaintiff who meets the requirements of complete diversity and the AIC to serve as the anchoring claim, and another plaintiff (not as part of the class), but wants a simple joinder under FRCP 20, except that second plaintiff still has to meet complete diversity.***

## Lecture Day 24

### Supplemental Jurisdiction (28 USC § 1367) and Class Actions

Prior to adoption in the FRCP there were precursors to class actions evolving through the equity jurisprudence. There were basically two questions about **how to use diversity jurisdiction when it interacts with class actions**:

- How do you measure complete diversity?
  - *Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur*
- How do you deal with the AIC?
  - *Exxon*

**BLL:** Prior to the adoption of the FRCP, the Supreme Court adjudicated *Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur* and held that ***for diversity purposes, you only look to the named plaintiff and not to the unnamed class members.***

Perhaps because of the permissiveness of that rule, by the time the SCOTUS got around to hearing the second question regarding whether or not each of the members of the class has to meet the AIC independently, it wanted to slow things down a bit.

In a case called *Zahn*, which took place prior to the Supplemental Jurisdiction statute being enacted, the unnamed class members had to each meet the AIC requirement on his or her own.

We have always had an **aggregation rule** in the FRCP, that says that single plaintiffs can aggregate (stack, for purposes of meeting the AIC) against single defendants, but multiple plaintiffs cannot aggregate.
- For example, two plaintiffs with $40k claims each could not aggregate those together.

The Supreme Court in *Zahn* interpreted this rule to mean that if you had two plaintiffs, not in a class action at all, both of which were diverse from the defendant, where only one met the AIC, the second one could not join because there was no Supplemental Jurisdiction statute at that time and there was no aggregation rule that would allow that, leaving that second plaintiff no independent basis of subject matter jurisdiction for his or her claim.

In 1990, Congress passed 28 USC § 1367, and did not word it as cleanly as it should have been.

### *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah*, 545 U.S. 546 (2005) (SCOTUS)

**Holding:** Where other elements of diversity jurisdiction are present and **at least one named plaintiff satisfies the amount-in-controversy requirement**, the court may exercise jurisdiction over other plaintiffs who might otherwise be properly joined but who do not allege damages which reach the jurisdictional amount. Also held that 28 USC § 1367 – Supplemental Jurisdiction statute invalidated *Zahn*.

#### Facts

- The United States Supreme Court consolidated two cases to resolve a split in the circuits regarding **amount-in-controversy in diversity cases**.
- The first case, *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah* (2005), involved a class action of 10,000 Exxon dealers who brought suit against Exxon, alleging that the company was overcharging them for fuel. Some of the dealers' damages did not rise to the amount required for diversity jurisdiction, but the district court and the United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit allowed joinder, holding that the unnamed members of a class action suit were not all required to meet the amount-in-controversy requirement, so long as at least one plaintiff did.
- The second case involved a nine-year-old girl who sued Starkist based on the unusually serious injuries she endured when she cut herself on a tuna can.
- She attempted to join her parents as plaintiffs as well, but the district court held that none of the parties had damages to the level of the jurisdictional amount.
- On appeal, the Court of Appeals for the First Circuit found that while the parents' damages were below the jurisdictional amount and therefore could not be properly joined, the girl's damages were sufficient.
- The United States Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve the split.

#### Issue

When other elements of diversity jurisdiction are present and at least one named plaintiff satisfies the amount-in-controversy requirement, may the court exercise jurisdiction over other plaintiffs who might otherwise be properly joined but who do not allege damages which reach the jurisdictional amount?

#### Holding and Reasoning (Kennedy, J.)

- **Yes.** Where other elements of diversity jurisdiction are present and at least one named plaintiff satisfies the amount-in-controversy requirement, the court may exercise jurisdiction over other plaintiffs who might otherwise be properly joined but who do not allege damages which reach the jurisdictional amount.
- It is well-settled that federal courts are courts of limited jurisdiction.
- Although diversity jurisdiction is available to parties from different states, to prevent flooding of the federal courts, jurisdiction is limited to cases that exceed a fixed amount of money.
- **Federal courts may also exercise supplemental jurisdiction over issues related to a case or controversy over which the court already properly has jurisdiction.**
- Here, this court must decide, based on this settled law, **whether under § 1367 the court may exercise jurisdiction over other plaintiffs who might otherwise be properly joined, but who do not allege damages which reach the jurisdictional amount.**
- **This Court finds that they can.**
- A failure for some claimants to meet the amount-in-controversy requirement does not affect the claims the way that, for example, allowing a case to stay in federal court when diversity jurisdiction were destroyed would.
- Furthermore, some parties must be joined as indispensable parties under Rule 19; to hold that the case-in-controversy requirement forbids this joinder would be illogical.
- The holding of the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit is affirmed, and the holding of the Court of Appeals for the First Circuit is reversed and the case remanded.

<!-- page: 8 -->

Here the basic question is whether the girl and her parents could sue together in a simple joinder suit. In a simple joinder situation, the parents are not ousted from meeting the requirements of the Supplemental Jurisdiction statute — 1367(b) — because they are not asserting a claim by a plaintiff against a person made a party under one of the rules listed in the statute, because in a single defendant case, there is no reason to resort to these rules to add a defendant.

This is one of the reasons why the court says that the 2 plaintiffs/single defendant case, or that the multi-plaintiff class action/single defendant case, are not subject to the requirements for jurisdiction under **28 USC 1367(b)**.

So they decided that the only question they really need to answer is if you have a single plaintiff in a diversity case, where there is complete diversity and AIC requirements are met, whether that is okay for an anchoring claim, and if so, then is it okay to join in the class action people who don't on their own meet the AIC requirement.

- **Because we have the single anchoring claim by the named plaintiff, which must have complete diversity and meet the AIC requirements on its own, and the rest are part of the "same claim or controversy."**

The *Exxon* decision makes no distinction in applying this rule to joinder situations or class actions.

The problem that the majority in *Exxon* had with that rule, is that essentially it was saying that in a diversity case, with more than one plaintiff, both (or all, if more than two) named plaintiffs had to meet the complete diversity requirement, but only one had to meet the AIC requirement. The court highlights that the diversity requirement is there to prevent bias against the out-of-state parties, whereas the amount-in-controversy requirement does not affect the case in the same way.

**BLL: Class actions and multi-plaintiff lawsuits against single defendants, when we have complete diversity, and we have 1 of the named representatives has more than $75k at issue, Supplemental Jurisdiction can be used for all of the other claims. BUT, we cannot do it when we have a single plaintiff suing multiple defendants.**

### Hypo Fact Pattern for Class Actions

**Bank – Fells Wargo** is programming computer systems in Austin, Texas (Western District)
- Bank has hundreds of thousands of individual bank accounts of individual consumers, including Plaintiff (**Southall's**) account
- Mid-level employee (**O'Quinn**) goes to his Supervisor (**Zhang**) with a scheme that would make the bank $150M in profits, in exchange for a promotion to an upper-level employee
- **Scheme:**
  - There are hundreds of thousands of consumer accounts with the bank
  - Once a month, we could take a $1 from each consumer account and put it in a bank account controlled by the bank for the course of 2 years ($24 per consumer account overall)
  - The question here is whether anyone will ever sue over $24 individually, especially taking into account that the filing fee will be more than the $24 lost, and therefore no one is likely to sue
- Zhang thinks that is a great idea and decides to do the scheme
- O'Quinn, from Austin, makes a computer program for the purpose of effecting this scheme
  - **NOTE:** Corporations which serve consumers can frequently be in a position to hurt a large number of people just by taking a very tiny little bit of money

We ended up determining that the named plaintiff (Southall) either had 4,000 individual accounts, or because her attorneys stated a claim for punitive damages on her behalf, that they met the diversity requirements (that she was completely diverse from the defendant) and that she met the AIC requirements. Therefore, it was fine for the unnamed class members to lack complete diversity (*Supreme Tribe*) and it was okay that they each did not meet the AIC (*Exxon*).

We later walked through the **FRCP 23(a) prerequisites** and found one of the **FRCP 23(b) types** of class action that might apply in this scenario.

#### Arguments Against FRCP 23(a) Certification by Defendant's Attorneys

- **Numerosity** – Have thousands of accounts involved (40+ plaintiffs automatically meets this requirement)
  - Not disputed

<!-- page: 10 -->

- **Commonality** – Class members must have suffered the *same injury*, not just a violation of the same law (*Walmart*) (Links class members' claims to each other)
  - In our hypo, all individuals suffered the same amount of money stolen by the same person at the same rate, but they all had different types of accounts, so the injury might affect each one differently, depending on the type of account he/she had, and it would be difficult to apply the same damages to all plaintiffs.
  - **CONTRACT THEORY** – Additionally, the contracts for each individual depending on the year they each opened their accounts might be different:
    - a. Contract language might vary across time
    - b. Contract language might vary depending on account type
  - **CONVERSION THEORY (TORT)** – There may be different rates of interest applied to the different accounts which would make it unfair to calculate a flat rate of damages for each individual.
    - Also state laws may vary as to the permissibility of contract terms for each individual customer. Whereas it would be okay to take $1/mo from each customer is okay in one state and may be not okay in another.
    - Also updates in contract terms may be okay without notice in one state, and not in another. (Goes to predominance)

- **Typicality** – Class representatives must be sufficiently similar to the rest of the class so that certifying those individuals to represent the class would not be unfair to the rest of the proposed class. (Links class members' claims to the named plaintiff)
  - If there are different contracts, there is no certainty that the named representative's contract is typical when compared to the other contracts, so resolving the named plaintiff's claim may not actually solve anyone else's claim.

- **Representativeness** – Worried about conflicts of interest between named representative and class members (in our hypo, we do not need to worry about this)

Some of the arguments dealt with how the laws differ in each state. We might ask ourselves, can we solve that problem simply by picking a forum state (New Jersey), where there is general jurisdiction over the defendant?

Even if the law in New Jersey is a bit less favorable, the plaintiffs' attorneys might be willing to trade that for uniformity to get the class certified, but under *Shutts*, this would not be allowed.

One of the rules coming out of *Shutts* says: In a class action, the **forum state must have a significant contact or aggregation of contacts creating a state interest in the claims asserted by each plaintiff class member** to ensure that the choice of the forum state's law is not arbitrary or unfair.

<!-- page: 11 -->

Therefore, if the laws are the same in each state regarding the specific issue, then there is no conflict and the forum law can be applied. However, if there is conflict, then as to each plaintiff, the forum must undergo some kind of choice of law analysis to apply the law to those out of state class member's claims, especially those that have no relation whatsoever to the forum. (*Due Process Clause*)

- Same kind of analysis as for personal jurisdiction (requiring some kind of contact), but much more permissive (smaller quantity and lighter touch of personal contacts)
  - For example, it almost certainly would be permissible to apply Texas law to all of the class members' claims because the program that was used to steal the money, and to deal with the permissibility in changes of contractual terms and to remove the money from each individual account, was coded in Texas. This would be enough of a contact with respect to all of the plaintiffs' claims.
  - It might not be okay to apply NJ law to this because NJ might only have contacts with their respective plaintiffs' claims only. (*Shutts*)

**So, stepping back now, we have explored two ways in which defense attorneys try to defeat class action certification:**
1. They emphasize variation in the facts applying to each individual class member
2. They emphasize variation in the law
   - *Shutts* tells us that they cannot solve that variation in the law problem simply by picking forum law and applying forum substantive law to all members' individual lawsuits unless there are some level of contacts with respect to the forum and all members of the lawsuit.

#### Judge Aruleba's Findings of Fact

- **Numerosity** – Have thousands of accounts involved (40+ plaintiffs automatically meets this requirement)
  - **Not disputed.**

- **Commonality** – Class members must have suffered the *same injury*, not just a violation of the same law (*Walmart*) (Links class members' claims to each other)
  - **Court finds that there is a common issue of fact that causes the same injury to all the customers under the Conversion theory of relief.**

- **Typicality** – Class representatives must be sufficiently similar to the rest of the class so that certifying those individuals to represent the class would not be unfair to the rest of the proposed class. (Links class members' claims to the named plaintiff)
  - **Court finds that the fact that the bank removed the money without permission is typical to all unnamed plaintiffs and to the named representative and that resolving the named plaintiff's claim would resolve all of the other plaintiffs' claims.**

<!-- page: 12 -->

- **Representativeness** – Worried about conflicts of interest between named representative and class members
  - **Court finds that the named plaintiff does not in this case have a conflict of interest such that would prevent her from representing the individual interests of the rest of the class.**

**Under 23(b)(3) – Damage Class Action, there are two additional requirements:**

**Predominance:** None of the individual plaintiffs have significantly higher claims than the others, so the common question of law or fact here predominates over any individual questions of law or fact that the separate individuals might have.

**Superiority:** Class treatment is a superior mode of adjudication because all of the individual claims are very low, and separating them would force each plaintiff to not get justice since the filing fee alone could be more than the amount of damages they would receive in the end. The best, most efficient way to be able to bring these claims to the court is by aggregating them. (Negative expected value)

**Therefore, under the FRCP 23(a) and (b) requirements (must meet the 23(a) AND at least one of the 23(b) requirements in order to get certified), the class is certified as a FRCP 23(b)(3) – Damage Class Action.**

### *Shady Grove Orthopedic Associates v. Allstate Insurance Company*, 120 S. Ct. 1431 (2010) (SCOTUS)

**Holding:** A state law that prevents certain types of damages from being pursued via class action may not limit federal class actions that satisfy the requirements of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23.

#### Facts

- Shady Grove Orthopedic Associates, P.A. (Shady Grove) (plaintiff) provided medical care to Sonia Galvez who sustained injuries in a car accident.
- As partial payment for the medical care, Galvez assigned to Shady Grove her rights to insurance benefits under a policy by Allstate Insurance Company (Allstate) (defendant).
- Although Allstate paid Shady Grove, it failed to do so within the statutorily required time and also refused to pay the statutory interest that had accrued on the late payment.
- Shady Grove filed a diversity class action suit in the Eastern District of New York to recover the statutory interest owed to it and similarly situated parties.
- The district court dismissed the suit for lack of jurisdiction.

<!-- page: 13 -->

- There, the court held that the statutory interest sought was a penalty and, consequently, New York Civil Practice Law § 901(b) precluded a class action suit to recover a "penalty" despite Fed. R. Civ. P. 23 which permits class actions.
- Shady Grove appealed and the court of appeals affirmed and held that Rule 23 and § 901(b) addressed different issues and thus did not conflict.
- The U.S. Supreme Court granted certiorari to review.

#### Holding and Reasoning (Scalia, J.)

- **No.** A state law that prevents certain types of damages from being pursued via class action may not limit federal class actions that satisfy the requirements of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23.
- A federal court may certify a class pursuant to Rule 23.
- A court may do this in each and every case in which the Rule's criteria are met.
- Allstate argues that Congress carved out certain cases in which Rule 23 does not apply, but rather leaves room for laws like § 901(b) to reject class certification.
- However, Congress has ultimate authority over the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, not the State of New York.
- The fact that Congress carved out specific exceptions to Rule 23 does not mean that the Rule does not apply in this case.
- If Rule 23 did not authorize class actions across the board, the exceptions would not be necessary.
- Rule 23 permits all class actions that meet its requirements and a state cannot limit that permission by structuring a state law to track Rule 23 on one hand and enact a part on the other that imposes additional requirements.
- Under the Rules Enabling Act, Congress authorized the Court to create procedural rules so long as they do not "abridge, enlarge, or modify any substantive right." 28 U.S.C. § 2072(b).
- If a court-created rule governs only "the manner and the means" of enforcing litigants' rights, it is valid.
- If the rule alters the "rules of decision by which [the] court will adjudicate [those] rights," it is invalid.
- Utilizing the test, the Court has found that Fed. R. Civ. P. 23 falls within § 2072(b)'s authorization and is valid in every federal court.
- It is not the substantive or procedural nature or purpose of the affected state law that matters.
- What matters is the substantive or procedural nature of the Federal Rule.
- The validity of a Federal Rule, including Rule 23, depends entirely upon whether it regulates procedure.
- If it does, which is the case here, it is authorized by § 2072 and is valid in all jurisdictions regardless of state-created rights.
- The judgment of the court of appeals is reversed, and the matter is remanded for further proceedings.

<!-- page: 14 -->

#### Concurrence (Stevens, J.)

- The majority is correct in holding that Fed. R. Civ. P. 23 must apply.
- But the dissent also correctly notes that there are some state procedural rules that a federal court must apply in diversity cases because they function as a part of the state's definition of substantive rights and remedies.
- When both a federal rule and state law appear to govern an issue, there is a two-step framework.
- Both steps require the court to determine what the federal rule and state law mean.
- First, a court must determine whether the scope of the federal rule is "sufficiently broad" to "control the issue" before the court, thereby "leaving no room for the operation" of conflicting state law. *Walker v. Armco Steel Corp.*, 446 U.S. 740, 749-50 (1980).
- If the federal rule does not apply or can operate alongside the state rule, then there is no Congressional act governing the question and the Court may conduct its analysis.
- Second, if the federal rule is broad enough that it creates a "direct collision," the court must decide if applying the federal rule is a valid exercise of rulemaking authority under the Rules Enabling Act.
- The majority fails to recognize this second limitation that a federal rule "not abridge, enlarge, or modify any substantive right."

#### Dissent (Ginsburg, J.)

- The majority focuses only on whether the class action may be maintained pursuant to Fed. R. Civ. P. 23 and fails to address the concern of New York to keep certain monetary awards reasonably bounded.
- If there is a conflict between a federal rule of civil procedure and state law, the federal rule should be narrowly construed to accommodate important state interests.
- There is no inevitable collision between Rule 23 and § 901(b).
- A court is able to address both equally.
- Plaintiffs may still pursue a class action under both laws if they forgo statutory damages and instead seek actual damages or injunctive or declaratory relief.
- Any putative class member who objects can opt out and pursue actual damages if available.
- Congress envisioned fewer, not more, class actions overall.

#### Simplifying the Issue

- By delaying the payment of claims of so many people, the insurance company could invest it and make money off of the interest paid under that investment, rather than the claimant (medical providers) getting their claim paid and being able to invest it themselves. In other words, by not paying money to the claimants, the insurance company could make even more money.

<!-- page: 15 -->

- In order to combat this issue, NY passed a law that imposed higher interest payments on delayed payments by the insurance company. So, if the insurance company took longer to pay the claims than what was allowed, then they would have to pay very high interest to the claimant providers.
- The NY legislature that passed this law knew that the insurance companies are making donations to their reelection funds. So, they would want to do something to *appear* to combat this problem, but to not actually work because if it worked then the insurance companies would get upset and no longer donate to their campaign funds.
- So how NY legislatures do this is by passing a law making delayed payments incur high interest rates, but prohibit class action lawsuits or suits seeking statutory minimum damages, making it very difficult to bring this to court because effectively this would result in each individual provider having to sue for $.04–.05 cents times the amount of the delayed payment medical reimbursement submissions that they have, which would likely be in the range of $500 in damages.
  - Due to the Negative Expected Value concept, most claimants would not be able to meet the AIC requirements to take this to federal court on their own without a class. For state court, without certification for a class available, most claimants would not think it's worth it to spend the money and time in filing fees and depositions for this amount of damages.
- Basically, then the only way to make this economical is to get a class certified, but seeing the state law and knowing that the class will not get certified in state court, Shady Grove and its plaintiffs tried to take it to federal court — but there is still one issue:
  - Due to the Class Action Fairness Act, the federal court will only have subject matter jurisdiction **ONLY IF** the class gets certified.

For our purposes, we do not care about that because if the class does not get certified the lawsuit will die anyways due to the economic considerations we went over yesterday.

But, we are in federal court, and one would think that we can just look at FRCP 23 and provided that we meet its FRCP 23(a) requirements for *numerosity*, *commonality*, *typicality* and *representativeness*, along with determining that such a certification would satisfy the *predominance* and *superiority* requirements of FRCP 23(b) for a Damages Class Action, we can get certification.

**EXCEPT — THIS IS A VCOL CONFLICT BETWEEN THE FRCP AND THE NY STATE LAW — *ERIE* ISSUE!**

The statutory interest sought was a penalty and, consequently, New York Civil Practice Law § 901(b) precluded a class action suit to recover a "penalty" despite Fed. R. Civ. P. 23 which permits class actions.

<!-- page: 16 -->

### *Erie* Analysis Applied to *Shady Grove*

Under the **four tests** for *Erie* analysis (see Class Notes for Days 10 and 11 for more detailed information):

#### 1) *York* – Outcome Determinative Test

- Is this outcome determinative?
  - If yes, apply state law
  - If no, apply federal law
  - Likely **yes**, because applying NY law would effectively kill the class action lawsuit, whereas applying FRCP 23 would allow the class action to proceed. → **Therefore, State law should govern under this test.**

#### 2) Harlan in *Hanna* – Primary Conduct Test

- Would this conflict in NY law § 901(b) and FRCP 23 affect pre-litigation conduct of the parties? (Remember, one of the parties here is an insurance company!)
  - If yes, then federal law would be considered to have wandered into a substantive sphere, so apply state law
  - If no, then federal law is purely procedural, so apply federal law
  - Likely **yes**, because insurance company might change its rates → **Therefore, State law should govern under this test.**

> ***Neither of these two tests above purport to be the Black Letter Law, but for exam purposes, they must be analyzed and addressed.***

#### 3) *Hanna* Majority – Valid and Applicable Test

The fundamental theory in these two tests is built on the Supremacy Clause of the Constitution. The Supremacy Clause says that:
- If federal law is **valid and applicable**, we are supposed to use it, notwithstanding any state law to the contrary.
- So, we need to question ourselves whether the federal law is **valid and applicable**.

Justices Ginsburg and Scalia agree on the handling of federal laws at issue which are either a **constitutional provision** or a **statute**. The real difference between the two justices is highlighted most when the federal issue at hand is a provision from the **Federal Rules of Civil Procedure**: In our case *Shady Grove*, it is **FRCP 23 – Class Actions**.

**ANALYSIS:**

Focus on the federal law and identify the nature of the federal law at issue:

**If federal law at issue is a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure (FRCP) — 2-PRONG ANALYSIS:**

<!-- page: 17 -->

**Validity** here is measured with reference to the Rules Enabling Act, provision 2072(b), which says that a rule cannot "abridge, enlarge, or modify state substantive right."
- We pay attention to that analysis **even though the rules always pass that test**
  - Congress passed the REA authorizing the creation and promulgation of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.
  - Additionally, FRCP 23 does not itself abridge, enlarge, or modify any state substantive right; → **Therefore, it is VALID.**
- Test for determining whether or not the FRCP abridges, enlarges or modifies a state substantive right is whether or not it **"really regulates procedure"**
  - FRCP 23 really regulates procedures because it explains **how** class actions need to be filed and **what** requirements need to be met.

*J. Stevens* felt that one should look at the state law at issue, define the substantive right at issue and see whether applying the FRCP would abridge, enlarge or modify that state substantive right. Even though this might vary from state to state, it is okay because this would effectuate the federalism policies that Congress was intending to further in enacting the Rules Enabling Act.

*J. Scalia* did not like that because then that would mean that the applicability of the FRCP would depend on the different states' laws, which would get too messy and not be uniform. If things are easier and more uniform, these rules would be known to people and make them easier to comply with (in terms of promoting access to justice.)

*J. Ginsburg* agreed with J. Scalia in terms of analyzing validity. Refusing to declare that a rule promulgated under the Rules Enabling Act is invalid, she says that concerns of validity should be channeled into the **applicability** prong.

**Applicability** (Validity concerns can be channeled through here into the Applicability analysis) (**Does not depend on the applicability of the state law in question AT ALL – only the applicability of the FEDERAL RULE in question**)

- **Justice Scalia** (in *Hanna* Majority):
  - He feels rules should be interpreted and effectuated in accordance with their plain language
    - Construed broadly (Example is *Hanna* Majority)
    - J. Scalia believes FRCP 23 is applicable because it governs this exact issue, which is whether or not to grant class certification, which is specifically what FRCP 23 speaks to.

<!-- page: 18 -->

- **Justice Ginsburg** (here in *Shady Grove* and earlier in *Walker*):
  - Feels that rules should be interpreted sensitively with state law policies in mind
    - Construed "sensitively" with state law policies in mind (Example of this was *Walker* — even though *Walker* did not concern federalism reasons for the narrow construction, the operation was the same)
    - J. Ginsburg says in *Shady Grove* that FRCP 23 is focused on procedure, whereas NYCP § 901 focuses on remedies, therefore, there is no VCOL conflict.

Even though we can see that NYCP § 901 directly speaks to whether a class can actually be maintained, and FRCP 23 also speaks to that issue, J. Ginsburg maintains that the federal law must be construed with the state interests in mind:

- She identifies that the NY legislature did not want to incentivize class lawsuits for statutory damages – kind of working like a damages cap to prevent damages from getting too high
- J. Ginsburg identifies the specific state interest of lessening the amounts of class action lawsuits, and not turning $5,000 cases into $5,000,000 cases
  - She claims "Plaintiffs **may still pursue a class action under both laws if they forgo statutory damages and instead seek actual damages or injunctive or declaratory relief.**"
    - And, any putative class member who objects can opt out and pursue actual damages if available.

<!-- page: 19 -->

In this case, one would think that instead of using this enacted law to sort of limit or cap the damages to a lawsuit, why couldn't they just enact a law capping the damages in the lawsuit? And it is likely that the reason why they did that is because they did not want to explicitly favor the NY businesses, even though effectively, it is what would be happening. Doing something transparently is something that the legislature (and all legislatures) strenuously avoid.

J. Ginsburg is basically saying that whether this is good or not, it is what the NY legislature wanted and what the NY interests were, and so they need to adhere to it.

> **IF, HYPOTHETICALLY**, the NY legislature instead supposed a damages cap in this case, it likely would be that J. Ginsburg would have an easier time articulating how the two laws would not be in collision, since as she correctly stated above, one speaks to remedies and the other to process. J. Scalia would still say that the FRCP still applies to the class certification part of it, because in practicality, it would not be feasible to bring a class action lawsuit with a cap of $750 for the whole class.

#### 4) Twin Aims of *Erie* Test

- **No need to consider the twin aims of *Erie* here** because that test applies only when the federal source of law at issue is **common law**. In this case, the federal source of law at issue is a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure, so this test would not be applicable at all.

---

So this discussion basically leaves us with a question as to what is the controlling opinion of *Shady Grove*.

- We cannot use J. Ginsburg's opinion because she dissents.

In opinions split like this, we are supposed to go with the "opinion that concurs on the narrowest grounds," but in this situation it is difficult to tell whether J. Stevens' or J. Scalia's is narrower.

In terms of what we get out of this, we believe we have 5 Justices in this case uniting behind the principle that **we should interpret FRCP with state interests in mind**. (4 justices with J. Ginsburg and J. Stevens explicitly say that), even though all five did not unite behind the holding. But, since they could not unite behind the holding, there is no telling how much force of law this principle would have.

But, for purposes of the exam and in practice, you have the clear example of the ways in which J. Scalia and J. Ginsburg apply the *Hanna* majority test in a situation where FRCP need to be interpreted.

<!-- page: 20 -->

**Scalia:**
- Plain language, read the rule, suppress any considerations of state law

**Ginsburg:**
- Start with state law policies, try to identify what the state legislature was trying to do, and see whether there is a creative interpretation of the Rules of Civil Procedure that would allow both to have force.
