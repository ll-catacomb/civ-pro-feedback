## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***Daimler AG v. Bauman*, 571 U.S. 117 (2014) (SCOTUS)**

  Establishes out of state "general" jurisdiction. In order for a foreign (out of state) defendant to be subject to general jurisdiction, it must not only possess **continuous and systematic contacts with a forum state**, but **those contacts must also surpass some unspecified level when viewed in comparison to the company's "nationwide and worldwide" activities**.

- ***McGee v. International Life Insurance Co.*, 355 U.S. 220 (SCOTUS, 1957)**

  A state court has jurisdiction over an out-of-state company if the company has **substantial connections** with the state.

- ***Hanson v. Denckla*, 357 U.S. 235 (1958) (SCOTUS)**

  A defendant is subject to personal jurisdiction only if **the defendant purposefully avails itself** of the privilege of conducting activities in the forum state. The unilateral activities of someone in the state who claims a relationship with a nonresident defendant are not sufficient to satisfy this minimal-contacts requirement. By conducting activities in the forum state, the defendant calls upon the benefits and protections of the state's laws.

- ***Kulko v. Superior Court*, 436 U.S. 84 (1978) (SCOTUS)**

  The due process clause of the Fourteenth Amendment limits the power of a state court to assert personal jurisdiction over a non-resident defendant. Foreseeability in a due process analysis refers to whether the defendant's contacts with the forum state are such that **he should reasonably expect being subject to the jurisdiction of the state's courts**.

- ***World-Wide Volkswagen Corp v. Woodson*, 444 U.S. 286 (1980) (SCOTUS)**

  Foreseeability alone is not sufficient to authorize a state court's assertion of personal jurisdiction over a non-resident defendant that has no contacts, ties, or relations with the forum state. The relationship between a non-resident defendant and the forum state **must be such that it is reasonable to require the non-resident to come to the state to defend the action**.

<!-- page: 2 -->

- ***J. McIntyre Machinery, Ltd. v. Nicastro***

  For a defendant to be subject to a state's personal jurisdiction, it must purposefully avail itself of the privilege of conducting activities within the forum State, thus invoking the benefits and protections of its laws.

## Recap of Lecture: Day 6

<!-- page: 3 -->

There are **three categories or concepts** used — which come from property concepts in history (the UK, and later inherited and developed by courts in the last 300–400 years) — **to establish personal jurisdiction** for a case:

1. **In rem**
2. **Quasi in Rem**
3. **In Personam**

### Bases of Jurisdiction Over Person or Property

| **In rem** | **Quasi-in rem** | **In personam** |
|---|---|---|
| Step 1: court "attaches" a piece of property "in" its geographic jurisdiction | Step 1: court "attaches" a piece of property "in" its geographic jurisdiction | Lawsuit 1, State A, usually more convenient for pla; pla sues defendant; defendant can either appear or litigate whether IPJ in State A |
| Step 2: in the same case, court adjudicates ownership over that piece of property as against the world | Step 2: pla proves that owner of property wronged pla in some way (usually unrelated to piece of property seized) | • If def wins, case dismissed; • If def loses on IPJ, litigate on merits, later appeal in State A; • Default |
| Lawsuit is against the piece of property | Step 3: if pla prevails, court sells property, gives pla the money up to the amount of the claim, gives defendant leftovers | If pla prevails State A court issues judgment, pla then finds def property in State B (sometimes A=B) |
| Claimant ordinarily claims some ownership interest (e.g., lien) | Pla's recovery limited to value of the property | Lawsuit two, in State B, usually more convenient for defendant; pla request State B court seize property, sell it to satisfy judgment |
| Funny names: "In re The Nautilus," "In re 221b Baker Street, London," "In re #4 Privet Drive" | Used when defendant hard to reach | • If def appeared in lawsuit 1, cannot object to IPJ (usually loses); • If def defaulted in lawsuit 1, can litigate whether State A court had IPJ (in State B!) |

---

### 1. In Rem

Court grabs hold of the piece of property in some way (essentially a lawsuit against the property, not the defendant).

- **Step 1:**
  - If a small piece of property (cell phone, etc.), sheriff can get it and hold it in the court vault while the litigation is happening.
  - If it is a large piece of property, like real estate, sheriff can put notice on it and seize the property ("arrest the ship").
- **Step 2:**
  - Person who asks for the seizure of property has (or believes he/she/they have) some type of ownership interest in it — person can file a "lien" or sue "in rem."
  - If Plaintiff proves the case, court can adjudicate that the property belongs to the claimant, or can force a sale.

> ***NOTE: Consider how difficult it is to locate bank accounts, incorporeal entities, modern forms of wealth, patents, money being transferred at a billionth of a second, etc. — causes a lot of trouble. The entire Personal Jurisdiction doctrine is difficult at the moment and we are still awaiting for the law to catch up to our current way of life.***

<!-- page: 4 -->

### 2. Quasi in Rem

Lawsuit against the defendant, but the defendant lives away and is hard to reach, so the court takes "possession" of property in its state to hold until the litigation against the defendant (located in a different state) is resolved, and if needed, sells it and pays the plaintiff (any excess returns to defendant).

- **Step 1:** Court "attaches"/seizes a piece of property in its geographic jurisdiction. Normally, the issue in litigation has nothing to do with the piece of property being seized.
- **Step 2:** If plaintiff prevails, then the court will sell the piece of property and give the proceeds to the petitioner.
  - Plaintiff's recovery is limited to the value of the property.
  - Ordinarily used when Plaintiff may have trouble reaching the Defendant in personam.

> ***Note that Quasi in Rem was not present in* Pennoyer*, but the result may have been different if it had been present in that case.***

### 3. In Personam

*(Assuming the defendant was served with process)*

**Lawsuit 1, State A:**
- The lawsuit gets filed in a state (State A) that is most convenient to the plaintiff.
- Defendant has a choice and can either:
  - **Appear and litigate** that there is no Personal Jurisdiction over him (must do so in State A):
    - If defendant wins → case dismissed for "lack of IPJ."
    - If defendant loses → case proceeds on the merits; defendant can later appeal on PJ grounds, but must do so again in State A.

<!-- page: 5 -->

      - If defendant ultimately loses on appeal on both merits and PJ, plaintiff can take the State A judgment to a state where defendant has property, ask that court to seize and sell the property.
        - Defendant cannot then re-argue in that property state that State A lacked PJ — that issue was already litigated and lost (preclusion).
  - **Default:** A default judgment is almost always automatically entered.
    - Plaintiff can take State A judgment to a state where defendant has property and ask that court to seize and sell it.
    - Defendant can, at this point, argue that State A lacked PJ, but if defendant loses on that PJ issue, that state will not allow defendant to litigate the merits.

<!-- page: 6 -->

**Lawsuit 2, State B:**
- If plaintiff prevails, State A issues judgment; plaintiff can find defendant's property in State B, induces the court to seize and sell it; defendant cannot then contest PJ of State A in State B.
  - If Defendant defaulted in Lawsuit 1: the PJ question can be litigated in State B, but if defendant loses, defendant cannot contest the merits.
  - **If Defendant prevails on the PJ question in State B** (i.e., State B determines State A did not have PJ over defendant), then the judgment from State A is null and void — it is as if there was no lawsuit to begin with.

***Pennoyer v. Neff*, 95 U.S. 714 (1877) — Sovereignty-based approach to IPJ**

**BLL RULE:** The "tag" is "**necessary and sufficient**" within the state's boundaries to establish IPJ over a client. (Court refers to the Constitution's due process requirement.)

> ***Note: This* Pennoyer *rule is almost overruled now in its application, but the Constitution's requirements of due process are still good law.***

In-state tag serves two parallel purposes:
1. State sovereignty rationale
2. Fairness — forum is not oppressive for the defendant to litigate

***International Shoe v. Washington*, 326 U.S. 310 (1945) — Minimum contacts**

For a defendant not present within the territory of a forum state to be subjected to a court's IPJ, due process requires that the defendant have **certain minimum contacts with the forum**.

With *International Shoe*, the court begins to distinguish the general topic of IPJ into two subcategories of **general** and **specific jurisdiction**.

<!-- page: 7 -->

**Specific Jurisdiction** (*International Shoe* is an example): A defendant's activity occurs in a particular state, and there is a minimum contact relationship among three things (*purposely availing oneself*):
1. Defendant
2. Activities
3. Subject Matter of the Case

**General Jurisdiction:** The relationship between the defendant and the forum is so strong that such state can exercise general jurisdiction over that defendant, regardless of the subject matter of the litigation, or the state where the events occurred. For example: the defendant's state of domicile.

**Minimum contacts test:**
- In a **general jurisdiction** case: whether there are minimum contacts among the defendant and the forum, **without regard to the subject matter** of the case.
- In a **specific jurisdiction** case: whether there are **sufficient contacts between the defendant, the activities, and the subject matter** of the case.

## Specific Jurisdiction Case Law

### 2 Contracts Cases

#### *McGee v. International Life Insurance Co.*, 355 U.S. 220 (SCOTUS, 1957)

A state court has jurisdiction over an out-of-state company if the company has **substantial connections** with the state.

**Facts:**
- Franklin, a resident of California, purchased a life insurance policy from International, a Texas company with no offices or agents in California.
- Franklin paid his premiums until his death, after which International refused to pay on the ground that Franklin had committed suicide.
- McGee, the beneficiary (plaintiff), brought suit in California state court and was awarded a judgment.
- Unable to collect in California, McGee filed in Texas state court but was rebuffed due to improper service. SCOTUS granted certiorari.

**Issue:** Whether a California court has personal jurisdiction over a Texas insurance company when the company denies a claim of a California resident.

**Holding and Reasoning:**
- Yes (9-0 ruling).
- A state court has jurisdiction over an out-of-state company if that company has **substantial connections** with the state.
- The life insurance contract was delivered to a California resident, and California has an interest in protecting the interests of its insured residents.
- It is not an undue burden on International to respond to allegations in California given the state of the national economy.
- From the opinion, it seems that this company did not sell a policy to any other customer in CA, or conduct any other business there — yet the court says the state has PJ (9-0).
  - Court says that if the insurance company wants to receive money from persons in that state, they cannot shirk responsibility arising from that state.

<!-- page: 9 -->

**McGee Specific Personal Jurisdiction Analysis:**

1. **Long-arm statute** — Yes; California at the time had one with both specific provisions and extension to the general limits of the Constitution. plus/and
2. **Permissible under the 14th Amendment's due process clause:**
   - **a. Minimal contacts linking defendant, subject matter of litigation, forum** — Yes, because they sold policies out of state and conducted business via the mail with California specifically.
   - **b. Fair and reasonable:**
     - i. **Extent of defendant's contacts** — Yes, sold policies and conducted business via mail with California specifically.
     - ii. **Defendant convenience** — Not addressed.
     - iii. **Plaintiff's interest/convenience** — Not addressed.
     - iv. **Forum's interest** — Court says CA has an interest in protecting its citizens by providing a means for them to bring suit; plaintiff's interests are protected by providing a means to litigate when wronged.
     - v. **Interest of judicial system** — Location of evidence (where the insured died and where the estate administrator lives) is in California.
     - vi. **Choice of law** — Not addressed.

*McGee* court says that all of these circumstances need to be considered, and rules unanimously.

#### *Hanson v. Denckla*, 357 U.S. 235 (1958) (SCOTUS)

A defendant is subject to personal jurisdiction **only if the defendant purposefully avails itself** of the privilege of conducting activities in the forum state.

**Background — Wills and Trusts:**

#### Wills and Trusts Diagram

```
                           Contract
Rich person --[$$$]--> Trust <-------> Trustee
                |                          |
                |                       Estate
            [$$$] [$$$]                    |
               /       \               [Will]
    Life            Death                  |
 beneficiaries   beneficiaries          Legatees
```

When rich people die, their will directs money to the "estate." People that benefit from the will are called **legatees**. Rich people sometimes create trusts — a legal entity like a corporation but not incorporated. A **trustee** (analogous to a corporate officer) manages the assets pursuant to a contract. If a rich person retains too much control over the trust, tax authorities may declare the trust invalid, causing the money to be taxed and go back to the decedent's estate.

<!-- page: 10 -->

In *Hanson v. Denckla*, the rich lady created a trust and designated herself as the life beneficiary (permissible). She designated one set of her granddaughters from daughter #1 as her death beneficiary in the trust, but not the other two daughters. The other two were the legatees in the will. They wanted the trust declared invalid so the money would flow through the will to them.

**Facts:**
- Dora Donner, a Pennsylvania resident, established a trust in Delaware with a Delaware bank as trustee.
- Subsequently Donner moved to Florida, where (after 8 years) she died and where her will was probated.
- One of Donner's three daughters, Hanson (defendant), was executrix of Donner's will and the primary recipient of the Delaware trust.
- Donner's other two daughters, Denckla (plaintiff) and Stewart, split the majority of the estate when the will was probated in Florida.
- Denckla wished the Florida court to exercise jurisdiction over the Delaware trust and trustee, in which case Hanson would have no share of the estate.
- The Florida court assumed jurisdiction over the Delaware trust and enjoined the Delaware trust from paying.
- The Florida Supreme Court upheld the decision that Florida law applied to determine the validity of the Delaware trust.
- Meanwhile, Hanson had filed a separate suit in Delaware. The Delaware court ruled that the trust was valid under Delaware law.
- The Delaware Supreme Court affirmed, holding that the Florida judgment was not entitled to full faith and credit because Florida lacked jurisdiction over the trust and had no personal jurisdiction over the Delaware trustee. SCOTUS granted certiorari.

<!-- page: 11 -->

**Issue:** Is the Delaware trustee subject to personal jurisdiction in Florida?

**Holding:** No.

**Reasoning (Majority):**
- Courts need the defendant to have at least minimal contacts with the state in order to have personal jurisdiction.
- The unilateral activities of someone in the state who claims a relationship with a nonresident defendant are **not sufficient** to satisfy the minimal-contacts requirement.
- A defendant is subject to personal jurisdiction **only if it purposefully avails itself** of the privilege of conducting activities in the forum state.
- By conducting activities in the forum state, the defendant calls upon the benefits and protections of the state's laws.
- Here, the Florida court could **not** exercise personal jurisdiction over the Delaware trustee.
- The trustee did nothing to purposefully avail itself of the benefits or protections of Florida's laws.
- The trustee had no office in Florida, transacted no business there, held no trust assets there, and solicited no business there.
- As the trustee did **not have substantial contacts with Florida**, the Florida court lacks jurisdiction over the Delaware trust.

**Dissent:**
- The minimum-contacts requirement ensures that jurisdiction does not offend "traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice." *Int'l Shoe v. Washington*, 326 U.S. 310 (1945).
- Here, there is nothing unfair about the Florida court exercising jurisdiction over the trustee.
- The trustee had business relations with Donner for the eight years she lived in Florida, and the trustee regularly communicated with Donner about trust business during that time.

<!-- page: 12 -->

After Donner reached Florida, it took about 8 years before she died. In the meantime, she was getting money from the trust for living expenses — the trustee knew she was in Florida because that is how he was sending the money. The trustee never told her they did not want to be her trustee, because it kept earning money during the whole time. This is why the dissent claims the trustee availed itself to Florida.

> **BLL Rule:** "The unilateral action of a third party out of the control of the defendant cannot create personal jurisdiction in a place where it otherwise would not exist."

**What distinguishes *Denckla* from *McGee*?**

**BLL: *McGee* did not have a unilateral action by a third party** (so it's obvious that the court is looking for a way to blame the defendant).

- *Denckla* failed the minimal contacts test on what the court thought was the absence of minimal contact linking the defendant, subject matter of the litigation, and the forum.
  - Court said that the rich lady moving to Florida was a "unilateral move by a third party out of the control of the defendant" — the resulting contact between the trustee and the rich lady is not attributable to the defendant (making a distinction between **beginning** a relationship and merely **continuing** it).

*McGee* had a long arm statute that specifically speaks to that matter; in *Denckla*, there was no specific long arm statute that speaks to this issue, only the general one that allows reaching the limits of the Constitution.

- For this reason, many states enact long arm statutes that both list specific situations and also state that the statute can reach the limits of the constitution.
  - By specifically listing situations that trigger the long-arm statute along with the general provision, the state not only shows it "has" an interest, it is also affirmatively "expressing" its interest.
  - By the state having this interest expressed in the statute, one could say that this also provides **notice** (therefore fulfilling the fair and reasonable requirement)?

States usually love when wealthy older people move into their jurisdictions (they pay taxes; they do not have trouble with the law). For this reason, Florida had an interest in this case — to ensure that FL respects its residents' wishes upon their death.

- After looking at the list, there is really something odd about why the court said that FL did not have jurisdiction, but we will need to figure that out when we discuss *WW VW*.

#### *World-Wide Volkswagen Corp v. Woodson*, 444 U.S. 286 (1980) (SCOTUS)

**Foreseeability alone is not sufficient** to authorize a state court's assertion of personal jurisdiction over a non-resident defendant that has no contacts, ties, or relations with the forum state.

**Background — Products Liability Chain:**

Under products liability law:
- **Customer** buys from a **Retailer**
- **Retailer** gets product from an **Importer/Exporter**
- **Importer/Exporter** got items from a **Manufacturer**

If a customer gets hurt because the product is improperly designed, the customer can sue anyone in that chain, or all of them.

**Facts:**
- Harry and Kay Robinson purchased a car from World-Wide Volkswagen Corp. (World-Wide) in New York while still residents of that state.
- The next year the family left New York to move to Arizona. On the way they were involved in an accident in Oklahoma — Mrs. Robinson and her children were seriously injured in the crash.
- The Robinsons brought a products-liability suit against World-Wide, claiming the fire was a result of the defective design of the car.
- World-Wide made a special appearance to the district court, claiming that Oklahoma's exercise of jurisdiction over them violated due process rights.
- World-Wide, a New York corporation, conducted no business in the State of Oklahoma.
- The district court ruled against World-Wide. World-Wide then sought a writ of prohibition in the Oklahoma Supreme Court to stop the district court's Judge Woodson from exercising PJ over them.
- The Oklahoma Supreme Court denied the writ. World-Wide then petitioned SCOTUS; certiorari granted.

**Issue:** Does Oklahoma have personal jurisdiction over WW VW?

**Holding:** No.

<!-- page: 14 -->

**Reasoning (Majority):**
- The due process clause of the 14th Amendment limits the power of a state court to assert PJ over a non-resident defendant.
- A state court may **only** exercise PJ over a non-resident defendant if the defendant has **sufficient contacts with the forum state**.
- The relationship between a non-resident defendant and the forum state must be such that it is **reasonable to require the non-resident to come to the state to defend the action**.
- Here, the plaintiffs had **no contacts, ties, or relations** with Oklahoma.
- The only basis for the district court's assertion of PJ is that the automobile, sold in New York to New York residents, was involved in an accident in Oklahoma.
- That the plaintiffs could **foresee** the automobile's use in Oklahoma is **not sufficient** to authorize PJ.
- In *Kulko*, the court explained that foreseeability refers to whether the defendant's contacts with the forum state are such that he **should reasonably expect being subject to the jurisdiction of the state's courts**.
- Because the plaintiffs conducted no activities in Oklahoma, it is unreasonable to say they should have anticipated being brought to court there.

**Dissent:**
- When determining PJ over a non-resident defendant, a court may consider the **interest of the forum state** in proceeding with the case, and the **actual burden** the defendant must bear, in addition to the defendant's contacts. *See McGee*.
- The minimum contacts test established in *International Shoe* may be outdated; given modern realities, fairness and reasonableness no longer require extreme concern for non-resident defendants.
- Oklahoma has a legitimate interest in proceeding — it involves enforcement of its traffic laws.
- Defending this action in Oklahoma would not unduly inconvenience the plaintiffs.
- When the plaintiffs sold the automobile to the Robinsons, they **purposefully injected the automobile into the stream of interstate commerce**.

<!-- page: 15 -->

**WW VW — The case in detail:**

- The purchasers bought the car in NY. The retailer sold only in NY. The distributor operated in only three states around NY — definitely not Oklahoma.
- The family was moving to Arizona and was injured while driving through Oklahoma.
- The retailer (dealership) and distributor (not the manufacturer) object to jurisdiction in OK.
- Court says: **jurisdiction does not move with the car**, and foreseeability is not enough.
- Court says it needs **minimal contacts, linking the defendant, the subject matter of the case, and the forum**.

**Analysis:**

1. **Long-arm statute** — Presuming OK has one, but there are not enough minimal contacts (per the majority).
2. **Permissible under the 14th Amendment's due process clause:**
   - **a. Minimal contacts linking defendant, subject matter of litigation, forum** — Missing here. The retailer never sold the car to anyone in OK, or any other car that entered OK; same with the distributor.
   - **b. Fair and reasonable — consider:**
     - i. Extent of defendant's contacts — not much other than this one car
     - ii. Defendant convenience — OK is convenient because plaintiff and evidence are there
     - iii. Plaintiff's interest/convenience — OK is convenient because plaintiff and evidence are there
     - iv. Forum's interest — car is in OK (key piece of evidence)
     - v. Interest of judicial system — all evidence was in OK
     - vi. Choice of law — OK would apply if a first restatement state; OK might know better about interpreting OK state law

There are many factors here that suggest OK would be a good place to litigate this case — yet the court says WW VW did not have minimal contacts.

<!-- page: 16 -->

**Structure Emerging from WW VW:**

> ***WW VW BLL: Only IF the court can find a minimal contact with the state, then it can consider the rest of the requirements.***

1. **For specific jurisdiction under *International Shoe*:** Will need a long-arm statute.
2. **For the exercise of that specific jurisdiction to be permissible under the 14th Amendment DPC:** Need enough minimal contacts linking the defendant, subject matter of the litigation, and the forum state.
3. **AND ONLY IF both requirements above are true:** Then we could consider whether the exercise of jurisdiction is **fair and reasonable** — only then are we going to consider the subfactors:
   - i. Extent of defendant's contacts
   - ii. Defendant convenience
   - iii. Plaintiff's interest/convenience
   - iv. Forum's interest
   - v. Interest of judicial system, including location of witnesses, evidence
   - vi. Choice of law

In *WW VW*, we had all of these factors, but it still was not enough because it lacked minimal contacts with the forum state.

**Purposeful Availment — Context:**

We have now discussed two contracts cases (*McGee* and *Denckla*), and one of two torts (products liability) cases (*WW VW* and *Nicastro*). A couple of observations:

- This "purposeful availment" idea tends to make more sense when talking about the construction of contracts — one purposely avails and reaches in and has a relationship with someone in a particular jurisdiction.
- It may also make more sense when discussing product liability — the idea is where and how you will sell your products (or are you going to use the stream of commerce?).
- It may **not** make sense in other settings. For example, in Family law (see *Kulko*): in what sense does a parent avail himself with respect to wherever children are located?

<!-- page: 17 -->

All of these cases (including *Nicastro*) are taking place in state court, but federal courts also have an interest in the outcomes.

**The only federal long-arm statute we will be studying in this class:** Fed. R. Civ. P. 4(k)(1)(A).

By this statute, the federal court (by choice) is subjecting itself to the **limits of the specific state's long-arm statutes** and subjecting itself to the **specific state's constitutional analysis**.

### *J. McIntyre Machinery, Ltd. v. Nicastro*

For a defendant to be subject to a state's personal jurisdiction, it must purposefully avail itself of the privilege of conducting activities within the forum State, thus invoking the benefits and protections of its laws.

**Facts:**
- New Jersey resident Robert Nicastro (plaintiff) became seriously injured while using a metal-shearing machine manufactured by J. McIntyre Machinery, Ltd. (defendant).
- Although the injury occurred in New Jersey, the machine was manufactured in England where J. McIntyre is incorporated.
- The New Jersey Supreme Court found that, even though J. McIntyre never marketed its goods in the state nor shipped them there, New Jersey courts had jurisdiction over the company because the manufacturer knew or should have reasonably known that its products were distributed through a nationwide distribution system that might lead to their products being sold in any of the 50 states, including New Jersey.
- J. McIntyre appealed and SCOTUS granted certiorari.

**Issue:** Does a court have personal jurisdiction over a foreign corporation if one of the company's products injures someone in the forum state?

**Holding:** *(Not specified in original notes.)*

<!-- page: 18 -->

**Reasoning:**

**J. Kennedy (Plurality — 4):** A domestic court has personal jurisdiction over a foreign company that **purposefully avails itself** of the privilege of doing business within the forum state. Kennedy specifically criticized the 1987 opinion in *Asahi* where J. Brennan introduced the **stream of commerce metaphor**, and stated that specific jurisdiction over a company is appropriate if the company could have **foreseen** its product ending in the state where the injury occurred. Kennedy tried to overrule *Asahi*, but did not get a 5th vote to create a majority. In its place, the court created the following test:

**Purposeful availment** can be shown in different ways:

- **General jurisdiction** may exist in a specific state if a defendant:
  1. Consents to be litigating in a specific state
  2. Accepts service of process there
  3. Incorporates there, or
  4. Has a principal place of business there

- **Specific Jurisdiction** generally exists in a specific state if a defendant:
  1. Has targeted the state for business (either goods or services, salespeople or advertisements, etc.)

**McIntyre TEST:**
- The state **has personal jurisdiction** if a company **targets** the state with its product for business, and the product causes injury in the state.
- The state will **lack jurisdiction** if it is merely **foreseeable** that a product might end up in a certain state and the product causes injury there.
  - Test is applied equally to foreign and domestic companies.

Here, even though the products eventually reached New Jersey, J. McIntyre's distributor agreed to sell its products throughout the United States, but **not to New Jersey specifically**, paid no taxes in New Jersey, owns no property there, did not advertise there, and did not send employees there. Because these facts indicate that J. McIntyre did not purposefully avail itself to New Jersey, the New Jersey courts **lacked personal jurisdiction** over J. McIntyre.

The Plurality, through its rule, basically implies that if no one likes the result, they could go to Congress to enact its own, broader long-arm statute.

<!-- page: 19 -->

- This analysis is really emphasizing the **sovereignty rationale**, rather than the fairness to the defendant rationale.
