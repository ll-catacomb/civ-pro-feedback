### Cases

- ***Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank*, 339 U.S. 306 (SCOTUS)(1950)**

  An elementary and fundamental requirement of due process…is notice reasonably calculated, under all the circumstances, to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections. If the address is known, MUST send them notice.

## Lecture Recap for Day 13

Yesterday we began Procedural Due Process. If the government wants to do something bad or adverse to anyone in society, they must give the person "**notice** and **opportunity to be heard**."

- **14th Amendment** — States must give due process in order to deprive anyone of life, liberty, or property.
- **5th Amendment** — Applies the same standard to the Federal Government.

**Two stages of analysis:**
1. **Notice**
2. **Opportunity to be Heard**

### Notice — Summary

Key case: ***Mullane*** — notice needs to be **reasonable under the circumstances**. We do not require that the defendant receive ACTUAL notice for a judgment to be valid. Attempts at notice, if reasonable, will be sufficient.

**How notice issues arise in litigation:**
1. **Like *Mullane***: The court appoints a speaker/guardian to represent people who did not get notice, inside the piece of litigation that is the adverse action.
2. **Collateral attack on a judgment**: First litigation has occurred; defendant defaulted (not shown up); judgment entered. Plaintiff tries to seize assets, and defendant finds out then. Defendant can argue he never got "constitutionally sufficient" notice. If the court finds constitutionally insufficient notice, it will render the first judgment null and void and the parties must start over.

> *Attorneys sometimes say they are "testing the constitutional adequacy of the notice scheme" — meaning: the plaintiff and court complied with applicable rules of procedure, but the defendant argues that compliance with those rules is still not enough to meet constitutional standards.*

> ***NOTE:** Even if one meets the constitutional floor, if Federal Rules of Civil Procedure set a **higher standard**, you still must meet the higher standard. You have the constitutional floor that rules cannot go below, but they can set higher requirements.*

The *Mullane* standard was articulated in subsequent cases regarding:
- Persons incarcerated
- Those living in housing projects
- Those losing social security benefits
- How to give notice to someone homeless (no address)
- The difference over time regarding certified vs. electronic mail

<!-- page: 4 -->

Key holding from ***Jones v. Flowers***: The duty to provide reasonable notice is not measured entirely as of the first time that notice is given. As additional information becomes available suggesting the initial attempt was unsuccessful, there may be a **continuing duty** to make further efforts to provide notice.
- Once the address of the person is known by the relevant agency, it is under a duty to send via regular mail, notice to that address.

## Lecture for Day 14

### Opportunity to be Heard — *Mathews v. Eldridge* Framework

The real doctrinal statement for **Opportunity to be Heard** comes from *Mathews v. Eldridge* (finished below). The court had considered some government benefits as sufficiently similar to property, such that taking them away triggered the due process clauses of the 5th and 14th Amendments.

The administrative state cannot deprive someone of one of these benefits without providing certain due process protections.

### The Three-Part *Mathews* Framework

1. **Strength and nature of the private interest** of the party whose property is being taken
2. **The risk of an erroneous deprivation** of this interest through the procedures currently used by the agency, **as compared to** the risk of erroneous deprivation of the scheme the party is proposing to be adopted
3. **The government's interest**, including the function involved and the fiscal and administrative burdens that additional or substitute procedural requirements would entail

*This test is a list of things to consider, weigh, and balance depending on their strength. Sometimes one of the interests turns out to be extremely powerful and compelling.*

When we get to *Connecticut v. Doehr*, we will see that test get adjusted to also take into account the interests of **both** private litigants, rather than just the interests of the one bringing the suit.

<!-- page: 5 -->

**Key insight — Why do we have process at all?**

> *HYPO: A convenience store robber goes to extraordinary lengths to leave perfect evidence — facial recognition on camera, DNA blood sample, preserved bullets, 10 fingerprints, eyewitnesses — and then confesses with Miranda warning. He insists on a trial anyway. Should we just incarcerate him without a trial?*

We cannot dispense with this process — not because it changes the outcome for this defendant, but because **process matters for society**:
- The defendant gets to decide whether there is a trial because that is the person whose rights are being taken away by the government.
- Even in the most simple and obvious cases, it is important to keep the process. This ensures that society can take the rules seriously and feel secured that there is in fact a process. **Promotes fairness and security.**
- Due process and equal protection ensure that everyone protected under the law is treated the same.
- Having a process helps people to voluntarily follow the rules and the law.
- Process matters for purposes of **consistency**, **equality**, **legitimacy**, and **fairness** (allowing people to tell their side of the story).

The trial is not just for the defendant. It is for the rest of society, so that members of society can voluntarily be persuaded to follow the law.

*In this hypo, the first Mathews consideration (private interest) is most compelling, and the risk of erroneous determination by the court is low. The Mathews test wants a correct answer — mostly concerned with the risk of erroneous determination and interests related to that risk. It does not have room for broader considerations of process value. So, we apply Mathews to "New Property."*

### *Connecticut v. Doehr*, 501 U.S. 1 (SCOTUS)(1991) — Old Property Case

**RLL:** A statute allowing pre-judgment attachment of property must require a bond be posted or a hearing be held prior to the attachment unless there is a danger that the defendant will hide, damage, or otherwise impair the plaintiff's ability to attach the property.

**Facts:**
- Connecticut law permits a plaintiff in a civil suit to obtain a pre-judgment attachment of a defendant's property without a pre-attachment hearing or the filing of a bond.
- The statute does not require a defendant be informed that the application for attachment has been filed until it has been granted.
- In 1988, Brian K. Doehr (defendant) was sued by John F. DiGiovanni (plaintiff) for **assault and battery**.
- Concurrent with filing the complaint, DiGiovanni filed an application for a pre-judgment attachment of Doehr's house in the amount of $75,000.
- The attachment was granted.
- When Doehr was informed of the attachment, he filed suit challenging the statute on Due Process grounds.
- The district court upheld the statute. The Second Circuit reversed.

<!-- page: 7 -->

**Holding:**
- The use of pre-judgment attachment, even in a civil suit between two private parties, implicates Fourteenth Amendment Due Process concerns due to **participation of state machinery** in the actual attachment.
- This case involves two private parties, not a private party and the government. The applicable test examines:
  1. The nature of the private interest that is affected by the attachment
  2. The possibility of a defendant improperly losing the use of his property and whether there are safeguards to protect against that outcome
  3. The interest of the party seeking the attachment along with that of the government in having to provide other procedures for attachment
- Under this test:
  - **Private interest**: The ability to enjoy one's property during a civil suit. Simply because a complaint has been filed does not necessarily mean a negative result for the defendant. Denial of property use during a suit injures the defendant.
  - **Safeguards in the Connecticut statute**: post-attachment hearing, notice of said hearing, and double damages if a suit is commenced without cause. These safeguards do **not** sufficiently protect the defendant's interests in the same manner that a pre-attachment hearing or bond would.
  - The fact that virtually every other state requires one or both of these prior to attachment evidences the simplicity of implementing these measures.
- There are some instances when measures similar to the Connecticut law would be proper — namely when there is a **risk of the defendant damaging the property or removing it from the jurisdiction**. That threat is not present here.
- Simply posting a bond would not be sufficient to satisfy Due Process concerns.
- **Rather, a state must permit a hearing prior to an attachment unless there is a showing of exigent circumstances.**

*This case sounds like a Quasi In Rem proceeding. Under Shaffer v. Heitner analysis, we might struggle to find contacts between the defendant's interest in this property, the subject matter (assault), and the forum. But the court doesn't even mention Shaffer — it is mostly concerned with procedural due process.*

<!-- page: 8 -->

**Applying the *Mathews* test in *Doehr*:**

**Private Interest of the Plaintiff:**
- Plaintiff does not have a sufficient interest in the defendant's property to outweigh the defendant's interest — no exceptional reason to seize it (such as if defendant was getting rid of the house, etc.).

**Risk of Erroneous Determination:**
- Likelihood of erroneous determination is **very high** (would have to litigate the merits of an assault case to figure out what happened).
- Unlike *Mathews*, this is **not a documents case** — the court cannot just look at documents and extract the validity of evidence.
- There is a lot more needed: witness and personal testimony, which would need to be done live or through affidavits (to prevent perjury).
- Something plaintiffs can do to reduce the risk of erroneous determination: show the likelihood of success on the merits (the court calls it "risk of erroneous determination").

**Defendant's Private Interest:**
- Doehr is unable to sell the house while it is "attached" — his interests would be heavily deprived.

### Bonds and Counterbonds in Procedural Due Process

<!-- page: 9 -->

**Plaintiff Bond:**
- An amount of money that the plaintiff gives the court to ensure that if they lose on the merits, they can pay the defendant for any harm caused by the attachment.
- Plaintiff bonds are required of plaintiffs to ensure protection of the defendant should the plaintiff lose the lawsuit.
- People who cannot afford a bond go to a bond company, which evaluates the strength of the claim to determine rates and whether to issue the bond at all.
- If the bond price is too high, the lawsuit will never get filed — the bond acts as compensation for the defendant if the plaintiff does not win.

**The bond serves two purposes:**
1. It is a **market signal** as to the risk of erroneous determination; and
2. **Protection against deprivation** of the defendant's interests.

**Defendant Bond (Counter-Bond):**
- Blocks a plaintiff's action or postpones payment of a judgment.
- Generally permits the defendant to regain control of contested property or postpone enforcement of a court judgment affecting his rights to the property.
- If the procedure allows the defendant to post a counter-bond, the court can safely remove the attachment on the property.

**Other ways courts can reduce risk of erroneous determination:**
- Requiring affidavits or evidentiary showing
- Delegating authority to administrative agencies
- Delegating authority to the Clerk of Court
- Considering which party has the burden of going forward; if the burden is on the plaintiff, that provides more due process for the defendant.
- Where both sides have a property interest (title disputes between mortgages and mortgagees; merchandise disputes between credit stores and their merchandise), balancing the private rights of the parties.

<!-- page: 10 -->

> ***NOTE (Exam/Assignment):** In the case of "old property," there are factors that flow into the *Mathews* test, and you need to articulate (in an assignment or exam) how they would fit in the scheme as to its constitutionality. For example: if there is a bond, that safeguard makes the case less vulnerable to a procedural due process attack, especially if the defendant can post a counterbond. You can discuss who is the decision maker, what the interests at stake are, who has the burden of going forward, etc.*

### *Vitek Correctional Director, et al. v. Jones*, 445 U.S. 480 (SCOTUS)(1980) — Liberty Interest

**RLL:** The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires notice and opportunity for a hearing, even under conditions of confinement, when they amount to an additional "grievous loss" of liberty.

The interest at issue in *Vitek* was the liberty interest in jail vs. what he would be subjected to in the mental institution:
- Forcing him to participate in **behavior modification programs** (euphemisms for forced treatment that we may now characterize as abuse and torture).
- **Stigma** — that he is not just a criminal, but that he is mentally unstable.

<!-- page: 11 -->

**Facts:**
- A Nebraska statute authorized the Director of Correctional Services (Vitek) to designate any available, suitable residence facility or institution as a place of confinement for any state prisoner and to transfer a prisoner from one place of confinement to another.
- When a designated physician or psychologist finds that a prisoner suffers from mental disease and cannot be given proper treatment in that facility, the director may transfer the prisoner for examination, study, or treatment.
- Jones was convicted of robbery and sentenced to 3–9 years in state prison.
- He was transferred to the state penitentiary hospital in January 1975.
- He was later put in solitary confinement where he set his mattress on fire, burning himself severely.
- Upon release from the burn unit of a private hospital, he was found to be suffering from a mental illness and could not receive proper treatment in the penal complex.
- He was transferred to the security unit of the state mental hospital under the jurisdiction of the Department of Public Institutions.

**Issue:**
- Whether the involuntary transfer of a Nebraska state prisoner to a mental hospital implicates a liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause?

**Holding:**
- **Yes.**
- First, a liberty interest is rooted in the state statute itself — a prisoner could reasonably expect that he would not be transferred to a mental hospital without a finding that he was suffering from a mental illness for which he could not secure adequate treatment in the correctional facility.
- Second, characterizing Jones as a mentally-ill patient and transferring him to the Lincoln Regional Center had "some stigmatizing" consequences which, together with the mandatory behavior modification treatment to which Jones would be subject, **constitute a major change in the condition of confinement amounting to a "grievous loss" that should not be imposed without the opportunity for notice and an adequate hearing.**
- A valid criminal conviction and prison sentence sufficiently extinguish a defendant's liberty "to empower the State to confine him in any of its prisons."
- Changes in conditions of confinement having a substantial adverse impact are not alone sufficient to invoke Due Process Clause protections "[as] long as the conditions or degree of confinement to which the prisoner is subjected is within the sentence imposed upon him."
- However, **the stigmatizing consequences of a transfer to a mental hospital for involuntary psychiatric treatment, coupled with the subjection of the prisoner to mandatory behavior modification as a treatment for mental illness, constitute the kind of deprivations of liberty that requires procedural protections.**

<!-- page: 12 -->

**Minimum procedures required before transferring a prisoner to a mental hospital:**
- A. **Written notice** to the prisoner that a transfer is being considered;
- B. **A hearing**, sufficiently after notice to permit the prisoner to prepare, at which evidence is disclosed and the prisoner has an opportunity to be heard in person and to present documentary evidence;
- C. **An opportunity to present testimony of witnesses** by the defense and to confront and cross-examine witnesses called by the state, except upon a finding of good cause for not permitting such presentation;
- D. **An independent decisionmaker**;
- E. **A written statement by the factfinder** as to the evidence relied on and the reasons for transferring the inmate;
- F. **Availability of legal counsel, furnished by the state**, if the inmate is financially unable to furnish his own; and
- G. **Effective and timely notice** of all the foregoing rights.

The court applied the *Mathews* factors:
- The **interest of the State** in segregating and treating mentally ill patients is strong.
- The **interest of the prisoner** in not being arbitrarily classified as mentally ill and subjected to unwelcome treatment is also powerful.
- The **risk of error** in making the determinations required by the statute is substantial enough to warrant appropriate procedural safeguards against error.

### *Lassiter v. Department of Social Services*, 452 U.S. 18 (SCOTUS)(1981) — Right to Counsel

**Context:** In *Lassiter*, the mother was in her third court proceeding in North Carolina:
1. First proceeding: removal of the child from her custody.
2. Second proceeding: convicted of Murder in the Second Degree (charged with 1st degree); sentenced to 25 to 40 years in prison.
3. Third proceeding: the state sought to **terminate the parental relationship** with the child entirely.

**RLL:** The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment does not mandate that indigent parties be assigned counsel in parental rights proceedings.

**Facts:**
- In spring 1975, Abby Gail Lassiter's (plaintiff) son, William, was adjudicated a neglected child and removed from her care and placed with the Durham County Department of Social Services (Department) (defendant).
- One year later, Lassiter was convicted of second-degree murder and sentenced to 25 to 40 years in prison.
- In 1978, the Department petitioned to remove Lassiter's parental rights.
- The evidence presented suggested that Lassiter was not fit to raise the child, and her mother was unable to take on the additional responsibility.
- The court held that Lassiter's parental rights should be terminated. She appealed, arguing that her Due Process rights had been violated because she was indigent and the judge had not appointed counsel.
- The North Carolina Court of Appeals affirmed. The Supreme Court of North Carolina also affirmed. SCOTUS granted certiorari.

<!-- page: 13 -->

**Holding and Reasoning (Stewart, J.):**
- The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment **does not guarantee indigent litigants the right to counsel** in proceedings where their parental rights are at stake.
- Due Process Clause protections are **strongest at times when a party's personal freedom is at stake**, and it is primarily in those situations that the court will find a per se right to counsel.

**Applying the *Mathews* factors:**

**(1) The private interests at stake:**
- Her parenthood over the child.
- The court mentions that this is probably the most compelling interest — even refers to it as a **"commanding interest."**

**(2) The risk of erroneous determinations (existing vs. proposed scheme):**
- The procedural addition she thinks is needed to reduce the risk of erroneous determination is **legal counsel**.
- The court says that legal counsel in this case **would not reduce** the erroneous determination risk.
- This case offers the **one exception** to the general rule that the court looks at the class of cases, not just the individual fact pattern — **the court finds it exceptional in this case** and looks only at the individual circumstances.

**(3) The government interest:**
- The court is concerned about protecting the welfare of the child.
- The court acknowledges there is not a large expense in litigation.

<!-- page: 14 -->

**Key rule:**
- In situations where we don't have **physical liberty** at stake, the court presumes there is no right to counsel.
- Sometimes, even when physical liberty is at stake, the court also presumes there is no right to counsel.
- In *Mathews*, the test balances the presumption that an indigent party will be appointed counsel only if personal liberty is at stake against three factors: (1) private interests, (2) government's interests, (3) risk of erroneous results.

**In *Lassiter*:**
- There were no complex evidentiary or legal issues.
- The clear weight of evidence suggested Lassiter and her mother were unable or unwilling to care for the child.
- Appointed counsel would not have changed the outcome.
- In other termination cases, however, an indigent litigant may be greatly aided by assigned counsel — in those cases the judge should consider doing so.
- Lack of counsel in parental rights cases will not deny indigent litigants their right to due process — therefore they do not have a per se right to counsel.

<!-- page: 15 -->

Many people and even court systems believe that if we can just get people attorneys, we would be fulfilling all of the due process procedures. But ask whether the fact that people are assigned counsel is actually working. Even those assigned an attorney have to share that attorney with hundreds of other cases.
