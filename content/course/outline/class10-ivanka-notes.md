## Lecture Recap for Day 9

Today we discussed venue and moving cases around.

- We study venue in the federal court system, even though there is a doctrine of venue in the state statutes.
- Venue is a doctrine concerned with convenience and efficiency of the parties, and there is nothing constitutional about it.
- It is covered entirely by federal statute and concerns which district, rather than state, a lawsuit can be filed in.
- It has the same effect as personal jurisdiction in that if venue cannot properly be laid by the plaintiff, a federal district court **MUST DISMISS or TRANSFER** over the objections of a defendant.
- Like personal jurisdiction, defendants can waive venue in order to have a more expedient litigation.
- We discussed cases and tried to figure out whether venue can be laid under:
  - **(b1):** where a defendant (or all defendants) resides in the same state
  - **(b2):** where a substantial portion of the events giving rise to the claim occurred
  - **(b3):** only available if neither (b1) nor (b2) are available — results in only being available when the events occurred outside the US:
    - If a single defendant case: a defendant who resides outside of the US

<!-- page: 4 -->

    - If a multiple defendant case: the defendants would have to be from different states
    - b(3) will very rarely be used because it is only applicable if b(1) or b(2) are not available.
- We went over the rules of residency (domicile) for both corporations and individuals.

### Two Doctrines for Moving Cases Around

#### Forum Non-Conveniens Doctrine

Either:
- Case is currently in a state court system and the suggestion is to move it to another state or to a court abroad.
  - Not used to move from state court to federal court (we would use removal for that).
- Case is pending in the federal court system and the suggestion is to move it abroad.
  - You would never move a federal court case to a state court:
    - Unless there is a forum selection clause;
    - If there is a way to remand, we could do that; or
    - The federal court system would transfer to another federal court that is more convenient.

Basically, the defendant would move to dismiss a case under **forum non conveniens** and the court would conduct the following analysis:
1. Presume that the plaintiff's choice of forum should be honored and the case would stay where it is;
2. Then consider the private interest and public interest factors and see if those factors overwhelm the plaintiff's choice of forum *(See Class notes for Day 9 for details)*.

> ***Piper***: In the federal system, the presumption that plaintiff's choice of forum is correct should **not** operate if the plaintiff is from abroad.

If we dismiss the case under the forum non-conveniens doctrine, the plaintiff would be able to file in another state.
- Permissible if case was dismissed under **forum non conveniens** doctrine for the plaintiff to be invited to file in a location that:
  - Has no personal jurisdiction over defendant
  - Venue is not proper
  - Statute of Limitations has expired
    - **SO LONG AS defendant has waived objections to these**
      - Defendants sometimes do that in exchange for more expeditious litigation.

#### Transfer Doctrine

We discussed the three transfer statutes, which are to be referred to collectively as the **"Transfer statutes"**, which give the following law:

1. If a case lacks personal jurisdiction, or venue is not proper, it **MUST BE** either transferred or dismissed.
   - UNLESS defendant waives objecting to both (we need both, or defendant waiver of objections for both).
2. Case may be transferred **ONLY** to a location in which the case **"could have been brought"**.
   - ***Hoffman*** interpreted that to mean a "**Happy court**."
     - **Happy court:** court has personal jurisdiction **and** venue is proper.
       - UNLESS plaintiff waives objection (cannot transfer over the plaintiff's objection to a court that lacks personal jurisdiction or venue is not proper).

<!-- page: 5 -->

- This is different from forum non-conveniens because under the Transfer doctrine, even if a defendant is willing to waive venue and Personal Jurisdiction objections in the transferee court (where the defendant would want to move the case to), the transfer statutes do not allow the case to be transferred there. (*Hoffman*)

### Three Questions Framework for Transfer

When we have a defendant wanting to transfer from one federal court to another:

**1. Must I transfer?**
- If PJ or venue is lacking, **MUST transfer**.

**2. Can/Should I transfer?**
- a. Can I transfer?
  - If can identify another court to which you **can** transfer (happy court), then you may transfer.
- b. Should I transfer?
  - ***Forum non-conveniens*** analysis.

**3. What happens if I do transfer?**
- This question gives us the Greiner "**happy court**" rule.
  - Primary thing at issue here is **Horizontal Choice of Law (HCOL)**.
    - Ordinarily, a federal court sitting in diversity will use the **choice of law analysis** of the state in which it is sitting (*Claxon*).
      - As a matter of vertical choice of law, choice of law rules are substantive; therefore, the federal district court uses the choice of law rules of the state in which it is sitting.

<!-- page: 6 -->

  - The issue is what to do in a "transfer" situation?
    - **Greiner happy court rule** tells you that if a transferor court is a **happy court** (has PJ and venue is proper) then the **choice of law analysis of the transferor court will follow the transfer** and the **transferee court will use the transferor's choice of law analysis** so that there are no major change in rules, in particular, **limitations periods**.
      - Remembering that a transferor court (federal court) will pretend that it's a state court for HCOL purposes, which means the state court, as a matter of HCOL will use **procedural law of the forum** (its own limitations period).
    - If transferor court is an **unhappy court** (venue not proper or PJ is lacking) then choice of law analysis of the transferor court will not follow the transfer and the transferee court's state's law choice of law analysis will govern in the case, including the transferee court's state limitation's period if that is an issue.

### Forum Selection Clause + Transfer

- If there is a motion in federal court to transfer the case from one court to another to comply with a **forum selection clause**:
  - Make sure transferee court is a place where the case could have been brought.

**But, must I transfer?**
- No, because if the transferor court is in some way "unhappy" you don't have to transfer, but you almost always will because forum selection clause affects the analysis in three ways (*Atlantic Marine*):
  1. You presume that you honor the forum's selection clause rather than the plaintiff's choice;
  2. You don't consider private interest factors at all, only the public interest factors, and they must overwhelm the presumption of the forum selection clause (which means case is almost always going to be transferred);
  3. Greiner **happy court** rule no longer applies. The horizontal choice of law rules of the transferee court (the forum selection court) will apply.

## *Atlantic Marine Construction Co. v. US District Court*, 571 U.S. 49 (SCOTUS) (2013)

When parties have entered into a valid forum-selection clause that designates a federal venue, the case should be transferred to the designated district unless extraordinary circumstances exist that are unrelated to the convenience of the parties.

**Facts:**
- Atlantic Marine Construction Co. (Atlantic) (defendant) contracted to construct a child-development center.
- Atlantic entered into a subcontract with J-Crew Management, Inc. (J-Crew) (plaintiff) for a portion of the work.

<!-- page: 7 -->

- The subcontract included a **forum-selection clause** designating either the circuit court in Norfolk, Virginia or the federal district court in Norfolk, Virginia as the forum for any disputes under the contract.
- J-Crew sued Atlantic in the Western District of Texas over a dispute about payment under the subcontract.
- Atlantic moved to dismiss under 28 U.S.C. § 1406(a) and Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(3) or, in the alternative, to transfer the case to the Eastern District of Virginia under 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a), based on the forum-selection clause.
- The district court denied the motions.
- Atlantic appealed to the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit, which affirmed.
- Atlantic then sought review from the Supreme Court of the United States.

**Issue:**
- When parties have entered into a valid forum-selection clause that designates a federal venue, should the case be transferred to the designated district absent extraordinary circumstances unrelated to the convenience of the parties?

**Holding:**
- **If parties have agreed to a valid forum-selection clause that designates a federal venue, the case should be transferred to the designated district.**
- As long as the forum-selection clause is a contractually valid provision, **courts should enforce the agreement of the parties**, unless extraordinary circumstances exist that are unrelated to the convenience of the parties.

## Vertical Choice of Law — The *Erie* Problem

<!-- page: 9 -->

### The *Erie* Problem

```
Federal Court in State A  ?  Federal Court in State B
         ‖?                            ‖?
    State A Court  ≠  State B Court
```

- In the US we have several state courts.
- We are anticipating litigation to take place in either State A or State B.
  - Due to federalism, states differ depending on what laws they enact.
    - Because of that, choice of law rules might differ from one state to another.
    - We want that because we would not necessarily want the results from State A to equal those of State B. We like the difference between states.
- However, we also have federal court in State A. And when a federal court is sitting in diversity, the federal court purports to apply state law, either the state law that the state has actually declared, or the state law as the federal court thinks the state law should be.
  - As regards the federal court in State A, we allow diversity because we want an **unbiased** decision maker.
    - And, in the federal system, we would want the Federal court in State A to equal or similar the result in State court in State A.
    - And, same thing with State B — in the federal system, we would want the Federal court in State B to equal or similar the result in State court in State B.
  - With respect to federal courts, because they are national courts, we have transfer statutes to move cases from one federal court to another, and so in the federal courts, we want to have the same results in federal court in State A as in federal court in State B (thus removing the bias that we specifically tried to get away from in the first place).
  - But that causes a problem — some equality from the above chart would be under extreme pressure.

<!-- page: 10 -->

- Congress is the only branch that could change this and make equal laws everywhere.
  - But if we make the same laws, we do not need states anymore.
- In ***Swift v. Tyson***, there was a lot of **dissimilarity** in the **state court law** and the **federal court law** in the **same location**.
- ***Erie*** instead has us **equalize the state court result and the federal court result in the same location**.
- ***Walker*** will end up telling us that there might be **some dissimilarity** between the **federal court result in State A** and the **federal court result in State B**.

## *Swift v. Tyson*, 41 U.S. (16 Pet.) 1 (SCOTUS) (1842)

**Rule:** The Rule of Decision Act (what jurisdiction's law applies to a given case) does not bind federal courts to state common law.

*(Note: "holder in due course doctrine" is background only; not tested in this class.)*

**Background — Holder in Due Course:**
- Back in the day it was difficult to get to a bank to cash checks. People came up with checks as promissory instruments.
- Sometimes checks would be endorsed multiple times and passed around.
- If someone decided they did not want the check to be honored, they could stop payment, which decreased the reliability of checks.
- **Holder in due course:** the person who took the check and passed it on without any knowledge that the check was fraudulent.
- Some states refuse to acknowledge the "holder in due course" doctrine.

**What is the "general law" that the SCOTUS tried to apply?**
- Maybe informed by custom at the time.
- Law that pre-existed human creation and human conscious.
- Renaissance history — "Reason" — the thought that one could discover human conduct in the same way that physicists could discover space.
- "True principles" of law.

- SCOTUS thought they were following this general law, and that they were "**finding**" the law, and not "**making**" it.
- In this world of general law finding, should federal law judges be bound to state laws? They believed no, unless Congress enacted a law that said that.
- BUT — Congress had already enacted 28 U.S.C. § 1652 (State laws as rules of decision):
  > The laws of the several states, except where the Constitution or treaties of the United States or Acts of Congress otherwise require or provide, **shall be regarded as rules of decision in civil actions in the courts of the United States**, in cases where they apply.
- Nonetheless, the SCOTUS thought it was not covered by this statute and that they were free not to follow "state common law" decisions.
- After *Swift* was decided, the federal system hardly ever deferred to state judicial decisions.

**Actual Facts of *Swift v. Tyson* (diversity case):**

<!-- page: 11 -->

```
Swift v. Tyson — Actual Facts
1. Creditor (Swift) ──$loan──► Swindlers (Narton, Keith)
                                    │"land" (doesn't exist)
                              Guarantor (Tyson)
                                    │ check (pay the loan)
2. Swindlers (Narton, Keith) ────────────► Creditor (Swift)
3. Swindlers disappear; Guarantor discovers fraud & stops payment on check
4. Creditor (Swift) ──NY federal court, plenty $$──► Guarantor (Tyson)
```

- Creditor makes a loan to possible swindlers and requires a guarantor to ensure payment.
- Creditor begins to get suspicious, so he requires them to find a guarantor.
- They find Tyson, but Tyson does not want to guarantee the loan for free, so the swindlers "gave him some land" in exchange for his guarantee.
- The swindlers did not actually own the land. They disappeared.
- Guarantor finds out that the swindlers left.
- Creditor realizes swindlers did not pay the loan, so wants money from the guarantor.
- Guarantors did not pay the loan because the swindlers did not give him the land.

**Which one will get stuck having to find the swindlers?**
- In NY law at the time, the fact that the land did not exist would immediately relieve the guarantor from having to pay the loan because he didn't get anything for the guarantee.
- BUT — under the "holder in due course" doctrine, Swift (creditor) was ignorant of the fraud, and gave something for value, so demands payment from guarantor.
- When guarantor stopped payment, creditor sued guarantor in **federal court** because he knew that in NY state he would not be able to enforce payment under NY law.
- The SCOTUS ended up **not applying** the NY state law (which would relieve the guarantor from having to pay). They said they need to consider it, but they're not forced to apply it.
- They ended up applying what they considered to be "**questions of general commercial law**."

<!-- page: 12 -->

**Holding:**
- The Rule of Decision Act does not bind federal courts to state common law.
  - The act provides that state laws should generally be viewed as rules of decision in applicable judicial cases.
  - An exception exists if the United States Constitution, treaties, or federal statutes conflict with state laws.
  - The act's term "**laws of the several states**" means only **state constitutions and statutes**.
  - State court decisions made on the basis of state common law **aren't part of the laws of the state** because court decisions merely apply the laws to specific cases.
  - Court decisions can also be reversed, overturned, or modified by later decisions.
  - Thus, the act doesn't bind federal courts to state common law.
  - The federal courts are therefore free to consider general common-law principles and **create a federal body of common-law decisions in diversity cases**.
  - Instead, a "better general common-law rule" is to allow valid pre-existing debts to qualify as consideration for bills of exchange.
  - Narton and Keith are validly indebted to Swift. Swift accepted the Tyson bill of exchange in partial payment of that debt, and Swift didn't know that Narton and Keith had defrauded Tyson. Therefore, Swift takes the bill of exchange free of the defense of fraud, and Tyson must honor the bill of exchange and pay Swift.
  - The trial court's judgment is reversed.

**Problems Due to This Case:**
- After this case, the courts just kept applying federal law, because it never found any situation so intensely local as to warrant state law.
- The federal courts were applying one system of **substantive law**, and the state courts were administering another system of **substantive law**, which almost led to inequalities — which later led to fact patterns that drove Justices Holmes and Brandeis crazy.
  - For example, the Taxi Cab case summarized in *Erie*.

## *Erie R. Co. v. Tompkins*, 304 U.S. 64 (SCOTUS) (1938)

**Rule:** A federal court sitting in diversity must apply state substantive law, whether statutory or common law. Overturns *Swift*.

<!-- page: 13 -->

**Facts:**
- While walking along the railroad tracks, Harry **Tompkins (plaintiff)**, **a citizen of Pennsylvania**, was injured by a train owned by Erie Railroad Co. (Erie) (defendant).
- Tompkins sued **Erie, a New York company**, for negligence in **New York federal court**.
- At trial, Erie argued that Tompkins was a trespasser and, under Pennsylvania state law, the company was not liable unless its conduct was wantonly negligent.
- Tompkins argued that federal general law should apply and determine Erie's duty and liability.
- The trial judge applied the rule from *Swift v. Tyson*, holding that **federal courts were only bound to apply state statutory and customary law, not state common law**.
- Thus, the trial court applied the general law. Because the majority rule was that a railroad company owed a duty of ordinary care to travelers like Tompkins, the district court granted recovery for Tompkins.
- The circuit court affirmed. The United States Supreme Court granted certiorari.

**Issue:** Is a federal court sitting in diversity bound to applying state common law?

<!-- page: 14 -->

**Holding:**
- Yes. *Swift v. Tyson* (1842) held that federal trial courts exercising diversity jurisdiction were free to disregard state law and exercise independent judgment on matters of general jurisprudence.
- The *Swift* decision had been **controversial from its inception** and had many judicial defects.
- The *Swift* rule prevented uniformity in state administration, created uncertainty for plaintiffs and defendants, and resulted in **discrimination and unequal treatment** under the law.
- For example, corporate parties could avoid applying state common law in a suit by reincorporating under the laws of a new state in order to establish diversity, e.g., *Black & White Taxicab v. Brown & Yellow Taxicab*, 276 U.S. 518 (1928).
- Thus, **to avoid injustice and further discrimination, the law of the state should be applied in diversity of citizenship cases on general matters, regardless of whether it is made by its courts or its legislature**.
- **A federal trial court exercising diversity jurisdiction must respect and enforce state law.**
- Refusing to use state law in general matters represents an unconstitutional invasion of state autonomy and a denial of its independence.
- **The Constitution does not give the federal courts the power to create a "general federal common law."**
- To do so would undermine the sovereignty of the state governments.
- Therefore *Swift* is overturned.

**Reed Concurrence:**
- The *Swift* decision was merely erroneous, not unconstitutional.
- It is unlikely that without federal statutory direction, federal courts would be required to follow state decisions.
- It is also doubtful that Congress does not possess the authority to declare what rules of substantive law govern federal courts.
- The majority's reasoning purports to entirely re-interpret the Constitution.

**Butler Dissent:**
- The only questions raised by Erie Railroad Co. were whether its duty should have been defined by Pennsylvania common law and whether Tompkins was contributorily negligent.
- The constitutional questions decided by the majority were not raised or argued, nor were they necessary to dispose of the case.
- The *Swift* doctrine has been in place "since the foundation of the government," almost without question.
- There is no suggestion that Congress did not have the authority to make the rule as it was interpreted under *Swift*.

<!-- page: 15 -->

- Further, **by statute, the federal government has a right to intervene in any case where the constitutionality of an act of Congress is challenged**.
- The majority should have assigned the case for re-argument of the constitutional issues and permitted the attorney general to intervene.
- Regardless, none of that should have even been considered, because under the law of Pennsylvania and pretty much everywhere else, Tompkins was contributorily negligent.

---

So in this case, Tompkins was suing the RR, basically attempting to go to federal court to avoid what he thought was a harsh rule to prove (gross negligence). The "general law" at the time under *Swift*, was that mere negligence was enough.

When we get to the SCOTUS, the court says that there is **no such thing as "general federal common law"** and we will be using the state law.

### Legal Foundations (Justifications) of *Erie*

**Court in *Erie* thought that judges were "making" law.**
- So therefore, there was no "general federal common law."
- They feel judges in *Swift* were making law, and judges should not be making law; they should be only applying law.
  - In diversity cases, state laws are what give causes of action.
- They feel that *Swift* violates several constitutional doctrines.

**One possible justification — WHAT IS LAW?:**
- One problem with *Swift* regime is that finding out what law is incorrect.
- They felt that what was wrong was judge-made federal common law.
  - And it was not limited to issues where the federal government would be justified in having a strong or powerful federal interest.

<!-- page: 16 -->

  - One potential issue here is that there might be a strong federal interest in specifically this fact pattern in *Erie* because it involves a railroad and thus involved interstate commerce.

> ***NOTE:*** So, if federal common law is something that judges make, not find, then all along in the *Swift* regime, federal judges have been imposing judge-made law in diversity cases which is federal law because they are federal judges and they made it up. This is wrong and illegitimate because they should have been following state law.

### Three Other Justifications for the *Erie* Decision

**1. Constitutional:**

- ***Federalism:***
  - **Neither Congress, nor judges, nor the executive branch has power to make general common law (*Erie*).**
  - Only states have power to make common law.
    - Federalism allows the federal government very limited power, regulated by listed powers in the US Constitution.
    - Most of governing should be done by the states.
    - No Constitutional authority for any other branch to make law.
    - So, if we adopt the law they make, it would be unconstitutional.

- ***Strong Separation of Powers:***
  - **Except as otherwise enacted by Congress, the state law is what should be applied.**
    - BUT Congress had already passed a law that says exactly how they wished for these situations to be handled, and it said that the "laws of the several states" should be regarded as the rules of decision in civil actions in the courts of the United States.
    - Court system should wait for congressional word before it makes common law and displaces state common law. The word has not come. In fact, the word says the opposite (28 U.S.C. § 1652) — to use state laws.

- ***Equal protection:***
  - **By being "diverse" plaintiffs, they could have an advantage (by being able to sue in federal court), in either their home court or the defendant's home court, over those in-state plaintiffs who were not diverse from the defendants and thus forced to sue in their own home state courts.**
    - If the diverse plaintiff liked federal law, plaintiff would sue in federal court rather than the state court.

<!-- page: 17 -->

    - If the diverse plaintiff liked state court rather than the federal court, they could sue there, but:
      - Theory is that if defendant liked the federal law, he could remove the case and get it in federal court.
        - But, defendant **"cannot remove"** to federal court of their home court because then it would be remanded back to state court, thus creating an unfair advantage.
    - So, in a diversity only case, an out of state plaintiff can drag a defendant to home state court by suing the defendant in that court, and that defendant could not remove.
  - Justice Brandeis said that it was an equal protection violation because this structure would not treat two similarly situated persons the same (out of state plaintiff v. in-state plaintiff).

**2. Statutory justifications (28 U.S.C. § 1652):**
- **Except as otherwise enacted by Congress, the state law is what should be applied.** (Very similar and related to separation of powers argument.)
  - Federal courts here had been encroaching on congressional power to pass statutes that declare what rules should be.
    - We might say that if Congress had been entirely silent on what law should apply in a diversity action, the federal courts should apply state law until Congress tells them what to do (*Erie*).
    - *Erie* goes further in that it explains Congress did not stay silent because they enacted the **Rules of Decision Act (28 U.S.C. § 1652)**.
    - *Erie* court says that the proper interpretation of the words "the laws of the several states" refers to the statutes **AND** the common law.
    - Also says that they will respect laws enacted by state legislature, but not by federal courts.
  - **There is an argument though that Congress should have the power to have a say in how lower courts operate:**
    - Constitution empowered Congress to create lower federal courts.
    - Congress created the lower federal courts.
    - Constitution also has the Necessary and Proper Clause, which says that Congress can create any law to enable it to carry out any of the enumerated powers, one of which is to create lower federal courts.

<!-- page: 18 -->

    - Therefore, Congress should have some say in what happens in lower federal courts.
      - BUT Congress has never tried to encroach so far in state law; in fact, it enacted statutes that say to follow state law.

**3. Prudence (probably the final justification for *Erie*):**
- There are problems with all of these rationales.
  - We are not sure what the right interpretation of the Rules of Decision Act is:
    - Could be that *Swift* got it right; or
    - Congress acquiesced in the interpretation of *Swift* by not enacting a law to the contrary.
  - Problems with all Constitutional arguments:
    - Federalism may be too strong.
    - Not clear from the separation of powers doctrine why the federal court should be different than all other courts.
      - All other courts in our system have been making common law for a very long time.
      - We inherited our court system from UK and they made common law.
      - We structured the federal court system after the state court system — why shouldn't federal courts make common law if everyone else can?
    - Equal protection rationale — Congress clearly knew that there would be some differences by creating a different court system, so how strong is the equal protection rationale?
      - Congress in fact wanted differences in decision making when it created different levels of courts system.
- They decided after considering these counterarguments, that "it was just a better idea to use state law, until someone tells us differently" — a **federal court prudence rationale**.

> ***NOTE:** Erie*, in making this very rule that says "there is no such general common law rule," the decision of *Erie* itself is a common law (judge-made) rule. *(Common law interpretation of Erie.)*

**So now we know *Erie* tells us to use state law in federal diversity cases.**
- So, does that mean we need to use state law to determine size of papers?
- Or how many days a respondent has to respond to a motion?

*Erie* was decided in 1938, at the same time as the federal rules of civil procedure.

<!-- page: 19 -->

- **Rules Enabling Act (28 U.S.C. § 2071)** sets a procedure or process by which the rules are created, which takes the SCOTUS to adopt the rule, and Congress can veto if they do not like the rule.
  - SCOTUS and Congress are limited by 28 U.S.C. § 2071, which says that these rules are not to **"abridge, enlarge, or modify SUBSTANTIVE rights."**

These acts came out in 1938, and we were expected still to have state to state variation in rules, including procedural rules. Congress did not intend that, but that is our main problem.

- How do we preserve some uniformity across the federal court system, consistent with Congress' intention when it enacted 28 U.S.C. § 2071, and still have *Erie*?
- How do we reconcile those two wants?

**Test needed: When to use federal law, or when to use state law in a diversity case.**

> Whenever a federal court is sitting in diversity:
> - If **PROCEDURAL** issue — **FEDERAL LAW** (Federal procedural rules)
> - If **SUBSTANTIVE** issue — **STATE LAW**

But now we have to figure out the line between substantive and procedural law:
- Negligence — substantive law
- Size of paper to file complaint — procedural law
- Some are in the middle, though, so we need another test.

<!-- page: 20 -->

By the end of the semester, we will have **four tests** established by the federal court system which will say how to tell substantive from procedural law.

> In an exam or assignment, when you get an *Erie* question, you will need to explain each of the four tests. Greiner will tell you which one(s) the court system purports to adopt in the exam or assignment, but you will need to **discuss all four tests**.

***Erie* did not give us a test.**

## *Guaranty Trust v. York*, 326 U.S. 99 (SCOTUS) (1945)

**Rule:** A federal court, exercising jurisdiction based strictly on diversity of citizenship, must abide by any state legal rule that would be outcome determinative if held in state court.

**Facts:**
- York (plaintiff) sued Guaranty Trust Co. (Guaranty) (defendant) for alleged breach of trust.
- The suit was a federal diversity action and New York substantive law governed. Guaranty claimed the suit was barred by the statute of limitations.
- York argued that since the action was "in equity," the federal court was not bound by the statute of limitations.
- The court of appeals agreed and permitted the suit to continue. The United States Supreme Court granted certiorari.

**Holding:**
- Yes. A federal court, exercising jurisdiction based strictly on diversity of citizenship, must abide by any state legal rule that would be outcome determinative if held in state court.
- *Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins*, 304 U.S. 64 (1938), sought to reorient the proper balance of judicial power between state and federal courts.
- No longer must courts inquire into whether the statute of limitations falls under procedural or substantive law.
- The inadequacies of this inquiry resulted in inequitable treatment of parties similarly situated.
- The outcome of their case was dependent on whether the party was in state or federal court.
- For this reason, when determining whether to apply federal or state law, **courts should consider the outcome of the litigation.**
- If applying federal law would result in a substantially different outcome, then the court should apply state law. Because in this particular case federal law would provide a remedy while New York law would bar York's suit, the court should apply state law.
- The court of appeals' decision is reversed and remanded for further proceedings consistent with this decision.

<!-- page: 21 -->

The federal law had no hard and fast limitations period which applied to this fact pattern, but state law did. Federal law, in absence of statute of limitations, used a system of equity rules which gave rise to judge-made rules, which said that if plaintiff waited "too late" (giving no precise number) to file his complaint, which left the defendant in a position of prejudice, the plaintiff could not continue with the lawsuit ("**latches**").

The court system in *York* was faced with the choice of applying the latches doctrine (meaning the case could continue under federal law), or applying the state limitations period (which would mean the case is dead). They decided to **apply state law**.

## Erie Test 1: *York* Outcome Determinative Test

**Looks at whether or not applying federal law would be "outcome determinative."**
- If **yes → use state law**
- But, this test caused a problem because anything could be made to be "outcome determinative."
  - This attempts go badly; but the following cases use this test:
    - ***Ragan*** — Same fact pattern as *Walker*. Court uses this "outcome determinative test" and applies state law.
    - ***Cohen*** — Decides under outcome determinative test. We will discuss more later in the semester.

**We get to *Hanna* and the court finally realizes that this test was no good. They look for another test.**
- *Hanna* tells us that the Outcome Determinative test was never supposed to be a talisman. We need to find another test.

## *Hanna v. Plumer* and *Walker v. Armco Steel* — Service of Process Background

### Starting a Lawsuit — Two Requirements

1. **Need to let the court know** → File a Complaint.
2. **Let the defendant know** → Summons.

These have to be provided to the defendant, which is called **service of process**. We have the federal rules (FRCP 4), which tells us how to do it.

**Two methods of service:**
- **"In hand"** = tag, hand complaint to defendant
  - Federal court: permissible but not required (see FRCP 4(e)(2)(A))
  - Some states (e.g., state court in *Hanna*): only way
- **Leave complaint at defendant's house**
  - Federal court: permissible, FRCP 4(e)(2)(B)
  - Some states: not acceptable

> Note: If a lawsuit is filed with the court, but the defendant is not served properly, the court can dismiss on its own, or a defendant can move to dismiss (even though to do so, the defendant had to have known about the lawsuit).

**Service of Process Enforcement:**
- Once we have these rules, we could make sure that they are enforced:
  - Court can monitor it. If we are missing an affidavit by plaintiff, court officer, or sheriff saying they served documents on the defendant, court can dismiss the case.
  - We could let the defendant object if he finds out the case has been filed and move to dismiss (but that assumes the defendant knows — the whole purpose of service of process is to inform the defendant).
  - Meantime, Statute of Limitations is ticking (time is of the essence):
    - If case is filed timely, it can proceed.
    - If time is tolling and complaint is not filed before the statute of limitations expires, case will not be able to be filed.
  - In order to have service of process, there needs to be a "tag": in hand; or send it to their home; or

<!-- page: 23 -->

    leave it at their home and give it to a responsible adult in the defendant's household.

**Statute of Limitations Timeline:**

```
Triggering event ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────►
                                                                    Plaintiff can file
                                                                    at any time after
Limitations Period ◄────────────────────────────────────────────── triggering event
                                                                    Once filed, must
                                                                    serve within a
                                                                    "grace period"

Limitations period expires  ◄── Grace period ──► Plaintiff files  ◄── (files timely)
                                                  Plaintiff serves

States and federal "grace period" lengths differ:
• Federal court: FRCP 4(m): 120 days
• OK (state at issue in Walker): 60 days
```

**After plaintiff files**, they have a time to complete service of process:
- If filed the day before SOL and serves process correctly within the allowed grace period, lawsuit can proceed.
  - If plaintiff does not serve process before the grace period expires, case will be dismissed.

**The Fun Part — *Hanna* vs. *Walker*:**
- Federal law and state law can differ as to the **method of service** (*Hanna*), and can differ as to the **grace period** (*Walker*).
  - All of this is connected to the same area — the **statute of limitations** period.
- When SCOTUS was faced with decisions on what law to use on method of service (*Hanna*), and subsequently whether to apply federal or state law with respect to the length of the grace period (*Walker*), it should have stayed consistent in which law it chose. But that did not happen.
  - In ***Hanna***, they went with **federal** rules.
  - In ***Walker***, they went with **state** rules.

## Erie Test 2: *Hanna* Majority Test

### The *Hanna* Majority Justification

**Because fed law is in "direct conflict" with state law, one needs to go with the federal rule.**
- But the battle of direct conflict does not actually matter. It is a distraction.
- Due to the **Supremacy Clause**:
  - If the law is **VALID; AND**
  - If the law is **APPLICABLE**
  - **THEN WE MUST USE FEDERAL LAW**

In *Hanna*, we have Fed. R. Civ. P. 4 (Service of Process):
- The federal law in this case is applicable.
- To measure its validity though, we need to see whether Congress had the power to enact it in the first place.
  - Did Congress have the power to create the apparatus that led to the adoption of the federal rules (28 U.S.C. § 2071)?
    - **Constitutional power:** Congress was given power by the Constitution to create lower federal courts and have a say in how they operate. Augmented by the necessary and proper clause.
    - Congress was limited in its power to create a rule that abridges, enlarges and modifies a substantive right.
      - We need to ask ourselves whether the rule in this case "abridged, enlarged, or modified" any substantive right.
        - If "yes" to any of the three, law in question would be **invalid**.
  - Courts would be reluctant to say that a rule is invalid.
    - The law therefore is **always VALID**.
    - SCOTUS proposes them to Congress through advisory committee. **This has NEVER happened.** Anytime there has been this question, they have always come back saying this rule is valid, and that it does not abridge, enlarge, or modify substantive rights.
      - However, if you ever feel like there is some type of abridgement, maybe the court would **narrowly interpret** the rule to make the rule **INAPPLICABLE** in this case (*Walker*).

### *Walker v. Armco Steel Corp.* — Grace Period

*Walker* was focused on determining which grace period applied:
- Federal rule had a **120-day** grace period.
- State rule had a **60-day** grace period.

<!-- page: 25 -->

- In this case, service of process was effectuated after 60 days but before the 120-day period.
- The SCOTUS says the rule about the grace period was supposed to target other rules in civil procedure, such as Rule 12, but **not** the grace period with respect to the statute of limitations.
  - SCOTUS must have had a really good reason to adopt an interpretation that narrow: The court says that they are **reluctant to overrule *Ragan***.
    - It was reluctant to overrule that prior decision (*Ragan*, which said that the grace period did not apply to the statute of limitations period, but was decided by using the *York* "outcome determinative test").
    - Court did not want to abandon the precedent, even though it was willing to abandon the test.
    - By this time, Justice Harlan had left the court, and the rest of the court was unwilling to overrule *Ragan* and instead **narrowly interpreted** the grace period rule.
      - Not willing to say it's invalid; instead,
      - Narrowly interprets and says that the law is **inapplicable** in this case.
- However, behind *Erie*, they could justify their result in *Walker* under the **federalism** rationale.
  - They figured that there might be future conflicts between state law and federal law, and that there might be circumstances more important than a mere "service of process." So, SCOTUS felt that there may be reasons to interpret other federal laws narrowly in order to allow state law the ability to operate.

## Summary: Erie Test 2 (*Hanna* Majority) and *Walker*

**The right test for an Erie question, for at least federal rules of civil procedure:**
- If the law is **VALID; AND**
  - Validity is measured by the Rules Enabling Act Provision (28 U.S.C. § 2071); and we **always** conclude that the rule is VALID.
    - But, we may channel the rules of invalidity into applicability and **narrowly interpret** the federal law to make it inapplicable (Court did not do this in *Hanna*, but did in *Walker* — overturning precedent or federalism may be good reasons).
- If the law is **APPLICABLE**
- **THEN WE MUST USE FEDERAL LAW**
