## Day 3 — Subject Matter Jurisdiction, Part 2

- Diversity Jurisdiction
- Begin Supplemental Jurisdiction
- Removal Jurisdiction

**Diversity Jurisdiction** has nothing to do with the subject matter of the litigation, but it has everything to do with the subject matter of the parties, specifically that the parties must be "citizens" of different states. Keep in mind what "citizenship" means for the purposes of Diversity Jurisdiction, and the way to establish domicile for humans, as opposed to for corporations and other unincorporated entities.

**Supplemental Jurisdiction:** The idea is that there are multiple theories of relief (a claim, or cause of action) that a plaintiff wants to assert against a defendant in a single lawsuit in federal court. There are certain theories of relief that have an independent basis of subject matter jurisdiction (whether through arising under (28 U.S.C. § 1331), or through diversity jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. § 1332)). However, there are theories of relief, or claims, that do not fall within either of those two independent avenues to obtaining federal subject matter jurisdiction. The plaintiffs nevertheless may stack these claims, which do not on their own have an independent basis of subject matter jurisdiction (referred to as the **"would be supplemental claim(s)"**), on to the lawsuit that has the claim an independent basis of federal subject matter jurisdiction (referred to as the **"anchoring claim(s)"**).

### Background Cases, Statutes, and References

#### Cases

- ***United Mine Workers of America v. Gibbs*, 383 U.S. 715 (1966) (SCOTUS) — Pendent Jurisdiction (now known as Supplemental Jurisdiction, since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367)**

  A state claim may proceed in federal court when it accompanies a federal claim that arises from a **common nucleus of operative facts**. (See Lecture Notes below)

  *Gibbs* situation: claim is against same parties AND we have an anchoring claim (arising under situation)

- ***Owen Equipment & Erection Co. v. Kroger*, 437 U.S. 365 (1978) (SCOTUS) — Supplemental Jurisdiction (since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367)**

  In an action in which federal jurisdiction is based on diversity, the court may not exercise jurisdiction over the plaintiff's claim against a third-party defendant if there is no independent basis for federal jurisdiction over that claim. (See Lecture notes below)

<!-- page: 2 -->

  *Kroger* situation: claim is against different parties and NO anchoring claim (diversity situation)

- ***Aldinger v. Howard*, 427 U.S. 1 (1976) (SCOTUS) — Supplemental Jurisdiction (since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367)**

  Federal supplemental jurisdiction does not necessarily extend to claims against parties over whom a federal court would not otherwise have jurisdiction.

  \*Where *Gibbs* focused on determining whether the federal court had "arising under" federal subject matter jurisdiction over the "cause of action," *Aldinger* focuses on whether the federal court has subject matter jurisdiction over an "**additional (nondiverse) party**."

- ***Finley v. United States*, 490 U.S. 545 (1989) (SCOTUS) — Pendent Jurisdiction (now known as Supplemental Jurisdiction, since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367)**

  A federal court may not assert pendent-party jurisdiction under the Federal Tort Claims Act with respect to additional parties over whom the court does not have independent jurisdiction.

  *— 28 U.S.C. § 1367 is later enacted in 1990 —*

- ***Guaranteed Systems, Inc. v. American National Can Company*, 842 F. Supp. 855 (M.D.N.C. 1994)**

  If a federal court's jurisdiction is founded on diversity of citizenship, 28 U.S.C. § 1367 prohibits the court from exercising supplemental jurisdiction over a claim by the plaintiff against a nondiverse third party impleaded under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 14.

- ***Shamrock Oil & Gas Corp v. Sheets*, 313 U.S. 100 (1941)**

  Plaintiff could not remove a state court action into federal court even if defendant interposed a federal (arising under) counterclaim.

### 2. Diversity Jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. § 1332)

**Constitutional and Statutory Bases diagram:** Concentric ovals — outer (orange) = U.S. Const. Art. 3, sec. 2, cl. 1 / "any federal issue"; middle-left (green) = "arising under," sec. 1331 / well-pleaded complaint; middle-right (blue) = "diversity," sec. 1332, with "minimal diversity" at constitutional level and "complete diversity" at statutory level; inner (solid green) = Exclusive federal court jurisdiction. All exterior regions = State courts only.

- Most state law causes of action can be brought to federal court if they have "complete Diversity."
  - Although in some cases there may be both, in order to be in federal court, we only need one.

- **Basic Semantic Terminology:**
  - Has nothing to do with where the court is located; only the parties on each side of the lawsuits.
  - **Minimal Diversity:** (Some states on both sides of the lawsuit) — this case would have had to be dismissed for lack of diversity.

    ```
    P1 (SC) ──────────────────→ D1 (VA)
    P2 (SC) ─────────────────╲→ D2 (SC)
    ```
    *(P2 and D2 share SC — minimal diversity only, insufficient for § 1332)*

  - **Complete Diversity:** (No state(s) on both sides of the lawsuit)
    - (*Strawbridge v. Curtiss*)
    - The US Constitution requires minimal diversity only, and Congress previously passed a statute requiring only minimal diversity.

    ```
    P1 (SC) ──────────────────→ D1 (VA)
    P2 (SC) ─────────────────╲→ D2 (NC)
    ```

<!-- page: 8 -->

  - The Diversity statute (28 U.S.C. § 1332) has two main requirements:
    1. Amount in controversy (AIC): $75,000; and
    2. Complete diversity (as interpreted in *Strawbridge*)

- One should assume the AIC is whatever the plaintiff says, unless it's obviously not right.

- To find "**citizenship**" for purposes of Diversity Jurisdiction:
  - **Corporations** (can be citizen of multiple states — makes it harder to get complete diversity):
    - Citizens of the state where they are incorporated; AND
    - The state where their Principal Place of Business is (where corporate decisions are made)
  - **Incorporeal entities that are not corporations** (business partnerships, labor unions, charities):
    - Citizen of every state in which their members are (for international unions, all 50 states)
    - If from "outside of the United States" on both sides, there is no complete diversity.
  - **Human beings** (see Professor Greiner's Life assignment):
    - State in which they are "**domiciled**" (see *Mas v. Perry* for CHANGE of domicile)

### Stacking (Amount in Controversy)

*(28 U.S.C. § 1332(a): "matter in controversy exceeds the sum or value of $75,000")*

Provided that a claim otherwise meets the requirements of complete diversity (*Strawbridge*):

**IF** there are more than one claim that can be filed by the **same plaintiff** against the **same defendant**: **THEN**
- the AIC for each separate individual claim can be less than $75K, so long as when the AIC of all the relevant claims is added together, the aggregate amount is over $75K. **IF SO**, then it is okay to **"stack"** the claims so that the AIC required is met.

**If same plaintiff files multiple claims against the same defendant, AIC CAN stack.**

**If same plaintiff files multiple claims against different defendants, AIC CANNOT stack, at least not via the STACKING RULE.** *(You may be able to do it under Supplemental Jurisdiction though; so, if this is the case on an assignment or exam, conduct both analyses before moving on.)*

### Determining Domicile

*(Summarizing what was discussed in detail in Class 2 — See Notes)*

- **Human beings** (see Professor Greiner's Life assignment):
  - State in which they are "**domiciled**" (see *Mas v. Perry* for CHANGE of domicile)

- **Domicile** for humans depends on the **intention to stay** in a specific state:
  - No **DEFINITE** leaving time
  - It does not matter the term of years
  - The test does not depend on "intention of *returning*" to a state"; instead, it depends on "no intention or definite time of *leaving* a state"

  - Analysis involves a **two-part test** (BOTH parts required to establish domicile):
    - **Part 1:** What location are you living in (not where your apartment is, or where you work, but where you actually live, even if physically not there much or even most of the time, or even at any time at all); **AND**

<!-- page: 10 -->

    - **Part 2:** What state is it for which you **intend to stay** and **you don't have a definite leaving point**

- When the statute was made, people were not able to move around as much as today.
- Today, people move around a lot, and sometimes do not need to if they can just use the internet.
- The rule is that you keep your previous domicile until you establish a new one — **"you are where you are until you get where you are going."**
- The only way to provide evidence of "intention" is by other extrinsic details.

## Supplemental Jurisdiction (Requires at Least One Anchoring Claim)

In a single lawsuit, multiple theories of relief claims are either against the same party(ies) or against different party(ies). Some theories of relief have independent basis of SMJ, and some do not. If a theory of relief does not have an independent basis of SMJ, it has to be dismissed.

- **Vocabulary: Do NOT use "ancillary" or "pendent" jurisdiction from now on. Only use "SUPPLEMENTAL JURISDICTION."**

- **WHEN USING SUPPLEMENTAL JURISDICTION, YOU WILL NEED TO PAY ATTENTION TO WHAT TYPE OF INDEPENDENT BASIS OF JURISDICTION YOU ARE USING (i.e., Arising Under or Diversity — as they each carry on different rules and consequences)**

**"What Do We Do With This?" Hypothetical** *(same diagram as above, p. 8)*

```
                    state law, > $75K
P (State A) ──────────────────────────→ Def. corp. 1 (State B, State B)
            ╲
              ╲  state law, > $75K
               ╲──────────────────────→ Def. corp. 2 (State B, State A)
```

**We have to determine whether there is independent SMJ when there are several claims.**
- We need to look at each arrow, and then look at all arrows and see what effect they have on one another.

- At least one state law claim has independent basis of SMJ (anchoring claim under "diversity" statute 28 U.S.C. § 1332):
  - Differing domiciliary states between plaintiffs and defendants (*Strawbridge*); AND

<!-- page: 11 -->

  - Amount in Controversy is over $75K

- Other state law claims do not have to have independent basis of SMJ.
  - However, they HAVE TO be state claims because if they were federal claims, they'd have independent basis of SMJ under the "arising under" statute (28 U.S.C. § 1331).

- BUT, unless the **anchoring claim** has "arising under" SMJ, it **must** comply with the **complete diversity** requirement rule (*Strawbridge*).

*(After* Finley*, Congress enacts 28 U.S.C. § 1367 (1990) — Supplemental Jurisdiction Statute)*

**Constitutional and Statutory Bases diagram (updated — now includes supplemental):** Same concentric ovals as before (outer orange = Art. III; left green = "arising under" / well-pleaded complaint; right blue = "diversity" / complete diversity; inner green = exclusive federal jurisdiction), with a new **"supplemental," sec. 1367** oval at the bottom, overlapping both the arising-under and diversity ovals, extending to the limits of Art. III.

**28 U.S.C. § 1367(a) — "We Have Power" (Congress' attempt to overturn *Finley*):**

- We still **need** an **anchoring claim**.
  - **If you can find an "arising under" anchoring claim, USE THAT ONE** (because the statute allows supplemental jurisdiction over all other claims that are so related that they are part of the "same case or controversy" under Article III of the CONSTITUTION — Congress' way to explicitly order the courts to interpret this statute broadly, to the extent of the Constitution, as opposed to what the courts were doing prior to this statute in *Finley*).
- "That form a part of the same case or controversy" — Congress wanted to grant the power to the extent of the limits of the Constitution and for the Supreme Court to stop interpreting the statutes enacted narrowly.
  - "If you have an anchoring claim, and you have supplemental claims under that anchoring claim, go to the limits of the Constitution."
- Additional parties are okay now.
  - Statutory language therefore overrules *Aldinger* and *Finley*.

<!-- page: 12 -->

**28 U.S.C. § 1367(b) — Congress' Attempt to Overturn *Aldinger*:**

- Focuses on **diversity jurisdiction**:
  - Requires Complete diversity

  **Hypothetical:**

  ```
                      state law, > $75K
  P (State A) ──────────────────────────→ Def. corp. 1 (State B, State B)
              ╲
                ╲  state law, > $75K
                 ╲──────────────────────→ Def. corp. 2 (State B, State A)
  ```

  ; AND
  - AIC ($75K)

<!-- page: 13 -->

- Requires claims by plaintiffs against persons made parties under certain other rules of civil procedure (see statute for list), but **excludes Rule 23** *(which is for class actions — will discuss later in* Exxon *and* Shady Grove*)* (does not apply to impleader by defendants, or crossclaims by defendants — this section ONLY focuses on claims by plaintiffs), when exercising jurisdiction over such claims would be arising of a "**common nucleus of operative facts**"
  - Applicable **ONLY** to claims with independent basis of SMJ under 28 U.S.C. § 1332 (Diversity-only claim)
    - **EXCEPT IF** adding a new claim which would be inconsistent under 28 U.S.C. § 1332 (break diversity) (thus preserving *Owen*)

> **NOTE: Analysis has to be done for BOTH § 1367(a) AND § 1367(b), and qualify under both statutes, in order for one to be able to exercise Supplemental Jurisdiction — EVEN WHEN both Sections 1367(a) and 1367(b) both seem to allow for Supplemental Jurisdiction, the court has discretion to decline such supplemental jurisdiction under § 1367(c).**

**28 U.S.C. § 1367(c) — "We Have Discretion":**

> (c) The district courts **may decline** to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over a claim under subsection (a) if—
> > (1) the claim raises a novel or complex issue of State law,
> > (2) the claim substantially predominates over the claim or claims over which the district court has original jurisdiction,
> > (3) the district court has dismissed all claims over which it has original jurisdiction, or
> > (4) in exceptional circumstances, there are other compelling reasons for declining jurisdiction.

District courts may **decline** to exercise supplemental jurisdiction under subsection (a) above if:
- The claim raises a new or complex issue of state law;
- The claim substantially predominates over the claim(s) over which the district court has original jurisdiction;
- The district court has dismissed all claims over which it has original jurisdiction; or
- In exceptional circumstances, there are other compelling reasons for declining jurisdiction.

<!-- page: 14 -->

*Gibbs* was similar, but included a slightly longer list of rules than the Supplemental Jurisdiction statute. Sometimes courts follow *Gibbs*, and some consider the statute. This is somewhat of a circuit split that the SCOTUS has not decided yet — no need to focus on that for purposes of this class.

> **NOTE:** Nothing in the Supplemental Jurisdiction Statute (§ 1367) allows the court to decline to exercise jurisdiction over the claims which have an independent basis of subject matter jurisdiction (either through Arising Under (§ 1331) or Diversity Jurisdiction (§ 1332)). It's only allowing discretion to decline to exercise jurisdiction over the claims that would be adjudicated under Supplemental Jurisdiction, AND ONLY IF one of the factors listed in § 1367(c) are present.

> **NOTE:** In order for A CLAIM to live in federal court, you will need to analyze each THEORY OF RELIEF of such claim under these steps above. The ones that qualify under the rules above can stay in federal court, but the theories of relief that do not qualify will need to be adjudicated by state court.

## Four Cases Illustrating the History of 28 U.S.C. § 1367

### 1. *United Mine Workers of America v. Gibbs*, 383 U.S. 715 (1966) (SCOTUS) — Supplemental Jurisdiction as Applied to "Arising Under" Claims (replaced in 1990 by 28 U.S.C. § 1367)

**Rule:** A state claim may proceed in federal court when it accompanies a federal claim that arises from a **common nucleus of operative facts**.

In other words, a state-law cause of action is justiciable in federal court alongside a federal claim if one would expect that the two claims be tried in one judicial proceeding.

If the court dismisses all the federal claims (anchoring claims), it also loses jurisdiction over the state claims. A lawsuit based on "arising under" jurisdiction that is left with only state claims may not proceed in federal court. (This rule changes later in 28 U.S.C. § 1367(c), when Congress allows for discretion.)

<!-- page: 15 -->

**Gibbs party diagram:**

```
                    Labor Management Relations Act (federal law)
Mine supervisor ──────────────────────────────────────────→ International union
                    Tortious Interference with contractual
                    relations (state law)
```

**Gibbs Analysis (Power + Discretion approach):**

*Power:* anchoring claim & would-be supplemental claim come from same transaction or occurrence

*Discretion:*
- Predominance of federal vs. state law
- Need for definitive state law interpretation
- Extent of case progress when anchoring claim dismissed
- Possibility of jury confusion
- Speculation regarding congressional intent

- Let's say in this case, the International Union has its PPB somewhere outside of Tennessee.
- Mine supervisor is domiciled in Tennessee.
  - Issue: Because it is an International Union, it is a citizen of every state; therefore, suing him would necessarily violate the *Strawbridge* rule of complete diversity.
- Labor Management Relations Act then creates a federal claim that qualifies under "arising under" jurisdiction.
- For the tortious claim (state law), there is no independent basis of SMJ.
- However, SCOTUS adjudicates both claims anyway (the anchoring federal law claim and the supplementing state claim).

**Gibbs Analysis steps:**

- **Step 1:** Find out whether we have power to exercise SMJ over the would-be supplemental claim — try all of the claims together and would it be efficient to do it that way?
- **Step 2:** Should we exercise the discretion?
  - Predominance of federal v. state claim (no great precision on how the court defined "predominant," but can ask where are the major issues or what are the most contested issues)
    - Federal claim should be predominant over the state law claim.
    - If state law is the predominant claim, then federal court must dismiss the state law claim and that would have to be filed in state court.
  - Last, if a lawsuit is predominantly based on state claims, dismissing the suit without prejudice may be advisable, so that the plaintiff can re-file in state court (remember there is no other way for the federal court to send the case back to state court).

*Gibbs* situation: claim is against same parties AND we have an anchoring claim (arising under situation).

### 2. *Owen Equipment & Erection Co. v. Kroger*, 437 U.S. 365 (1978) (SCOTUS) — Supplemental Jurisdiction as Applied under Diversity Jurisdiction (replaced in 1990 by 28 U.S.C. § 1367)

*Kroger* situation: claim is against different parties and NO anchoring claim (diversity situation).

**Owen v. Kroger party diagram:**

```
                        negligence = state law, > $75K
Dead guy (IA) ──────────────────────────────────────→ Power company (NE, NE)
              ╲
                ╲  negligence = state law, > $75K         ↓ impleader
                 ╲────────────────────────────────→ Crane operator (NE, NE → NE, IA)
```
*(impleader = "if I have to pay, you should, because it was your fault")*

Kroger (individual, citizen of Iowa) brought a Diversity (28 U.S.C. § 1332) (negligence = state law) cause of action against Omaha Public Power District (OPPD) (corporation, PPB in Nebraska, citizen of Nebraska), for the wrongful death of her husband, who was electrocuted at work while working when the beam of a steel crane came too close to a high tension electric line next to where he was walking.

OPPD, claiming that it was the fault of Owen Equipment & Erection Co., the owner-operator of the crane, filed a third-party claim against Owen.

Because Owen (as an additional defendant) was brought to the suit by an impleader from OPPD (the original defendant), as opposed to the plaintiff, there did not need to be an independent basis of jurisdiction in order to add Owen to the lawsuit. The plaintiff later amended her complaint to add Owen as an additional defendant, alleging that it was a Nebraska corporation with a PPB in Nebraska.

However, once Owen became an additional defendant, OPPD asked, and the court granted, summary judgment in their favor, so they were able to leave the lawsuit. This left Owen as the only defendant remaining.

During the trial, it was discovered that Owen was a Nebraska corporation, but it actually had its PPB located in Iowa, which is the same as the plaintiff, thus violating the complete diversity rule (*Strawbridge*). (Remember that corporations can be a citizen of more than one state.)

<!-- page: 17 -->

Court reiterated the requirement for complete diversity and dismissed this case due to lack of subject matter jurisdiction (no arising under claim, and no diversity between the plaintiff and the now sole defendant).

### 3. *Aldinger v. Howard*, 427 U.S. 1 (1976) (SCOTUS) — Supplemental Jurisdiction (since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367)

Federal supplemental jurisdiction does not necessarily extend to claims against parties over whom a federal court would not otherwise have jurisdiction.

**Aldinger party diagram:**

```
                         sec. 1983 = federal law
County employee (WA) ──────────────────────────→ Supervisor-individual (WA)
                    ╲
                      ╲  state tort claim (vicarious liability)
                       ╲──────────────────────────────────────→ County (WA)
```

\*Where *Gibbs* focused on determining whether the federal court had "arising under" federal subject matter jurisdiction over the "cause of action," *Aldinger* focuses on whether the federal court has subject matter jurisdiction over an "**additional (nondiverse) party**."

A Washington State citizen (Aldinger) brought a federal civil rights action under **42 U.S.C. § 1983** against several state officials under "arising under" jurisdiction (**28 U.S.C. § 1343(3)**). *Gibbs* requires an "arising under" anchoring claim.

She later wanted to add Spokane County as an additional defendant, but by statute at that time, Counties were not liable under 28 U.S.C. § 1343(3) (because the County was not defined as a "person" at the time, although it would have been easy for Congress to include that as part of the statute had they wanted to).

So, in this case, SCOTUS decided that even though this new claim shared a "**common nucleus of operative facts**" (and thus satisfied *Gibbs*), supplemental jurisdiction could not be exercised because it **added an additional party over which a claim would not have been able to be brought independently to federal court even if using the same operative facts**.

The distinguishing facts: The propriety of exercising pendent jurisdiction is questionable when plaintiffs seek to join not only issues but **parties over whom a federal court would otherwise have no independent basis of federal subject matter jurisdiction**. In such situations, parties should be joined only if:

<!-- page: 18 -->

> (1) the case falls within the federal court's constitutional jurisdiction, and
> (2) the congressional statute conferring subject-matter jurisdiction on the federal court does not explicitly or implicitly remove the party in question from that jurisdiction (in this case, the County).

This is the opposite reasoning used in *Gibbs*.
- *Aldinger* is an arising under situation and the only difference is that there is an additional party added.
- In *Aldinger*, we end up seeing the court trying to take a case-by-case adjudication approach.
- The court here interprets the Congressional silence in the statute as an affirmative command not to exercise jurisdiction, whereas in *Gibbs* it determined that it *could* interpret the law.

### 4. *Finley v. United States*, 490 U.S. 545 (1989) (SCOTUS) — Supplemental Jurisdiction (since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367)

A federal court may not assert supplemental-party jurisdiction under the Federal Tort Claims Act (**federal cause of action — and, happens to be an exclusive jurisdiction statute**) with respect to additional parties over whom the court does not have independent jurisdiction.

**Finley party diagram:**

```
                    Federal Tort Claims Act = federal law
Dead guy, 2        ──── exclusive federal jurisdiction ────→ FAA (??)
dead kids (CA)  ╲
                  ╲  negligence = state law, > $75K
                   ╲──────────────────────────────────────→ City of San Diego (CA, CA)
                 ╲
                   ╲  negligence = state law, ≤ $75K
                    ╲─────────────────────────────────────→ Utility co. (CA, CA)
```

This case is different from *Gibbs*:
- *Gibbs* had a federal law cause of action which added a state law claim which shared a "common nucleus of operative facts";
- Here, there is a federal law cause of action, instead, the plaintiff was trying to bring a **state claim** against a **party that was not named in any claim that had independent basis of federal subject matter jurisdiction** (called "supplemental-party" jurisdiction).

There is no diversity subject matter jurisdiction in this situation over the state-law claims (we are left with just an anchoring claim under the Federal Torts Claim Act).

<!-- page: 19 -->

The additional parties being added here were not named in an anchoring claim, or a claim that would on its own have a basis for independent federal subject matter jurisdiction.

Court (J. Scalia):

> No parties allowed to be added **ever**.
> Not allowed in *Kroger*. (No independent diversity jurisdiction over additional party)
> Not allowed in *Aldinger*. (No independent subject matter jurisdiction specifically over the additional claim, either through diversity, or through arising under jurisdiction)
> *Finley* involves not only an "arising under" claim, but specifically involves one that requires **EXCLUSIVE** federal jurisdiction (Federal Tort Claims Act).

## Removal

**Court hierarchy diagram** *(same as Video 1 in prior classes):*

```
                Supreme Court of the United States
               /                                  \
   State High Court                    United States Court of Appeals
          |                                 for the _____ Circuit
State Intermediate                                  |
Appellate Court                                     |
          |                               United States District Court
State Trial Court System                    for the _______ of _______
  [Family | Criminal | Housing]
```

We discussed that most of the claims can begin in the state court system.
- We asked ourselves which ones can begin in the federal law system.
- We also discussed that the **plaintiff** can choose a forum of where to file a claim.

We now ask under what circumstances can a **defendant** choose to "**remove**" a lawsuit that is filed in state court back to the federal court.

Technical rules: Claim is filed in state court. Defendant may not know that there even have been any complaints filed. (For this purpose, we are going to assume that the defendants have been properly tagged.)

If **defendant** wants to **remove** (**plaintiffs cannot remove** since they chose the initial filing forum) the case, they file a petition for removal in the **federal court**, attaching a copy of the complaint and any proceedings thus far in state court.

Federal court will send the message to the state court that the case has been removed to the federal court system and the state court then has to stop litigating it.

<!-- page: 20 -->

(**\*\*\*NOTE** that Removal (send the case to federal court from state court) then is **automatic**)

**Rule: Basically a defendant can remove to federal court anytime that the plaintiff could have filed there originally.**

- We make this automatic to ensure efficiency.
- We do not want the state judges to exercise discretion on whether federal courts should adjudicate a case.
- We want the federal courts to take the case without discretion of the state courts and decide later whether they want to keep it or remand it.

We then are left with a series of bases upon which the federal courts should or could **Remand** (send the case back to state court):
- If they lack federal subject matter jurisdiction (no time limits for this); or if
- A party (both plaintiff and defendant) files a motion to **remand** (only defendant can remove, but both can file to remand).

**Rules for Remand:**
- It must be filed within **30 days of removal** (or gets denied automatically, unless the grounds is lack of SMJ).
- Remand would get granted under 3 additional circumstances:
  1. The removal was **too late** (30 days after the **removable event** — an event that renders the lawsuit subject to the federal court subject matter jurisdiction) (**note that in certain circumstances, the filing of the lawsuit can be THE removable event**)
     - Trying to place a stricter burden on the defendant to incentivize speedy process, rather than merely fighting about where we are going to fight.
  2. If the party filing the motion for remand points out that this is a **diversity-only case** (meaning only meeting the requirements under 28 U.S.C. § 1332 and not 28 U.S.C. § 1331), and **any of the defendants is a citizen of the state in which the lawsuit was filed** (again, to prevent **homecooking**); or
  3. A party filing a motion for remand can point out that there are **multiple defendants** in the case, and **not all defendants signed the removal petition**.

- **NOTE:** In **diversity-only cases**, if case has been stagnant in state court for over a year, then a petition for **remand** will be granted.

## *Guaranteed Systems, Inc. v. American National Can Company*, 842 F. Supp. 855 (M.D.N.C. 1994)

If a federal court's jurisdiction is founded on diversity of citizenship, 28 U.S.C. § 1367 prohibits the court from exercising supplemental jurisdiction over a claim by the plaintiff against a **nondiverse third party** impleaded under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 14.

**Guaranteed System party diagram:**

```
General contractor   Breach of contract = state law, > $75K
(NC, NC)          ──────────────────────────────────────────→ Buyer (DE, DE)

[Buyer removes to NC federal court]

General contractor   Breach of contract = state law, > $75
(NC, NC)          ──────────────────────────────────────────→ Buyer (DE, DE)
                  ╲  Breach of contract = state law, > $75K
                   ╲
                    ↓ Impleader (= if I have to pay, you have to pay because it's your fault)
                   Subcontractor (NC, NC)
```

Guaranteed (plaintiff), an NC corporation, sued National Can (defendant), a DE corporation, in a NC **state court**, on account of National Can's failure to pay Guaranteed for construction work performed (**contract claim — state law**). National Can "**removed**" the case to **federal court** on the basis of "**diversity**," then counterclaimed against Guaranteed for negligence (**tort counter-claim — state law**) in its performance of the work.

Pursuant to Rule 14(b) of the FRCP (**Impleader**), Guaranteed (general contractor, plaintiff) impleaded HydroVac, a NC entity (subcontractor — would be additional defendant), alleging claims for indemnity and contribution in the event that National Can prevailed in its counterclaim against Guaranteed (**tort counter-claim — state law**). HydroVac moved to dismiss the **third-party complaint (Impleader)** on grounds that the court lacked **diversity** subject matter jurisdiction over HydroVac (because it violates **complete diversity — *Strawbridge***).

The third-party action in this case corresponds most closely to the typical ancillary impleader claim the Supreme Court used as a benchmark to distinguish the claim the *Owen* plaintiff attempted to assert. In *Owen*, the Supreme Court noted that "in determining whether jurisdiction over a nonfederal claim exists, the context in which the nonfederal claim is asserted is crucial." The Court went on to say that the claim at issue in that case was "simply not ancillary to the federal one in the same sense that, for example, the impleader by a defendant of a third-party defendant always is because an impleader claim has 'logical dependence' on the original claim; it is not a 'new and independent' claim."

Court granted the third-party defendant's motion to dismiss the third-party action.

<!-- page: 22 -->

**28 U.S.C. § 1367 Statutory Analysis for *Guaranteed Systems*:**

1. Do we have an anchoring claim **(28 U.S.C. § 1367(a))**? *(In this case, yes)*
2. Do we have a claim that is so related that it forms a part of the same controversy **(28 U.S.C. § 1367(a))**? *(Here, yes)*
3. Is it okay that the new claim involves an additional party **(28 U.S.C. § 1367(a))**? *(Here, yes)*

**ONLY IF ALL these requirements are met, THEN we move on to 28 U.S.C. § 1367(b):**

4. Was this a claim by the plaintiff **(28 U.S.C. § 1367(b))**? *Yes.*
5. Would exercising jurisdiction be **inconsistent with the requirements of § 1332** **(28 U.S.C. § 1367(b))**? *Unfortunately, in this case, yes — breaks complete diversity.*
