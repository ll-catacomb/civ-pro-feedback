## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***Harris v. Balk*, 198 U.S. 215 (1905) (SCOTUS)**

  A judgment of garnishment or attachment against a debt that is handed down in one state is entitled to full faith and credit when the parties to the debt are residents of another state. "This obligation can be enforced by the courts of the foreign state after personal service of process therein, just as well by the courts of the domicile of the debtor."

- ***Kulko v. Superior Court*, 436 U.S. 84 (1978) (SCOTUS)**

  The due process clause of the Fourteenth Amendment limits the power of a state court to assert personal jurisdiction over a non-resident defendant. Foreseeability in a due process analysis refers to whether the defendant's contacts with the forum state are such that **he should reasonably expect being subject to the jurisdiction of the state's courts**.

- ***Schaffer v. Heitner*, 433 U.S. 186 (SCOTUS) (1977)**

  Quasi in rem jurisdiction may only be asserted when the interests of the persons in the property seized have sufficient contacts, ties, or relations to the state. In other words, "**minimum contacts**" are the **minimum constitutional threshold that due process requires** for **any type** of personal jurisdiction. ***Therefore, all "in personam," "in rem," and "quasi in rem" jurisdictions must all satisfy minimum contacts.***

- ***Burnham v. Superior Court*, 495 U.S. 604 (SCOTUS) (1990)**

  A non-resident is properly served if he is physically present in the forum state, and the forum state may exercise personal jurisdiction over him without violating due process.

- ***Grace v. McArthur*, 170 F. Supp. 442 (E.D. Ark.) (1959)**

  A person moving in interstate commerce is not on that account alone exempt from service of process while in transit.

<!-- page: 2 -->

- ***Griffis v. Luban*, 646 N.W.2d 526 (Minn. 2002)**

  In order for the court to exert PJ over the defendant over an act committed over the internet, the Plaintiff must point to specific activity indicating that the defendant **expressly aimed its tortious conduct at the forum**.

### Bases for in Personam Jurisdiction

| **General** | **Specific** |
|---|---|
| No relationship between forum and subject matter of lawsuit needed | Requires: (1) Long-arm statute, **plus/and** (2) Permissible under the 14th Amendment's due process clause |
| **"Really at home":** State(s) of defendant citizenship; for corp. and humans, use diversity rules. Does not require long-arm statute, **OR** | Minimal contacts linking defendant, subject matter of litigation, forum, **plus/and** |
| ***Daimler*, "At home":** Long-arm statute, **plus/and** substantial, continuous, and systematic contacts (>50% of business), **OR** | Fair and reasonable — consider: extent of defendant's contacts; defendant convenience; plaintiff's interest/convenience; forum's interest; interest of judicial system (witnesses, evidence); choice of law |
| **Consent:** Failure to object (quickly!), **OR** advanced consent (forum selection clause) | |
| **Tag?** Not sure (see *Burnham*) | |

> **\*\*Note:** If it is a federal court, you will need BOTH Subject Matter Jurisdiction (either through "arising under" (28 U.S.C. § 1331) or "diversity" (28 U.S.C. § 1332)), **AND** one of the three bases of Personal Jurisdiction.

As long as you can come up with one of the three ways listed, a court would have personal jurisdiction over a defendant.

---

### General Jurisdiction (*International Shoe*) — Need One of the Following Three Choices

*(No relationship between the state (forum) and subject matter of the litigation — only a sufficiently strong relationship between defendant and forum)*

**1. "Truly home" court**
- No need for long-arm statute
  - For a human — state of domicile
  - For a corporation — state of incorporation, PPB
  - For an incorporated association — PPB

**2. "Essentially at home" (***Daimler***)**
- Need a long-arm statute (because we have an out-of-state defendant who is not domiciled in that state, no SMJ citizenship, etc.)
- Substantial, continuous, systematic contacts
  - Under *Daimler*, contacts that are pretty overwhelming with respect to the particular defendant that have occurred in the particular state
    - For business — location of place where large fraction (>50%) of business is conducted
  - For a person — spending a substantial amount of time in that state (e.g., living in Cambridge while attending school)

<!-- page: 4 -->

**Three different types of state long-arm statutes:**
1. One which **specifies grounds** in which a court can exercise jurisdiction upon an out-of-state defendant;
2. One which allows the state to go to the **limits of the Constitution** and essentially allows the state to interpret the constitutional analysis; and
3. One which is a **combination of the two**, which includes specific grounds as well as allows the state to go to the limits of the Constitution.

**Federal long-arm statute:**
- Federal courts can piggy-back under the state long-arm statute by using Fed. R. Civ. P. 4(k)(1)(A) — the only federal long-arm statute studied in this class.
- By this statute, the federal court (by choice) subjects itself to the limits of the specific state's long-arm statutes and the specific state's constitutional analysis.
  - So basically a federal court can exercise personal jurisdiction over a dispute sitting in the same state.

**3. Consent (by defendant)**
- Failure to object quickly; OR
- Advanced consent through a forum selection clause
- Neither of these require a long-arm statute since they require the defendant's consent.

---

### Specific Jurisdiction

1. **Long-arm statute required** from the forum state.
   - If state court: see which type of long-arm statute it is; make sure it covers this type of litigation.
   - If federal court: find the state in which you are sitting and do the same thing.

2. **Permissible under the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause**, which requires two things simultaneously:

<!-- page: 5 -->

   **i. Minimal Contacts** — linking subject matter of the litigation, defendant, and the forum; **AND**

   **ii. Fair and Reasonable** — when considering the following:
   1. Extent of defendant's contacts
   2. Defendant convenience
   3. Plaintiff's interest/convenience
   4. Forum's interest
   5. Interest of judicial system, including location of witnesses, evidence
   6. Choice of law (depending on whether it is a first or second restatement case)

**Minimal contacts requirements were met in:**
- ***International Shoe*** — company had purposely availed itself in its ability to do business in Washington
- ***McGee*** — even though there was only one contract, the litigation was about that contract, so it was enough to create the requisite minimal contact

**They were absent in:**
- ***Denckla*** — unilateral action of a third party was not sufficient to create the requisite minimal contacts
- ***Nicastro*** (sold products through a corporate distributor) & ***WW VW*** (plaintiff drove car into another state) — the fact that the accident occurred in a particular state was not enough to create minimal contacts absent more

Through these cases (because they fail to meet the minimal contacts requirements), you realize you don't get to the part of analysis where you discuss fair and reasonable factors. If you do have minimal contacts, then you would analyze whether the exercise of jurisdiction would be fair and reasonable.

### Family Law Cases (*Kulko* & *Burnham*)

#### *Kulko v. Superior Court*, 436 U.S. 84 (SCOTUS) (1978) — child-support modification case

A state may not exercise personal jurisdiction over a parent in a custody proceeding based solely on the parent's act of sending his or her child to live in the forum state.

**Facts:**
- Sharon Kulko Horn (plaintiff) and Ezra Kulko (defendant) were residents of New York who married in California in 1959, during a brief stopover as Kulko traveled to Korea for military duty.
- Upon the termination of Kulko's military service, the couple lived together in New York for 13 years, during which time they had a son and a daughter.
- After separating, Horn moved to California. She returned briefly to New York to sign a separation agreement in which the couple agreed to joint custody: the children would reside in New York during the school year and in California during summer and holiday vacations.
- Kulko was to pay $3,000 in child support annually to cover the periods during which the children resided with Horn.
- After entering the separation agreement, Horn traveled to Haiti and obtained a divorce that incorporated the separation agreement.
- In 1973, the couple's daughter sought to live with Horn during the school year. Kulko bought her a one-way ticket to California, and from then on, the girl spent the school year in California and vacations in New York.
- In 1976, the couple's son told Horn he wanted to live in California as well. Horn bought him a plane ticket and he relocated.
- One month later, Horn moved a California court for recognition of the Haitian divorce decree as a California judgment, for full custody of the children, and for an increase in child support.
- Kulko challenged the court's personal jurisdiction over him, but the court ruled that it could exercise jurisdiction based solely on Kulko's act of sending the daughter to California. He appealed.

<!-- page: 7 -->

**Issue:**
- May a state exercise personal jurisdiction over a parent living out of state in a custody proceeding based solely on the parent's act of sending his daughter to live with her mother in the forum state?

**Holding and Reasoning:**
- No. Due process requires that there be "minimum contacts" between California and Kulko in order for the state to establish jurisdiction over him.
- A court must consider whether Kulko's activity vis-à-vis the state of California makes it "reasonable," "fair," and expectable that Kulko could be called to defend himself in a California forum.
- The Court concludes that the **contacts in this case are not sufficient** to satisfy due process requirements.
- The California court rightly disregarded Kulko's brief stopover in California, his marriage there, and the fact that his children resided there part of the year in determining jurisdiction.
- The first two contacts were too fleeting and long ago to provide a basis for jurisdiction.
- If jurisdiction had been based on the third contact — the children's part-year residence in California — parents could be arbitrarily subjected to jurisdiction wherever their exes chose to live.
- The California court's ultimate decision that jurisdiction was established by Kulko's single act of sending his daughter to California was unreasonable.
- Kulko's contacts with California were extremely limited.
- The separation agreement at issue was made and signed in New York.
- In sending his daughter to California, Kulko did not "purposefully avail" himself of California state benefits because any benefits received were enjoyed by the daughter, not the father.
- Furthermore, even though Kulko paid for the daughter's plane ticket, the move was made at her desire, not her father's.
- Although California has a legitimate interest in the welfare of children living there, that interest does not create jurisdiction.
- Consequently, the decision is reversed.

In this case, we are talking about **specific jurisdiction** because there is no assertion that general jurisdiction applies.

The court says that CA is lacking specific PJ as to the father here, because analysis of contacts that would be present in a business case is misplaced in this situation. They are really looking for contacts based on **actions by the defendant**.

**Analysis:**

1. **Long Arm Statute** — we assume yes
2. **Permissible under the 14th Amendment's DPC:**

   **a. Minimal Contacts linking defendant, subject matter and forum:**
   - Defendant has sent one of his children over to live in California, as a result of a request from the child. The other child also went, but without permission from the father. Although we do have actions that the defendant took, we don't want to blame them on the defendant. We don't want to disincentivize them.
   - **(Court says this is not enough — likely because if it says that jurisdiction is available in CA, they might fear that the next familial dispute might cause future parents not to send their children wherever the other spouse is, due to child support litigation concerns — court was skeptical.)**

<!-- page: 8 -->

   **b. Fair and Reasonable** (if we WOULD HAVE gotten to this part of the analysis):

   i. **Extent of defendant's contacts:** He has contacts in California because his children and the ex-spouse are there.

   ii. **Defendant's convenience:** Worried about the defendant's ability to travel back and forth to California; he is already visiting anyway, but this would be of interest.

   iii. **Plaintiff's interest/convenience:** The ex-spouse is in California; it would be difficult for her to go to NY to dispute there. She might have special interest in being able to attend the court that adjudicates her support for the care of the children.

   iv. **Forum's interest:** The state has an interest because in the event that she cannot afford to care for the kids, the state's welfare budget might have to assist with resources.

   v. **Interest of judicial system, including location of witnesses, evidence:** The judicial system has an interest to be efficient and less costly, given the subject matter of the litigation. All of the witnesses are in California, the evidence is in California, the children are in California.

   vi. **Choice of law:** This part still remains unknown for child custody disputes.

There is a lot to say that California would have contacts with the defendant, even more compelling than the situation in *World-Wide Volkswagen*. The court still decides that there are not enough contacts here to authorize personal jurisdiction, and continues to look for **action on the part of the defendant** to establish minimum contacts for purposes of personal specific jurisdiction.

So we now have explored about six cases applying specific jurisdiction (*International Shoe*; *McGee*; *Denckla*; *WW VW*; *Nicastro*; *Kulko*) trying to figure out what in-personam jurisdiction is.

### Quasi in Rem Revisited — *Harris v. Balk*

The question then is about how Quasi in Rem worked. To understand the changes that happened in the law, it's important to go back in time and find the historical analog in the Quasi in Rem area to *Pennoyer v. Neff*:

#### *Harris v. Balk*, 198 U.S. 215 (1905) (SCOTUS)

A judgment of garnishment or attachment against a debt that is handed down in one state is entitled to full faith and credit when the parties to the debt are residents of another state. "This obligation can be enforced by the courts of the foreign state after personal service of process therein, just as well by the courts of the domicile of the debtor."

#### *Harris v. Balk* Party Diagram

```
Debts Before Lawsuits:
  Harris owes Balk
  Balk owes Epstein

Lawsuit 1 (MD state court):
  Epstein ──────────────────► Debt Harris owes Balk
  (quasi in rem — lawsuit against the debt)

Lawsuit 2 (NC state court):
  Balk ────────────────────► Harris
```

**Facts (Quasi in rem suit against the debt that Harris owed Balk):**
- Balk (plaintiff), a resident of North Carolina, owed $344 to Epstein, a resident of Maryland.
- Harris (not a defendant in Lawsuit #1, because Lawsuit #1 was a **Quasi in Rem** action — the lawsuit is against the debt, not the person), also a resident of North Carolina, owed $180 (**quasi in rem debt**) to Balk.
- While Harris was travelling to Baltimore, Maryland, he was served (tagged) with a writ of attachment from Epstein for the debt that he owed to Balk.
- After returning to North Carolina, Harris consented to the attachment.
- Shortly after this consent, Balk sued Harris in North Carolina state court for the $180.
- Harris argued that he had already paid the debt and that the Maryland judgment was entitled to full faith and credit by the North Carolina court.
- The North Carolina state courts held for Balk, finding that the judgment was not entitled to full faith and credit given that Harris was only temporarily in Maryland.

**Issue:**
- Is a judgment of attachment that is made in one state entitled to full faith and credit in other states?

**Holding and Reasoning:**
- Yes. Garnishment is a product of state law.
- For that reason, so long as a plaintiff has proper personal jurisdiction over a potential garnishee, state law controls regardless of whether the garnishment is proper.

<!-- page: 10 -->

- Furthermore, it is not the intention of the judicial system to force a debtor to pay his debt twice.
- For that reason, so long as a garnishment or attachment action is properly prosecuted, then it is entitled to full faith and credit in every state.
- This is particularly true in the context of debts, which have the legal effect of attaching to the debtor wherever he or she travels.
- In this case, Harris had no defense to the attachment case. Therefore, it was in his best interest to concede to the judgment, which he did. That decision has no implications on the recognition of the judgment by other states.
- The only affirmative duty that the garnishee has to the debtor in this situation is to inform him of the fact of the garnishment itself.
- Here, while there is no evidence of specific communication between Harris and Balk, the facts indicate that Balk was made aware of the judgment.
- Not only was Harris required to post a bond that would allow Balk to return to the Maryland court in the following year and a day to contest the judgment, but Balk also sued Harris shortly after the filing of the judgment. This indicated that Balk was fully aware of the judgment. However, had he not been made aware of it, the judgment would likely not have received full faith and credit.

**Is the debt in Maryland?**
- According to the high court, when Harris travels to Maryland, he carried the debt with him.

**What about the policy?**
- We can say as a policy matter where we want the debt to be:
  1. **The debt is wherever the debtor is.** An advantage is that the debtor would be responsible regardless of location, which would be easy to do because the debt would be attached to a physical object (a person) and we find the object and therefore find the debt.
  2. **The debt is wherever it incurred.** In this case, NC, where the relationship was. The advantage may be the state where the debt incurred has a greater interest because people are conducting business in the state. Also, the evidence would be located there (at least during that era).
- In the modern era we might have exploded this idea because we are able to borrow money digitally, so basically things will work differently now.
- But in *Balk*, the court says that the debt follows the debtor, and when Epstein pursued Balk's debt via Harris, that was an appropriate exercise of quasi in rem jurisdiction because they properly tagged the debt.

### *Schaffer v. Heitner*, 433 U.S. 186 (1977)

**BLL:**
1. **Quasi in rem after *Schaffer* is an exploded myth.** We are recognizing that the category of Quasi in Rem, when asserted, is a lawsuit against the defendant's interest in the property, and the defendant.
2. **To exercise personal jurisdiction over the property in personal jurisdiction questions, we still need to use *International Shoe* (minimal contacts) analysis.** (We still do not know how to do this after *Schaffer* because: can't tell yet whether we should focus on the relationship (for minimal contacts purposes) between the **defendant, subject matter of the litigation and the forum** OR the **defendant's interest in the property, subject matter of the litigation and the forum**.)

Quasi in rem jurisdiction may only be asserted when the interests of the persons in the property seized have sufficient contacts, ties, or relations to the state. In other words, "**minimum contacts**" are the **minimum constitutional threshold that due process requires** for **any type** of personal jurisdiction. ***Therefore, all "in personam," "in rem," and "quasi in rem" jurisdictions must all satisfy minimum contacts.***

#### *Schaffer* Corporation Diagram

```
                              ┌─── Chief Executive Officer
                              │
         Corporation ─────────┼─── Chief Financial Officer
        /  │  │  │  \         │
       /   │  │  │   \        └─── Other Directors & Officers
     SH1  SH2 SH3 SH4 ... SHn
```

**Facts:**
- Heitner (plaintiff) brought a shareholder derivative suit in Delaware (state law) against the Greyhound Corporation, incorporated in Delaware, represented by Schaffer, alleging acts that took place in Oregon.
- Heitner also filed a motion for an order of **sequestration of shares** (what the court seizes and says cannot be sold) of Greyhound stock owned by the individual defendants named in the complaint, who move to vacate the sequestration order on

<!-- page: 12 -->

the grounds that the ex-parte sequestration violated their due process rights and that the property seized was not capable of attachment in Delaware.
- The court rejected this challenge on the ground that the suit was brought as a quasi in rem proceeding, which is traditionally based on **seizure of property present in the jurisdiction rather than contacts between the defendant and the state**.

**Holding:**
- Quasi in rem jurisdiction may only be exercised over a person **if the interests of the person in the things seized meet the minimum contacts standard established for personal jurisdiction in *International Shoe Co. v. Washington***, 326 U.S. 310 (1945), which established the minimum requirements for exercising jurisdiction in a manner consistent with the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.
- Thus, if there were not the minimum contacts required by *International Shoe*, the Delaware sequestration statute deprived Shaffer of his property without due process.
- Here, the property seized was statutorily located in Delaware, but it did not have any relation to the subject matter of the litigation.
- The defendants had nothing to do with the state of Delaware; their only connection to it was that they had accepted directorial positions in a company incorporated in Delaware, but accepting these positions does not mean they can be taken to a Delaware court.
- Accordingly, Delaware's assertion of jurisdiction over the defendants was inappropriate and the judgment below is reversed.

**Concurrence (Stevens, J.):**
- Purchasing stocks on the open market, where the purchaser may not know in what state the corporation is incorporated, is not a sufficient contact with the state to establish jurisdiction. However, the scope of the Court's decision is uncertain and might be applied inappropriately in other contexts.

**Concurrence (Powell, J.):**
- The *International Shoe* rationale should not necessarily extend to other forms of property, such as real property, located within the state.

**Concurrence/Dissent (Brennan, J.):**
- The *International Shoe* standard should apply to the case. However, in accepting positions with a Delaware corporation, the defendants elected to assume powers and to undertake responsibilities wholly derived from Delaware's rules and regulations, which amounted to sufficient contacts to justify exercise of jurisdiction.

This court basically exploded the whole fiction that the lawsuit was Quasi in Rem (against the property) and said that the lawsuit was over the person's interest in that property, which is basically over the person. Therefore, there is a minimal contacts (the **defendant's interest in the property, the subject matter of the litigation, and the forum**) requirement to meet.

- Quasi in Rem supposedly did not need to work that way, because supposedly the property did not have to have any relationship to the dispute. The whole theory of

<!-- page: 13 -->

dominion and why the court could exercise dominion over the property is because it did not need to be related to the subject matter of the jurisdiction.
- We understand how the court got past step 1, but in order to reach the court's results, we need to be able to say that there is no relationship among the defendant's interest in the property or the defendant, the subject matter of the litigation, and the forum.
- Heitner tried to claim that the minimal contacts test was satisfied here because the officers and directors own stock and hold office in a Delaware corporation and thus should expect Delaware lawsuits concerning the corporation.
- But Delaware's jurisdictional statute did not work that way. The defendants here were not being sued in their capacities as stockholders (owners), but instead as officers and directors (therefore they had not created the requisite minimal contacts linking the subject matter of the litigation, the forum, the defendant, and the **defendant's interest in the property** since they were sued in their shareholder capacity (ownership)).
- Separately, since they were unable to meet the specific jurisdiction minimal contacts test, we would have to look under the next way of establishing personal jurisdiction (Consent). Under the DE statute at the time, no one consented to personal jurisdiction by merely holding an office in a Delaware corporation. Instead, Delaware's Quasi in Rem jurisdictional statute focused solely on ownership of property (i.e., their stock).
- In other words, if the defendants had been sued in their ownership capacity, the state would have had **quasi in rem jurisdiction over them because that would satisfy the minimum contacts test**. However, because they were sued in their capacity as officers of the corporation only, the state did not have authority to exercise personal jurisdiction over them because that did not satisfy the minimum contacts test.

**Delaware could not satisfy the minimum contacts test because:**

```
Specific Jurisdiction ───────────────────► No. The relevant events happened in Oregon.
General Jurisdiction  ───────────────────► No. The defendants do not have a continuous,
                                               systematic presence in DE.
```

- The court, contrary to the facts in front of it, finds a lack of minimal contacts.
- Implications of the BLL that emerge out of *Schaffer* are large:
  - **No such thing as quasi in rem anymore**, because if we now need links between defendant, property, and forum, there is no longer a need for quasi in rem.
  - In *Schaffer*, court seems irritated that we tried to force the defendants into the state of Delaware.
  - This case would suggest that if someone tried to seize property now, the court would immediately look for something linking the relationships between defendant's interest in the property, subject matter of claim, and forum.

***\*\*\*Schaffer* strengthened *International Shoe*'s focus on reasonable expectations and paved the way for *Burnham*, which undercuts the reasoning in *Schaffer*.**

<!-- page: 14 -->

In order to understand why *Schaffer* may not be good law anymore, we need to understand how what happens in the area of In Personam jurisdiction has implications for what happens in Quasi in Rem jurisdiction, which led us to the idea that *Schaffer* cast away some fictions.

We can look at the chart and realize that in *Pennoyer*, **in-state tag** was both **necessary AND sufficient** for the exercise of personal jurisdiction, regardless of the subject matter of the lawsuit.

But, in *International Shoe*, cast aside some ideas and learned that in-state tag was necessary for the exercise of jurisdiction, and we created the idea of specific jurisdiction, but left aside the idea of whether the **in-state tag was sufficient** for the exercise of general jurisdiction in a certain location.

### *Burnham v. Superior Court*, 495 U.S. 604 (SCOTUS) (1990)

A non-resident is properly served if he is physically present in the forum state, and the forum state may exercise personal jurisdiction over him without violating due process.

**Facts:**
- Dennis Burnham (plaintiff) and Francie Burnham were married from 1976 until July 1987, at which point they decided to separate.
- At that time, they were living in New Jersey with their two children.
- They agreed to divorce on the grounds of irreconcilable differences and that Francie would move to California, where she would have custody of both children.
- She moved to California, and in October 1987, Dennis filed for divorce in the state of New Jersey on the grounds of desertion, but never attempted to serve her.
- In January 1988, while in California for business and to visit his children, Dennis was served (tagged) with a California court summons and Francie's divorce petition.
- Subsequently, he returned to New Jersey.
- Later that year, Dennis filed a motion to quash on the basis that it was a violation of the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment for California to assert

<!-- page: 15 -->

jurisdiction over him, because he lacked the minimum contacts necessary for personal jurisdiction.
- The superior court denied the motion, and the California Court of Appeal denied mandamus relief, holding that it was enough that Dennis was present in the forum and personally served with process.
- Dennis appealed, and the United States Supreme Court granted certiorari.

**Issue:**
- May a non-resident party be properly tagged while temporarily visiting a state for purposes unrelated to the suit?

**Holding and Reasoning (Scalia, J., along with 3 other justices):**
- Yes. A non-resident party may be properly served with process while temporarily visiting a state without violating due process.
- It is **well established that states have jurisdiction over non-residents who are physically present in the state**, no matter how long that individual plans to stay in the state.
- This rule has a long and continuing tradition of being enforced by the courts, across jurisdictions.
- Burnham argues that he lacks the minimum contacts necessary for the court to properly apply personal jurisdiction, but that is a perversion of the standard; indeed, the **minimum contacts standard was created for use in the absence of a party's physical presence in the state**, which is not necessary in this case, as Dennis was physically present in California when he was served.
- For the same reason, Dennis cannot rely on the holding in *Shaffer v. Heitner*, which held that a state lacks jurisdiction over an individual unless the lawsuit arises out of his activities in the state, because the non-resident litigant in that case was an absent one, unlike Dennis.
- Justice Brennan's concurring opinion disregards the fact that **this body of law is based in traditional notions of due process**, not fleeting or contemporary notions.
- **Because Dennis was physically present in California when he was served with process, the state properly asserted personal jurisdiction over him, and the holding below is affirmed.**

**Concurrence (White, J.):**
- The rule that a non-resident party may be served if he physically enters the forum state is not so well accepted that it would be impossible to strike down. It is, however, a useful rule to prevent flooding the courts with fact-specific inquiries relating to personal jurisdiction.

**Concurrence (Brennan, J.) (supported by 3 other justices):**
- A long history of enforcement of personal jurisdiction when a non-resident party is present in a state, while informative and persuasive, is not necessarily dispositive. It does mean, however, that defendants have notice that they might be served if they enter a different forum state.
- **By entering the state, the defendant avails himself of the benefits made available by the state, such as police protection and use of the roads.**

<!-- page: 16 -->

- Furthermore, the **burden on the non-resident defendant is small; modern travel is not a great inconvenience** for an individual who wishes to defend himself in a foreign state.

**Concurrence (Stevens, J.):**
- This holding, like the holding in *Schaffer*, is unnecessarily broad.

This is a **general jurisdiction** case with an in-state tag.

- **J. Scalia** argues that the basis for this logic is rooted strongly in tradition, and that if you are **tagged in a certain state, then you have reasonable notice** that you may be sued in that state (like, back to *Pennoyer*).
  - If we follow this logic, the pedigree **Quasi in Rem** (in-state tag) would have ruled, and this would undercut *Schaffer* because he is saying that if there is an in-state tag, nothing else should matter regarding minimal contacts under the court's constitutional analysis.
- **J. Brennan** is saying that it is okay to exercise PJ over the spouse in this case, but not just due to the tag. He stated the tag is not sufficient and that we should look at the ruling of *International Shoe* and analyze the reasonable fairness of it all. And that while *Schaffer* might be limited to quasi-in rem, the mode of analysis was not, and so we still should do the *Schaffer* analysis here.
  - He mentions that in addition to being tagged in the state, the spouse visited there and benefitted from the protection of the state because he was there for a few days, used the roads, and if he would've gotten hurt, he would go to the hospital in the state.

> ***\*\*\*NOTE — If you encounter on an exam a fact pattern that has one of these:***
> 1. If you take *Schaffer* seriously, you must do the *International Shoe* type of analysis.
> 2. If you say that *Schaffer* is now undercut, then you need to do the regular Quasi in Rem analysis.
>
> *In practice in the real world, you would argue whichever is best for your client.*

In the last case, there was an **in-state tag in CA** while the guy was visiting his children. In the next case, the guy gets **tagged on an airplane** while it was flying over Arkansas.

- What they are suggesting is that if the air above a state does not belong to a state, then it might be a lawless area. This may be a big reason why the court decided that the plane, while flying over the state, is in the state.

### *Grace v. McArthur*, 170 F. Supp. 442 (E.D. Ark.) (1959)

A person moving in interstate commerce is not on that account alone exempt from service of process while in transit.

**Facts:**
- Defendants claim that they were not served within the "territorial limits" of Arkansas because they were on a flight.

**Issue:**
- Whether for service purposes, the passengers on a commercial aircraft are **within the territorial limits** of the State over which the plane happens to be flying at a particular time?

**Holding:**
- Yes. At the time the Marshal served the summons on the defendant, the plane and its passengers were within the "territorial limits" of the State of Arkansas, as that term is used in Rule 4(f) (now Rule 4(k)(1)(A)).

**Reasoning:**
- An aircraft flying over the State and its passengers are within the borders or limits of the State.
- The result here reached seems to be just, equitable, and practical.
- It cannot seriously be contended that a person moving in interstate commerce is on that account exempt from service of process while in transit, and it makes no practical difference whether he is traveling on a plane, a bus, a train, or in his own car.
- True, if going by plane the duration of presence in the State will probably be much shorter than if availing oneself of some other means of transportation, but that is a difference of degree only, not of principle.

Why does the tag matter so much? These are things to think about during Constitutional Law class.

If globalization of commerce had not shattered our conceptualizations of personal jurisdiction, the internet now certainly has. We are still trying to figure out how to deal with these issues today.

### Personal Jurisdiction in an Internet Age — *Griffis v. Luban*

#### *Griffis v. Luban*, 646 N.W.2d 526 (Minn. 2002)

**Facts:**
- Griffis brought suit against Luban in Alabama, alleging defamation and invasion of privacy arising out of statements made by Luban on an internet newsgroup website.
- Luban did not appear in the Alabama action, and the Alabama district court entered a default judgment for $25,000 in damages and issued an injunction prohibiting Luban from making certain statements in the future.
- Griffis filed the Alabama judgment in Ramsey County District Court (MN) and Luban brought a motion to vacate, challenging the jurisdiction of the Alabama court.
- The Ramsey County District Court (MN) upheld personal jurisdiction of the Alabama court over Luban, and the court of appeals affirmed. Luban sought and was granted review by the Minnesota Supreme Court.

**Issue:**
- Whether the Ramsey County District Court correctly determined that the Alabama district court had personal jurisdiction over Luban so that the Alabama judgment is entitled to full faith and credit in the Minnesota courts?

**Legal Framework Applied:**
- The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment limits the power of a state court to exercise personal jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant to circumstances where the defendant has "minimum contacts" with the state so that "maintenance of the suit does not offend 'traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice'" (*International Shoe*).
- Where the defendant had "continuous and systematic" contacts with the forum state, the court can exercise "general" jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant for all purposes, even for a claim not related to the defendant's contacts with the forum state.
- Griffis does not contend that the Alabama courts could exercise general jurisdiction over Luban. Where the non-resident defendant's contacts with the forum state are not sufficient for general jurisdiction, the defendant may nonetheless be subject to "specific" jurisdiction — jurisdiction over a claim that allegedly arose out of the defendant's contacts with the forum.
- Griffis contends that Luban had sufficient contacts with Alabama, out of which her claims arose, to support the Alabama court's exercise of specific jurisdiction.
- In judging minimum contacts for purposes of assessing the validity of specific jurisdiction, a court focuses on the "relationship among the defendant, the forum, and the litigation" (*Shaffer v. Heitner*).
- For the minimum contacts requirement to be satisfied, the defendant must have "purposefully availed" herself of the privilege of conducting activities within the jurisdiction (*Hanson v. Denckla*).

<!-- page: 19 -->

- The defendant's conduct and connections with the forum state must be such that the defendant "should reasonably anticipate being haled into court there" (*World-Wide Volkswagen*).
- Although it has been argued that foreseeability of causing injury in another state should be sufficient to establish such contacts there when policy considerations so require, the Court has consistently held that this kind of foreseeability is not a "sufficient benchmark" for exercising personal jurisdiction (*World-Wide Volkswagen*).
- Instead, "the foreseeability that is critical to due process analysis . . . is that the defendant's conduct and connection with the forum State are such that he should reasonably anticipate being haled into court there" (*World-Wide Volkswagen*).
- We conclude that something more than mere effects in the forum state is required, and that the Supreme Court did not "carve out a special intentional torts exception to the traditional specific jurisdiction analysis, so that a plaintiff could always sue in his or her home state."

**The *Griffis* Three-Prong Test (Internet Torts):**

We adopt the following three-prong test which requires the plaintiff to show that:

1. The defendant **committed an intentional tort**;
2. The **plaintiff felt the brunt of the harm** caused by that tort in the forum such that the forum state was the **focal point of the plaintiff's injury**; and
3. **P must show that the D knew that the P would suffer the brunt of the harm caused by the tortious conduct in the forum, and point to specific activity indicating that the defendant expressly aimed its tortious conduct at the forum.**

> **Court:** While the record supports the conclusion that Luban's statements were intentionally directed at Griffis, whom she knew to be an Alabama resident, **we conclude that the evidence does not demonstrate that Luban's statements were "expressly aimed" at the state of Alabama (the forum). Case was reversed and judgments vacated.**

- The third prong of the three-prong test is the only one the court said mattered, because only if the third prong was satisfied would the other two apply.

With this case, we are struggling to locate someone's reputation, even though we always do that with incorporeal entities.

The reason why the *Luban* ruling is important is due to more recent cases that have been decided, that have adopted their own version of the test that this court applied.
