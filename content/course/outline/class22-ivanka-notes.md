## Background Cases, Statutes and References

### Cases (Common Law)

- ***Blonder-Tongue Laboratories v. University of Illinois Foundation*, 402 U.S. 313 (SCOTUS)(1971)** *(sample of defensive nonmutual collateral estoppel)*
  A defendant in a subsequent action may invoke nonmutual issue preclusion against a plaintiff who lost on the same issue in a prior action if the plaintiff had a full and fair opportunity to litigate.

- ***Parklane Hosiery Co., v. Shore*, 439 U.S. 322 (1979)(SCOTUS)** *(sample of exception to offensive nonmutual collateral estoppel — when a party could not have joined L1)*
  A litigant who was not a party to a prior judgment may nonetheless use that judgment offensively to prevent a defendant from relitigating issues resolved in the earlier proceeding, provided that (1) the plaintiff could not easily have joined in the earlier action and (2) use of the judgment will not result in unfairness to the defendant.

- ***Semtek Int'l Inc. v. Lockheed Martin Corp.*, 531 U.S. 497 (SCOTUS)(2001)**
  The preclusive effect of a federal diversity judgment is such as would belong to judgments of the state courts rendered under similar circumstances and may not be accorded any higher sanctity or effect.

- ***Migra v. Warren City School District Bd. of Education et al.*, 465 U.S. 75 (1984)(SCOTUS)**
  Federal courts (if L2) are obligated to apply rendering state court (L1) issue preclusion rules to determine whether the issue preclusive effect of a prior state court judgment, even in a section 1983 action, should be extended to claim preclusion.

- ***Hart v. American Airlines, Inc.*, 304 N.Y.2d 810 (1969, NY Supreme Court)**
  If rendering courts would not preclude, then we have a question whether or not the subsequent court will preclude or not.

## Lecture Recap for Day 21

We finished going over the third element of **claim preclusion** and discussed what "**final**" and "**on the merits**" means, plus additional factors that the courts may consider when someone pleads claim preclusion.

**Elements of Claim Preclusion:**
1. Same party (or in privity)
2. Same claim; and
3. A judgment that is both
   - **a. Final; AND**
   - **b. On the merits.**

*Plus other stuff:*
- Rule in *Marresse*
- Rendering court can limit claim preclusive effect of judgment

**Finality** applies whenever there was a trial court judgment, even if that judgment was on appeal.

"**On the merits**" is more of a policy judgment about when we want to have claim preclusion attach.

Obviously "on the merits":
- Judgment after jury trial
- Judgment after bench trial
- Summary Judgment
- Successful Motion to Dismiss

<!-- page: 5 -->

Not so obvious, but still considered to be "**sufficiently on the merits**":
- Dismissal for failure to prosecute
- Limitations-based dismissal (sometimes, depending on the jurisdiction)\*

**Not** considered "on the merits":
- Dismissal for lack of subject matter jurisdiction
- Dismissal for lack of personal jurisdiction
- Lack of ability to lay venue
- Limitations-based dismissal (sometimes, depending on the jurisdiction)\*

**Rule in *Marrese*:** When the rendering court in L1 lacks the capacity (especially jurisdictional capacity) to litigate a theory of relief, that theory of relief will not be claim precluded.

A **rendering court can limit** the claim preclusive effect of a judgment by saying that the adjudication in L1 is without prejudice to a refiling of some theory of relief.

### Issue Preclusion

**Elements of Issue Preclusion:**
1. Same issue
2. Actually litigated, AND
3. Necessary to decision
4. ??mutuality?? (depends on applicable state law)

**Plus other stuff:**
- Judgment of sufficient quality/finality
- Sufficient opportunity, incentive to litigate
- Only preclude party who lost in L1, or those in privity
- Only issues litigated which are FACTUAL (for legal issues, we rely on *staré decisis*)

Elements 1–3 recap:

1. **Same issue** — defined narrowly (factual; smaller than theories of relief; much narrower than transactions or occurrences)
2. **Actually litigated** — only preclude issues that were actually brought up and litigated
3. **Necessary to decision** — *Russell v. Place* (federal rule: multiple independent grounds → can't tell which ground jury relied on → issue preclusion fails); *Rios v. Davis* (state preference variation)

<!-- page: 6 -->

**Mutuality — four types:**

1. Same parties in L1 and L2, same procedural posture (strictest)
2. Same parties, different procedural postures allowed
3. Federal court common law rule (*Parklane Hosiery*)
   - Defensive nonmutual ordinarily allowed
   - Offensive nonmutual ordinarily not allowed unless P2 could not have joined L1
4. No mutuality requirement (New York Rule — *Hart*)

### Four Types of Issue Preclusion "Mutuality" (Jurisdictions Differ)

(Either party could use the issue preclusion doctrine)

1. **Same parties in L1 and L2; same procedural posture** (strictest — now the law in hardly any jurisdiction)
   - Plaintiff in L1 = Plaintiff in L2
   - Defendant in L1 = Defendant in L2

2. **Same parties, different procedural postures allowed**, so long as:
   - They were adverse in the previous litigation on the same issue
   - Other elements of issue preclusion are met

3. **Federal court common law view** — more complex (see below)
   - *Parklane Hosiery* (offensive/defensive collateral estoppel)
   - *Semtek* (When rendering court is a federal court sitting in diversity, subsequent court must use the preclusion law of the state in which the rendering federal court is sitting)

4. **No mutuality requirement** (New York Rule — *Hart*)

You don't need all four types. So long as you have one of them, you are fine. If the jurisdiction does not require mutuality at all, move on without it.

<!-- page: 8 -->

**Plus other stuff (issue preclusion):**
- Judgment of sufficient quality/finality
- Sufficient opportunity, incentive to litigate
- Only preclude party who lost in L1, or those in privity
- Only issues litigated which are FACTUAL (for legal issues, we rely on *staré decisis*)

**The third type — Federal common law view:**

First, defining offensive vs. defensive collateral estoppel:

**Offensive** (left column in diagram):
- A plaintiff is seeking to estop a defendant from relitigating issues which the defendant previously litigated and lost against another plaintiff
- L1: P1 sues D1 — Plaintiff prevails (D1 loses)
- L2: P2 sues D1 — P2 wants to use collateral estoppel against D1
- *Parklane Hosiery*

**Defensive** (right column in diagram):
- A plaintiff is estopped from asserting a claim that the plaintiff had previously litigated and lost against another defendant
- L1: P1 sues D1 — Plaintiff loses
- L2: P1 sues D2 — D2 can take advantage of P1's loss in L1
- *Blonder-Tongue*

**\*\*\* Note:** When we refer to L1 and L2, we are talking about which one gets to judgment first, not which one gets filed first.

<!-- page: 9 -->

**Ordinarily:**
- **Defensive nonmutual** issue preclusion → **allowed**
- **Offensive nonmutual** issue preclusion → **not allowed** UNLESS Plaintiff in L2 could not have joined L1

<!-- page: 10 -->

### *Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore*, 439 U.S. 322 (1979)(SCOTUS)

A litigant who was not a party to a prior judgment may nonetheless use that judgment offensively to prevent a defendant from relitigating issues resolved in the earlier proceeding, provided that **(1) the plaintiff could not easily have joined** in the earlier action and **(2) use of the judgment will not result in unfairness** to the defendant.

**Facts:**
- Shore (plaintiff) brought a stockholder's class action against Parklane Hosiery Co. (defendant), alleging that Parklane had issued a proxy statement containing materially false and misleading information in violation of federal securities laws and SEC regulations.
- Before trial, the SEC also sued Parklane on the same allegations.
- Following a nonjury trial, the district court entered a declaratory judgment in favor of the SEC, finding that the proxy statement was materially false and misleading. The Second Circuit affirmed.
- Shore then moved for partial summary judgment, asserting that Parklane was collaterally estopped from relitigating whether the proxy statement was materially false and misleading.
- The district court denied the motion, holding such use of collateral estoppel would deny Parklane its Seventh Amendment jury trial right. The court of appeals reversed. SCOTUS granted certiorari.

**Issue:**
- May a litigant who was not a party to a prior judgment nonetheless use that judgment offensively to prevent a defendant from relitigating issues resolved in the earlier proceeding?

**Holding and Reasoning (Stewart, J.):**
- Yes. The purpose of collateral estoppel is to protect litigants from relitigating an identical issue and to promote judicial economy.
- Until 1971, collateral estoppel was limited by the doctrine of mutuality (neither party could use a prior judgment as an estoppel unless both parties were bound). *Blonder-Tongue* abandoned mutuality for defensive use.
- The present case involves **offensive** use — a plaintiff seeks to estop a defendant from relitigating issues the defendant lost against another plaintiff.
- Offensive use may be unfair where: defendant was sued for nominal damages and did not vigorously defend in L1; defendant was denied certain procedural advantages in L1; or the judgment is inconsistent with one or more previous judgments in defendant's favor.
- Despite potential unfairness, trial courts should be granted **broad discretion** to determine when offensive collateral estoppel should be applied.
- **Where a plaintiff could easily have joined in the earlier action or application of offensive estoppel would be unfair to defendant, a trial judge should not allow it.**
- None of those circumstances is present here:
  - Shore probably could not have joined the SEC's injunctive action (SEC barred private intervention by statute)
  - Parklane had every incentive to litigate the SEC lawsuit vigorously
  - The SEC judgment was not inconsistent with any previous decision
  - Parklane was not presented with procedural disadvantages in L1

<!-- page: 11 -->

**Simplified:**
- Company issued securities and proxy statements.
- SEC sued, alleging the proxy statements were materially misleading. SEC lawsuit went to judgment first (equity/injunctive side — no jury).
- SEC prevailed.
- Class plaintiffs (investors) tried to use that SEC judgment via offensive nonmutual collateral estoppel to stop the company from denying it issued misleading proxy statements.
- It's "nonmutual" because the plaintiff in L1 was the SEC, not the class.
- SCOTUS: Offensive nonmutual collateral estoppel does not generally promote judicial economy (it incentivizes P2 to wait and see rather than joining L1).
  - BUT exception: If P2 had no ability to intervene in L1 (SEC statute bars private intervention), then the incentive concern is irrelevant.

<!-- page: 12 -->

**Policy — offensive nonmutual collateral estoppel:**
- Ordinarily creates a "wait and see" incentive for subsequent plaintiffs
- Court concludes that ordinarily it would incentivize two lawsuits rather than one joined lawsuit
- **Exception:** If P2 could not have joined or intervened in L1 (*Parklane*)
- Still need all other elements of issue preclusion

**Policy — defensive nonmutual collateral estoppel:**
- Precludes the single P1 from relitigating issues already decided (efficiency)
- No due process issue because P1 had their chance to litigate
- Incentivizes P1 to join all defendants at the same time
- If P1 prevailed in L1, P1 cannot use collateral estoppel against D2 (D2 was not in L1)

(All other requirements of issue preclusion must still be met before issue precluding.)

<!-- page: 13 -->

### Analysis of Issue Preclusion in *Parklane*

1. **Same issue** — Yes
2. **Actually litigated** — Yes
3. **Necessary to decision** — Yes (SEC prevailed and got an injunction)
4. **Mutuality** — Yes; federal common law exception — P2 could not join L1

**Plus other stuff:**
- Judgment of sufficient quality/finality — Yes
- Sufficient opportunity, incentive to litigate — Yes
- Only preclude party who lost in L1, or those in privity — Yes
- Only issues litigated which are FACTUAL — Yes

**Note:** *Parklane* involved "arising under" federal subject matter jurisdiction (SEC is a federal agency), not diversity.

**The fourth type of mutuality — No requirement at all:**
- Some states simply do not have the mutuality requirement. (*Hart*)

This concludes the basic elements of issue preclusion.

## Intersystem Preclusion Problem

All prior discussions assumed L1 and L2 from the same adjudicatory system (e.g., MA state court in both, or federal court in both). Now we must determine what happens when **the preclusion law of the rendering court (L1) differs from the preclusion law in the jurisdiction litigating L2**.

<!-- page: 15 -->

### Hypo 1 (State to State)

**Facts:**
- Greiner (D) kills Izzy the dog in NH by driving over the dog with a car, then drives back to MA.
- NH SOL: 4 years (limitations dismissals are on the merits)
- MA SOL: 2 years (limitations dismissals not on the merits)
- P files in year 3 in MA state court.
- Greiner claims SOL has passed.

**In L1 (MA):**
- MA court could dismiss on limitations grounds (with or without prejudice).
- Suppose MA court does not specify → dismisses on limitations grounds.

**P refiles in NH (L2):**
- NH likely has personal (specific) jurisdiction over Greiner:
  - Minimal contacts: drove car in NH; relationship between the defendant, subject matter, and forum
  - Fair and reasonable: NH has state interest in regulating misconduct within its borders; Greiner was present; evidence/witnesses may be in NH
  - NH long-arm statute must reach

<!-- page: 16 -->

**Can Greiner file Motion to Dismiss for failure to file within the limitations period?**
- No.
  - For HCOL purposes, Statutes of Limitation are **procedural** (not substantive)
  - SOL is procedural → court applies the **procedural law of the forum** (NH)
  - NH's 4-year SOL applies, and it's within 4 years → motion to dismiss not granted

**Greiner pleads issue preclusion:**
- Issue: "whether plaintiff filed within limitations period"
- Will not work — it is a **different issue** (the 2-year MA SOL vs. the 4-year NH SOL → different issue)

**Greiner claims claim preclusion:**
1. Same party — Yes
2. Same claim (transaction or occurrence) — Yes
3. Judgment that is both:
   - Final — Yes
   - On the merits — **No** — In NH, limitations dismissals are on the merits, but we look to the **rendering court** (MA), where limitations-based dismissals were NOT on the merits.

**→ Claim preclusion fails.**

**Why look to the rendering court?**

<!-- page: 17 -->

**Source of law that imposes full faith and credit:**
- Depends on who the rendering court is.
- In all instances, subsequent court must give full faith and credit, but **how much** credit depends on the source of law imposing the obligation.
- Intersystem preclusion rules are **not** conceptualized as HCOL choices (they deal with the *effects* of judgments, not choice of law).
- Full faith and credit obligations apply to **judgments ONLY**.

**BLL:**
- **If rendering court would preclude → subsequent court must also preclude.**
- **If rendering court would not preclude → subsequent court has a question whether it will preclude or not.** (*Hart*)

When the rendering court would preclude, sometimes the rendering court itself must decide what preclusion law to apply. (*Semtek*) E.g., federal courts sitting in diversity need to act like state courts in some circumstances.

<!-- page: 18 -->

## Sources of Law That Impose Full Faith & Credit Obligations

### U.S. Constitution: Article IV § 1 – Full Faith and Credit Clause

> Full faith and credit shall be given in **each state** [L2 – subsequent state court] to the public acts, records, and judicial proceedings of **every other state** [L1 – rendering state court].

- Covers: state rendering court → state subsequent court
- Does **not** cover: state rendering → federal subsequent; or federal rendering → any subsequent

### Statute: 28 U.S.C. § 1738

<!-- page: 19 -->

- Says that records and proceedings of **rendering state courts** shall have the same full faith and credit in **every court within the United States** (both state and federal).
- Covers: rendering state court → any subsequent court (state or federal)
- **Overlaps** with the Constitution for state-to-state; **extends further** by also covering state rendering → federal subsequent.
- **Does NOT cover**: rendering court is a federal court.

Neither the Constitution nor § 1738 tells us what to do when the **rendering court is a federal court**.

### Federal (Specific) Common Law

*Erie* says there is no federal **general** common law, but there can be **specific** federal common law pertaining to a particular issue.

When the rendering court is a federal court, we use **federal common law** to decide preclusive effects.

<!-- page: 19 -->

### *Semtek Int'l Inc. v. Lockheed Martin Corp.*, 531 U.S. 497 (SCOTUS)(2001)

The preclusive effect of a **federal diversity judgment** is such as would belong to **judgments of the state courts rendered** under similar circumstances and may not be accorded any higher sanctity or effect.

**Facts:**
- Semtek (plaintiff) sued Lockheed Martin (defendant) in California state court for breach of contract and business tort claims.
- Case was removed to federal court on diversity grounds. Lockheed moved to dismiss based on California's 2-year SOL.
- Federal court dismissed the claim on its merits. Semtek refiled in Maryland state court (where a 3-year SOL applied).
- Maryland court dismissed, holding that the federal court's merits dismissal precluded the claim.

<!-- page: 20 -->

**Simplified timeline:**
- P sues D in CA state court
- D removes to federal court (diversity)
- D moves to dismiss on limitations grounds → case dismissed on its merits
- P refiles in Maryland state court
- D tries to remove to Maryland federal court → fails (no diversity in MD; Mottley bars arising-under claim via defense only)
- D tries to get Maryland federal court to enjoin Maryland state court litigation → fails
- D moves to dismiss on preclusion grounds in Maryland state court → issue reaches SCOTUS

<!-- page: 21 -->

**Holding:**
- Existing federal law does not resolve the issue; no need for a uniform federal rule.
- To hold otherwise would encourage forum shopping — parties would remove to federal court under diversity for the claim-preclusive effect on state court cases.
- No federal interest in giving California's 2-year limitations dismissal more effect (in Maryland) than California state courts themselves could impose.
- Matter remanded.

**BLL Sources for Full Faith & Credit Obligation:**

| Rendering Court | Source of Law |
|---|---|
| State court | 28 U.S.C. § 1738 (regardless of subsequent court) |
| State court → state subsequent | 28 U.S.C. § 1738 AND U.S. Const. Art. IV § 1 |
| Federal court | Federal common law (regardless of subsequent court) |

*Memory aid: "Statute when the rendering court is a state; federal common law when the rendering court is federal."*

<!-- page: 22 -->

**When rendering court is federal — further distinction by type of jurisdiction:**

- **Sitting in diversity** → use the preclusion law of the **state in which the federal court sits** (*Semtek*)
  - Rationale: vertical uniformity (same result in fed court as in state court) preferred over horizontal uniformity
- **Arising under** → use **uniform federal common law** (*Parklane Hosiery*)

*Semtek* rule: As a matter of federal common law, when a federal court sits in diversity, it must research whether limitations-based dismissals are considered "on the merits" in the state law of the state where the federal court is sitting.

*Cromwell* and *Russell v. Place* were pre-*Erie*; *Parklane* was post-*Erie* ("arising under"); *Semtek* was "diversity."

<!-- page: 23 -->

### *Migra v. Warren City School District Bd. of Education et al.*, 465 U.S. 75 (1984)(SCOTUS)

Federal courts (if L2) are obligated to apply rendering state court (L1) issue preclusion rules to determine whether the issue preclusive effect of a prior state court judgment (even in a 42 U.S.C. § 1983 action) should be extended to claim preclusion.

**Facts:**
- Petitioner was employed as supervisor of elementary education by the Warren, Ohio, Board of Education under annual written contracts.
- The Board adopted a resolution renewing her employment for 1979–1980; petitioner accepted in writing.
- After learning she would be teaching about desegregation, the Board voted 3-to-1 to not renew her employment.
- Petitioner brought suit (L1) in the Ohio Court of Common Pleas alleging breach of contract and wrongful interference by individual board members.
- Trial court: petitioner's acceptance created a binding contract; Board's action had no legal effect; awarded reinstatement and compensatory damages. Court also granted petitioner's motion to dismiss without prejudice the tort/conspiracy count.
- Ohio Court of Appeals affirmed; Ohio Supreme Court denied review.
- Petitioner then filed (L2) in Federal District Court under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, alleging Board members violated her First, Fifth, and Fourteenth Amendment rights by not renewing her contract because of her work on a desegregation plan and social studies curriculum. Sought injunctive relief and damages.
- District Court granted summary judgment for defendants on claim preclusion grounds. Court of Appeals affirmed.

<!-- page: 24 -->

**Holding:**
- With respect to petitioner's § 1983 claim, which was not litigated in state court, petitioner's state court judgment has the same claim preclusive effect in federal court that the judgment would have in Ohio state courts.
- In the absence of federal law modifying 28 U.S.C. § 1738, the preclusive effect in federal court of petitioner's state court judgment is determined by **Ohio law**.
- § 1738 embodies the view that it is more important to give full faith and credit to state court judgments than to ensure separate forums for federal and state claims.
- § 1983 does not override state preclusion law and guarantee petitioner a right to proceed to judgment in state court on state claims and then turn to federal court for adjudication of federal claims.
- Case remanded for District Court to interpret Ohio preclusion law in the first instance.

**Simplified:**
- P won on state-law breach of contract in Ohio state court (L1).
- State court dismissed the tort count without prejudice.
- P brought § 1983 claim in federal court (L2) — "arising under" jurisdiction.
- We only care about the rendering court's type of court: it was a **state court** → § 1738 applies.
- Dispute: how Ohio defines "claim" for claim preclusion purposes.
- If Ohio would preclude, the federal court must also preclude.

**Irony:** § 1983 was enacted due to distrust of state courts, yet ordinary preclusion rules apply even in a subsequent § 1983 action; the state court's preclusion law governs.

<!-- page: 25 -->

### Analysis of Claim Preclusion in *Migra*

1. Same party — Yes
2. Same claim (transaction or occurrence) — Yes
3. Judgment that is both:
   - Final — Yes
   - On the merits — Yes (except the state tort claim, which was dismissed without prejudice)

Dispute = how to interpret "claim." → Look to Ohio state law to define "claim" for claim preclusion purposes.

### *Hart v. American Airlines, Inc.*, 304 N.Y.2d 810 (1969, NY Supreme Court)

If rendering courts would not preclude, then we have a question whether or not the subsequent court will preclude or not.

**Facts:**
- An American Airlines plane traveling from New York to Kentucky crashed, killing 58 people.
- Numerous lawsuits filed in various jurisdictions.
- One suit was brought in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Texas (L1).
- The Texas action ended in a judgment for plaintiffs (final and on the merits) — 5-week jury trial; key issue was whether American Airlines was at fault.
- Loretta Hart and other estate representatives (L2) brought suit against American Airlines in New York state court.
- Plaintiffs moved for summary judgment based on the Texas judgment.

<!-- page: 26 -->

**Holding and Reasoning (Frank, J.):**
- To invoke collateral estoppel (issue preclusion), the issues must be identical to the previously decided case and the party against whom the doctrine is invoked must have had a full and fair opportunity to contest the issues.
- This rule applies even if the subsequent case is in a different jurisdiction.
- Issue of American Airlines' liability is identical to what was decided in the Texas action. (Same issue)
- American Airlines had a full and fair opportunity to litigate its liability in Texas. (Actually litigated)
- Collateral estoppel applies.

\*\* Note: In Texas at the time, **mutuality was required** for issue preclusion to run.

**Simplified:**
- Rendering court: federal district court in Texas (L1)
- *Semtek* tells us: when rendering court is a federal court sitting in **diversity**, use the preclusion law of the state in which it sits → **Texas state law**

<!-- page: 27 -->

### Analysis of Issue Preclusion in *Hart*

1. Same issue — Yes
2. Actually litigated — Yes
3. Necessary to decision — Yes
4. Mutuality — Texas required mutuality; it was a **different plaintiff** → TX would **NOT preclude**

**BLL:**
- If rendering court would preclude → subsequent court must also preclude.
- If rendering court would NOT preclude → subsequent court has a question whether it will preclude or not. (*Hart*)

Since Texas would not preclude, New York state court can **choose** whether or not to preclude.

In *Hart*, the NY court chose to invoke issue preclusion (even though the rendering court would not), on policy grounds:
- Similar to abstention doctrine; similar to *forum non conveniens*
- Policy considerations: cost, time, long trial

\*\* Note: *Hart* was a NY state trial court judgment — persuasive but not binding except in NY trial courts.

## Intersystem Preclusion: When the Rendering Court is an Agency

<!-- page: 27 -->

The **Second Restatement of Judgments § 83** is the source explaining when a subsequent adjudicator should give preclusive effect to an agency adjudication:

<!-- page: 28 -->

Requirements for agency preclusion:
1. Notice
2. Opportunity to be heard

These requirements are similar to procedural due process.

**Federal Common Law (from the Restatement):** Both states and federal courts should structure common law such that when the rendering agency gives **sufficient procedural due process protections** to the litigant, that agency's judgment is entitled to receive **full faith and credit**.

All other requirements of claim and issue preclusion must still be met.

If the rendering agency is a **state agency**, the law of that rendering state defines the claim/issue preclusive effects of the agency's judgment.

Differences from judicial due process:
- No requirement for live hearing (hearing on the papers is fine)
- No requirement for any form of assistance/access to justice

**BLL: Generally, if agencies give sufficient procedural due process, they are entitled to receive full faith and credit on their preclusive effect.**
