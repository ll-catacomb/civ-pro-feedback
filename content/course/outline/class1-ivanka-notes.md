**Professor: Jim Greiner | TA: Ivanka D. Canzius | Sep 2, 2021**

The first seven of eight classes will discuss where a civil lawsuit can be brought in (in a systemic and geographic sense).

The first four of these will specifically discuss whether the court will begin in a federal trial court (called federal district courts) or state courts (the systemic beginning of filing a civil suit). We will specifically discuss the rules that decide this issue.

The latter three will focus on where it will begin geographically.

- The US government structure is different than that of many other countries.
- The US has state sovereigns and an overall federal sovereign; states are supposed to be sovereign within their spheres, and the federal government is supposed to be sovereign within its spheres of defined power.
- The Supremacy Clause (from the US Constitution) states that when there is a conflict between state law and federal law, the federal law wins because it is supreme.
- The original idea was that the states do most of the governing for their states, even though it hasn't quite worked out that way.
- We have a system of two sovereigns (states and federal government), and you are subject of the state in which you are operating, as well as subject to the federal law.

<!-- page: 2 -->

- Additionally, there are two types of court systems within our country: the state court system and the federal court system.
- This chart shows a little of the relationship between the two court systems.

### Federal Courts

- **US District Courts for the \_\_\_\_\_ of the \_\_\_\_\_\_\_**
  - These are Federal trial courts, analogous to state trial courts
  - Can hear evidence
  - Can sit juries
  - Defined within regions
  - None cross state lines
  - May have more than one in each state

- **US Court of Appeals for the \_\_\_\_th Circuit**
  - Federal intermediate appellate courts, analogous to State Intermediate Appellate Courts
  - Regionally defined
  - Ordinarily encompass more than one state (except for DC, which is treated like a state for these purposes)
  - Circuits are intermediate appellate courts:
    - Cannot hear testimony
    - Cannot try cases

- **United States Supreme Court**
  - Highest court in the United States

### State Courts

- **State Trial Courts**
  - Generally known as courts of general jurisdiction (except for those issues that Congress determines are exclusively under federal jurisdiction — e.g., Sherman Act (antitrust cases), patent/copyright litigation, lawsuits against the United States)
  - Frequently has subdivisions within it (Housing, Criminal, Family, etc.)
  - Some can sit juries, some cannot:
    - No jury in family courts
    - No jury in housing courts
    - Some juries available in some criminal courts
  - Geographically defined within the state

- **State Intermediate Appellate Courts**
  - Geographically defined within the state
  - Not trial courts, only appellate review

<!-- page: 3 -->

  - No hearing evidence
  - No sitting juries

- **State High Courts**
  - Geographically defined within the state
  - Not trial courts, only appellate review
    - No hearing evidence
    - No sitting juries
  - Frequently, though not always, referred to as the State Supreme Courts

- **United States Supreme Court**
  - Highest court in the United States
  - Sits above both state and federal court systems

- Just like there are federal and states' court systems, there is both federal law and state law (in fact, 50 different states' laws).
- The Supreme Court sits above both state and federal court systems because federal law issues can begin in either the state or federal court systems.
- Being in the FEDERAL COURT SYSTEM tells one nothing about the issues being decided in that court, or whether they are federal law issues or state law issues.
- Federal law issues that began in a state court system may be appealed all the way up to the Supreme Court (dotted/dashed line); however, state law issues that are appealed typically get the final word by the State High Court (and do not reach the US Supreme Court, because the US Supreme Court is a federal court, and typically does not have authority over state law (*Erie*)).
- If a case begins in the federal court system, however, and involves state law issues, it still stays in the federal court system.

**Solid lines v. dashed lines**
- Solid lines indicate that an appeal is "as of right" — if a party loses in the relevant court system, it has the right to appeal (if it believes there was an error of law) to the next higher court, and the next higher court has to adjudicate on that case.
- Dashed lines indicate that the appeal is not "as of right" — in order to get the case heard or adjudicated in that higher court, it must first ask the court to hear the appeal, and the higher court can either agree to or decline to hear (a Petition to hear the case).
  - Same situation on the federal side.
  - For the Supreme Court, it is called a Petition for Certiorari (or Cert Petition).

- The chart above is about the identity of the court system, not about the law that the relevant system is trying, as both federal and state laws are tried in state and federal courts.

## Day 1 – When Is It Permissible to Begin a Case in a Federal District Court?

**(Federal Court Subject Matter Jurisdiction)**

For the next four classes, we will be exploring the answer to this question.

**Statutory grants of power to be discussed:**
- Arising Under Jurisdiction
- Diversity Jurisdiction
- Supplemental Jurisdiction (only applicable if premised on Arising Under Jurisdiction)

**Intro to a lawsuit, subject matter jurisdiction (of the federal court system) — Part 1**
- Stages of a civil lawsuit
- Constitutional versus statutory basis of subject matter jurisdiction
- Federal question/arising under subject matter jurisdiction
- Well-pleaded complaint rule
- Declaratory judgment as an exception to the well-pleaded complaint rule

A case must begin in a trial court. The state courts are generally willing to hear any type of dispute, whether dealing with federal law or state law issues, except under circumstances where the federal courts have exclusive jurisdiction as directed by Congress.

The state courts can hear just about any case, but the doctrine of Subject Matter Jurisdiction of the Federal District Courts will explain when it is appropriate for the federal courts to hear the case as well.

This doctrine does not just focus on the type of subject matter that is litigated in the court, although sometimes such can be the focus, and happens to be the focus when there is a cause of action that is created by federal law (what is referred to as "arising under"), even though the actual cause of action was created by state law.

### Background Cases, Statutes, and References

#### Cases

- ***Cooper v. Aaron*, 358 U.S. 1 (1958) (SCOTUS) — Supreme Court says what the law is**
  School (Cooper) asked to get approval of a delay to desegregate. SCOTUS denied. They held that the state officials and state legislatures are bound by orders of the SCOTUS based on its interpretation of the US Constitution.

- ***Osborn v. Bank of the United States*, 22 U.S. (9 Wheat.) 738 (1824) (SCOTUS) — Arising Under Jurisdiction**
  Federal courts have original jurisdiction over any matter that involves a federal question (any case which arises under (federal) laws of the United States — see U.S. Const. Art. III, § 2, cl. 1 for full list). Case explains that we interpret the "arising under" language in the Constitution much more broadly than the same language found in 28 U.S.C. § 1331. Justice Marshall explains here that the constitutional language is much more

<!-- page: 5 -->

  broader than Justice Moody's interpretation of the 28 U.S.C. § 1331 language. And, to this date, we are not sure exactly how broad the constitutional language is.

- ***Moore v. Chesapeake & Ohio Railway Co.*, 291 U.S. 205 (1934) (SCOTUS) — What does not fit within the scope of Arising Under Jurisdiction**
  A suit brought under a state statute which defines liability to employees who are injured while engaged in intrastate commerce, and brings within the purview of the statute a breach of the duty imposed by the federal statute, should not be regarded as a suit arising under the laws of the United States and cognizable in the federal court in the absence of diversity of citizenship.

#### Statutes

- **28 U.S.C. § 1331 — Arising Under Jurisdiction**
  The (federal) district courts shall have original jurisdiction of all civil actions arising under the Constitution, laws, or treaties of the United States.

- **28 U.S.C. § 2201(a) — Creation of a Remedy**
  In a case of actual controversy within its jurisdiction, except with respect to Federal taxes other than actions brought under section 7428 of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986, any court of the United States, upon the filing of an appropriate pleading, may declare the rights and other legal relations of any interested party seeking such declaration, whether or not further relief is or could be sought. Any such declaration shall have the force and effect of a final judgment or decree and shall be reviewable as such.

#### Constitutional References

- **U.S. Constitution, Article III, § 1 and § 2, cl. 1:**

  **Section 1:** The judicial power of the United States, shall be vested in one Supreme Court, and in such inferior courts as the Congress may from time to time ordain and establish. The judges, both of the supreme and inferior courts, shall hold their offices during good behavior, and shall, at stated times, receive for their services, a compensation, which shall not be diminished during their continuance in office.

  **Section 2, cl. 1:** The judicial power shall extend to all cases, in law and equity, arising under this Constitution, the laws of the United States, and treaties made, or which shall be made, under their authority;--to all cases affecting ambassadors, other public ministers and consuls;--to all cases of admiralty and maritime jurisdiction;--to controversies to which the United States shall be a party;--to controversies between two or more states;--between a state and citizens of another state;--between citizens of different states;--between citizens of the same state claiming lands under grants of different states, and between a state, or the citizens thereof, and foreign states, citizens or subjects.

- ***Tafflin v. Levitt*, 493 U.S. 455 — Presumption of Concurrent Jurisdiction (CB 273, note 4)**
  When Congress enacts a federal statute, the presumption is that both the federal and state courts have competence to enforce the law — referred to as the presumption of concurrent jurisdiction. Congress may confer jurisdiction exclusively on the federal courts, but the presumption of concurrency can be rebutted only "by an explicit statutory directive, by unmistakable implication from legislative history, or by a clear incompatibility between state-court jurisdiction and federal interests."

### Main Cases

- ***Louisville & Nashville Railroad v. Mottley*, 211 U.S. 149 (1908) (SCOTUS) — Federal Jurisdiction**

  **BLL 1:** For a suit to arise under the Constitution and laws of the United States, giving a federal court jurisdiction to hear the case, a plaintiff must allege a federal cause of action based upon those laws or that Constitution (not merely anticipating a federal defense). (In other words, if a case begins in the wrong court system (like in this breach of contract (state law) case, they brought it in federal court), you cannot fix it later. Case must begin in a court of proper jurisdiction (contracts, torts, property = state law.))

  **BLL 2:** A federal court must notice independently on its own (*sua sponte*) whether it has proper subject matter jurisdiction. If it lacks jurisdiction, a federal court MUST DISMISS THE CASE. This is true even if the parties both agree that the case can be heard in federal court.

  **BLL 3:** A case arises under federal law only if there is a FEDERAL CAUSE OF ACTION. If it is a STATE LAW CAUSE OF ACTION, it does not matter whether there are federal issues that arise in the defense; what matters only is the initial CAUSE OF ACTION.

  In order for "arising under" jurisdiction to be granted, a plaintiff's statement must show that her original cause of action arises under the Constitution or a federal law. It is not sufficient that the plaintiff anticipates that the defendant will raise a federal statute in defense. Instead, the plaintiff's "well pleaded complaint" must state that the defendant directly violated some provision of the Constitution, laws or treaties of the United States.

  ***Note:*** Even though the Supreme Court would generally have jurisdiction over cases that began in state courts just as well as in federal courts, because this one began in a federal court, without an "arising under" cause of action (without a statute enacted by Congress authorizing a federal court to hear this case), the SCOTUS did not have the authority to adjudicate the case as an appellate federal court because it lacked subject matter jurisdiction.

<!-- page: 7 -->

  Article III, Section 2, Clause 1 provides that federal courts have jurisdiction over all cases "arising under" federal law. This is a very broad interpretation and would give the federal courts jurisdiction over too many cases. To limit even further the cases federal courts can hear, Congress has the power to control that jurisdiction as it seems fit through statutory enactments. Here, 28 U.S.C. § 1331 was created, and gave a narrower interpretation, limiting the cases federal courts can hear.

  Although SCOTUS reversed the trial court's decision based on the lack of jurisdiction in *Mottley*, they could still hear the case under Article III, because the federal court may still use the broader Constitution Article III version of "arising under" language where "the issue must be important enough to warrant bringing the case to federal (as opposed to state) court, and of a type where federal adjudication will benefit the administration of national policy and not overburden the federal courts going forward." But, unless there is a grant of authority enacted by Congress, the claim must be brought through state court.

  The Mottleys re-filed a complaint in state court and it reached the Supreme Court once again, through the state courts chain. The court used the Article III meaning of "arising under" to hear the *Mottley* case again because the issue was important enough to warrant bringing the case to federal (as opposed to state) court (remember: state courts can hear federal law issues as well).

- ***Grable & Sons Metal Products, Inc. v. Darue Engineering & Manufacturing*, 545 U.S. 308 (2005) (SCOTUS) — Federal Jurisdiction over State Cause of Action Exception**

  A federal court may have jurisdiction over a state cause of action, if the action has a substantial federal component in actual controversy (in the cause of action), and federal jurisdiction would not disrupt the balance of labor between state and federal courts.

  In other words, a state-law cause of action may give rise to federal question jurisdiction, if the case relies on the application and analysis of a federal law. A state-law cause of action may give rise to a federal question, and thereby give the federal court "arising under" jurisdiction, if the right to relief is based in the application of federal law. In order for "arising under" jurisdiction to be proper, the federal law must actually be in controversy, and this controversy must be substantial. Furthermore, the court must consider whether taking the case would upset the balance of labor between state and federal courts. **(Smith-Grable Exception)**

- ***Smith v. Kansas City Title & Trust Co.*, 255 U.S. 180 (1920) (SCOTUS)**
  For purposes of "arising under" jurisdiction, a case arises under the Constitution if a plaintiff's right to relief depends on the court's interpretation of the Constitution.

- ***First Federal Savings & Loan Association of Bowling Green, Kentucky v. McReynolds*, 297 F. Supp. 1159 (W.D. Ken. 1969) — Federal Jurisdiction**

<!-- page: 8 -->

  "Declaratory action may be entertained in federal court only if the coercive action which would have been brought, absent declaratory judgment procedure, might have been so brought."

### Lecture: Why Would a Plaintiff Care Where a Case Is Initiated?

- Plaintiffs care where cases are initiated even if they believe their case will end up in the SCOTUS.
- A plaintiff might care if they believe that the federal court is more familiar with federal law, and the state courts might be more familiar with state law.
- Federal courts and state courts can resolve federal law and state law. The character of the court one is in can be different than the law it is resolving.
- If plaintiffs believe that their case is such that it would benefit if the judge is familiar with the type of law being litigated.
- Plaintiffs might feel that a certain judge might be more amicable than another. Even though one may not be able to pick a specific judge, one might have an idea of what the judicial panel looks like in a certain area.

**How do federal judges get a seat?**
- Federal judges are nominated and appointed by the president with the consent of Congress
  - Have tenure
  - Can be removed only if they are impeached
    - House of Representatives has to "impeach" (take the official to court)
    - Senate holds trial to find someone "guilty"
- State judges are elected
  - May be more influence of politics since they are elected

In *Cooper v. Aaron*, it was not a close call where the plaintiffs may want to file the case, due to the time in which the case was being litigated. In their case, it was important to have the protection of an appointed official rather than an elected one.

Depending on where the federal district court is geographically located, they would draw jury panels from the entire state, and others from just a county. For this reason, there may be a predictable difference in the demographics of the jury being selected for a specific trial. These demographics may help the probabilities of judge being pro or against plaintiffs, etc. This is an important concept to remember when representing clients in court.

In the next few classes, we will discuss "when" there is a choice of where to file.

### Constitutional and Statutory Bases for Federal District Court Jurisdiction

**Diagram description:** Concentric ovals showing jurisdictional scope from broadest to narrowest:
1. Outer region (orange oval): U.S. Const. Art. 3, sec. 2, cl. 1 — "any federal issue" — **State courts only** handle matters outside the inner rings
2. Middle ring (green oval): "arising under," sec. 1331 — well-pleaded complaint
3. Inner circle (solid green): Exclusive federal court jurisdiction

**BLL:** State courts can hear any matter, including those that include "arising under" federal issues. However, in order for federal courts to hear a case, there needs to be § 1331 arising under power, and we need to be within the small circle (meaning, there needs to be a statute enacted by Congress that creates the cause of action). Thus, the **"well-pleaded complaint rule"** states that in order for there to be federal jurisdiction over a case, the complaint must allege a § 1331 arising under issue in the complaint itself, and must include the statute created by Congress which states such issue can be brought in federal court (as opposed to presupposing an affirmative defense).

### Discussion of *Mottley*

- The railroad made a contract (**contract law is usually a state law issue, along with tort law, property law, and some criminal law**) with the Mottleys and gave them the lifetime passes in exchange for settling out of court for a previous injury. A new law passed, and after over 30 years, the railroad company stopped allowing them to use the passes due to the new law that passed.
- Mottleys filed suit against the defendants for breach of contract (state law cause of action) in federal court.

  **Causes of action in the Complaint:**
  1. Breach of contract

  **Two issues come up:**
  1. Mottleys want the Railroad to honor their passes
     - a. Railroad's **Affirmative Defense (Answer)** claims that federal law says they cannot comply
       - i. If that is true, Mottleys then respond to Railroad's Answer and claim that if the Railroad is in fact correct about the federal law prohibiting them to honor the contract, then their (the Mottleys') 5th Amendment's due process rights (due to the removal of their

<!-- page: 10 -->

       property) are violated if the federal law does prohibit the railroad from honoring the passes.

- Because jurisdiction comes from the original cause of action, and the cause of action was a state law cause of action, the federal trial court lacked jurisdiction.
  - **BLL: Any kind of appellate court has jurisdiction ONLY IF the trial court had jurisdiction to begin with.**
  - **BLL: If a federal court believes it lacks jurisdiction, it must pause litigation, and must determine whether it does or does not have subject matter jurisdiction (SMJ), and if it lacks jurisdiction, it MUST dismiss or remand the case back to state court, regardless of how far up the chain the case has reached.**
    - **In *Mottley*, neither party had contested jurisdiction. Nonetheless, the federal court has an INDEPENDENT DUTY to determine whether it has or lacks jurisdiction and can raise it "*sua sponte*".**

- ***NOTE:*** In most cases, if one of the parties does not bring up an issue, the court does not bring it up. In some cases even, if an issue is not brought up at the right time, the court will not allow the parties to bring it up later. With **SMJ**, this is different. The court has an INDEPENDENT DUTY to examine whether it has jurisdiction over the case being litigated.

- The Supreme Court ended up adjudicating this case in the end anyway, when it was brought up through the state court chain, because as an appellate court with appellate review authority only, when reviewing the case that had initially begun in the federal court when the federal court lacked jurisdiction, it did not have the authority to adjudicate.
  - Supreme Court was thinking of precedent and **public policy** in this case. They did not want the system to be circumvented and wanted to discourage "forum shopping" within the court system.

Justice Moody began to quote the statute, and stated that because *Mottley* was not premised on Diversity Jurisdiction, the court lacked subject matter jurisdiction because it did not "arise under the Constitution," as it mentions in the statute that it should.

- There are two federal issues in this case:
  1. Whether the federal statute did in fact apply in this case, prohibiting the railroad to honor the lifetime passes; and
  2. Whether if it applied, it violated the Mottleys' 5th Amendment rights to due process prior to removing their property.

Justice Moody's interpretation of the statute is that the **original cause of action** being asserted by the plaintiff has to arise under federal law, and that it is a **FEDERALLY**

<!-- page: 11 -->

**CREATED CAUSE OF ACTION (i.e., NOT BREACH OF CONTRACT) (known as the "Well Pleaded Complaint Rule").**

The court then further discusses, interprets and decides that the statute's ("arising under" language) limits are narrower than the Constitutional ("arising under" language) limits. Because, if the constitutional limits were the same as the statutory limits, then the Supreme Court, as a federal court, could not then adjudicate over state law cause of action claims; which would be disastrous.

**BLL: The Constitutional Article III, Section 2 phrase, therefore, must mean something different than the same phrase in the statute (28 U.S.C. § 1331).**

- However, it is not self-executing:
  - The Article III, Section 2 judicial "grant of power" is not granted to the judiciary, but instead granted to Congress, and they have to pass a statute invoking and effectuating the power, so that federal courts can hear the case.
  - If Congress has not given the lower federal courts power to hear a certain type of case (enacted a statute), then the lower federal courts cannot hear it. If there is a federal cause of action, but not a statute granting power to the federal courts to hear it, then there is still a federal cause of action, but the case goes to the state courts.
  - In other words, a federal court only has subject matter jurisdiction if two things happen:
    - If the cause of action arises under the Constitutional Power; **AND**
    - If Congress has enacted a statute that allows the federal courts to hear the cause of action.
      - The rationale behind this is that the Constitution creates Congress and has given Congress the authority to create the courts and the laws; so the courts are beholden to Congress' laws; and thus, Congress must create a law (enact a statute) before federal courts can have arising under jurisdiction over a certain cause of action.

**Public policy and precedent:** The Supreme Court wanted to keep federalism, separation of powers and effectuate the principle of checks and balances. The overwhelming business of the government should be done by the states. That way, the federal courts could keep a limit on which cases they could adjudicate.

Just like *Mottley* defines that the limits of the statute (28 U.S.C. § 1331) are narrower than those of the US Constitution "arising under" language, *Osborn* then attempts to define what the limits of the "arising under" language is within the Constitution.

After creating a clean rule that states that to bring a case to a federal court all you need to have is a federal cause of action (*Mottley*), the courts then immediately create an exception to it (Smith-Grable Exception).

### Smith-Grable Exception

**1) A federal issue:**
- (a) embedded within a state-law cause of action that is
- (b) substantial, and
- (c) disputed

**2) Balance among the following factors:**
- Federal interest at stake (significance for federal system)
- Balance of federal court versus state court business
- Law versus fact (from *Empire*) (??)
