## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases (Common Law)

- ***Marrese v. American Academy of Orthopedic Surgeons*, 470 U.S. 373 (1985)**

  If the adjudicator in Lawsuit 1 lacked (subject matter jurisdictional) power to adjudicate certain theories of relief, we will not *claim preclude* in Lawsuit 2, even if all *claim preclusion* elements are met otherwise.

- ***Cromwell v. County of Sac*, 94 U.S. (4 Otto) 351 (SCOTUS)(1876)**

  Where a second action is between the same parties and involves the same transaction or occurrence as an initial judgment, that judgment does not bar the second action if the second action is upon an issue that was not adjudicated in the initial action.

- ***Russell v. Place*, 94 U.S. (4 Otto) 606 (1876)(SCOTUS)**

  Collateral estoppel (issue preclusion) will not prohibit a party from raising an issue that was asserted in a previous action between the same parties if it is unclear from the face of the record or extrinsic evidence that the specific issue was actually decided.

- ***Rios v. Davis*, 373 S.W.2d 386 (Tx Ct Civ App) (1963)**

  If findings of fact in a prior action are not essential to the judgment, then a later action, which is based on those facts, is not subject to issue preclusion or claim preclusion.

- ***Blonder-Tongue Laboratories v. University of Illinois Foundation*, 402 U.S. 313 (SCOTUS)(1971)**

  A defendant in a subsequent action may invoke nonmutual issue preclusion against a plaintiff who lost on the same issue in a prior action if the plaintiff had a full and fair opportunity to litigate.

### Federal Rules of Civil Procedure

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 41 — Dismissal of Actions

**(a) Voluntary Dismissal.**

**(1) By the Plaintiff.**

> **(A) Without a Court Order.** Subject to Rules 23(e), 23.1(c), 23.2, and 66 and any applicable federal statute, the plaintiff may dismiss an action without a court order by filing:
>
> > (i) a notice of dismissal before the opposing party serves either an answer or a motion for summary judgment; or
> >
> > (ii) a stipulation of dismissal signed by all parties who have appeared.
>
> **(B) Effect.** Unless the notice or stipulation states otherwise, the dismissal is without prejudice. But if the plaintiff previously dismissed any federal- or state-court action based on or including the same claim, a notice of dismissal operates as an adjudication on the merits.

<!-- page: 3 -->

**(2) By Court Order; Effect.** Except as provided in Rule 41(a)(1), an action may be dismissed at the plaintiff's request only by court order, on terms that the court considers proper. If a defendant has pleaded a counterclaim before being served with the plaintiff's motion to dismiss, the action may be dismissed over the defendant's objection only if the counterclaim can remain pending for independent adjudication. Unless the order states otherwise, a dismissal under this paragraph (2) is without prejudice.

**(b) Involuntary Dismissal; Effect.** If the plaintiff fails to prosecute or to comply with these rules or a court order, a defendant may move to dismiss the action or any claim against it. Unless the dismissal order states otherwise, a dismissal under this subdivision (b) and any dismissal not under this rule—except one for lack of jurisdiction, improper venue, or failure to join a party under Rule 19—operates as an adjudication on the merits.

**(c) Dismissing a Counterclaim, Crossclaim, or Third-Party Claim.** This rule applies to a dismissal of any counterclaim, crossclaim, or third-party claim. A claimant's voluntary dismissal under Rule 41(a)(1)(A)(i) must be made:

> (1) before a responsive pleading is served; or
>
> (2) if there is no responsive pleading, before evidence is introduced at a hearing or trial.

**(d) Costs of a Previously Dismissed Action.** If a plaintiff who previously dismissed an action in any court files an action based on or including the same claim against the same defendant, the court:

> (1) may order the plaintiff to pay all or part of the costs of that previous action; and
>
> (2) may stay the proceedings until the plaintiff has complied.

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 42 — Consolidation; Separate Trials

**(a) Consolidation.** If actions before the court involve a common question of law or fact, the court may:

> (1) join for hearing or trial any or all matters at issue in the actions;
>
> (2) consolidate the actions; or
>
> (3) issue any other orders to avoid unnecessary cost or delay.

<!-- page: 4 -->

**(b) Separate Trials.** For convenience, to avoid prejudice, or to expedite and economize, the court may order a separate trial of one or more separate issues, claims, crossclaims, counterclaims, or third-party claims. When ordering a separate trial, the court must preserve any federal right to a jury trial.

## Lecture Recap for Day 20

We spent the last class period discussing claim preclusion and how claim preclusion works. We dived into the first two of its elements:

### Analysis for Claim Preclusion

1. **Same party (or in privity)**
2. **Same claim (same transaction or occurrence)**
   - i. Related in time
   - ii. Related in space
   - iii. Are they similar issues?
   - iv. Will there be common evidence?
   - v. What would be the policy implications if we were to interpret "same claim" this broadly?
3. **A judgment that is both**
   - a. Final; AND
   - b. On the merits.

*Plus other stuff:*
- Rule in *Marrese*
- Rendering court can limit claim preclusive effect of judgment

We used *Gonzalez* as the primary teaching case where we had an alleged land swindle. We decided that they were not claim precluded because they were not the same party, nor were they in privity with the *Rios'* plaintiffs (as the *Gonzalez* plaintiffs did not exercise substantial control nor were they successors in interest of the *Rios'* earlier litigation).

However, we did go over some examples in which parties were "in privity":

1. "**Same party**" requirement is not literal.
   - Parties do not have to be formally the same, they could be in privity.
     - **Privity = "substantial control," or "successor in interest"**

<!-- page: 5 -->

   - In the background of this element is the Due Process Clause, basically saying that we will not preclude people in litigation who are not themselves parties, so as to not deprive them of the opportunity to be heard, which would violate the Due Process Clause.

We also moved on to discuss Claim Preclusion Element 2 — what does "same claim" mean?

> ***Notice the meaning here is different than it was in the appellate process sense, where "same claim" meant "a theory of relief, tied to a specific set of facts, against a particular defendant."***

2. **Same claim (same transaction or occurrence)**
   - i. Related in time
   - ii. Related in space
   - iii. Are they similar issues?
   - iv. Will there be common evidence?
   - v. What would be the policy implications if we were to interpret "same claim" this broadly?

In *Gonzales'* dictum, the court mentioned that the swindle with different plots of land were the "same transaction or occurrence" and we questioned that rule based on the understanding of the bonds and coupons concept:

**BLL: Coupons on the bonds can be clipped and become separate promises, and therefore, each coupon and each bond in a transaction is considered a separate "transaction or occurrence" for claim preclusion purposes, even if bonds or coupons are:**
- **Bought or issued on the same day;**
- **Sold by the same company;**
- **Or bought by the same buyer.**
  - (Because we believe that making them the same transaction or occurrence would hurt the market for bonds or coupons, or investment purposes in general.)

<!-- page: 6 -->

By analogy, we decided that in the *Gonzalez* case, it is not so clear that the plots of land should be thought of as being of the same transaction or occurrence because once the land is subdivided, we might want the plots to represent different claims in the claim preclusion context.

We also went over the complicated ways in which the same Transaction or Occurrence test interacts with policy preferences with respect to **installment payments, provided that there are no acceleration clauses involved**:

- **General rule:** any payments missed as of the date of the filing of the lawsuit are of the same Transaction or Occurrence, but any future payments in that same installment, that may or may not be missed, are not part of the same Transaction or Occurrence for *claim preclusion* purposes.
  - Policy favors the plaintiff bringing lawsuits with all of the past payments because they will have a lot of facts in common and can be resolved efficiently and expeditiously. However, we do not want to bring in future payments because we do not know if they will be missed or not by the alleged debtor.

We also discussed how this general rule changes if there was an **installment payment contract with an acceleration clause** in the *Jones* case:

- **General rule:** Once the first payment is missed in an installment contract that has an acceleration clause, all of the other payments are immediately due, thus making them all missed, and therefore part of the same Transaction or Occurrence for *claim preclusion* purposes (lawsuit needs to include all of them, and they will all be claim-precluded).

In the lecture today, we will discuss the last two elements of *claim preclusion* and specifically, what it means for a judgment to be "final" and "on the merits."

## Lecture for Day 21

Today we will discuss the third element of *claim preclusion*, that a judgment be "**final and on the merits**", and some additional "stuff" that the courts do not add as elements but that they generally require or that are relevant with respect to *claim preclusion*:

**3) A judgment that is both**
   - **a. Final; AND**
   - **b. On the merits.**

The confusing part is that "*Final*" does not always mean final in the ordinary way in which people think it means, and "*Merits*" does not always mean merits.

<!-- page: 7 -->

### Hypo 1: "Finality" When Case Is on Appeal

- Plaintiff sues Defendant (Greiner) for horrible civil procedure class (L1)
  - Ruined the education experience
  - Inflicted emotional distress
- AIC is $100K
- Case is litigated; Defendant Greiner prevails
- Judgment on jury verdict for Defendant; Plaintiff appeals
- As the appeal progresses, Plaintiff files another lawsuit (L2)
- Defendant pleads claim preclusion and moves for Summary Judgment

**Analysis:**

1. Same party (formally or in privity) — Yes
2. Same claim (Transaction or Occurrence) — Yes
3. A judgment that is both:
   - **Final** — yes, *because a trial court judgment is sufficiently final for claim preclusion purposes, even if case is on appeal*; AND
   - **On the merits** — yes

**"Finality" BLL: a *trial court judgment* is *sufficiently final,* even if on appeal.**

Advantages of determining that L1 judgment is *not* sufficiently final:
- Appeal may reverse the trial court judgment

Advantages of determining that L1 judgment *is* sufficiently final:
- Interest in efficiency and expediency in the litigation
- Resources have been expended and used
- Policy favors trusting trial courts to get things right most of the time

<!-- page: 8 -->

In *appellate jurisdiction* in the appellate system (**28 U.S.C. § 1291**), there is a strong preference to wait to file appeals until the end of the trial case. Because of this, we presume that the trial court likely gets the cases right. In *claim preclusion*, we feel the same, and that a trial judgment is *sufficiently final*, even if on appeal.

### "On the Merits" Hypos

**Upcoming Hypos — Stipulations:**
- Same parties
- Same claim
- Judgment is final as to the litigation
- Question is whether the judgment is "*on the merits*"

**Hypo 1:** Jury verdict — **Yes.** Jury made the judgment, which always implies that it is on the merits.

> ***Anytime that there is a jury or a judge entering a judgment on a verdict or a regular judgment (if no jury verdict), it is unquestionably *on the merits****

**Hypo 2:** Summary Judgment — **Yes.** Although it is less than a jury trial, it is still *sufficiently on the merits* for *claim preclusion* to kick in.

**Hypo 3:** Motion to Dismiss under FRCP 12(b)(6) — **Yes.** **Motion to Dismiss for *failure to state a claim* under FRCP 12(b)(6) is considered on the merits.**

<!-- page: 9 -->

**Hypo 4:** Dismissal for lack of subject matter jurisdiction — **No.** Court could not reach the merits due to lack of subject matter jurisdiction.

- **Dismissal for lack of SMJ does not reach the merits.**
- **Dismissal for lack of personal jurisdiction does not reach the merits.**
- **Dismissal for inability to lay venue does not reach the merits.**

**Hypo 5:** Dismissal for failure to prosecute (plaintiff not being ready or diligent) — **Yes.** Dismissal for failure to prosecute the lawsuit will generally be considered to be "sufficiently on the merits."

- Settlement agreements are also considered to be "sufficiently on the merits" for claim preclusion purposes.

<!-- page: 10 -->

**Hypo 6 (Intersystem Preclusion):** Dismissal on Statute of Limitations grounds — **Courts (state law) differ on this issue.** (In practice, one would have to look up the specific state law.)

**Hypo 7 (Sometimes within same system Preclusion):** SOL dismissal within same jurisdiction — **Again, courts (state law) differ on this issue.**

### BLL for "on the merits"

What is "obviously" on the merits:
- Trial court jury verdict and final judgments
- Bench (by a judge alone) trial court judgments
- Summary Motion Judgments
- Motions to Dismiss Judgments

Sometimes we have things that do not actually reach the merits, which are still considered to be "on the merits":
- Dismissals for failure to prosecute
- Dismissals for failure to comply with limitations (differs by state law)

<!-- page: 11 -->

For this reason (because "*on the merits*" sometimes does not mean actually on the merits), some jurisdictions do not use the phrase "on the merits" but most do. Despite the effort in the Second Restatement of Judgements to eliminate the term, state courts continue to use it. For our purposes, although we will use it in this class, keep in mind that it will not be interpreted "textually."

#### *Marrese v. American Academy of Orthopedic Surgeons*, 470 U.S. 373 (1985)

**BLL: If the adjudicator in Lawsuit 1 lacked (subject matter jurisdictional) power to adjudicate certain theories of relief, we will not *claim preclude* in Lawsuit 2, even if all *claim preclusion* elements are met otherwise.**

**Facts:**
- Marrese was an orthopedic surgeon who, along with another surgeon (plaintiffs), was denied membership in the American Academy of Orthopedic Surgeons (Academy) (defendant).
- The plaintiffs brought suit in Illinois state court, alleging violation of their associational rights.
- The state court dismissed the claim.
- The plaintiffs then brought this action in federal court, alleging that the Academy violated federal antitrust laws.
- The court of appeals held that federal claim preclusion—the full faith and credit rule—barred the federal suit because Illinois antitrust law was similar to federal antitrust law.
- The plaintiffs appealed.

**Holding:**
- In determining whether a state court judgment precludes a federal claim, federal courts must first look to the state's preclusion law and if the state law would not bar the federal action, then the federal action is not precluded.
- This is true even if the federal claim is within the exclusive jurisdiction of the federal courts.
- Thus, a state court judgment does not have preclusive effect on a federal claim if the federal claim could not have been raised in the state proceeding.
- In this case, the court of appeals precluded the plaintiffs' federal claim based on federal claim preclusion. This was improper as the court should have looked to Illinois law first.
- The case is therefore remanded for a determination on Illinois state preclusion law.

**Concurrence (Burger, C.J.):** Although the majority's holding is correct, it does not go far enough. The Court should have proposed a federal rule to resolve this issue when it arises in the future.

<!-- page: 13 -->

To make this case easier to understand: two orthopedic surgeons applied to join the "cool doctor's club" (the Academy) and were denied. They determined the denial was anticompetitive behavior in violation of the **Sherman Antitrust Act**.

**Sherman Antitrust Act:** § 1 states that "[e]very contract, combination in the form of trust or otherwise, or conspiracy, in restraint of trade or commerce among the several States, or with foreign nations, is declared to be illegal." § 2 prohibits monopolization or attempts at monopolizing any aspect of interstate trade or commerce.

Two possible avenues:
1. An IL state law that says that agreements in restraint of trade are illegal.
2. Federal law says that agreements in restraint of trade are illegal (*Sherman Act*)

The Sherman Antitrust Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 1-2) is an **exclusive jurisdiction** cause of action — only federal courts can hear it.

<!-- page: 14 -->

The lawyers brought suit (L1) in IL state court with only the IL state antitrust cause of action. After jury verdict for the County, they filed (L2) in federal court stating the Sherman antitrust cause of action.

**Analysis for claim preclusion:**
1. Same party (or in privity) — Yes
2. Same claim (same transaction or occurrence) — Yes
3. A judgment that is both:
   - Final — yes
   - On the merits — yes (jury trial)

*Plus other stuff:*
- Rule in *Marrese*
- Rendering court can limit claim preclusive effect of judgment

BUT — The court in L1 (state court of IL) could not, even if it would have wanted to, have litigated the Sherman Antitrust Act cause of action because it is reserved under exclusive jurisdiction of the federal courts. It lacked subject matter jurisdiction.

**Marrese BLL: If the adjudicator in Lawsuit 1 lacked (subject matter jurisdictional) power to adjudicate certain theories of relief, we will not *claim preclude* in Lawsuit 2, even if all *claim preclusion* elements are met otherwise.**

<!-- page: 15 -->

**Analysis for Supplemental Jurisdiction:**
- Do we have an anchoring claim that has an independent basis of SMJ — yes (Sherman Act claim)
- It is the same case or controversy — yes
- Claim by a plaintiff, against a person named a party, where the exercise of such jurisdiction would be inconsistent with the diversity statute? — No (the anchoring claim is arising under, so using Supplemental Jurisdiction here would not conflict with the diversity SMJ)

So the rule in *Marrese* does not seem efficient in this case — we could have a rule that says that if there is a place that one can go to get all theories of relief adjudicated at once one has to, otherwise one would be claim precluded — **we do not have that rule.** We are still working under the rule in *Marrese*.

#### Rendering Court Can Limit Preclusive Effect of Judgment

**Hypo:** P sues Greiner in MA state court alleging two different theories of relief. Case goes to judgment. Court awards P $50K on tort theory. Court dismisses the contract theory claim "**without prejudice**." P refiles the breach of contract theory a month later.

**Question:** Is P claim precluded?

- **No, because it was dismissed "*without prejudice.*"**

The rendering court (L1) can limit the claim preclusive effect of its own judgment by explicitly and expressly using the words "*dismissed without prejudice*."
- Although it cannot expand it (*Semtek* — to be discussed later).

<!-- page: 16 -->

Is it possible that even if claim preclusion can't run (because of the prior jurisdictional competency rule in *Marrese*), could *issue preclusion* be able to run?

- Maybe yes. We need to see if it will otherwise satisfy the requirements of *issue preclusion*.

## Issue Preclusion

**Elements of Issue Preclusion:**

1. Same issue
2. Actually litigated, AND
3. Necessary to decision
4. ??mutuality?? (depends on applicable state law)

**Plus other stuff:**
- Judgement of sufficient quality/finality
- Sufficient opportunity, incentive to litigate
- Only preclude party who lost in L1, or those in privity
- Only issues litigated which are FACTUAL (for legal issues, we rely on *staré decisis* or precedent)

<!-- page: 17 -->

### General Rules Regarding the "Other Stuff"

- In *issue preclusion*, we only generally preclude parties who **lost** on that specific issue in L1 (note that although in *claim preclusion* it did not matter, here, it does.)
  - Rules of *privity* also apply here
- We will not issue preclude people who **were not parties formally or in privity** with those who litigated the issue in L1
  - Doing so would violate **Due Process** requirements of *Notice and Opportunity to be Heard*
- In order for issue preclusion to run, the party must have had **sufficient opportunity and incentive to litigate**
  - Maybe an amount in controversy for L1 was too low; or
  - Maybe the party was unable to call witnesses.
  - In this case, the court would retain the discretion to deny issue preclusion, even if that party lost in L1, if there was an insufficient opportunity to litigate
- We require a **judgment of sufficient quality and finality**
  - Issue preclusion will also attach to a trial court judgment even if that judgment is on appeal
- We only use issue preclusion with respect to issues that are **actually litigated**, that are **factual** (as opposed to issues of law), or a mixed question of law and fact (like negligence)
  - Issues of law are generally prevented from being re-litigated due to the doctrine of *staré dicisis* (precedent)
- Last, **same issue** and **actually litigated** will bleed into each other; without one, you don't have the other.

### Actually Litigated — *Cromwell v. County of Sac*

Our primary teaching case on this is *Cromwell*.

<!-- page: 18 -->

#### *Cromwell v. County of Sac*, 94 U.S. (4 Otto) 351 (SCOTUS)(1876)

Where a second action is between the same parties and involves the same transaction or occurrence as an initial judgment, that judgment does not bar the second action if the second action is upon an issue that was *not adjudicated in the initial action*.

**Facts:**
- The County of Sac (County) (defendant) issued bonds and coupons for interest, maturing at four different times, so that a courthouse could be built. The courthouse was never built.
- Samuel Smith, on behalf of Cromwell (plaintiff), had previously brought suit against the County, seeking to recover on early-maturing coupons. Cromwell had possessed the coupons, but did not prove in this previous suit that he had given value for them before they reached maturity.
- Therefore, the Court ruled that Cromwell could not recover upon the coupons.
- Subsequently, Cromwell brought this suit against the County, seeking to cash in four different bonds and coupons for interest.
- Cromwell sought to introduce evidence that he had paid value for the bonds before they reached maturity.
- The lower court ruled that Cromwell could not introduce that evidence because the previous judgment estopped him from doing so. Cromwell appealed.

**Holding and Reasoning (Field, J.):**
- Where a second action is between the same parties and involves the same transaction or occurrence as an initial judgment, that judgment does not bar the second action if the second action is upon an issue that was not adjudicated in the initial action.
- There can be *multiple issues* in any given case beside the actual merits.
- Simply because a judgment on the merits is reached between two parties is **not a reason that one of the parties may not litigate another, unadjudicated issue based on the same transaction or occurrence** in the future.
- The bonds and coupons at issue in this case are different than those at issue in the Smith case.
- The fact that Cromwell could not prove that he had paid value before maturity for the bonds in the Smith case "is not even presumptive, much less conclusive" of whether Cromwell paid value before maturity for the distinct bonds in this case.
- **Cromwell should have been given a chance to offer that proof in this case and the lower court did not give him that chance.**

<!-- page: 19 -->

The County wanted to build a new courthouse but did not have enough cash, so it issued bonds (promises to pay in the future). The County Judge, not very ethical, gave bonds to the construction company as a bribe, and both the judge and construction company disappeared with no courthouse built. Time passes, and then the county refuses to pay on the bonds.

We want people to be able to rely on the bonds that they buy. We call the person who buys the bond prior to the maturity date of the bond (without knowledge of the fraud), the *holder in due course*.

In *Cromwell*, Cromwell's agent sued, but only to compel payment on two of his bonds (L1). Case goes to judgment and loses because the county proves it was swindled and there was no courthouse.

<!-- page: 20 -->

In L1, Cromwell never argued that he was also a *holder in due course*. Now in L2, he sues on the other 18 bonds.

**Analysis for Claim Preclusion in *Cromwell*:**
1. Same party (or in privity) — YES
2. Same claim (same transaction or occurrence) — **NO** (bonds are considered separate transactions or occurrences) — we can stop analyzing

After this analysis, we see that Cromwell is not *claim precluded* in L2 because L2 was not for the same claim. Each bond is always considered a separate claim — "transaction or occurrence."

**Analysis for Issue Preclusion in *Cromwell*:**
1. Same issue — **NO** — We can stop analyzing. In L1, he did not litigate that he was the *holder in due course*, which is what he wants to litigate in L2 (thus, is a different issue).

Because the issue in L2 was not litigated in L1, he is not *issue precluded* either.

Although *Cromwell* states the current black letter law, the key point is that the "**same issue**" requirement in *issue preclusion*, regardless of whether it is the specific party's own fault that it was not brought up in L1, does not preclude that party from bringing it up in L2.

In *Cromwell*, the court defines the issue as "whether he was the holder in due course" (extremely narrowly) because not doing so would effectively re-define the issue as the same "transaction or occurrence (too much like *claim preclusion*)" — that is why issue preclusion must be much more narrow in scope.

<!-- page: 21 -->

**The rule generally is that we define issue pretty narrowly:**

- We have a *transaction or occurrence*
  - such as the issuance of the bonds, or
  - not letting the cool doctors into the cool doctor's club
- We may have multiple *theories of relief* that arise under that same "transaction or occurrence"
  - State law cause of action(s): **Within each of these theories of relief we may have multiple *issues***
    - Issue 1: Tort (Negligence)
      - Issue 1: Was there a duty
      - Issue 2: Was that duty breached by the Defendant
      - Issue 3: Was there comparative negligence by the Plaintiff, etc.
  - Federal law cause of action(s)
    - Sherman Anti-Trust Act
      - Issue 1: Was there an agreement

<!-- page: 22 -->

Even in *Cromwell*, the issue of whether he was the *holder in due course* might actually be too broad, and would more than likely be looked at separately, as whether 1) he bought the bonds; 2) without knowledge as to the fraud; and 3) did he pay value for them.

- They are always **narrow**; and
- They are always **factual**.

### Necessary to the Decision — *Rios v. Davis*

Now let's discuss the issue preclusion requirement of **Necessary to the Decision**.

<!-- page: 23 -->

#### *Russell v. Place*, 94 U.S. (4 Otto) 606 (1876)(SCOTUS) — (federal case issue preclusion necessity requirement)

Collateral estoppel (issue preclusion) will not prohibit a party from raising an issue that was asserted in a previous action between the same parties if it is unclear from the face of the record or extrinsic evidence that the specific issue was actually decided.

**Facts:**
- Nathan Russell (plaintiff) originally filed for a patent on two preparations of leather: one involving treatment with "fat liquor" and another involving treating bark tanned lamb or sheepskin with a certain compound.
- The initial patent was defective; Russell corrected it, and the patent was reissued.
- Russell sued Isaac Place et al. (Place) (defendants), alleging that Place was manufacturing, using, and selling the invention without a license.
- Judgment was entered for Russell. Russell brought another suit for patent infringement.
- Place sought to defend on the grounds that the patent was invalid for lack of novelty and there was a difference between the invention and the reissued patent.
- Russell argued that his favorable judgment in the previous suit *precluded* Place from raising these defenses.
- The record in that proceeding did not indicate which of the two preparations Place had infringed.
- The lower court ruled in favor of Place. Russell appealed.

**Holding and Reasoning (Field, J.):**
- A party will only be *issue precluded* from asserting an issue in a subsequent lawsuit **if the specific issue was actually decided in the previous lawsuit.**
- **This must either be apparent on the face of the record or demonstrated by extrinsic evidence.**
- If multiple issues were raised in an action, **and it is unclear on which ground the court's decision was based, any of the issues may be raised again in a subsequent lawsuit.**
- In this case, **it is not clear from the record which process in the patent the previous court concluded that Place had infringed.**

<!-- page: 24 -->

The plaintiff ended up prevailing in L1, but the court did not submit special interrogatories to the jury to ask about whether the jury is finding infringement based on the fat liquor, or separately, infringement of the tanned lambskin. Defendant discontinues using the tanned lambskin process but continues using the fat liquor process. Plaintiff brings L2 for infringement of the fat liquor.

- Plaintiff here is not *claim precluded* because L2 is due to the Defendant not refraining from using the process, which is an event that occurred after L1, so it is not part of the "same transaction or occurrence" (analogous to the installment payments).
- Plaintiff is also not issue precluded because it was unclear whether the issue of fat liquor infringement was **necessary to the jury's decision** in L1 — all damages could have been awarded based on the tanned lambskin theory alone.

In *Russell v. Place*, we learned the federal rule: issue preclusion will not prohibit a party from raising an issue if **it is unclear from the face of the record or extrinsic evidence that the specific issue was actually decided** (if the jury could have decided on multiple grounds).

When there is a judgment that did not involve the jury, we seem not to have this issue because judges have to write or announce their findings of facts and their conclusions (FRCP 52) — precisely to prevent this issue.

<!-- page: 25 -->

#### *Rios v. Davis*, 373 S.W.2d 386 (Tx Ct Civ App) (1963)

If findings of fact in a prior action are not essential to the judgment, then a later action, which is based on those facts, is not subject to issue preclusion.

**Facts:**
- In an action prior to this one, Popular Dry Goods Company (Dry Goods) sued Davis (defendant) for property damages, which resulted from an automobile accident.
- In the same prior case, Davis alleged contributory negligence and joined Rios as a third party defendant.
- The jury in that case found that Dry Goods, Davis, and Rios were negligent. Dry Goods was denied recovery from Davis, and Davis was denied recovery from Rios.
- Rios (plaintiff) then instituted the present action against Davis. Davis alleged contributory negligence and objected to the action on the basis of *issue preclusion*, asserting that the present action was based on facts already presented and subject to findings in the prior action. The district court held that the present action was barred on the basis of *issue preclusion*, and Rios appealed.

**Holding and Reasoning (Collings, J.):**
- If findings of fact in a prior action are not essential to the judgment, then a later action, which is based on those facts, is not subject to *issue preclusion*.
- As judgment was entered against Davis in the prior case, the jury's findings as to Rios' negligence are immaterial.
- **That is, if the jury's finding as to Rios' negligence had been material, then Davis would have been able to recover.**
- Since **the jury's finding as to Rios' negligence was not essential to the judgment, this action, which is based on the facts in the earlier case, is not subject to *issue preclusion*.**

More simply stated:

- Dry Goods sues Davis asserting a tort cause of action (theory of relief) **(L1)**
  - Asserts Negligence
- Davis alleges an affirmative defense against Dry Goods
  - Asserts Contributory negligence (not comparative negligence)
    - If the defendant can prove that the Plaintiff was at all negligent, no matter how small, that would bar all recovery of damages
- Davis also sues Rios under a separate tort cause of action
  - Asserts Negligence
- Rios alleges an affirmative defense
  - Asserts Contributory negligence against Davis (not comparative negligence)
- No compulsory counterclaim; Rios chooses not to file a counterclaim against Davis.
- Case goes to the jury; jury says that all three of them are negligent.
- In L1, no one got money because everyone was found to be contributorily negligent.

- Rios sues Davis asserting a tort cause of action **(L2)**
  - Asserts Negligence
- Davis alleges an affirmative defense against Rios
  - Contributory Negligence; AND
  - *Issue Preclusion* (against Rios) (as to the contributory negligence defense)
    - Asserting that in L1, the court also found that Rios was contributorily negligent
- Court found that Rios was not *issue precluded*

**Analysis of Issue Preclusion in *Rios v. Davis*:**
1. Same issue — yes
2. Actually litigated — YES, AND
3. Necessary to decision — **NO** — can stop analyzing
4. ??mutuality??

**Plus other stuff:**
- Judgement of sufficient quality/finality
- Sufficient opportunity, incentive to litigate
- Only preclude party who lost in L1, or those in privity
- Only issues litigated which are FACTUAL (for legal issues, we rely on *staré decisis*)

The jury found that Rios was negligent in L1, but it was **not necessary to the judgment in L1**, because in L1, no one got money. The only finding necessary to prevent Davis from getting money from Rios was that Davis himself was contributorily negligent. The jury could have found that Rios was not negligent, and still could have issued the same judgment in L1.

(**\*** If you switch the finding around and still reach the same decision, then that finding was clearly unnecessary to the decision.)

## Issue Preclusion Mutuality (Jurisdictions Differ on This)

**There are four different interpretations of mutuality** (remember that either party could use the issue preclusion doctrine):

1. **Same parties are involved in L1 and L2; and they have to be in same procedural posture** (strictest)
   - Plaintiff in L1 is the same as Plaintiff in L2
   - Defendant in L1 is the same as Defendant in L2

2. **Same parties, but no requirement that they are in same procedural posture or role**

3. **Federal common law rule** (will follow tomorrow)
   - Ordinarily, defensive, nonmutual *issue preclusion* will be allowed
   - Ordinarily, offensive nonmutual *issue preclusion* will not be allowed UNLESS Plaintiff in L2 could not have joined L1 (which is what happened in *Parklane*)
     - *Russell*
     - *Blonder-Tongue*
     - *Parklane Hoisiery*

4. **There is no mutuality requirement** (will follow tomorrow)
   - Referred to as the New York Rule
     - *Hart*
