## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***Dioguardi v. Durning*, 139 F.2d 774 (2d Cir. 1944)**

  A complaint must contain a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief.

- ***Gillispie v. Goodyear Service Stores*, 128 S.E.2d 762 (N.C. 1963)**

  In order to properly state a cause of action, a pleading must allege facts, not merely conclusions.

- ***Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly*, 550 U.S. 544 (SCOTUS)(2007)**

  A complaint must allege sufficient facts that, if accepted as true, would "state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face" in order to defeat a motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim under Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(6).

- ***Ashcroft v. Iqbal*, 556 U.S. 662 (SCOTUS)(2009)**

  Under *Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly*, a complaint will only survive a motion to dismiss if it alleges nonconclusory facts that, taken as true, state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face.

### Statutes

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 8 — General Rules of Pleading

**(a) Claim for Relief.** A pleading that states a claim for relief must contain:

> (1) a short and plain statement of the grounds for the court's jurisdiction, unless the court already has jurisdiction and the claim needs no new jurisdictional support;
>
> (2) a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief; and
>
> (3) a demand for the relief sought, which may include relief in the alternative or different types of relief.

<!-- page: 2 -->

**(b) Defenses; Admissions and Denials.**

> (1) **In General.** In responding to a pleading, a party must:
>
> > (A) state in short and plain terms its defenses to each claim asserted against it; and
> >
> > (B) admit or deny the allegations asserted against it by an opposing party.
>
> (2) **Denials—Responding to the Substance.** A denial must fairly respond to the substance of the allegation.
>
> (3) **General and Specific Denials.** A party that intends in good faith to deny all the allegations of a pleading—including the jurisdictional grounds—may do so by a general denial. A party that does not intend to deny all the allegations must either specifically deny designated allegations or generally deny all except those specifically admitted.
>
> (4) **Denying Part of an Allegation.** A party that intends in good faith to deny only part of an allegation must admit the part that is true and deny the rest.
>
> (5) **Lacking Knowledge or Information.** A party that lacks knowledge or information sufficient to form a belief about the truth of an allegation must so state, and the statement has the effect of a denial.
>
> (6) **Effect of Failing to Deny.** An allegation—other than one relating to the amount of damages—is admitted if a responsive pleading is required and the allegation is not denied. If a responsive pleading is not required, an allegation is considered denied or avoided.

**(c) Affirmative Defenses.**

> (1) **In General.** In responding to a pleading, a party must affirmatively state any avoidance or affirmative defense, including:
>
> - accord and satisfaction; arbitration and award; assumption of risk; contributory negligence; duress; estoppel; failure of consideration; fraud; illegality; injury by fellow servant; laches; license; payment; release; res judicata; statute of frauds; statute of limitations; and waiver.
>
> (2) **Mistaken Designation.** If a party mistakenly designates a defense as a counterclaim, or a counterclaim as a defense, the court must, if justice requires,

<!-- page: 3 -->

> treat the pleading as though it were correctly designated, and may impose terms for doing so.

**(d) Pleading to Be Concise and Direct; Alternative Statements; Inconsistency.**

> (1) **In General.** Each allegation must be simple, concise, and direct. No technical form is required.
>
> (2) **Alternative Statements of a Claim or Defense.** A party may set out 2 or more statements of a claim or defense alternatively or hypothetically, either in a single count or defense or in separate ones. If a party makes alternative statements, the pleading is sufficient if any one of them is sufficient.
>
> (3) **Inconsistent Claims or Defenses.** A party may state as many separate claims or defenses as it has, regardless of consistency.

**(e) Construing Pleadings.** Pleadings must be construed so as to do justice.

---

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)-(c)

**(b) How to Present Defenses.** Every defense to a claim for relief in any pleading must be asserted in the responsive pleading if one is required. But a party may assert the following defenses by motion:

> (1) lack of subject-matter jurisdiction;
>
> (2) lack of personal jurisdiction;
>
> (3) improper venue;
>
> (4) insufficient process;
>
> (5) insufficient service of process;
>
> (6) failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted; and
>
> (7) failure to join a party under Rule 19.

A motion asserting any of these defenses must be made before pleading if a responsive pleading is allowed. If a pleading sets out a claim for relief that does not require a responsive pleading, an opposing party may assert at trial any defense to that claim. No defense or objection is waived by joining it with one or more other defenses or objections in a responsive pleading or in a motion.

<!-- page: 4 -->

**(c) Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings.** After the pleadings are closed—but early enough not to delay trial—a party may move for judgment on the pleadings.

---

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 65

**(a) Preliminary Injunction.**

> (1) **Notice.** The court may issue a preliminary injunction only on notice to the adverse party.
>
> (2) **Consolidating the Hearing with the Trial on the Merits.** Before or after beginning the hearing on a motion for a preliminary injunction, the court may advance the trial on the merits and consolidate it with the hearing. Even when consolidation is not ordered, evidence that is received on the motion and that would be admissible at trial becomes part of the trial record and need not be repeated at trial. But the court must preserve any party's right to a jury trial.

**(b) Temporary Restraining Order.**

> (1) **Issuing Without Notice.** The court may issue a temporary restraining order without written or oral notice to the adverse party or its attorney only if:
>
> > (A) specific facts in an affidavit or a verified complaint clearly show that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result to the movant before the adverse party can be heard in opposition; and
> >
> > (B) the movant's attorney certifies in writing any efforts made to give notice and the reasons why it should not be required.
>
> (2) **Contents; Expiration.** Every temporary restraining order issued without notice must state the date and hour it was issued; describe the injury and state why it is irreparable; state why the order was issued without notice; and be promptly filed in the clerk's office and entered in the record. The order expires at the time after entry—not to exceed 14 days—that the court sets, unless before that time the court, for good cause, extends it for a like period or the adverse party consents to a longer extension. The reasons for an extension must be entered in the record.
>
> (3) **Expediting the Preliminary-Injunction Hearing.** If the order is issued without notice, the motion for a preliminary injunction must be set for hearing at the earliest possible time, taking precedence over all other matters except hearings on older matters of the same character. At the hearing, the party who obtained the order must proceed with the motion; if the party does not, the court must dissolve the order.

<!-- page: 5 -->

**(c) Security.** The court may issue a preliminary injunction or a temporary restraining order only if the movant gives security in an amount that the court considers proper to pay the costs and damages sustained by any party found to have been wrongfully enjoined or restrained. The United States, its officers, and its agencies are not required to give security.

**(d) Contents and Scope of Every Injunction and Restraining Order.**

> (1) **Contents.** Every order granting an injunction and every restraining order must:
>
> > (A) state the reasons why it issued;
> >
> > (B) state its terms specifically; and
> >
> > (C) describe in reasonable detail—and not by referring to the complaint or other document—the act or acts restrained or required.
>
> (2) **Persons Bound.** The order binds only the following who receive actual notice of it by personal service or otherwise:
>
> > (A) the parties;
> >
> > (B) the parties' officers, agents, servants, employees, and attorneys; and
> >
> > (C) other persons who are in active concert or participation with anyone described in Rule 65(d)(2)(A) or (B).

### The Four Erie Tests

#### Erie Test 1 — *York*'s "Outcome Determinative" Test

**ANALYSIS:**
- Whether the difference between using state or federal law for the specific issue is outcome determinative in the case:
  - If **yes**, then must use State Law
  - If **no**, then you may use federal law
- This test almost always leads to state law because most of the issues that the parties fight about are outcome determinative — which is why they fight about and care about them in the first place.
- This test may not work because we know that it will always end up in state law no matter how trivial or procedural.

---

#### Erie Test 2 — J. Harlan's "Primary Conduct Test" (*Hanna*)

- Asks about whether the difference between state and federal law and the ability of a federal forum might cause primary conduct of clients to change.

  **Examples of things that CHANGE primary conduct (illustrative):**
  - Where one is incorporated
  - Where one gets married
  - Where is the contract
  - What does the contract say
  - Investment strategies

  **Examples of things that do NOT change primary conduct (illustrative):**
  - How you serve process
  - How lawyers serve process
  - When does one serve process
  - And other things about the process of where lawsuits are filed

- J. Harlan does not worry too much about forum shopping, but cares about whether the state government or federal government is the one regulating the primary conduct of the individuals in society.

<!-- page: 7 -->

**ANALYSIS:**
- If the federal law at issue **causes** primary conduct of the parties to change → apply State law.
- If the federal law at issue **does not cause** primary conduct of the parties to change → may apply federal law.

---

#### Erie Tests 3 & 4 — The "Valid and Applicable Test"

*(Hanna Majority/Scalia Interpretation) & (Shady Grove/Ginsburg Interpretation)*

Both tests are built on the **Supremacy Clause**: if federal law is valid and applicable, we are supposed to use it, notwithstanding any state law to the contrary.

**ANALYSIS — Focus on the nature of the federal law at issue:**

**If federal law at issue is a constitutional provision:**
- Presume it is valid and ask whether it is applicable (speaks to the issue at hand).
- If it does speak to the issue at hand, then apply federal law (courts unlikely to construe narrowly).

**If federal law at issue is a statute:**
- Measure its **validity** — find the constitutional provision granting Congress power to enact that statute (almost always found). Examples:
  - Power to Regulate Commerce
  - Power to Create Lower Federal Courts (and rules about how they operate)
  - Necessary & Proper Clause
  - Full Faith & Credit Clause
- Measure its **applicability** — use statutory interpretation to determine whether the federal law speaks to the issue at hand (*Stewart Organization v. Ricoh* is an example); courts unlikely to construe narrowly.

<!-- page: 8 -->

**Justices Ginsburg and Scalia agree** on handling of constitutional provisions and statutes. The real difference emerges when the federal law at issue is a **Federal Rule of Civil Procedure (FRCP)**:

**If federal law at issue is a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure (FRCP):**

- **Validity** is measured under the Rules Enabling Act, 28 U.S.C. § 2072(b), which says a rule cannot "abridge, enlarge, or modify state substantive right." The rules always pass this test.
- **Applicability** (where Scalia and Ginsburg diverge):
  - **Justice Scalia** — rules should be interpreted and effectuated in accordance with their plain language; construed **broadly** (example: *Hanna* Majority).
  - **Justice Ginsburg** — rules should be interpreted sensitively with state law policies in mind; construed **creatively (or narrowly)** with state law policies in mind (example of operation: *Walker v. Armco Steel*, even though *Walker* did not concern federalism reasons for the narrow construction).

<!-- page: 9 -->

**If federal law at issue is Federal Common Law:**
- Both Justice Scalia and Ginsburg agree: apply the **"Twin Aims of Erie" test**:
  1. Forum shopping; and
  2. Inequitable administration of laws

  If a difference between the federal common law and state law would cause forum shopping or inequitable administration of the laws, the court system should apply state law in a diversity case. This almost always focuses on an outcome determinative analysis — ***York* test revived** (almost always leads to state law).

### Motion to Dismiss — FRCP 12(b)(6): Level of Specificity

**Three theories under which a defendant might file a MTD for failure to state a claim:**

1. If the facts in the complaint are too fantastical to be credited in any serious way.
2. When the legal theory that Plaintiff cites and relies on does not apply to the facts pled.
3. **When there is an insufficient level of specificity** (related to Rule 8(a)(2)).

**Rule 12(b)(6) is tied to Rule 8(a)(2):** If the Complaint does not "contain a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief," the proper motion is Rule 12(b)(6) to dismiss all or part of the Complaint.

#### Two Basic Philosophies on Level of Specificity

**NOTICE PLEADING (*Dioguardi*)**
- Sufficient facts and maybe a hint towards a legal theory that are enough to allow the defendant and his attorney:
  - Notice of the nature of the transaction that is to be sued upon;

<!-- page: 11 -->

  - Basic facts that need to be relied on in order to begin an investigation and prepare a defense.

**CODE PLEADING (*Gillespie*)**
- When we want the complaint to give us enough facts and reasoning such that the court will say: "if the plaintiff can prove them, the plaintiff will likely win the lawsuit on the merits."

**PLAUSIBILITY PLEADING STANDARD (*Bell Atlantic* + *Iqbal*)**
- The best way to understand it: the "Plausibility Pleading Standard" is simply Code Pleading, but operationalized in a two-step process.

> *SIDE NOTE: If you think that the difference between Notice Pleading and Plausibility Pleading is abstract, take a look at the 2d Circuit decision dismissing President Trump's 2d Amended Complaint filed in the SDNY, seeking an injunction against the Grand Jury's subpoena to President Trump's business accounts. Instead of disputing the subpoena in NY state court, President's attorneys filed a civil Complaint in federal court, and the NY AG moved to dismiss; half of the court's opinion is the Plausibility standard. No one would seriously doubt that if the federal rules had continued to apply a notice pleading standard, President Trump's Complaint would have survived the MTD.*

### *Dioguardi v. Durning*, 139 F.2d 774 (2d Cir. 1944) — Notice Pleading

**RLL:** A complaint must contain a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief.

<!-- page: 12 -->

**Facts:**
- Dioguardi (plaintiff) attempted to import medicinal tonics from Italy.
- He got into a dispute with his shipper as to how much he owed for the shipment and as a result, the Collector of Customs at the Port of New York (Durning) (defendant) held the tonics for one year and then sold them at public auction pursuant to federal law.
- Dioguardi brought suit, asserting in his "home drawn" complaint that (1) the Collector sold the bottles to another party for $10 less than the plaintiff bid at the auction, and (2) three weeks before the sale, two cases of the tonics disappeared, presumably at the fault of the Collector.
- The district court dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim upon which relief could be granted.
- The plaintiff appealed.

**Issue:**
- If a complaint demonstrates that a plaintiff is entitled to relief, may a trial court dismiss a case for failure to state a claim upon which relief may be granted?

**Holding and Reasoning (Clark, J.):**
- A complaint does not have to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.
- **Under the new federal rules of civil procedure, a complaint now only must contain a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief (Rule 8).**
- The standard for a Rule 12(b) motion to dismiss is that the complaint fails to state a claim upon which relief can be granted.
- The court must assume that the plaintiff's contentions in the complaint are true and then make its decision on a motion to dismiss based on that assumption.
- In this case, although the plaintiff's complaint is "inartistically" drawn, **he has stated enough facts to withstand a motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim** — even if he has not stated enough to withstand a potential future motion for summary judgment.
- Assuming the plaintiff's allegations to be true, the Collector improperly conducted the auction and improperly converted two of the plaintiff's bottles of tonic.
- **These allegations, if true, would entitle the plaintiff to relief.**
- Accordingly, dismissal for failure to state a claim is inappropriate.

**Notice Pleading Standard — ANALYSIS:**
- Do we have enough to allow the defense attorney to open up an investigation and to pull the relevant documents?

The *Dioguardi* complaint included: name of the parties; enough to allow attorney to open up investigation; where to find the documents. It did NOT include: a date, or specificity in finding records of evidence. The court said it was sufficient.

<!-- page: 13 -->

*Dioguardi* set the standard of how the courts were going to operate for about 75 years (1938 until *Bell Atlantic*).

---

### *Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly*, 550 U.S. 544 (SCOTUS)(2007)

**RLL:** A complaint must allege sufficient facts that, if accepted as true, would "state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face" in order to defeat a motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim.

**Antitrust Substantive Law background:**
- When companies make an **agreement** to non-compete: making an "agreement" not to compete violates antitrust laws (§ 1 Sherman Act).
- When companies **do not make an agreement** to non-compete (but do so anyway): companies can simply not compete with one another, keep prices the same, and all raise prices the same way. Without an "agreement," it is not in violation of antitrust laws. (**Allegations of conscious parallelism**)

The difficulty: plaintiff would have to allege underlying facts that, if proven true, could support the inference of an agreement. Companies are unlikely to keep copies of any such agreement, and plaintiff would have to rely on defendant to obtain supporting documentation through discovery.

<!-- page: 14 -->

**Facts:**
- William Twombly (plaintiff), on behalf of a putative class of telephone and high-speed internet subscribers, filed a complaint alleging that Bell Atlantic Corporation (defendant) violated § 1 of the Sherman Act, which prohibits conspiracies in restraint of trade.
- The complaint alleged that Bell Atlantic conspired with other local telephone companies by means of conscious parallelism to inhibit the growth of upstart telecommunications companies and to eliminate competition with each other.
- The alleged purpose of the conspiracy was to allow each involved local telephone company to dominate a specific market.
- Twombly did not introduce any additional evidence of an agreement between the companies.
- In district court, Bell Atlantic moved to dismiss under FRCP 12(b)(6). The court granted this motion. The Second Circuit overturned the dismissal.
- SCOTUS granted certiorari.

**Holding and Reasoning (Souter, J.):**
- **(1)** To state a claim under § 1 of the Sherman Act, **the complaint must contain enough factual material to suggest that an agreement existed between the defendants (plausible requirement).**

<!-- page: 15 -->

- This requirement is to protect defendants from meritless claims in which plaintiffs hope to find some form of evidence in the discovery process. Discovery is expensive and time-consuming. Accordingly, the district courts must act as gatekeepers.
- Previous decisions had suggested that as long as a complaint leaves open the possibility that the plaintiff would find some fact to support recovery, the complaint cannot be dismissed. **This way of pleading is no longer workable.**
- Conclusory statements of claims will no longer survive a motion to dismiss.
- Instead, **a complaint must contain enough facts to make the claim plausible on its face and not merely speculative.**
- The allegations must raise a reasonable expectation that the discovery process will reveal relevant evidence to support the claim.
- Here, the complaint contained only conclusory allegations of a conspiracy. It did not include enough facts to plausibly suggest the existence of an agreement. Accordingly, the trial court appropriately dismissed the complaint.
- **(2)** To prove an illegal conspiracy under § 1 of the Sherman Act, the plaintiff must introduce evidence that tends to exclude the possibility that the alleged conspirators acted independently.
- Conscious parallelism is not by itself illegal.
- A plaintiff must also show evidence of some sort of agreement to restrain trade or commerce.
- Requiring this extra proof above and beyond conscious parallelism helps to prevent false positives — findings of antitrust violations for conduct that the Sherman Act did not actually intend to criminalize.

---

### *Ashcroft v. Iqbal*, 556 U.S. 662 (SCOTUS)(2009)

**RLL:** Under *Twombly*, a complaint will only survive a motion to dismiss if it alleges nonconclusory facts that, taken as true, state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face.

**Substantive Law background:**
- Policies toward holding Muslim men in highly restrictive conditions, once arrested:
  - If those are intentional and designed to punish Muslim men because of their religion, origin, or race (discriminatory animus) → the policies are **illegal** (disparate **intentional** treatment = illegal).
  - If the FBI and AG arrest many people as to whom there is some evidence of involvement in the Sep 11 occurrences, and they just so happen to be Muslim → **no illegality** (disparate **impact** = not illegal).

<!-- page: 16 -->

- To prove the case at trial, plaintiff would have to prove that Muller and Ashcroft had a prohibitive mindset — either acting with animus, or acting with deliberate indifference to their animus.

**Facts:**
- Javaid Iqbal (plaintiff) was arrested and detained during the investigation of the September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks.

<!-- page: 17 -->

- Iqbal claimed the conditions of his custody violated the First and Fifth Amendments and sued former USAG John Ashcroft, FBI Director Robert Mueller, and other officials (defendants) in district court.
- The complaint accused Ashcroft of being the "principal architect" and Mueller of being "instrumental" in the implementation of a discriminatory policy of confining individuals in harsh conditions based solely on their "religion, race, and/or national origin."
- Ashcroft and Mueller claimed qualified immunity and moved to dismiss for failure to state a claim.
- The district court denied the motion to dismiss. The Second Circuit affirmed. SCOTUS granted cert.

**Holding and Reasoning (Kennedy, J.):**
- Under *Twombly*, **a complaint must allege sufficient facts that, if taken as true, "state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face"** in order to defeat a motion to dismiss.
- **Facial plausibility means that the facts alleged permit a "reasonable inference" that the defendant is, in fact, liable.**
- The plausibility determination in any particular case is left to the trial court's judgment.
- **Although the facts in the complaint must be taken as true, the court is not required to afford legal conclusions the same deference.**
- The court may begin its inquiry by determining which allegations need not be taken as true.
- **Here, the allegations regarding Ashcroft and Mueller's knowledge or intent are not "unrealistic or nonsensical," but they are conclusory and need not be accepted as true.**
- **Further, even though the allegations of discrimination are "consistent" with Iqbal's claims, there are other, more reasonable, alternative explanations.**
- Although the complaint may state a claim against other defendants, the charges against Ashcroft and Mueller do not plausibly indicate anything more than a policy of keeping individuals suspected of terrorism under tight security pending a full investigation.
- **Additional facts are needed to take the complaint from conceivable to plausible.**
- FRCP 8 sets forth the basic requirements for pleadings in any civil action, and the SCOTUS' interpretation of the rule in *Twombly* is applicable to all such actions regardless of subject matter.
- Even though a plaintiff may plead subjective knowledge and intent "generally" under FRCP Rule 9(b), this does not negate the requirements of Rule 8.
- Because Iqbal has not made the required showing of plausibility, the complaint does not state a claim against Ashcroft and Mueller.

<!-- page: 18 -->

The court in this case did not accept the legal conclusions in the Complaint as true — **this is where the real change came from.** The court characterizes "mere recitations of the cause of action" as "conclusory factual statements," which the court refused to accept as true.

### Two-Step Process of the "Plausibility Pleading Standard"

1. **Accept** the factual allegations in the complaint as true, but **NOT** the legal conclusions in the complaint as true (conclusory statements — line them out).

2. **Ask** whether the combination of the facts that are accepted as true, taken together, plausibly nudge the Complaint across the line from **possibility** to **"plausibility"** of a legal violation.

> *Example of conclusory statement: "In Bell Atlantic, the telephone companies engaged in an agreement." Instead, we need the underlying facts.*

In *Iqbal*, the court noted that the facts pled could have another plausible alternative explanation — e.g., that the conspirators of the Sep 11 attack were predominantly connected to Arab nations and were predominantly Muslim, which could support a non-discriminatory explanation for the disparate impact.

If more details had been pled — time, place, who was affected, etc., with sufficient specificity — the complaint might have crossed the line. Courts consider more than one plausible explanation for the conduct alleged in the complaint.

## Preliminary Injunction

Now we will consider a Plaintiff who walks into court after filing a Complaint but is unable to wait long, and needs immediate relief by freezing the status quo — even though the lawsuit has not been won yet.

> ***NOTE:** All of administrative law discussed in the* Thane Coat *case is not good law. You are only to take the **preliminary injunction law** from this case.*

### Elements Required to Consider a Preliminary Injunction

<!-- page: 19 -->

1. Plaintiffs are **substantially likely to succeed on the merits**
2. **Immediate and Irreparable Harm**
3. **Balance of the Equities** (Harm to others)
4. **Public Interest**

### Classroom Exercise — *Thane Coat*: Oral Arguments

#### Opening Statements

**Counsel for the Plaintiff (Thane Coat):**
- 1 year, no charges! No admin enforcement! Then seizure of property.
- Then the TDO, bullying and punishment.
- Can't show a near-term violation.
- Statutory showing.
- Irreparable harm!

**Counsel for the Defendant (Dept of Commerce):**
- Executive branch, foreign relations.
- PI is extreme step, plus national security.
- Different standard, exceptional showing required.
- Merits: imminent does not mean only immediate.
- No evidence of legit business dealings, no use of exception process, no urgency.

**Greiner comments on oral opening statements:**
- Great job to both sides! Shows great understanding of the topic!
- Both had complicated structures. Trying to explain a lot and very fast. Want to speak slower so you can get interrupted by the judge to ask questions and address the court's concerns.
- Will likely get through only one or two points. What do you want the judge to remember? (May not be the most legally relevant — example: Olivia's point about the government targeting the defendant by planning this thing for years.)
- Also, you want to get to the statutory terms.

<!-- page: 20 -->

#### Likelihood of Success on the Merits

**Counsel for Plaintiff (Thane Coat):**
- 1 year, no charges! No admin enforcement! Then seizure of property.
- Then the TDO, bullying and punishment.
- Can't show a near-term violation.
- Statutory purpose: TDOs not for punishment, but preventative; need a hearing, or else misused; no evidence, nothing recent, no immediate plans.
- Irreparable harm!

**Counsel for Defendant (Dept of Commerce):**
- Executive branch, foreign relations.
- PI is extreme step, plus national security.
- Different standard, exceptional showing required.
- Merits: imminent does not mean only immediate; immediate not in statute, and case law no evidence in past year? Plaintiffs adept at getting around.
- TDO not too broad, law already prohibited exports to specified countries; TDO needs to go further than the preexisting law.

**Greiner comments:**
- Great job emphasizing things you both wanted the court to remember.
  - TDO is not supposed to be used for punishment.
  - Overbreadth argument.
- Focusing the court exclusively on what you wanted the court to think about can be risky because it did not allow for time to address what the opposing counsel stated.

#### Irreparable Harm

**Counsel for the Plaintiff (Thane Coat):**
- Irreparable harm! No way to compensate for lost business relationships — these can't be proved.
- Suppliers have stopped dealing, losing sales.
- Customers afraid of seizure.

**Counsel for the Defendant (Dept of Commerce):**
- Plaintiff failed to make an exceptionally strong case.
  - They failed to apply for exceptions.

<!-- page: 21 -->

  - They delayed seeking this injunction.
  - Their claims are conclusory.

**Greiner comments:**
- Doctrine of sovereign immunity says that Thane Coat cannot come after the Government for damages.
- Try to use every thread you can to remind the judge of facts favorable to your case.

#### Balance of the Equities

**Counsel for the Plaintiff (Thane Coat):**
- Injuries that would be: opportunity, credibility, goodwill.
- Defendant will likely not suffer any injuries.

**Counsel for the Defendant (Dept of Commerce):**
- Preliminary Injunction would undermine National Security. Libya represents an extraordinary threat to US.
- Without PI the US would be unable to protect its citizens.
- Plaintiff does not make a strong showing that their harm is irreparable.
- Interests of National Security far outweigh.

**Greiner comments:**
