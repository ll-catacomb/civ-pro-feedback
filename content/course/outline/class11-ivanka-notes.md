## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***Guaranty Trust v. York*, 326 U.S. 99 (SCOTUS) (1945)**

  A federal court, exercising jurisdiction based strictly on diversity of citizenship, must abide by any state legal rule that would be outcome determinative if held in state court.

- ***Ragan v. Merchants Transfer & Warehouse Co.*, 337 U.S. 530 (SCOTUS) (1949)**

  A state-law cause of action that is time-barred in state courts is also time-barred in federal courts.

- ***Hanna v. Plumer*, 380 U.S. 460 (SCOTUS) (1965)**

  If a plaintiff serves a defendant properly under the federal rules, the plaintiff can proceed with a state-law claim that requires a different method of service for establishing liability.

- ***Walker v. Armco Steel Corp.*, 446 U.S. 740 (SCOTUS) (1980)**

  Rule 3 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure does not toll a state statute of limitations or preempt state tolling rules.

- ***Stewart Organization, Inc. v. Ricoh Corp.*, 487 U.S. 22 (SCOTUS) (1988)**

  A federal court sitting in diversity should apply federal law in adjudicating a motion to transfer a case to a venue provided in a contractual forum-selection clause.

- ***Gillispie v. Goodyear Service Stores*, 128 S.E.2d 762 (N.C. 1963)**

  In order to properly state a cause of action, a pleading must allege facts, not merely conclusions.

### Statutes

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 8 — General Rules of Pleading

**(a) Claim for Relief.** A pleading that states a claim for relief must contain:

<!-- page: 2 -->

> (1) a short and plain statement of the grounds for the court's jurisdiction, unless the court already has jurisdiction and the claim needs no new jurisdictional support;
>
> (2) a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief; and
>
> (3) a demand for the relief sought, which may include relief in the alternative or different types of relief.

**(b) Defenses; Admissions and Denials.**

> (1) **In General.** In responding to a pleading, a party must:
>
> > (A) state in short and plain terms its defenses to each claim asserted against it; and
> >
> > (B) admit or deny the allegations asserted against it by an opposing party.
>
> (2) **Denials—Responding to the Substance.** A denial must fairly respond to the substance of the allegation.
>
> (3) **General and Specific Denials.** A party that intends in good faith to deny all the allegations of a pleading—including the jurisdictional grounds—may do so by a general denial. A party that does not intend to deny all the allegations must either specifically deny designated allegations or generally deny all except those specifically admitted.
>
> (4) **Denying Part of an Allegation.** A party that intends in good faith to deny only part of an allegation must admit the part that is true and deny the rest.
>
> (5) **Lacking Knowledge or Information.** A party that lacks knowledge or information sufficient to form a belief about the truth of an allegation must so state, and the statement has the effect of a denial.
>
> (6) **Effect of Failing to Deny.** An allegation—other than one relating to the amount of damages—is admitted if a responsive pleading is required and the allegation is not denied. If a responsive pleading is not required, an allegation is considered denied or avoided.

**(c) Affirmative Defenses.**

<!-- page: 3 -->

> (1) **In General.** In responding to a pleading, a party must affirmatively state any avoidance or affirmative defense, including:
>
> accord and satisfaction; arbitration and award; assumption of risk; contributory negligence; duress; estoppel; failure of consideration; fraud; illegality; injury by fellow servant; laches; license; payment; release; res judicata; statute of frauds; statute of limitations; and waiver.
>
> (2) **Mistaken Designation.** If a party mistakenly designates a defense as a counterclaim, or a counterclaim as a defense, the court must, if justice requires, treat the pleading as though it were correctly designated, and may impose terms for doing so.

**(d) Pleading to Be Concise and Direct; Alternative Statements; Inconsistency.**

> (1) **In General.** Each allegation must be simple, concise, and direct. No technical form is required.
>
> (2) **Alternative Statements of a Claim or Defense.** A party may set out 2 or more statements of a claim or defense alternatively or hypothetically, either in a single count or defense or in separate ones. If a party makes alternative statements, the pleading is sufficient if any one of them is sufficient.
>
> (3) **Inconsistent Claims or Defenses.** A party may state as many separate claims or defenses as it has, regardless of consistency.

**(e) Construing Pleadings.** Pleadings must be construed so as to do justice.

---

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)-(c)

**(b) How to Present Defenses.** Every defense to a claim for relief in any pleading must be asserted in the responsive pleading if one is required. But a party may assert the following defenses by motion:

> (1) lack of subject-matter jurisdiction;
> (2) lack of personal jurisdiction;
> (3) improper venue;
> (4) insufficient process;
> (5) insufficient service of process;
> (6) failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted; and
> (7) failure to join a party under Rule 19.

A motion asserting any of these defenses must be made before pleading if a responsive pleading is allowed. If a pleading sets out a claim for relief that does not require a responsive pleading, an opposing party may assert at trial any defense to that claim. No defense or objection is waived by joining it with one or more other defenses or objections in a responsive pleading or in a motion.

**(c) Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings.** After the pleadings are closed—but early enough not to delay trial—a party may move for judgment on the pleadings.

### References

#### Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964

- Prohibits adverse action related to a plaintiff's employment "because of," among other things, race and sex.
- Has been interpreted to include the following theories of relief:
  - **Discharge, failure to hire, failure to promote** because of race or sex
    - "Intent": In certain types of cases, requires a plaintiff to prove that the defendant "intentionally" took the adverse employment action because of race or sex. Courts developed a **three-part burden-shifting mode of analysis** (*see* class discussion on burden-shifting):
      1. **Prima facie case:** Plaintiff is of one race (sex), that she suffered adverse employment action, and that an applicant/employee of the other race (sex) did not suffer adverse employment action. Remaining (currently unanswered) question: whether the prima facie case requires "something more" of a plaintiff that supports an inference of discrimination.
      2. **Def's reasons:** Defendant produces evidence of a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for the adverse action taken.
      3. **Pretext:** Plaintiff attempts to prove the defendant's reasons are unworthy of credence — i.e., a pretext for discrimination.
  - **Sexual harassment**
    - "Quid Pro Quo": Adverse employment action because of the refusal by a plaintiff (ordinarily a woman) to engage in sexual conduct with an employee (usually a superior officer) of the defendant.
    - "Hostile Workplace": The maintenance of a working environment in which sexual or racial harassment is so pervasive that it changes the terms or conditions of employment.
- Provides a cause of action against the employer for actions taken by employees within the scope of their employment (the "respondeat superior" theory).
- Has tight (comparatively speaking) limitations provisions involving presentation of the claim to an administrative agency before a lawsuit may be filed.
- Includes as an affirmative defense allowing an employer to impose an adverse employment action because of "religion, sex, or national origin" when "religion, sex, or national origin is a bona fide occupational qualification reasonably necessary to the normal operation of that particular business or enterprise."

#### Sherman Act, Section 1

- Prohibits "conspiracy" in restraint of trade.
- Has been interpreted to include two types of analyses: "per se" violations and "rule of reason" cases.
  - **Per se violations:**
    - Ultimate example is a "horizontal" agreement by persons engaged in the same industry or selling the same product to fix prices.
    - Another example is an agreement by horizontal competitors to divide a market in a specified way (e.g., to avoid competition in each other's "areas").
  - **Rule of reason:** If defendants' conduct is not a per se violation, then it is governed by the "rule of reason," which means that it may be illegal if the plaintiffs can convince the court that the conduct is in restraint of trade; typically requires extensive economic theory and analysis to prove that a particular transaction has anti-competitive effects.
- Requires a plaintiff to prove an "agreement" in restraint of trade, thus rendering proof of "conscious parallelism" insufficient.
- "Conscious parallelism" is action by competitor A that mirrors action by competitor B despite the absence of any sort of agreement between A and B.
- "Conscious parallelism" is sometimes seen in airline ticket pricing — where multiple companies have the same prices for, say, a trip from Boston to Chicago, even though (according to the airlines) they are not communicating with each other directly over prices.

## Lecture Recap for Day 10

*Erie* comes into play when you have the possibility of state law and what we now recognize as federal law applying to the same set of issues, and we need to know how to deal with this **Vertical Choice of Law** problem.

In the *Swift v. Tyson* era, we thought there was this thing called the "general" federal common law that provided substantive rules of decisions on things like torts, contracts, etc. *Erie* came along and swept that away.

**Possible legal foundations of *Erie*:**
- Constitutional: Federalism; Separation of Powers; Equal protection
- Statutory: § 1652
- Common law: federal court prudence

### "What Is Law" Rationale for *Erie*

When we recognize that law is an act or will of a sovereign, then all of this "general common law" is in fact federal common law (judge-made law), which would be inconsistent with other values that we have, by having federal judges creating or imposing rules for diversity clients.

### Three Other Justifications

- **Constitutional — Federalism:** The federal government, by having the federal courts create common law on rules of decision for federal diversity cases, is imposing in areas that the states were supposed to be governing.
- **Constitutional — Strong Separation of Powers:** It's not that the federal government cannot "encroach," because the federal government has the power to reach certain transactions (like issues involving the commerce clause), but rather that **Congress** should be the entity setting those rules if it wants to federalize the law; and the courts doing it without Congressional command was in effect imposing its will on the legislature.
- **Constitutional — Equal protection:** When under *Swift* regime, the court system was creating/finding different rules of decision than those that would be imposed by the state courts. By having the diversity statute structured in the way it was, especially the prohibition on removal by defendants sued in their home court, the out-of-state plaintiffs had the option of choosing between state law and federal common law.
  - In a diversity only case, they could choose state law by suing a defendant in its home court and thereby trap that defendant in state court and state law (the defendant would be unable to remove).
  - If they sued the defendant in federal court invoking diversity jurisdiction, they would get the federal common law — a type of choice that in-state plaintiffs would not have.
- **Statutory justifications (28 U.S.C. § 1652):** The laws of the several states are supposed to be rules of decision in civil actions of courts of the US where those laws apply. Under *Erie*, the laws of the several states includes the state common law and all forms of state law.

<!-- page: 6 -->

- We also realized that maybe none of the above justifications are actually compelled. They decided after considering these counterarguments, that "it was just a better idea to use state law, until someone tells us differently" — a **federal court prudence rationale**.

At the same time that *Erie* was adjudicated, Congress promulgated the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (Rules of Decision Act — 28 U.S.C. §§ 2071 and 2072). By enacting these statutes, Congress and the court system both expressed a desire to create **uniform rules of civil procedure** across the courts system.

The federal court system recognized that it would apply some state law and some federal law in diversity cases and therefore needed tests to decide when to use which type of law.

## Lecture Day 11

### The Four Erie Tests

**Overview Diagram:**

| Test | Source | Key Question | Tends toward |
|------|---------|--------------|--------------|
| York — Outcome Determinative | *Guaranty Trust v. York* | Would using federal law change outcome? | State law (almost always) |
| Hanna Majority — Valid + Applicable | *Hanna v. Plumer* (Scalia interpretation) | Is federal law valid and applicable? | Federal law (almost always) |
| Harlan Concurrence — Primary Conduct | *Hanna* concurrence | Would federal law change parties' primary conduct? | State law if substantive |
| Ginsburg — *Shady Grove* | *Shady Grove* (Ginsburg interpretation) | Same Valid + Applicable, but narrowly construe FRCP | Federal law, unless narrowly construed |

<!-- page: 7 -->

---

### Erie Test 1: *York* Outcome Determinative Test

**ANALYSIS:**
1. Whether the difference between using state or federal law for the specific issue is **outcome determinative** in the case:
   - If **yes → must use State Law**
   - If **no → may use federal law**
2. This test almost always leads to **state law** because most of the issues that the parties fight about are outcome determinative — which is why they fight about and care about them in the first place.
3. This test may not work because we know that it will always end up in state law no matter how trivial or procedural.

So we needed another test that would actually be helpful in determining when to use state law versus when to use federal law in federal diversity cases.

The Supremacy Clause says that federal supersedes state law, but in order to do that, the law must be 1) **valid**; and 2) **applicable**.

---

### Erie Test 2: *Hanna* Majority — "Valid & Applicable Test"

**a. When the federal law at issue is a Constitutional Provision:**
1. We should assume that the Constitutional provision is valid.
2. In determining whether it applies, we can use Constitutional statutory interpretation to determine what the Constitutional provision means or purports to say.
3. If the interpretation of the constitutional provision speaks to the issue at hand, we apply federal law.
4. **Courts are very unlikely to construe this narrowly**, so this test will always result in the use of federal law.

<!-- page: 8 -->

**b. When the federal law at issue is a Federal Statute:**

*Ricoh* involved the Transfer Statutes (which are federal statutes). In terms of applying this *Erie* test, we might want to inquire if the transfer statutes are valid and applicable.

**i. IT MUST BE VALID:**
- We look to see whether the federal statute is Constitutional.
  - The Constitution authorizes Congress to enact certain enumerated, or limited, powers (Federalism principle).
  - We need to make sure that Congress actually has the power to enact the specific statute we are looking at:
    1. Find a constitutional provision that grants that specific power to Congress.
    2. Then you marry it up with the **Necessary and Proper Clause** because that allows Congress the ability to exercise any means necessary and proper to carry out the enumerated powers.
  - **Application to *Ricoh*:** For the Transfer Statutes specifically, we can point to the Constitution's Article III § 1, which vests the Judicial Power in the Supreme Court, and vests the power in Congress to create lower federal courts. Due to Congress' power to create the lower federal courts, we presume it has power to determine what happens in these courts (housekeeping rules, etc.).
- This analysis suggests that *Ricoh*'s transfer statutes were in fact valid.

**ii. IT MUST BE APPLICABLE:**
- If the law is valid, and it "**speaks to the issue at hand**," then the Supremacy Clause says that we must apply it, notwithstanding what the state law says.
- **Application to *Ricoh*:** The Transfer statutes say to the court what to do when they have a motion to transfer. So, they have a statute that was **validly** enacted by Congress, and **speaks to the issue at hand**; therefore, they do not need to care what the state law says.
- Constitutional provisions to invoke for federal statutes:
  1. The power to create the federal courts (Article III § 1)
  2. The Commerce Clause power (Article I § 8)
  3. The Necessary and Proper Clause (Article I § 8)
- Courts are unlikely to construe this narrowly, so almost always, you will use the federal law.

<!-- page: 9 -->

**c. When the federal law at issue is a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure:**

Things begin to get more complicated when the federal law at issue is specifically a **Federal Rule of Civil Procedure**, and we know this because of the bizarre circumstance that resulted after the *Hanna* and *Walker* cases, where the court system uses federal law rules of procedure in *Hanna* and state law rules of procedure in *Walker*. We still need to apply the Valid and Applicable Test.

**Justices Ginsburg and Scalia** agree on the handling of federal laws at issue which are either a constitutional provision or a federal statute. The real difference between the two justices is highlighted most when the federal issue at hand is a provision from the **Federal Rules of Civil Procedure**:

1. **Validity** here is measured with reference to the **Rules Enabling Act, §§ 2071-2072(b)**.

<!-- page: 10 -->

   - This statute was passed by Congress to set up the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.
   - Congress grants "rule-making power generally" to the courts.
   - The courts have set up a system by which there are numerous committees which make recommendations to rules to the SCOTUS, which then votes to adopt them and passes them to Congress. Congress has veto power by passing a statute, but if Congress does not veto, the statute is preserved and becomes the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.
   - Congress also prescribed a **limit**: any federal rules of civil procedure passed shall not "**abridge, enlarge, or modify a substantive right**."
   - Because SCOTUS has a role in making these rules and is aware of the limit, one can assume it will be very unlikely that the SCOTUS would adopt a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure that "abridges, enlarges or modifies a substantive right" to begin with.
   - Courts would be reluctant to say that a rule is invalid — **the rule therefore is always VALID**. This has **NEVER** happened.
   - However, if there is some type of abridgement, the court may instead **narrowly interpret** the rule to make it **INAPPLICABLE** in this case (*Walker*).

2. **Applicability** (Validity concerns can be channeled through here into the Applicability analysis):
   - **Justice Scalia** finds this proposition awful. He feels rules should be interpreted and effectuated in accordance with their plain language — construed broadly (Example: *Hanna* Majority).
   - **Justice Ginsburg:** Feels that federalism is an important value in the *Erie* context. Feels that rules should be interpreted sensitively with state law policies in mind — willing to construe creatively (or narrowly) with state law policies in mind (Example: *Walker*).

> ***NOTE:*** If you encounter an *Erie* fact pattern on an assignment, exam, or in practice, and it is better for your client to use the state rule of civil procedure as opposed to the federal rule, what one needs to do is articulate a **narrow construction** of the rule itself that would allow the state law to apply, arguing that the federal law is inapplicable. For this, you can cite *Walker* for using that type of operation, and later, use J. Ginsburg's opinion in *Shady Grove*, as opposed to J. Scalia's.

**To review:** If the source of the federal law at issue is a **federal rule of civil procedure**, we still apply the "Valid and Applicable Test" analysis. For validity, the source is 28 U.S.C. § 2072, which states that the rule cannot "abridge, enlarge or modify a substantive right" — the outcome will always be that it's **valid**. Any concerns of validity can be channeled to the applicability prong and sometimes it might be more beneficial to narrowly construe so that while the rule is valid, it may be **inapplicable**, so that the state law can have force.

<!-- page: 11 -->

### Erie Test 3: "Twin Aims of *Erie*" Test

**Used when the source of the federal law at issue is Federal Common Law.** Both Justice Scalia and Justice Ginsburg agree that we should apply the "**Twin Aims of Erie test**."

**The twin aims of *Erie* are to prevent:**
- **(a) Forum shopping; and**
- **(b) Inequitable administration of laws.**

The idea is that if a difference between the federal common law that is at issue and the state law would cause forum shopping or inequitable administration of the laws, then the court system should apply state law in a diversity case.

When people are deciding to go to court, they normally not only forum shop for the court which they believe would be most favorable, but they also focus on what the outcome of the case would be depending on the court which they decide to file their case. This sounds like an **outcome determinative test** (as we previously seen in *York*).

When analyzing a federal common law in a vertical choice of law situation, you don't say that you are applying an "outcome determinative test," but instead you purport to apply the **Twin Aims of Erie test**. And, just like with *York*, when the source of the federal law is federal common law, and there is conflicting state law, we are **almost always going to end with state law**.

*Erie* said that there was no "general common law" but there still is specific federal common law on specific issues. Some examples: *forum non-conveniens* and *issue preclusion*.

---

### Erie Test 4: "Primary Conduct Test" — J. Harlan's Concurrence in *Hanna*

**Only one of the Erie tests purports to define what substantive versus procedural is.**

Justice Harlan in the *Hanna* case asks about whether the difference between state and federal law and the ability of a federal forum might cause **primary conduct of clients** to change.

<!-- page: 12 -->

**Examples of things that change primary conduct** (illustrative list):
- Where one is incorporated
- Where one gets married
- Where is the contract
- What does the contract say
- Investment strategies

**Examples of things that do NOT change primary conduct** (illustrative list):
- How you serve process
- How lawyers serve process
- When does one serve process
- And other things about the process of where lawsuits are filed

J. Harlan does not worry too much about forum shopping, but cares about whether the state government or federal government is the one **regulating the primary conduct of the individuals in society**.

**ANALYSIS:**
1. If the federal law (from any source) at issue **causes primary conduct of the parties to change**, that means the federal law has wandered into a substantive sphere and therefore the court should apply **State law**.
2. If the federal law at issue does **not cause primary conduct** of the parties to change, then it is purely procedural and the court **could apply federal law** after making sure that the federal law is tightly wound to a federal Constitution and enabling provision.

The primary conduct test is concerned with what the **individual clients** would do, not the attorneys.

**Application — Cohen (Shareholder derivative suit litigation bond):**
- There is a rule in shareholder derivative suits that in order to file a lawsuit, the plaintiff has to file a litigation bond — a promise to pay certain expenses a plaintiff might cause a defendant to incur if the plaintiff loses and is unable to pay (costs, not attorney's fees).
- The state government is using this litigation bond apparatus to regulate the **frequency of lawsuits**, and we can assume that the frequency of the lawsuits affects the investment strategies of corporations.
- In this case, Justice Harlan, applying the "primary conduct test," would see that this specific law is **changing the primary conduct** of the litigation parties and therefore say that this law is **substantive**, and would apply the state law.
- This is not a hypothetical case; it's exactly ***Cohen***. Justice Harlan explicitly references *Cohen* in the *Hanna* concurrence.

<!-- page: 13 -->

**Hypo — Limitations period:**
Apply each of the four tests to a statute that Congress passes specifying that for diversity purposes, the statute of limitations is 4 years, and the applicable state limitation is 2 years.

This test seems to actually provide a definition for substance versus procedure. No one has ever adopted this test except for Justice Harlan. But, it seems to be one of the factors informing Justice Ginsburg in her narrow interpretation of federal laws — basically thinking that when there is powerful state law policies at issue, we should try to interpret federal law with sensitivity to the state policies in mind. And, when we get to *Shady Grove*, we will see another example of her narrowly interpreting federal laws to preserve state law policies.

> ***NOTE:*** In a Civil Procedure Exam or Assignment, you want to **apply all four tests**, analyzing one by one, explaining the rules of each test, how the rule of each would be applied to the issue in the exam or assignment, and which would be the outcome if using each test, whether the state law or federal law would be applied.

### FRCP 12(b) — Motion to Dismiss

If a defendant feels that there is something fundamentally wrong with the Complaint and wants to bring it to the attention of the court rather than providing a formal Answer, they would file a **MOTION TO DISMISS** under FRCP 12(b). This Rule lists several different defenses that a defendant would assert in order to get a complaint dismissed:

1. lack of subject-matter jurisdiction;
2. lack of personal jurisdiction;
3. improper venue;
4. insufficient process;
5. insufficient service of process;
6. **failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted**; and
7. failure to join a party under Rule 19.

---

### FRCP 12(b)(6) — Failure to State a Claim

**Three different theories under which a defendant might file a MTD for failure to state a claim:**
1. If the facts in the complaint are too fantastical to be credited in any serious way.
2. When the legal theory that Plaintiff cites and relies on does not apply to the facts pled.
3. **When there is an insufficient level of specificity** (which is related to Rule 8(a)(2)).

**Insufficient level of specificity — Rule 12(b)(6) is tied to Rule 8(a)(2):**

> "Rule 8. General Rules of Pleading
> **(a) Claim for Relief.** A pleading that states a claim for relief must contain:
> **(2) a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief**"

If 12(b)(6) is the counterpart to Rule 8(a)(2), it is saying that if the Complaint does not "contain a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief" then the proper motion is Rule 12(b)(6) — dismiss all or part of the Complaint.

---

### Two Pleading Philosophies

There are two philosophies that govern how specific a Complaint has to be to survive a MTD for failure to state a claim:
1. **Code Pleading**
2. **Notice Pleading**

Today we will see what each of these philosophies look like:
- **Code Pleading** illustrated by *Gillispie* (state law case)
- **Notice Pleading** illustrated by *Dioguardi* (next class)

Up until about 8-9 years ago everyone understood that in the federal court system, we used Notice Pleading, and then the SCOTUS surprised everyone when they adjudicated ***Twombly*** and ***Iqbal*** (which Greiner calls "**Twiqbal**").

The SCOTUS in *Iqbal* discarded decades of practice of a **Notice Pleading** standard and took everyone to **Code Pleading**, but called it **Plausibility Pleading**, which involves a two-step process (and recognizing that the philosophy behind it is just Code Pleading).

> ***NOTE:*** On Greiner exams and assignments, if there is a Complaint in your exam, you know for a fact that Greiner is going to test you on the level of specificity. If there is not a Complaint, you can safely not worry about it.

## *Gillispie v. Goodyear Service Stores*, 128 S.E.2d 762 (N.C. 1963) — Code Pleading

**Rule:** In order to properly state a cause of action, a pleading must allege facts, not merely conclusions.

**Facts:**
- Gillispie (plaintiff) brought suit against Goodyear Service Stores (Goodyear) (defendant), alleging the following in the complaint: "the defendants...**maliciously came upon and trespassed** upon the premises occupied by the plaintiff as a residence, and by the **use of harsh and threatening language** and physical force directed against the plaintiff **assaulted the plaintiff** and **placed her in great fear, and humiliated and embarrassed her by subjecting her to public scorn and ridicule**, and caused her to be seized and exhibited to the public as a prisoner...."
- The trial court sustained Goodyear's demurrers to the complaint on the grounds that Gillispie's complaint did not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Gillispie appealed.

**Holding and Reasoning (Bobbit, J.):**
- In order to properly state a cause of action, a **pleading must allege facts, not merely legal conclusions**.
- Gillispie's complaint in this case states only legal conclusions accompanied with no supporting facts.
- The complaint does not allege **exactly what happened**, **where it happened**, **when it happened**, or any other facts that describe the circumstances of the incident.
- Rather, the complaint identifies mere legal conclusions, including trespassing and assault, and thus the complaint does not sufficiently state a cause of action.

In *Gillispie*, the Complaint does not seem to include too many details about what exactly happened, but it says that she was "removed from her house by the defendants and taken to jail." Additionally, it says that this happened in her house. The court therefore knows who did what, where it happened, and who did what. However, it would still be difficult from this complaint for the defendant or the defendant's attorneys to open up an investigation and start gathering evidence to support their defense.

<!-- page: 17 -->

In this specific case, it seems that the defendants were likely government officials, and so that is why the court was hesitant. If they were police officers, the court would be interested in whether they were acting within their authorized powers. Therefore, these are the sort of facts that would be helpful in the complaint to help defendants prepare their defense.

---

### Notice Pleading vs. Code Pleading — Summary

The *Gillispie* complaint likely was sufficient to place the defendant on "**notice**." But if we ask ourselves whether the Complaint included enough information that the court would know enough such that if the plaintiff proves these facts, they are likely to win the litigation — **likely not**.

- **Code Pleading:** If we wanted the Complaint to include that level of information, we would call it Code Pleading.
- **Notice Pleading:** Says that we only want the Complaint to give us the amount of information necessary to **put the defendants and the defendant's attorneys on notice of the allegations** against them, and to allow them to **begin an investigation**.
- **Code Pleading:** We want to **test the legal theory** and the sufficiency of the facts that the plaintiff is going to be able to prove.
