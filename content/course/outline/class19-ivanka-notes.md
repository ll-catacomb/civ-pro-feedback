## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***Liberty Mutual Insurance Co. v. Wetzel*, 424 U.S. 737 (SCOTUS)(1976)** — Sample case analyzing 28 USC 1292(a)(1) and 28 USC 1292(b) exceptions to final order doctrine.
  A finding of liability that does not finally dispose of the plaintiff's prayers for relief is not appealable to the court of appeals. In other words, in order to be able to file an appeal, there must be a final order (judgment) from the trial court.

- ***Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp.*, 337 U.S. 541 (SCOTUS)(1949)** — Case establishing the Collateral Order Doctrine.
  An appeal may be taken from a lower court's interlocutory ruling that conclusively determines a disputed issue that is separate from, and collateral to, the merits of the action and would be effectively unreviewable after a final judgment in the action.

- ***Atlantic City Electric v. General Electric Co.*, 337 F.2d 844 (2d Cir. 1964)**
  Pre-trial leave to appeal applications should only be granted when the filing party will not have a full opportunity to assert its rights on appeal of the final judgment.

- ***La Buy v. Howes Leather Co.*, 352 U.S. 249 (SCOTUS)(1957)**
  A court of appeals has the power to issue a writ of mandamus compelling a district judge to vacate his order referring an antitrust case for trial before a master.

### Statutes

<!-- page: 2 -->

- **28 USC § 1291 – Final Decisions of District Courts ("final order doctrine")**
  The courts of appeals (other than the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit) shall have jurisdiction of appeals from all final decisions of the district courts of the United States, the United States District Court for the District of the Canal Zone, the District Court of Guam, and the District Court of the Virgin Islands, except where a direct review may be had in the Supreme Court. The jurisdiction of the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit shall be limited to the jurisdiction described in sections 1292(c) and (d) and 1295 of this title.

- **28 USC § 1292 – Interlocutory Decisions** (exceptions to the "final order doctrine"; "Interlocutory" comes from the Latin word for *Interrupt*)
  - **(a)** Except as provided in subsections (c) and (d) of this section, the courts of appeals shall have jurisdiction of appeals from:
    - **(1) Interlocutory orders** of the district courts of the United States, the United States District Court for the District of the Canal Zone, the District Court of Guam, and the District Court of the Virgin Islands, or of the judges thereof, **granting, continuing, modifying, refusing or dissolving injunctions, or refusing to dissolve or modify injunctions**, except where a direct review may be had in the Supreme Court;
  - **(b)** When a district judge, in making in a civil action an order not otherwise appealable under this section, **shall be of the opinion that such order involves a controlling question of law as to which there is substantial ground for difference of opinion and that an immediate appeal from the order may materially advance the ultimate termination of the litigation**, he shall **so state in writing in such order**. The **Court of Appeals** which would have jurisdiction of an appeal of such action may thereupon, **in its discretion**, permit an appeal to be taken from such order, **if application is made to it within ten days after the entry of the order**. Application for an appeal hereunder shall not stay proceedings in the district court unless the district judge or the Court of Appeals or a judge thereof shall so order. (*Atlantic City* illustrates exercise of appellate discretion in a 28 USC 1292(b) case.)

- **28 USC § 1651 – Writs**
  - (a) The Supreme Court and all courts established by Act of Congress may issue all writs necessary or appropriate in aid of their respective jurisdictions and agreeable to the usages and principles of law.
  - (b) An alternative writ or rule nisi may be issued by a justice or judge of a court which has jurisdiction.

### Federal Rules of Civil Procedure

<!-- page: 3 -->

- **Fed. R. Civ. P. 23(f) – Appeals.**
  A court of appeals may permit an appeal from an order granting or denying class-action certification under this rule, but not from an order under Rule 23(e)(1). A party must file a petition for permission to appeal with the circuit clerk within 14 days after the order is entered or within 45 days after the order is entered if any party is the United States, a United States agency, or a United States officer or employee sued for an act or omission occurring in connection with duties performed on the United States' behalf. An appeal does not stay proceedings in the district court unless the district judge or the court of appeals so orders.

- **Fed. R. Civ. P. 54(b) – Judgment; Costs.** (an exception to the final order doctrine of 28 USC 1291)
  - **(b) Judgment on Multiple Claims or Involving Multiple Parties.** When an action presents more than one claim for relief—whether as a claim, counterclaim, crossclaim, or third-party claim—or when multiple parties are involved, the court may direct entry of a final judgment as to one or more, but fewer than all, claims or parties only if the court **expressly determines that there is no just reason for delay**. Otherwise, any order or other decision, however designated, that adjudicates fewer than all the claims or the rights and liabilities of fewer than all the parties does not end the action as to any of the claims or parties and may be revised at any time before the entry of a judgment adjudicating all the claims and all the parties' rights and liabilities.

### FRCP 50 – Judgment as a Matter of Law in a Jury Trial; Related Motion for New Trial; Conditional Ruling

- **(a) BEFORE JURY VERDICT** — Empowers a judge to issue **judgment as a matter of law** when one party has:
  1. Been fully heard on the issue; and
  2. Introduced all the evidence it wants to introduce on the case,
  and the other party moves for Judgment as a Matter of Law (very similar to Summary Judgment).

- **(b) AFTER JURY VERDICT** — There has been a jury verdict and the party who lost moves for a **judgment as a matter of law**.

<!-- page: 4 -->

**Analysis by the court:**

1. Examine the **trial record** (not the discovery record, as in Summary Judgment).
2. **(BEFORE VERDICT – FRCP 50(a)(1))** Draw all reasonable inferences in favor of the non-moving party and ask whether a reasonable trier of fact could find in favor of the non-moving party with respect to whatever issue is the target of the motion.
   - Example (negligence): plaintiff had to prove **duty, breach, causation, and damages**; defendant can still move for JMOL saying that even construing all evidence in the light most favorable to the plaintiff, there is insufficient evidence to prove causation.

**OR, IF THERE HAS BEEN A VERDICT, THEN THE PARTY WHO LOST CAN FILE A MOTION:**

3. **(AFTER VERDICT)** Draw all reasonable inferences in favor of the non-moving party and ask whether a reasonable trier of fact could have found in favor of the non-moving party with respect to whatever issue is the target of the motion.

- **FRCP 50(a) – JMOL** – Motion made prior to the jury verdict.
- **FRCP 50(b) – JNOV** – If the court does not grant the JMOL, it is considered to have deferred it; after the jury renders a verdict the losing party may file a "renewal" of the JMOL, now referred to as a **JNOV** (Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict).
  - **In order to file the post-verdict motion, you have to have filed pre-verdict.** Policy: puts the court and opposing party on notice; sometimes the court may allow the other party to submit additional evidence.

- **REMEMBER FOR APPEALS:** A judge's grant or denial of a Judgment as a Matter of Law will be reviewed **"De Novo."**
  - District court gets no deference.
  - Appellate court is in the same position as the district court to determine "reasonableness."

### FRCP 59 – Motions for a New Trial

<!-- page: 5 -->

- **Middle of trial:** May be made during trial but will likely not be granted.
- **After trial:** May be made after the jury renders its verdict, or even after the judge enters entry on the judgment.

**Three different grounds for the grant of a new trial:**

1. **Erroneous ruling** — There was some evidentiary or legal ruling by the judge so obviously wrong that, if appealed, it would for sure be reversed by the appellate court (clearly **not harmless error**). Judges will be reluctant to admit prior errors — very rare.

2. **Verdict "against the great weight of the evidence"** (*Yeatts*) — Even though the jury's verdict was rational, the court is persuaded the decision is against the great weight of the evidence.
   - In a **Rule 50 (JMOL)** ruling, the judge construes factual conflicts in the light most favorable to the non-moving party and denies the motion.
   - But if a judge was really convinced a witness was lying, the judge might grant a Rule 59 Motion for a New Trial — very rare.

3. **Additur/Remittitur** — When a party files a motion for a new trial:
   - **Additur** — looking to the Defendant to accept higher damages as a condition to deny the motion for a new trial.
   - **Remittitur** — looking to the Plaintiff to accept lower damages as a condition to deny the motion for a new trial.
   - **Federal courts:** Only **Remittitur** is constitutional (7th Amendment).

<!-- page: 6 -->

   - **State courts:** Both are permissible (7th Amendment does not apply to individual states):
     1. Some have only Remittitur available.
     2. Some have both available.
     3. None have only Additur available.

### FRCP 6(b)(2) and Finality

We moved on to discussing the importance of finality in the federal courts system.
- Policy justification: Important to stop fighting at some point.
- FRCP 6(b)(2) limits the circumstances in which time limits can be extended.

### FRCP Rule 60 – Relief from Judgment

Judgments get upset all the time; nonetheless, it is very seldom that the courts will grant relief from a judgment. This is illustrative of the overarching importance of finality in the federal court system.

### Appellate Jurisdiction (28 USC 1291 and 28 USC 1292)

In the federal court system, appellate jurisdiction is jurisdictional in the same way as SMJ: an appellate court has an independent obligation to notice a potential defect in its appellate jurisdiction on its own, and if there is in fact a defect, it must **dismiss the appeal** (not the case!), and maybe hear the appeal later.

**Final Decisions**

- **Final decisions** — The rule in the federal system is to only allow appeals from final decisions. (**28 USC § 1291**)
  - Typically this means the actual "entry" of judgment by a judge.
  - This is the overwhelming preference of the federal system.
  - Examples:
    - Grants of motions for new trial = **NOT** final decisions
    - Denial of a motion for a new trial = **FINAL** decision
    - Grant or denial of a Rule 50 JMOL = **FINAL** decision
    - Judge enters final judgment without any Rule 50 or Rule 59 motions = **FINAL** decision

- **Exceptions to the final order doctrine (28 USC § 1291):**
  - **FRCP Rule 54(b)** – Judgment on Multiple Claims or Involving Multiple Parties

<!-- page: 7 -->

  - Requirements under FRCP 54(b) for partial judgment:
    - When an action presents more than one claim for relief, the court may direct entry of a final judgment as to one or more, but fewer than all, claims **ONLY IF:**
      1. It **expressly finds or determines** (orally and in writing, but at least orally stated by the judge); AND
      2. That there is **no just reason for delay** (claims as to which the court is entering final judgment are unrelated to the claims still being litigated).
    - If both requirements are met, the court can enter final judgment as to some but not all of the claims.
  - **Greiner definition of "claim"** for partial judgment purposes:
    1. A set of facts; and
    2. A legal theory; and
    3. A party.
    - If any of these three are different in a case with "multiple" issues, then you have more than one "claim."

### *Liberty Mutual Insurance Co. v. Wetzel*, 424 U.S. 737 (SCOTUS)(1976)

<!-- page: 8 -->

**Holding:** A finding of liability that does not finally dispose of the plaintiff's prayers for relief is not appealable to the court of appeals. There must be a final order (judgment) from the trial court.

**Facts**
- Wetzel (plaintiff) brought suit against Liberty Mutual Insurance Co. (defendant) in the U.S. District Court for the Western District of Pennsylvania, alleging that Liberty's insurance benefits and maternity leave regulations violated Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964.
- Wetzel requested injunctive relief ordering Liberty to cease its discriminatory practices and establish non-discriminatory practices, and damages including back pay and exemplary damages.
- The district court found for Wetzel but granted none of the requested relief.
- Liberty appealed to the court of appeals, which affirmed, then petitioned the U.S. Supreme Court for certiorari.

**Holding**
- The district court's decision is **not appealable to the court of appeals, because the district court's decision does not finally dispose of any of Wetzel's requests for relief**.
- Wetzel **set forth a single claim**, and while the district court found for her, it left unresolved what relief would be granted.
- The **district court's order was merely a grant of partial summary judgment** on the issue of Liberty's liability.
- Grants of **partial summary judgment are interlocutory**, not final.
- The only **appealable interlocutory judgments are grants of injunctive relief**.
- **Since the district court made no ruling on the request for injunctive relief, this was not a basis for appeal.**
- The district court and court of appeals also found the decision appealable as a final judgment under FRCP 54(b), which allows entry of judgment on fewer than all claims in a multiple-claim action.
- **This, however, was not a multiple-claim action. Rather, there was a single claim and prayers for several types of relief.**

**28 USC § 1292(a)(1)** (another exception to the final order doctrine of 28 USC 1291): An appeal is appropriate when there is an "interlocutory order granting, continuing, modifying, refusing or dissolving injunctions, or refusing to dissolve or modify injunctions…"
- Why we allow immediate appeals on injunctions:
  - Immediate consequences for the parties
  - Avoiding irreparable harm
- Failure to rule on injunction is not listed in 28 USC § 1292(a)(1) — failure to rule (even though it effectively has the same effect as a refusal to grant) does not provide a basis for appeal (*Thane Coat*).

<!-- page: 9 -->

> **Note:** The right remedy when a district court is not ruling on something is NOT to appeal the failure to rule. The right remedy is to **petition for a writ of mandamus** — an order in the appellate circuit forcing the district court to issue a ruling, which you can later appeal from.

In *Liberty Mutual*, 28 USC § 1292(a)(1) did not provide a basis for appeal because the district court had not ruled.
- The court also noted that if the refusal to rule were construed as a denial of a preliminary injunction, the plaintiffs would have appealed; but here the defendants were the ones to appeal, making clear this was not a § 1291 situation.

---

### 28 USC § 1292(b) – "Double Discretion Doctrine"

Another exception to the final order doctrine of 28 USC 1291. An interlocutory appeal is appropriate when the district judge is of the opinion that an order not otherwise appealable:
1. **Involves a controlling question of law** as to which there is substantial ground for difference of opinion; **AND**
2. **An immediate appeal from the order may materially advance the ultimate termination of the litigation** — he shall so state in writing.

The district judge must make this finding after just issuing an order:
1. Controlling question of law
2. **Substantial ground for difference** of opinion; and
3. If appealed now, it can get us to the end of the case faster (**materially advance the ultimate termination**)

- The judge is basically admitting this was a tough call, and that it is better to find out now if the appellate court sees it differently.
- Unlike the exception under FRCP 54(b), **28 USC § 1292 requires that the judge issue this determination in writing**:
  - Because this decision should not be made lightly or often.
  - Higher burden because the trial pauses until this is done.
  - **Time limit for this exception is 10 days only!**
- In order to use this exception, **the court of appeals must also agree** — hence "**double discretion**."

<!-- page: 10 -->

- District judge uses its discretion in making this determination.
- Appellate judges (must be at least 2 out of 3 judges on the panel) must agree to hear it.

In *Liberty Mutual*, 28 USC § 1292(b) also did not provide a basis for appeal because the defendant had not appealed within the 10-day limit, and there was no indication that the defendant had asked the district court to make these 3 determinations in writing.

### *Atlantic City Electric v. General Electric Co.*, 337 F.2d 844 (2d Cir. 1964)

Sample case for the "double discretion" doctrine (28 USC 1292(b)).

**Holding:** Pre-trial leave to appeal applications should only be granted when the filing party will not have a full opportunity to assert its rights on appeal of the final judgment.

**Facts**
- General Electric Co. (GE) (defendant) asked Atlantic City Electric Co. (AC) (plaintiff) certain interrogatories seeking to discover whether AC shifted relevant damages on to their electric customers.
- AC objected to the interrogatories and the district court sustained the objections.
- GE filed an application for an interlocutory appeal on the grounds that the district court, when sustaining the objections, **foreclosed defendants from pre-trial discovery of facts relating to their defense** (that plaintiffs did not incur any damages because they passed them on to customers).
- District court certified its order sustaining the objections involved a controlling question of law with substantial grounds for difference of opinion.

<!-- page: 11 -->

**Issue**
- Should the court allow the appeal (of the sustained objection to the interrogatories) to move forward while the rest of the trial awaits?

**Holding**
- Pre-trial leave to appeal applications should **only be granted when the filing party will not have a full opportunity to assert its rights on appeal of the final judgment**.
- Such applications must be judged "against the backdrop of the entire case."
- Allowing piecemeal appeals of several distinct questions is not efficient, and **not necessary if the party is able to raise the issues on appeal of the final judgment**.
- In the case at bar, GE's rights to its "passed-on damages" defense are not lost by not allowing this interlocutory appeal.
- GE will have full opportunity to appeal the district court's decision on the interrogatories after the district court's final judgment in the case.
- By making GE wait, the court enables GE to combine its appeal on this issue with any other errors the district court committed or will commit.
- **GE's application for interlocutory appeal is denied.**

**Background context:**
- The allegation was that suppliers collaborated to agree on an artificially high set price by eliminating market competition, violating Sherman Act § 1 (antitrust).
- The utility (plaintiff) would likely pass on the higher cost to its customers rather than incur harm itself — the "pass-on defense."
- In our justice system, we want people to sue when they are hurt themselves, not on behalf of someone else who may be hurt.
- The supplier defendant was trying to get discovery from the utility company to see whether the utility was actually harmed or whether it passed the harm on to customers.
- The utility company sought a protective order from having to provide the discovery — arguing the Sherman Act does not allow the "pass-on" defense and that the mere fixing of prices is the illegal act.

<!-- page: 12 -->

- The district court granted the protective order, preventing plaintiffs from having to provide the requested discovery.
- The defendants asked the court to make a 28 USC 1292(b) determination — the court agreed to make such determinations.
- Nonetheless, **the appellate court exercised discretion and decided not to take the appeal at the moment**.
  - If the district court had instead allowed the discovery and the plaintiff had appealed, the appeals court might have ruled differently and heard the appeal because costs were being incurred.
  - Additionally, if the defendant appealed after trial and there was found to be no agreement to set prices, there would be no harm done — no actual need to reach the pass-on defense issue.

### "Cohen" or Collateral Order Doctrine

<!-- page: 12 -->

In addition to FRCP 54(b), 28 USC 1292(a)(1) and 28 USC 1292(b), another exception to the *final order doctrine* is the **"Cohen" or Collateral Order Doctrine** — allowing appellate review of district court rulings that are separable from the merits of the case, final, urgent, and involve an important, unsettled question of law.

<!-- page: 13 -->

The **collateral order doctrine** is a narrow exception to the final-judgment rule, which normally forces parties to wait for final judgment before appealing any rulings. This doctrine emerged in *Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp.* In that case, the SCOTUS held to be appealable those orders which "finally determine claims of right separable from, and collateral to, rights asserted in the action, too important to be denied review and too independent of the cause itself to require that appellate consideration be deferred until the whole case is adjudicated."

The **collateral order doctrine** allows appeals from interlocutory rulings so long as those rulings **conclusively decide an issue separate from the merits of the case** and **would be effectively unreviewable after final judgment**.

**Hypothetical — Litigation Bond:**
- If a lawsuit requires a bond and one party moves to require the other to post a bond or dismiss the litigation, and the judge denies the motion requiring the bond:
  - Is that an appealable decision under 28 USC § 1291 (final order doctrine)? **No.** Litigation is continuing.
  - Does it qualify for immediate appeal under any exceptions to 28 USC 1291?
    - 1292(a)(1): Not an injunction — **No.**
    - FRCP 54(b): Not a "claim" (theory of relief + facts + party) — **No.**
    - 28 USC § 1292(b): Not a controlling issue of law, not substantial ground for difference of opinion, would not materially advance termination — **No.**
  - This is where the **Collateral Order Doctrine** comes in — because if the appeal to the bond requirement is not allowed immediately, it will be pointless later after the trial is fully litigated (no bond issuer willing to issue a bond after a loss).

<!-- page: 14 -->

**Key limitation:** SCOTUS says we might not allow collateral order jurisdiction in a circumstance where the issue is not a clean "yes or no" legal issue, but instead requires messy, fact-specific discretionary decisions by the district court.

#### Collateral Order Doctrine — Black Letter Law

An immediate appeal can be granted if the circumstance fits **all three elements**:

<!-- page: 15 -->

1. **(Most important)** If the appeal is **not allowed immediately**, the issue would be **effectively unreviewable after final judgment** of the trial.
2. The issue (the subject matter of the immediate appeal) **not be related to the merits of the case** being litigated.
3. It must be **final as to the particular issue**.

**UNLESS** it is:
- A circumstance where the issue is **not a clean "yes or no" legal issue** but requires a messy, fact-specific discretionary decision by the district court; or
- A **venue (transfer) issue — forum selection clause** (*Lauro Lines*); or
- An issue of **compelling testimony of attorney-client privileged information** (*Mohawk*);

**BUT CAN be used if it is:**
- A **"stay" (abstention doctrine) issue** (*Colorado River*)

---

### Examples: Circumstances That Do NOT Fit the Collateral Order Doctrine

#### 1. *Lauro Lines S.R.L. v. Chasser* — Venue Issues

<!-- page: 15 -->

**Hypothetical:**
- Airline business. Passenger purchases a ticket with a forum selection clause designating California as the litigation forum.
- Airline denies passenger boarding. Plaintiff sues in federal district court in MA alleging breach of contract.
- Citing *Atlantic Marine*, airline lawyers ask the court to transfer the case per the forum selection clause.
- District court in MA (ignoring private interest factors, finding public interest factors overwhelmingly point to MA) keeps the case in MA despite the forum selection clause.
- Airline wants to appeal this venue ruling immediately.

**Analysis:**
- **28 USC 1291:** Is it a final order? No.
- **FRCP 54(b):** Is it a "claim"? No.
- **28 USC 1292(a)(1):** Is it an injunction? No.
- **28 USC 1292(b):** Is it a controlling question of law to which there might be substantial grounds for a difference of opinion? No.
- **Collateral Order Doctrine:** **No.**
  1. Issue effectively unreviewable after final judgment? **No.** (*Lauro Lines*)
  2. Not related, but collateral to, merits of the case? Yes.
  3. Decision final as to the particular issue? Yes.

*(Fails element 1.)*

<!-- page: 16 -->

#### 2. *Mohawk Industries, Inc. v. Carpenter* — Attorney-Client Privilege

**Hypothetical:**
- Party A's lawyer, taking Party B's deposition, asks Party B whether he had a conversation about the case with his lawyer. Party B's lawyer claims attorney-client privilege. Party A believes Party B is using the privilege to hide a crime (fraud), which would be an exception to the privilege.
- Party B moves for a protective order; Party A moves for an order compelling testimony.
- District court decides Party B must answer.
- Party B wants to appeal the order compelling testimony of attorney-client privileged information immediately.

**Analysis:**
- **28 USC 1291:** Is it a final order? No.
- **FRCP 54(b):** Is it a "claim"? No.
- **28 USC 1292(a)(1):** Is it an injunction? No.
- **28 USC 1292(b):** Is it a controlling question of law? No.
- **Collateral Order Doctrine:** **No.**
  1. Issue effectively unreviewable after final judgment? **No.** (Although the attorney-client privileged information should never be revealed, SCOTUS decided that the trial can finish all the way through, and then the party can move for a new trial asserting the jury never should have heard the privileged conversation as evidence; if that motion is denied, the denial of the new trial could be appealed.)
  2. Not related, but collateral to, merits of the case? Yes.
  3. Decision final as to the particular issue? Yes.

*(Fails element 1.)*

<!-- page: 17 -->

### Example: Circumstance That DOES Fit the Collateral Order Doctrine

#### *Colorado River Water Conservation District v. United States*, 424 US 800 (1976) (SCOTUS) — Abstention Doctrine

When a district court grants a motion refusing to proceed with federal district court litigation in deference to state court proceedings, it ordinarily **stays** the federal case in place, allowing the state case to go to judgment. Anticipating claim preclusion, the judgment in the state case will effectively kill the federal litigation. The question: Is a *Colorado River* "stay" immediately appealable under the Collateral Order Doctrine?

**Analysis:**
- **Collateral Order Doctrine:** **Yes.**
  1. Is the "stay" effectively unreviewable after final judgment? **Yes.**
     - State court litigation will continue to judgment. The defendant will bring the judgment into federal court and claim the issue is precluded from litigation under claim preclusion. The district court will lift the "stay" and dismiss the case. If plaintiff then appeals and says the "stay" should never have been issued, the appeals court will say the basis of dismissal was not the "stay" — it was "claim preclusion." So the stay issue is effectively unreviewable.
  2. Not related, but collateral to, merits of the case? Yes.
  3. Decision final as to the particular issue? Yes.

### *Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp.*, 337 U.S. 541 (SCOTUS)(1949)

<!-- page: 18 -->

**Holding:** An appeal may be taken from a lower court's interlocutory ruling that conclusively determines a disputed issue that **is separate from, and collateral to**, the merits of the action and would be **effectively unreviewable after a final judgment** in the action.

**Facts**
- A shareholder derivative action is a lawsuit in which a shareholder alleges the corporation is being managed to benefit management, not shareholders.
- The shareholder must make a demand to the company that it re-coup its losses from managers who took excess benefits; if the company does not comply, aggrieved shareholders can sue.
- Because such litigation can be very cumbersome, **New Jersey adopted a law providing that if a plaintiff in a shareholder derivative suit owned less than 5 percent of the shares, that shareholder must post a bond** to institute the litigation. If the shareholder did not win, he was required to pay the corporation's attorney's fees.
- Cohen (plaintiff) owned approximately .0125 percent of Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp. (defendant), which did business in New Jersey.
- Cohen believed Beneficial's management was enriching itself at the expense of the corporation and brought suit in federal district court.
- **The district court held that since the FRCP did not require the filing of a pre-litigation bond, it was not bound to follow the New Jersey statute because, under the rule of *Erie R. Co. v. Tompkins*, 304 U.S. 64 (1938), the statute was merely procedural.** — "Erie (VCOL) issue!!!" (NJ state law conflicting with a FRCP)
- The court of appeals reversed and ordered a bond to be posted.
- The United States Supreme Court granted certiorari.

**Issues** (this case had two major legal issues — the VCOL "Erie" issue, and the Collateral Order Doctrine issue):
1. May an appeal be taken from a lower court's interlocutory ruling that conclusively determines a disputed issue that is separate from, and collateral to, the merits of the action and would be effectively unreviewable after a final judgment in the action?
2. Is a statute requiring that a bond be posted before a shareholder brings a derivative suit both constitutionally permissible and sufficiently substantive to be applied by a federal court sitting in diversity?

<!-- page: 19 -->

**Holding and Reasoning (Jackson, J.)**
- **(1) Yes.** An appeal may be taken from a lower court's interlocutory ruling that conclusively determines a disputed issue that is separate from, and collateral to, the merits of the action and would be effectively unreviewable after a final judgment in the action.
  - In general, an appellate court may not hear an appeal from any matter that is tentative or unfinished.
  - An appellate court may not hear an appeal from a final ruling that represents one step toward the final resolution of a matter, as these rulings will eventually merge together into one appealable final judgment.
  - However, **in some situations, a lower court may issue a final decision on a disputed matter which is separable from the merits of the action and which is too important to deny review or delay review until the entry of final judgment.**
  - **Interlocutory appellate review is appropriate in these situations.**
  - In this case, the district court's order refusing to apply the New Jersey bond statute is a final disposition of the question of the right to posted security, which is an important and disputed issue.
  - The resolution of this issue has **nothing to do with the merits** of the underlying shareholder derivative suit, and the order will **not be merged into the final judgment**.
  - **By the time a final judgment has been entered, it will be too late to effectively review this order**, and any right to security will have been irreparably lost.
  - **Accordingly, the district court's order is appealable at this stage of the litigation.**

- **(2) Yes.** A statute requiring that a bond be posted before a shareholder brings a derivative suit is **both constitutionally permissible and sufficiently substantive to be applied by a federal court sitting in diversity.**
  - A state has an interest in ensuring a minority shareholder does not bring an action that depletes the corporation's accounts and injures all other shareholders.
  - The corporate managers accused of wrongdoing have been chosen and are supported by the majority of shareholders.
  - The directors bear the imprimatur of the majority, while a minority shareholder bringing a derivative suit does not.
  - It is well within the state's power to require the plaintiff to post a bond ensuring he can pay the corporation's fees if litigation is unsuccessful.

<!-- page: 20 -->

  - New Jersey's statute does not raise an unconstitutional barrier to entering the courts.
  - New Jersey's rule creates a new liability for Cohen that would not have existed without the law.
  - Accordingly, it is **not a state procedural rule but a substantive rule**, which binds the federal courts.
  - The courts cannot ignore New Jersey's choice by holding that New Jersey's substantive requirement was merely a procedural matter.

### Writ of Mandamus

The last grounds for an immediate appeal that appellate courts would seriously consider.

**28 USC § 1651 – Writs**
- (a) The Supreme Court and all courts established by Act of Congress may issue all writs necessary or appropriate in aid of their respective jurisdictions and agreeable to the usages and principles of law.
- (b) An alternative writ or rule nisi may be issued by a justice or judge of a court which has jurisdiction.

A petition for a writ is a **last resort** to get something in front of an appellate court when none of the other exceptions work. It is not really an appeal; instead, it is more like initiating a new lawsuit in the court of appeals, called a **writ of mandamus** — a separate lawsuit asking the appellate court to order a district judge (the **respondent**) to do or not do something. The petitioner is a regular person; the respondent is the district judge, not the opposing party.

This is an awkward situation because the petitioner is:
- In the middle of a trial before the district judge (wanting to remain on the judge's good side); but
- Simultaneously suing the district judge in the appellate court to force a ruling.
  - First, an appellate court is not normally where litigation is initiated.
  - Second, the party having to litigate before the appellate court is the district judge him/herself.

Appellate courts rarely grant petitions for writs of mandamus. When they do, the circumstances are one of two situations:

<!-- page: 21 -->

**1. Regular Writ:** When the petitioner is able to convince the court that:
1. There is a **really big consequence** that will occur if the petition is not granted;
2. They have a **clear and indisputable right** to this relief; and
3. That generally it would be **a good idea** for the appeal court to grant this petition.

> **Note:** These factors parallel the preliminary injunction factors:
> - a) Irreparable harm (= really big consequence)
> - b) Likelihood of success on the merits (= likelihood of success on the merits)
> - c) Balance of the equities/public interest (= generally a good idea to grant petition)

Although writs of mandamus are always an option, they are very rarely successful.

---

### *La Buy v. Howes Leather Co.*, 352 U.S. 249 (SCOTUS)(1957)

**Holding:** A court of appeals has the power to issue a writ of mandamus compelling a district judge to vacate his order referring an antitrust case for trial before a master.

**Facts**
- Two antitrust actions were filed in the district court.
- They involved multiple plaintiffs and defendants and were "burdensome" on the district judge, Judge La Buy.
- Due to the burden and complexity of the cases, along with the congestion in his court, Judge La Buy entered orders referring the cases for trial before a "master."
- Petitions for mandamus were filed seeking a **"supervisory" writ of mandamus** ordering La Buy to vacate the orders.
- The United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit issued the writs under the All Writs Act, ordering La Buy to vacate his orders and hear the case. La Buy appealed.

*(Essentially: two complex antitrust lawsuits happening simultaneously; the judge did not want to do the work so he referred the cases to a "special master" due to a congested calendar. Both parties filed writs of mandamus against Judge La Buy so the judge would be forced to be the trier of fact rather than shift his duties to the special master.)*

**Holding**
- **A court of appeals has the power to issue a writ of mandamus compelling a district judge to vacate his order referring an antitrust case for trial before a master.**
- Writs of mandamus are only to be issued in **extreme circumstances**.
- The **use of a master in a case is a tool designed to aid judges in the performance of specific judicial tasks; masters are not designed to displace the court.**
- The Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit was correct in **issuing the writs of mandamus as Judge La Buy exceeded his authority** and deprived the parties of the opportunity for trial.
- La Buy's reasons for referral are not sufficient to warrant the referral.

<!-- page: 22 -->

- Congestion on the court calendar is common and certainly not an exceptional circumstance calling for referral to a master.
- Although the cases were complex and burdensome, most if not all antitrust cases embody those characteristics — that does not mean the cases should be referred to a master, depriving antitrust litigants of a trial.
- Allowing this referral may begin the slippery slope toward "mak[ing] references the rule rather than the exception."
- As a result, the writs of mandamus were properly granted.

> **Supervisory writ:** When the appellate court sees district courts doing something it does not like and issues a writ for all of them to stop — not just to the one respondent, but as a general writ to all lower district courts.

**Practical note:** After writs of mandamus get issued, the case will go right back to the original district court judge, who will likely be irritated with the party(ies) requesting the writ. Yikes!

> For further reading on interlocutory appeals, the collateral order doctrine, and writs of mandamus: https://www.subscriptlaw.com/blog/interlointerlocutory-appeals-collateral-orders-writs-of-mandamus
> Disclaimer: if there is any difference in the law on this blog and the BLL explained in class, the class contents supersede the blog.

### Federal Rules of Civil Procedure for Expanding the Lawsuit

#### 1. Original Suit

A basic lawsuit: one Plaintiff (P1) sues one Defendant (D1) on a claim. This is what we have been considering all along.

```
P1 ─────────────────► D1
        P1 Sues D1
```

#### 2. Counterclaim — FRCP 13(a) and (b)

<!-- page: 24 -->

P1 sues D1 and D1 also counter-sues P1.

- **Example 1:** Breach of contract where both parties allege the other breached.
- **Example 2:** Declaratory judgment where each party files a simultaneous declaratory judgment — this is powerful because it forces the other party to countersue in the court of the first party's choosing, or forfeit the ability to countersue later.

```
P1 ◄─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─► D1
         D1 brings counterclaim
     T or O: Compulsory, Rule 13(a)
                  OR
     Not T or O: Permissive, Rule 13(b)
```

**FRCP 13(a) – Compulsory:** If the defendant does not file a counterclaim at this point and the case goes to judgment, the Defendant will **not be allowed** to file this counterclaim later. It **must** be filed now, if at all.
- **If the counterclaim arises under the same Transaction or Occurrence** that is the subject matter of the opposing party's claim, the counterclaim is **COMPULSORY**.

**FRCP 13(b) – Permissive:** If the defendant does not file a counterclaim at this point and the case goes to judgment, the Defendant **can** choose to file this counterclaim sometime later.
- **If the counterclaim does NOT arise under the same Transaction or Occurrence** that is the subject matter of the opposing party's claim, the counterclaim is **PERMISSIVE** and there is no requirement to file it.

#### 3. New Plaintiff — FRCP 20(a)(1)

<!-- page: 25 -->

An additional Plaintiff (P2) wants to sue the same Defendant (D1) at the same time as P1 in a "Joint Complaint."

```
P1 ◄─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─► D1
P2          Permissive Joinder
            Same T or O, plus
          common issue of law or fact
                Rule 20(a)(1)
```

**FRCP 20(a)(1) – Permissive Joinder (plaintiffs):** P2 never has to join with P1 — they can always sue D1 later. But it is permissible to join together and sue a defendant so long as P2's claim **arises under the same Transaction or Occurrence** as P1's claim, **PLUS** there must be **at least one common issue at law or fact**.
- It will (and should) be easy to come up with a "common issue at law or fact" whenever the joining lawsuit arises out of the "same transaction or occurrence."

> **COMPULSORY RULE TRIGGERED:** Since there is now a separate (although joining) lawsuit against D1, after analyzing FRCP 20(a)(1) we need to analyze again to see if D1 must file a **compulsory counterclaim (FRCP 13(a))** against P2 or not.

#### 4. New Defendant — FRCP 20(a)(2)

<!-- page: 26 -->

P1 and P2 want, in a single lawsuit, to simultaneously sue D1 and a new Defendant (D2). *(Note: this is two plaintiffs suing two defendants at the same time in one lawsuit — NOT adding a new defendant to an already-filed lawsuit.)*

```
P1 ◄─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─► D1
P2          Permissive Joinder           D2
            Same T or O, plus
          Common issue of law or fact
                Rule 20(a)(2)
```

**FRCP 20(a)(2) – Permissive Joinder (defendants):** It is permissible for two plaintiffs to join together and sue two separate defendants so long as the claim arises under the **same Transaction or Occurrence**, **PLUS** at least **one common issue at law or fact**.

> **COMPULSORY RULE TRIGGERED AGAIN:** After analyzing FRCP 20(a)(2), we need to analyze again to see if D2 must file a **compulsory counterclaim (FRCP 13(a))** against P1 and/or P2 or not.

#### 5. Cross-Claim — FRCP 13(g)

<!-- page: 27 -->

D1 wants to assert some theory of relief against D2. This will almost always come after the original complaint has been filed.

**FRCP 13(g) – Cross-Claim (one defendant to another):** It is permissible for one defendant to sue the other defendant in a lawsuit where both defendants have been sued by the same plaintiff(s), so long as the cross-claim **arises under the same Transaction or Occurrence, OR involves the same piece of property**. This FRCP 13(g) is **ALWAYS PERMISSIVE**.

```
P1 ◄─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─► D1
P2     Cross-Claiming Co-Party    ↓ D2
       Same T or O, or same property;
           Always Permissive Rule 13(g)
```

> **COMPULSORY RULE TRIGGERED AGAIN:** After analyzing FRCP 13(g), since D1 has filed a cross-claim against D2, we need to analyze again to see if D2 must file a **compulsory counterclaim (FRCP 13(a))** against D1 or not.

#### 6. Inter-Plaintiff Cross/Counter Claims

<!-- page: 28 -->

When one Plaintiff files a lawsuit against the other Plaintiff.

**FRCP 13(g) – Cross-Claim (one plaintiff to another):** It is permissible for one plaintiff to sue the other plaintiff in a lawsuit so long as the cross-claim **arises under the same Transaction or Occurrence, OR involves the same piece of property**. This FRCP 13(g) is **ALWAYS PERMISSIVE**.

<!-- page: 29 -->

> **COMPULSORY RULE TRIGGERED AGAIN:** Since a cross-claim has been filed by one Plaintiff against the other (essentially making the sued Plaintiff act like a defendant), we need to analyze again to see if the Plaintiff sued under the cross-claim must file a **compulsory counterclaim (FRCP 13(a))** against the plaintiff who filed the cross-claim or not.

> **NOTE on SMJ:** In a diversity action, Plaintiffs suing each other may not be diverse from each other — which would break complete diversity and cause a problem.

**What about Supplemental Jurisdiction (28 USC § 1367)?**

If the anchoring claim is an "arising under" 28 USC 1331 claim, there is no issue.

**HYPO:** If it is a "diversity only" 28 USC 1332 claim, and the Plaintiffs suing each other are not diverse, we must look further.

**28 USC § 1367(a) – Supplemental Jurisdiction:**
- Must have an anchoring claim: the diversity claim between P1 and D1/D2; or the diversity claim between P2 and D1/D2.

<!-- page: 30 -->

**28 USC § 1367(b) – Prevents use of Supplemental Jurisdiction if it is:**
- A claim **by a Plaintiff**; AND
- Against a person made a party under the list of FRCP; AND
- Where the exercise of such (supplemental) jurisdiction would be inconsistent with the requirements of 28 USC 1332 (diversity).

Conclusion: Even though the FRCP allow Plaintiffs in a diversity-only case to sue each other regardless of citizenship, **if they are in violation of the (independent basis of) Subject Matter Jurisdictional statutes, the lawsuit cannot be filed by one Plaintiff against another**.
- For **Defendants** suing each other, however, we do **not** have to worry about 28 USC 1367(b) because the claim would not be "by a Plaintiff."

#### 7. Third Party Practice — FRCP 14(a)

<!-- page: 30 -->

D1 wants to bring a **Third-Party Defendant (TPD)** — a brand-new person brought by one of the defendants to the litigation.

This is the only time we do not need to adhere to the "same transaction or occurrence rule." This rule allows a **shifting of liability** from one of the defendants to a new one.

Frequent third-party defendants include bond issuers and insurance companies. This is why there may be a question as to whether the insurance policy comes from the same transaction or occurrence as the underlying event giving rise to liability.

<!-- page: 31 -->

**FRCP 14(a) – Shifts Liability to a Third Party:** For the addition of a Third-Party Defendant to be brought into the lawsuit by a defendant, **it must be that it is shifting liability to that Third Party Defendant**. The same transaction or occurrence requirement does not apply here.

```
                         TPD
P1 ◄─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─► D1 ──────────► TPD
P2        Third Party Practice              ↑
          Not technically T or O;          D2
          Permissive, if shifts liability
          Rule 14(a); 14-day rule
```

> **COMPULSORY RULE TRIGGERED AGAIN:** After analyzing FRCP 14(a), since D1 has filed a third-party (liability shifting) claim against the TPD, we need to analyze again to see if the TPD must file a **compulsory counterclaim (FRCP 13(a))** against D1 or not.

In some complicated cases, the third-party defendant can assert a claim against a **Plaintiff**, rather than a defendant. This is **always permissive**, subject to the same transaction or occurrence test.
- Because the TPD (by shift of liability) is suing a plaintiff, we do **not** have to worry about 28 USC 1367(b) because the claim would not be "by a Plaintiff."

<!-- page: 32 -->

- **BUT**, in this case, because the TPD has sued a plaintiff, it triggers the **COMPULSORY RULE**, so IF the Plaintiff:
  - has to file a counterclaim;
  - in a diversity-only case;
  - against the third-party defendant; **THEN**
  - that counterclaim against the TPD is subject to the **Supplemental Jurisdiction requirements** (if that counterclaim against the third party would need Supplemental Jurisdiction).

#### 8. Permissive Joinder of "Claims" — FRCP 18(a)

<!-- page: 33 -->

Any claim that a party who is already suing another party might have — **no matter how unrelated to the original claim** — once there is already a lawsuit by Party A against Party B, then **Party A can bring any other additional claim against Party B**, even if that other additional claim does not meet the "same transaction or occurrence" test as the original claim.

*(Also known as "The Kitchen Sink")*

This is done to address all the concerns of parties who do not get along, for purposes of efficiency and cost savings.

**FRCP 42** allows the trial court to cut or sever the pieces of the trial into smaller ones depending on how it sees fit.
