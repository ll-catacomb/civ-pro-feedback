#### 28 U.S.C. § 1367 — Supplemental Jurisdiction

**(a)** Except as provided in subsections (b) and (c) or as expressly provided otherwise by Federal statute, in any civil action of which the district courts have original jurisdiction, the district courts shall have supplemental jurisdiction over all other claims that are so related to claims in the action within such original jurisdiction that they form part of the same case or controversy under Article III of the United States Constitution. Such supplemental jurisdiction shall include claims that involve the joinder or intervention of additional parties.

**(b)** In any civil action of which the district courts have original jurisdiction founded solely on section 1332 of this title, the district courts shall not have supplemental jurisdiction

<!-- page: 2 -->

under subsection (a) over claims by plaintiffs against persons made parties under Rule 14, 19, 20, or 24 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, or over claims by persons proposed to be joined as plaintiffs under Rule 19 of such rules, or seeking to intervene as plaintiffs under Rule 24 of such rules, when exercising supplemental jurisdiction over such claims would be inconsistent with the jurisdictional requirements of section 1332.

**(c)** The district courts may decline to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over a claim under subsection (a) if—

> (1) the claim raises a novel or complex issue of State law,
>
> (2) the claim substantially predominates over the claim or claims over which the district court has original jurisdiction,
>
> (3) the district court has dismissed all claims over which it has original jurisdiction, or
>
> (4) in exceptional circumstances, there are other compelling reasons for declining jurisdiction.

**(d)** The period of limitations for any claim asserted under subsection (a), and for any other claim in the same action that is voluntarily dismissed at the same time as or after the dismissal of the claim under subsection (a), shall be tolled while the claim is pending and for a period of 30 days after it is dismissed unless State law provides for a longer tolling period.

**(e)** As used in this section, the term "State" includes the District of Columbia, the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico, and any territory or possession of the United States.

### Abstention Doctrine — Exam Expectations

*(Which is generally about what sorts of cases the federal court refuses to hear)*

> ***Note: For the exam, you will not be responsible for knowing and applying the three different branches of abstention doctrine (or the black letter law content of each one), but merely what they are. You ARE responsible for knowing what the contours of* Colorado River *are.***

Out of the three branches of abstention doctrine, we need to know the **types of concerns** that led courts to create those three abstention doctrines:

1. Concern about interfering with intricate and complicated state law regulatory schemes (*Younger* — issues of criminal cases, tax collection, etc.)
2. Concern about inability to definitively interpret state law (only state courts can issue a decision that definitively interprets state law); and,
3. Concern about how that inability to definitively interpret state law regulatory schemes might mix together with the desire of federal courts to avoid unnecessary decision of constitutional issues.
   - Federal courts prefer not to decide a constitutional issue if they can find another basis on which the case can be decided.

### *Colorado River* — Deep Dive

***Colorado River Water Conservation District v. United States*, 424 U.S. 800 (1976) (SCOTUS) — (know 3 bases of abstention)**

Federal courts may defer jurisdiction to concurrent state proceedings, even where the doctrine of abstention does not apply, for reasons of wise judicial administration.

<!-- page: 6 -->

**Background context for the case:**

1. **28 U.S.C. § 1345** provides federal district courts with SMJ over any lawsuit in which the United States is a party.
2. The doctrine of **sovereign immunity** precludes suits against the United States (with certain exceptions, including one allowing suits for injunctions against allegedly unconstitutional federal action), and although Congress has the power to waive sovereign immunity, it historically has not done so.
3. The **federal government** often acts on behalf of Indian tribes, either formally as a "trustee" or informally as a protector or asserter of their rights. Federal government is viewed as less hostile to Native American interests than state governments.
4. The federal government at one time owned a great deal of the land that now constitutes the western portion of the United States.
   - It sold or granted most of this land to people or entities that would use it in accordance with the government's wishes, usually by living on it or otherwise developing it.
   - In these grants, the federal government would sometimes "reserve" rights corresponding to the land, including some water rights.

**The case:**

- Water was a big issue out west at the time.
- Due to the fight over water rights, there were constant pieces of never-ending litigation regarding when to draw water when water is scarce.
- The litigation in this case involved about 1,000 or more parties in ongoing pieces of litigation, fighting about whose rights are superior when it comes to getting water.
- Congress passed a statute (the **McCarran Amendment**) which said it is okay to sue the federal government in either state or federal court to adjudicate claims involving water rights (otherwise the doctrine of sovereign immunity would not allow the US to be sued).
- The US sometimes represents Native Americans when it comes to land or water rights because it is usually less hostile to Native Americans than states are.
- So there is ongoing state litigation, but instead of filing in state court, the US initiates litigation in federal court, sues around 1,000 defendants who are also still litigants in state court, in order to get a declaration on what its water rights are (and on behalf of the Native American rights it is representing).
- One of the state litigants then brings the US into the state litigation as well, leaving us with federal litigation amongst substantially the same parties, and parallel state litigation amongst substantially the same parties, simultaneously.
- Some of the parties then went to the federal court and asked it to "abstain" because there are two pieces of parallel litigation going on.
- US did not "**remove**" the case back to federal court because it could not obtain consent from all of the defendants (as required for removal — see notes above).

<!-- page: 7 -->

- The SCOTUS even says that the federal court has an **unflagging obligation** to adjudicate the case — nevertheless, SCOTUS approved a stay for the federal litigation and allowed the state litigation to move forward.
- However, they admit that this is not the norm, and that this case is exceptional.
- A district court may only abstain from exercising jurisdiction in *exceptional circumstances*, which have been confined to three general categories:
  - SCOTUS was concerned about interfering with intricate and complicated state law regulatory schemes — they believed CO was better suited to understand the CO regulatory scheme.
  - They also mentioned that the state court proceeding had been going on before the federal proceeding — and when deciding to dismiss, the court feels it should consider which one has progressed further (whichever gets judgment first will kill the other through a doctrine of **preclusion**, which we will hear at the end of the course).
  - They also mentioned that because the case involves so many litigants who reside specifically in the location closer to the state court than the federal court, the state court was more conveniently suited.

**BLL: You don't even think about *Colorado River*, unless you have:**
1. Parallel state and federal proceedings going on concurrently;
2. Amongst substantially the same issues; and,
3. Substantially the same parties.

**Otherwise, FORGET ABOUT IT.**

- If you have that, you may consider a stay.

The court would issue a stay so that the federal government can continue with the case, since it has an unflagging obligation to litigate, UNLESS it has those exceptional circumstances.

In this case, no federal constitutional issue was involved, the state law is well-settled in the area of water rights and does not raise any new issues of public policy, and so the final category is not at all applicable.

So, even though the abstention doctrine does not apply, there are very limited instances where wise judicial administration dictates that federal jurisdiction should give way to a concurrent state proceeding in order to conserve judicial resources and have a comprehensive disposition of the litigation.

The SCOTUS decided ultimately that it was wise judicial administration to defer to state jurisdiction, because the McCarran Amendment itself consents to state jurisdiction, the federal suit was in its infancy, and the state court location is a more convenient forum.

<!-- page: 8 -->

Therefore, the district court dismissal was justified, and the court of appeals judgment is reversed.

- The 7th Circuit here seems to have created a new test, which illustrates that it is not a good idea to have concurrent litigation (in state and federal court) progressing at the same time. Therefore, it says that the federal court should "stay" while the state court is progressing with the litigation.
- Courts are divided in this area. In some circuits it is very difficult to get a *Colorado River* stay; others are more liberal.
- SCOTUS still has not provided what is the right answer on this matter.

***Marilyn Clark, on behalf of Sears, v. Alan Lacy*, 376 F.3d 682 (8th Cir. 2004)**

A more recent application of the *Colorado River* rule and factors.
- Is there anything exceptional about this fact pattern?
  - Also, is it a good idea?
  - Seems like the court does not take its own test seriously because it sets out 10 different factors knowing that the average person could probably remember a few at most.
  - There was nothing exceptional about this case.

**Policy question to think about:** In both of these cases, we have everything needed to go forward in federal court, and the federal court still refused to go forward. Is that ever okay?

## Horizontal (Other States) Choice of Law (HCOL)

- This is when more than one state law might apply to a proceeding.
- This has nothing to do with *where* the case can be litigated — instead, it's *what* state law the court will apply.
- Today's question: If the case is filed in state court of SC, will the state court apply SC, NC, or MA law?
- Next week, we will ask when the case can be filed in any particular location (Personal Jurisdiction).

**When one is encountering an HCOL problem, one should say:**

1. Is this issue — where there is law that differs from one state to another, both of which could apply — **procedure or substance**? (**\*Note: if law does not differ, we do not bother to do HCOL analysis.**)

   **a. If procedure is the issue, analysis will be short:**

<!-- page: 9 -->

> **i. Wherever the case is filed, that is the law which will apply to the situation (Law of the Forum)**

**Need to know when something is procedure, and when it is not:**
- Time limits on when a motion is due: **Procedural**
- Whether one has to file on a specific type of paper: **Procedural**
- Whether negligence applies to a specific issue: **Substantive**

The issue is that any delineation of procedure or substantive is going to have some cases that lie right on the line.

- **Statute of Limitations (SOL)** period: Time limits within which a plaintiff has to file a case for specific types of actions.
  - Created by Congress.
  - Can be seen as more procedural because it invokes the manner and means of when to file a case.
  - Some SOL situations greatly affect investment strategies.

**b. If substantive** (defined as something that alters pre-litigation party behavior — not lawyers, but clients), **things get more complicated.**

- Take for example an insurance company:
  1. They take premiums that are paid and invest the money that they receive; they have complicated investment concerns.
  2. Aggressive vs. Conservative investing:
     - Aggressive = more risk, higher return
     - Conservative = less risk, lower return (checking/savings account = not earning much money at all)
  3. When a client's claim falls under the statute of limitations, the insurance will no longer need to reserve money for that.
     - Short SOL = Less money that companies need to reserve in the conservative investments.

<!-- page: 10 -->

  4. For this reason, **Statutes of Limitations (except for when the plaintiff is an insurance company) are viewed as procedural.**

> ***NOTE: If the SOL is the issue, because it's viewed as procedural, we use the law of the forum. (Both Restatements are in agreement.)***

### Restatements

- Restatements are written by several self-appointed, self-proclaimed "smart" people, which includes many lawyers and academics. A group called the **American Law Institute** gets together, reads many cases on some issue, researches a whole lot of law (students sometimes help), and basically RESTATE what the general law is of all the state laws. They sometimes annotate when there is a difference in majority vs. minority view.
- They include 1–2 sentences of actual restatement, and volumes worth of commentary.
- **First Restatement** was written about 1000 years ago, and everyone agreed on basic rules for HCOL.
- Then, in the middle of the 20th century, they decided the 1st Restatement was out of date, and created the **2d Restatement**. Suggested new theories and ideas about how HCOL should be done, but they did not have a lot of case law behind them.
- Under the conflicts of law, the 2d Restatement is what these smart people thought the law should be. About **48 states** ended up adopting the 2d Restatement and followed.
- Let's see whether the 2d Restatement is a substantial improvement over the 1st.

### Important Term Definitions

**Guests Statute:** (*Babcock / Neumeier*) (Substantive Rule): Guest statute that a legislature passes that prevents the passenger of the car (guest of the driver) from suing the driver of the car for simple negligence that results in the guest passenger's injury.
- Available in some jurisdictions, and not others.
- Some jurisdictions only recognize higher levels of disregard (i.e., driver's **gross negligence** rather than the simple negligence standard).

**Fellow Servant Rule:** (*Great Alabama Railroad*) (Substantive Rule): Employee sued the company for the negligence of something that another employee of the company had done.
- Old common law rule (mostly the law 100–200 years ago) that says that employees who are plaintiffs suing **companies** for something that **fellow employees** did, the **company is not liable**. They can still sue fellow employees individually though.
  - The issue is that the individual fellow employees do not have money; the company does, so most people want to sue the company because the institution has more money than the single employee.

**Contributory negligence ("contrib") vs. Comparative fault** (Substantive Rule):
- **Contributory negligence:** Common law doctrine that says that if the plaintiff was in any way — no matter how small — at fault for the negligence, the plaintiff **cannot recover damages**.

<!-- page: 11 -->

- **Comparative fault:** Jury can generally allocate fault by percentage, or some other mechanism, assign liability and award damages based on that assignment. Some fault on part of the plaintiff will **not bar ALL damages**.

The issue now is that the state court will have to choose between applying the law of State A or law of State B.

If we say that when judges are confronted with this issue, they shall pick a rule that does the most justice in the case, is that a good policy?
- It might cause forum shopping.
- Takes power away from state-level legislatures.

**Concentrating in torts law cases for purposes of illustrating HCOL doctrine because the principles are easier to illustrate when it comes to determining horizontal choice of law.**

**First thing to learn about HCOL is that a court may be faced with conflicting law at any moment with any case.**

## First Restatement Approach (Vested Rights Approach)

*(Great Alabama Railroad)*

### *Alabama Great Southern RR v. Carroll*, 11 So. 803 (1892) (Supreme Court of Alabama)

**Facts:**
- W.D. Carroll (plaintiff), an AL citizen, was employed as a brakeman under an Alabama contract with the AL Great Southern RR Co (AL Southern) (defendant).
- While working on a freight train running from AL to MS, Carroll was injured on account of a defective link between two cars.
- The injury occurred in **MS**, but Carroll alleged that its cause happened in **AL** because evidence showed that fellow employees of Carroll **were negligent in failing to check the link or fix it**.
- Carroll sued AL Southern in AL court.
- He alleged that the employment contract rendered Alabama Southern subject to AL law even if Carroll were working outside of the state. (AL recognized the "respondeat superior rule.")
- If MS law were applicable, AL Southern could not be liable because MS subscribed to the common law rule that "a master is not liable for harm to a servant caused by a fellow servant."
- An Alabama employers' liability statute, however, modified the common law in that state so as to make a master liable for injury to a servant.
- A jury decided in favor of Carroll.
- AL Southern appealed.

**Issue:** Which state law (either Alabama or Mississippi) governed in this case?

**Holding:** MS

**Rule:** When a plaintiff's injury occurs in a state in which the law does not allow recovery against the defendant, the plaintiff may not seek recovery under another state's law.

**Reasoning:**

<!-- page: 13 -->

- Because Carroll suffered the injury in **MS**, he may not sue under Alabama law.
- In this case, the common law as enforced in MS does not allow recovery by a servant against the master for negligence by a fellow servant.
- Thus, AL Southern may not be held liable under the laws of the state where Carroll's injury occurred.
- AL's employers' liability act is not relevant because the force of that act does not extend beyond AL.
- Carroll's right to sue arose in **MS, where and when the injury occurred.**
- It is important because the restatement says that the "**last event**" that took place determines what law applies.
- There is no authority to support the position that his right to sue arose in AL even though the negligent conduct may have occurred there.
  - Nor does the parties' employment contract have any bearing on the analysis.
  - The contract simply establishes the master-servant relationship.
  - It does not serve to extend the reach of Alabama law beyond Alabama.
- In accordance with the analysis, the judgment was reversed and the case remanded.

- AL law would allow this plaintiff to recover from the railroad.
- MS law would not allow the plaintiff to get any money.

> ***BLL RULE ("LAST EVENT THEORY"): IN A TORT CASE, THE STATE IN WHICH THE LAST EVENT CAUSING RISE TO THE CLAIM IS WHAT DETERMINES THE LAW THAT WILL APPLY — DAMAGES.***

### First Restatement (Vested Rights) Approach — Summary

"The law of the place where the damages occur governs" ("**Where the blood/harm/injury actually is**")

- What state determines the standard of conduct
- Whether the defense follows under that conduct
- Any affirmative defenses are governed under that state law
- Damages, whether offered, etc., is governed by that state law
- **ALL IN THE SAME PLACE**
- Will not cut the case into small pieces

**Why is this approach called the "vested right" approach?**
- We look to the place where the last event necessary to vest the right to sue occurred, because that would be the place that is the most "vested" in all the issues.

**LECS (law) LOCI (location) DELICTI (harm)**

"The law of the place of the damages governs all the stuff."
"Where the **harm** occurred" (not the negligence; the actual injury or harm!)

**Advantages of this approach:**
- Easy to apply

<!-- page: 14 -->

- Simple rule
- No difference in state of law applied to different pieces of the lawsuit
  - No "**Frankenlaw**" — pieces of law taken together to create something that no one advocated for

**Criticisms:**
- What tells me what the "last event necessary was," in order to know what caused the cause of action to arise?
  - There is no theoretical coherence to the First Restatement because people care about (and will sue over) different things.
  - States may have differing interests in different areas.

Because people are different, and different events could cause legal situations to arise, there began to be some problems with the First Restatement approach:
- Can't figure out what type of law to apply, even just to figure out what the "last event causing rise to an action" is.
- Another problem is that events can happen anywhere, and it is arbitrary where they happen.

### *Babcock* and *Neumeier* — Comparison

The fact pattern in ***Babcock*** and ***Neumeier*** both involve a car accident that occurred in Ontario, but:
- In *Babcock*, all of the passengers are from NY and the car is registered in NY, and they are on a weekend trip leaving from NY.
- In *Neumeier*, everything seems to be happening in Ontario, and the plaintiff is from Ontario.

In both cases, two people get in a car in NY and drive to Ontario, where there is a car accident, and the driver is allegedly negligent. There is a lawsuit against the driver in both fact patterns.

- Under the First Restatement (where the blood is), Ontario law would apply.
  - Passengers get no money because of the guest statute in Ontario that bars recovery.
  - Under NY law, there is no guest statute.

- The *Babcock* case took the **Second Restatement** approach instead and said that NY law would apply.
- It abandons the First Restatement because it says NY will have the stronger interest in the issue presented:
  - Trip was starting from NY
  - All passengers are from NY
  - Car is from NY
  - Everyone is returning to NY
  - Claims Ontario is just a passing point, much like when you travel over a state.

<!-- page: 15 -->

- However, the *Babcock* court says that if the issue is:
  - One of standard of care, and
  - Whether the defendant's conduct was below the standard of care,
    - Then the jurisdiction where the alleged negligent conduct occurred would have preclusive interest in the outcome of the case.

## Second Restatement Approach

### *Babcock v. Jackson*, 191 N.E.2d 279 (N.Y. 1963) (NY Court of Appeals)

**Facts:**
- Fact pattern takes place in NY. All parties are from NY.
- Parties were driving on their way to Ontario, Canada.
- When Jackson was driving the car in Ontario, he lost control and had an accident.
- When they returned to NY, Babcock brought the claim in state court.
- Ontario has a guest statute which protects the driver when he or she negligently harms a passenger in their car.
- Jacksons moved to dismiss because according to them, the harm happened in Ontario and its guest statute would bar recovery for this specific harm.

**Issue:** Shall the law of the "place of the tort" invariably govern the availability of relief for the tort, or shall the applicable choice of law rule also reflect a consideration of other factors which are relevant to the purposes served by the enforcement or denial of the remedy?

**Holding:** No. Additional factors should be considered when applying the choice of law.

**Reasoning:** Court was worried about arbitrariness and being biased by the state where the harm was caused, against guests from other states.

> ***With respect to the Babcock approach, we have a court that says we will no longer go by* lecs loci delicti. *Instead, we will from now on look at what states have an interest in a particular issue, and the one with the most interest in that specific issue will govern it. (Frankenlaw.) That will allow state policies to be more effectuated.***

**What counts as an interest?**

In *Babcock*, it appears that the purpose of the area of law is to prevent insurance fraud.

If instead the interest is something else — such as *Neumeier* said, that the passengers are ungrateful and should not sue those who are trying to be helpful — Ontario might have a larger interest in the case.

**Analysis for Second Restatement — when considering what interests count and how much:**

<!-- page: 16 -->

- One way to find interests is to consider the **purposes of enacting the law**, but:

  - States have an interest to **regulate what happens in their borders**.

## Recap: Horizontal Choice of Law (HCOL) Analysis — Complete Framework

1. **Check to see whether the issue is procedural or substantive.**
   - If issue is **procedural**, you use **forum law** (includes SOL issues).
   - If issue is **substantive**, then you need to find out whether it is a:

     **i. First Restatement State:**
     - *Lecs loci delicti* (where the injury occurred)
     - Use that law for **all** of the issues in that case:
       - Standards of conduct
       - Damages, etc.

     **ii. Second Restatement State:**
     - **Presume** *lecs loci delicti* law **UNLESS** —
     - There are factors in **Section 6 of the Second Restatement** to consider, to see whether there is other state law that should apply to that particular issue (key problem/feature: "**frankenlaw**"):

<!-- page: 17 -->

       - State A Law for what the standard of care is; and
       - State B Law with respect to what a possible defense is.
     - Other factors considered when deciding whether to depart from the *lecs loci delicti* rule:
       - **State interests:**
         - Interests in governing conduct that occurs within one's boundaries
         - Protecting one's own residents:
           - Fair treatment; or
           - Wants their resident to win
         - We look for the **state that has the most significant relationship to the issue**.
         - We look to the **policies that might impute to the substantive area of the law**.
