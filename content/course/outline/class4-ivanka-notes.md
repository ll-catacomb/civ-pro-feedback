## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***United Mine Workers of America v. Gibbs*, 383 U.S. 715 (1966) (SCOTUS) — Supplemental Jurisdiction (since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367)**

  A state claim may proceed in federal court when it accompanies a federal claim that arises from a **common nucleus of operative facts**.

- ***Owen Equipment & Erection Co. v. Kroger*, 437 U.S. 365 (1978) (SCOTUS) — Supplemental Jurisdiction (since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367)**

  In an action in which federal jurisdiction is based on **diversity**, the court may not exercise jurisdiction over the plaintiff's claim against a third-party defendant if there is no independent basis for federal jurisdiction over that claim.

- ***Aldinger v. Howard*, 427 U.S. 1 (1976) (SCOTUS) — Supplemental Jurisdiction (since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367) — *See lecture notes below***

  Federal supplemental jurisdiction does not necessarily extend to claims against parties over whom a federal court would not otherwise have jurisdiction.

- ***Finley v. United States*, 490 U.S. 545 (1989) (SCOTUS) — Supplemental Jurisdiction (since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367) — *See lecture notes below***

  A federal court may not assert supplemental jurisdiction under the anchoring claims with respect to additional parties over whom the court does not have independent jurisdiction.

--------------------------------------- 28 U.S.C. § 1367 is enacted in 1990 ---------------------------------------

- ***Guaranteed Systems, Inc. v. American National Can Company*, 842 F. Supp. 855 (M.D.N.C. 1994)**

  If a federal court's jurisdiction is founded on diversity of citizenship, 28 U.S.C. § 1367 prohibits the court from exercising supplemental jurisdiction over a claim by the plaintiff against a nondiverse third party impleaded under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 14.

- ***Shamrock Oil & Gas Corp v. Sheets*, 313 U.S. 100 (1941)**

  Plaintiff could not remove a state court action into federal court even if defendant interposed a federal counterclaim.

<!-- page: 2 -->

- ***Colorado River Water Conservation District v. United States*, 424 U.S. 800 (1976) (SCOTUS)**

  Federal courts may defer jurisdiction to concurrent state proceedings, even where the doctrine of abstention does not apply, for reasons of wise judicial administration.

### Statutes

#### 28 U.S.C. § 1367 — Supplemental Jurisdiction

**(a)** Except as provided in subsections (b) and (c) or as expressly provided otherwise by Federal statute, in any civil action of which the district courts have original jurisdiction, the district courts shall have supplemental jurisdiction over all other claims that are so related to claims in the action within such original jurisdiction that they form part of the same case or controversy under Article III of the United States Constitution. Such supplemental jurisdiction shall include claims that involve the joinder or intervention of additional parties.

**(b)** In any civil action of which the district courts have original jurisdiction founded solely on section 1332 of this title, the district courts shall not have supplemental jurisdiction under subsection (a) over claims by plaintiffs against persons made parties under Rule 14, 19, 20, or 24 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, or over claims by persons proposed to be joined as plaintiffs under Rule 19 of such rules, or seeking to intervene as plaintiffs under Rule 24 of such rules, when exercising supplemental jurisdiction over such claims would be inconsistent with the jurisdictional requirements of section 1332.

**(c)** The district courts may decline to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over a claim under subsection (a) if—

> (1) the claim raises a novel or complex issue of State law,
>
> (2) the claim substantially predominates over the claim or claims over which the district court has original jurisdiction,
>
> (3) the district court has dismissed all claims over which it has original jurisdiction, or
>
> (4) in exceptional circumstances, there are other compelling reasons for declining jurisdiction.

**(d)** The period of limitations for any claim asserted under subsection (a), and for any other claim in the same action that is voluntarily dismissed at the same time as or after the

<!-- page: 3 -->

dismissal of the claim under subsection (a), shall be tolled while the claim is pending and for a period of 30 days after it is dismissed unless State law provides for a longer tolling period.

**(e)** As used in this section, the term "State" includes the District of Columbia, the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico, and any territory or possession of the United States.

#### 28 U.S.C. § 1441 — Removal of Civil Actions

**(a) Generally.**

Except as otherwise expressly provided by Act of Congress, any civil action brought in a State court of which the district courts of the United States have original jurisdiction, may be removed by the defendant or the defendants, to the district court of the United States for the district and division embracing the place where such action is pending.

**(b) Removal Based on Diversity of Citizenship.**

> (1) In determining whether a civil action is removable on the basis of the jurisdiction under section 1332(a) of this title, the citizenship of defendants sued under fictitious names shall be disregarded.
>
> (2) A civil action otherwise removable solely on the basis of the jurisdiction under section 1332(a) of this title may not be removed if any of the parties in interest properly joined and served as defendants is a citizen of the State in which such action is brought.

**(c) Joinder of Federal Law Claims and State Law Claims.**

> (1) If a civil action includes—
>
> > (A) a claim arising under the Constitution, laws, or treaties of the United States (within the meaning of section 1331 of this title), and
> >
> > (B) a claim not within the original or supplemental jurisdiction of the district court or a claim that has been made nonremovable by statute,
>
> the entire action may be removed if the action would be removable without the inclusion of the claim described in subparagraph (B).
>
> (2) Upon removal of an action described in paragraph (1), the district court shall sever from the action all claims described in paragraph (1)(B) and shall remand the severed claims to the State court from which the action was removed. Only defendants against whom a claim described in paragraph (1)(A) has been asserted are required to join in or consent to the removal under paragraph (1).

We said that Days 1–4 would discuss that even though a case can almost always start in the state courts, when is it also permissible for it to begin in the federal court system. The name of the doctrine we have studied to answer this question is Subject Matter Jurisdiction, specifically Subject Matter Jurisdiction of the federal district courts.

We also said in Days 1–3 that although the name of this doctrine is Subject Matter Jurisdiction, it only partially has to deal with the subject matter that is litigated in the federal courts. Yesterday, Class Day 3, we discussed **Supplemental Jurisdiction (as historically applied, before 28 U.S.C. § 1367 was enacted)**: The idea is that there are multiple theories of relief (claims, or causes of action) that a plaintiff wants to assert against a defendant in a single lawsuit in federal court. There are certain theories of relief that have an independent basis of subject matter jurisdiction (whether through arising under (**28 U.S.C. § 1331**), or through diversity jurisdiction (**28 U.S.C. § 1332**)). However, there are also other theories of relief, or claims, that do not fall within either of those two independent avenues to obtaining federal subject matter jurisdiction. The plaintiffs nevertheless may tack these claims, which do not on their own have an independent basis of subject matter jurisdiction (referred to as the "**would-be supplemental claim(s)**"), on to the lawsuit that has the claim with an independent basis of federal subject matter jurisdiction (referred to as the "**anchoring claim(s)**"). We asked ourselves under what circumstances it would be permissible for a federal court to exercise SMJ over these claims (*illustrated in* ***Gibbs, Kroger, Aldinger, Finley***).

We discussed ***Gibbs*** *(See Class Day 3 Notes for analysis) — (Had "arising under" anchoring claim)*:

<!-- page: 6 -->

- ***Gibbs*** had multiple claims, but all claims (multiple claims) involved the same plaintiff (single plaintiff) and same defendant (single defendant). One of the claims had arising under jurisdiction, so it was the anchoring claim.
- **Rule:** A state claim may proceed in federal court when it accompanies a federal claim that arises from a **common nucleus of operative facts**.
- Focused on determining whether the federal court had "**arising under**" federal subject matter jurisdiction over the "**cause of action**."
- SCOTUS said: Despite the lack of statutory authorization, the district court can exercise jurisdiction of the state law claim because it had also filed a federal "arising under" anchoring claim.
- If the court dismisses all the federal claims (**anchoring claim(s)**), it also loses jurisdiction over the state claims. A lawsuit based on "arising under" jurisdiction that is left with only state claims may not proceed in federal court. (This rule changes later in 28 U.S.C. § 1367(c), when Congress allows for exercise of discretion.)

## Lecture for Day 4

We have federal district courts to litigate substantial issues of federal law. However, case law suggests that the emphasis is more on the cause of action, and only under certain circumstances.
- We try to limit the jurisdiction of federal courts in order to preserve federalism.

Congress however has enacted laws for federal courts to be created, and laws for federal courts to adjudicate.

If Congress grants federal courts federal subject matter jurisdiction, it must want them to exercise that power. The theory is that they have an "**unflagging**" obligation to exercise that right. However, federal courts continue to not exercise jurisdiction under certain circumstances:
- Probating wills
- Issuing divorce decrees

The federal courts claim they lack the expertise.

### *Colorado River* Abstention Doctrine

***Colorado River Water Conservation District v. United States*, 424 U.S. 800 (1976) (SCOTUS) — (know 3 bases of abstention)**

Federal courts may defer jurisdiction to concurrent state proceedings, even where the doctrine of abstention does not apply, for reasons of wise judicial administration.

A district court may only abstain from exercising jurisdiction in **exceptional circumstances**, which have been confined to **three general categories**:

1. Cases involving a federal constitutional issue that may be mooted or otherwise resolved by a state court decision under applicable state law;

<!-- page: 8 -->

2. Questions of state law involving important public policy concerns that transcend the results of that particular case; or

3. Where federal jurisdiction was invoked to restrain state criminal, nuisance or tax collection proceedings.
