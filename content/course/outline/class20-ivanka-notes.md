## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases (Common Law)

- ***United States v. Hayward-Robinson*, 430 F.2d 1077 (2d Cir. 1970)**

  For a counterclaim to be compulsory it must have a **logical relationship with the original claim**.

- ***Smuck v. Hobson*, 408 F.2d 175 (DC Cir. 1969)**

  Intervention is appropriate when the moving party has an **interest in the outcome of the case**, that outcome may **impair the party's ability to protect its interests if it does not intervene**, and the **party is not otherwise adequately represented** in the case.

- ***Gonzalez v. Banco Central Corp.*, 27 F.3d 751 (1st Cir. 1994)**

  Claim preclusion bars a nonparty from suing the same defendant for similar claims where final judgment was entered in the earlier case, but only if the nonparty had **substantial control over** or was **virtually represented** in the earlier case.

- ***Jones v. Morris Plan Bank of Portsmouth*, 191 S.E. 608 (Va. 1937)**

  If the same evidence will support multiple actions arising from the same contract, there can only be one cause of action.

- ***Cromwell v. County of Sac*, 94 U.S. (4 Otto) 351 (SCOTUS)(1876)**

  Where a second action is between the same parties and involves the same transaction or occurrence as an initial judgment, that judgment does not bar the second action if the second action is upon an issue that was not adjudicated in the initial action.

### Federal Rules of Civil Procedure

- **Fed. R. Civ. P. 13 — Counterclaim and Crossclaim**

  **(a) Compulsory Counterclaim.**

  > (1) **In General.** A pleading must state as a counterclaim any claim that—at the time of its service—the pleader has against an opposing party if the claim:
  >
  > > (A) arises out of the transaction or occurrence that is the subject matter of the opposing party's claim; and
  > >
  > > (B) does not require adding another party over whom the court cannot acquire jurisdiction.
  >
  > (2) **Exceptions.** The pleader need not state the claim if:
  >
  > > (A) when the action was commenced, the claim was the subject of another pending action; or
  > >
  > > (B) the opposing party sued on its claim by attachment or other process that did not establish personal jurisdiction over the pleader on that claim, and the pleader does not assert any counterclaim under this rule.

  **(b) Permissive Counterclaim.** A pleading may state as a counterclaim against an opposing party any claim that is not compulsory.

  **(c) Relief Sought in a Counterclaim.** A counterclaim need not diminish or defeat the recovery sought by the opposing party. It may request relief that exceeds in amount or differs in kind from the relief sought by the opposing party.

  **(g) Crossclaim Against a Coparty.** A pleading may state as a crossclaim any claim by one party against a coparty if the claim arises out of the transaction or occurrence that is the subject matter of the original action or of a counterclaim, or if the claim relates to any property that is the subject matter of the original action. The crossclaim may include a claim that the coparty is or may be liable to the crossclaimant for all or part of a claim asserted in the action against the crossclaimant.

  **(h) Joining Additional Parties.** Rules 19 and 20 govern the addition of a person as a party to a counterclaim or crossclaim.

<!-- page: 3 -->

  **(i) Separate Trials; Separate Judgments.** If the court orders separate trials under Rule 42(b), it may enter judgment on a counterclaim or crossclaim under Rule 54(b) when it has jurisdiction to do so, even if the opposing party's claims have been dismissed or otherwise resolved.

- **Fed. R. Civ. P. 14 — Third-Party Practice**

  **(a) When a Defending Party May Bring in a Third Party.**

  > (1) **Timing of the Summons and Complaint.** A defending party may, as third-party plaintiff, serve a summons and complaint on a nonparty who is or may be liable to it for all or part of the claim against it. But the third-party plaintiff must, by motion, obtain the court's leave if it files the third-party complaint more than 14 days after serving its original answer.
  >
  > (2) **Third-Party Defendant's Claims and Defenses.** The person served with the summons and third-party complaint — the "third-party defendant":
  >
  > > (A) must assert any defense against the third-party plaintiff's claim under Rule 12;
  > >
  > > (B) must assert any counterclaim against the third-party plaintiff under Rule 13(a), and may assert any counterclaim against the third-party plaintiff under Rule 13(b) or any crossclaim against another third-party defendant under Rule 13(g);
  > >
  > > (C) may assert against the plaintiff any defense that the third-party plaintiff has to the plaintiff's claim; and
  > >
  > > (D) may also assert against the plaintiff any claim arising out of the transaction or occurrence that is the subject matter of the plaintiff's claim against the third-party plaintiff.
  >
  > (3) **Plaintiff's Claims Against a Third-Party Defendant.** The plaintiff may assert against the third-party defendant any claim arising out of the transaction or occurrence that is the subject matter of the plaintiff's claim against the third-party plaintiff. The third-party defendant must then assert any defense under Rule 12 and any counterclaim under Rule 13(a), and may assert any counterclaim under Rule 13(b) or any crossclaim under Rule 13(g).
  >
  > (4) **Motion to Strike, Sever, or Try Separately.** Any party may move to strike the third-party claim, to sever it, or to try it separately.
  >
  > (5) **Third-Party Defendant's Claim Against a Nonparty.** A third-party defendant may proceed under this rule against a nonparty who is or may be liable to the third-party defendant for all or part of any claim against it.

<!-- page: 4 -->

  > (6) **Third-Party Complaint In Rem.** If it is within the admiralty or maritime jurisdiction, a third-party complaint may be in rem. In that event, a reference in this rule to the "summons" includes the warrant of arrest, and a reference to the defendant or third-party plaintiff includes, when appropriate, a person who asserts a right under Supplemental Rule C(6)(a)(i) in the property arrested.

  **(b) When a Plaintiff May Bring in a Third Party.** When a claim is asserted against a plaintiff, the plaintiff may bring in a third party if this rule would allow a defendant to do so.

- **Fed. R. Civ. P. 18 — Joinder of Claims**

  **(a) In General.** A party asserting a claim, counterclaim, crossclaim, or third-party claim may join, as independent or alternative claims, as many claims as it has against an opposing party.

  **(b) Joinder of Contingent Claims.** A party may join two claims even though one of them is contingent on the disposition of the other; but the court may grant relief only in accordance with the parties' relative substantive rights. In particular, a plaintiff may state a claim for money and a claim to set aside a conveyance that is fraudulent as to that plaintiff, without first obtaining a judgment for the money.

- **Fed. R. Civ. P. 20(a) — Permissive Joinder of Parties**

  **(a) Persons Who May Join or Be Joined.**

  > (1) **Plaintiffs.** Persons may join in one action as plaintiffs if:
  >
  > > (A) they assert any right to relief jointly, severally, or in the alternative with respect to or arising out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences; and
  > >
  > > (B) any question of law or fact common to all plaintiffs will arise in the action.
  >
  > (2) **Defendants.** Persons — as well as a vessel, cargo, or other property subject to admiralty process in rem — may be joined in one action as defendants if:
  >
  > > (A) any right to relief is asserted against them jointly, severally, or in the alternative with respect to or arising out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences; and
  > >
  > > (B) any question of law or fact common to all defendants will arise in the action.

<!-- page: 5 -->

  > (3) **Extent of Relief.** Neither a plaintiff nor a defendant need be interested in obtaining or defending against all the relief demanded. The court may grant judgment to one or more plaintiffs according to their rights, and against one or more defendants according to their liabilities.

- **Fed. R. Civ. P. 21 — Misjoinder and Nonjoinder of Parties**

  Misjoinder of parties is not a ground for dismissing an action. On motion or on its own, the court may at any time, on just terms, add or drop a party. The court may also sever any claim against a party.

- **Fed. R. Civ. P. 24 — Intervention**

  **(a) Intervention of Right.** On timely motion, the court must permit anyone to intervene who:

  > (1) is given an unconditional right to intervene by a federal statute; or
  >
  > (2) claims an interest relating to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action, and is so situated that disposing of the action may as a practical matter impair or impede the movant's ability to protect its interest, unless existing parties adequately represent that interest.

  **(b) Permissive Intervention.**

  > (1) **In General.** On timely motion, the court may permit anyone to intervene who:
  >
  > > (A) is given a conditional right to intervene by a federal statute; or
  > >
  > > (B) has a claim or defense that shares with the main action a common question of law or fact.
  >
  > (2) **By a Government Officer or Agency.** On timely motion, the court may permit a federal or state governmental officer or agency to intervene if a party's claim or defense is based on:
  >
  > > (A) a statute or executive order administered by the officer or agency; or
  > >
  > > (B) any regulation, order, requirement, or agreement issued or made under the statute or executive order.
  >
  > (3) **Delay or Prejudice.** In exercising its discretion, the court must consider whether the intervention will unduly delay or prejudice the adjudication of the original parties' rights.

<!-- page: 6 -->

  **(c) Notice and Pleading Required.** A motion to intervene must be served on the parties as provided in Rule 5. The motion must state the grounds for intervention and be accompanied by a pleading that sets out the claim or defense for which intervention is sought.

- **Fed. R. Civ. P. 42 — Consolidation; Separate Trials**

  **(a) Consolidation.** If actions before the court involve a common question of law or fact, the court may:

  > (1) join for hearing or trial any or all matters at issue in the actions;
  >
  > (2) consolidate the actions; or
  >
  > (3) issue any other orders to avoid unnecessary cost or delay.

  **(b) Separate Trials.** For convenience, to avoid prejudice, or to expedite and economize, the court may order a separate trial of one or more separate issues, claims, crossclaims, counterclaims, or third-party claims. When ordering a separate trial, the court must preserve any federal right to a jury trial.

We concluded our consideration of appellate jurisdiction, having discussed the final order doctrine (28 U.S.C. § 1291) and one of its exceptions (FRCP 54(b)) in a previous class.

<!-- page: 7 -->

### Final Decisions

- **Final decisions doctrine** — The rule in the federal system is to only allow appeals from final decisions.
  - **28 U.S.C. § 1291**
    - Typically this means the actual "entry" of judgment by a judge.
    - This is the overwhelming preference of the federal system.
    - Examples of what are and are not final decisions:
      - Grants of motions for new trial = **NOT** final decisions
      - Denial of a motion for a new trial = **FINAL** decision
      - Grant or denial of a Rule 50 JMOL = **FINAL** decision
      - Judge enters final judgment without any Rule 50 or Rule 59 motions = **FINAL** decision

- **Exceptions to final order doctrine (28 U.S.C. § 1291)**
  - **FRCP Rule 54(b) — Judgment on Multiple Claims or Involving Multiple Parties**
    - When an action presents more than one claim of relief, the court may direct entry of a final judgment as to one or more, but fewer than all, claims **ONLY IF:**
      1. It **expressly finds or determines** … (orally and in writing, but at least orally stated by the judge)
      2. That there is **no just reason for delay** — claims as to which the court is to enter final judgment are unrelated to the claims the court will continue to litigate in the case.
    - If court finds both requirements are present, it can enter final judgment as to some but not all of the claims.
    - **Greiner definition of "claim" for partial judgment purposes:**
      - A set of facts; **and**
      - A legal theory; **and**
      - A party.
      - If any of these three are different in a case with "multiple" issues, then you have more than one "claim."

### Additional Exceptions to the Final Order Doctrine (28 U.S.C. § 1291)

- **28 U.S.C. § 1292(a)(1)** — Appeals from injunctions; or
- **28 U.S.C. § 1292(b)** — If not an injunction, a controlling question of law
- ***Cohen*, or Collateral Order Doctrine**

**28 U.S.C. § 1292(a)(1) — Preliminary Injunction** (exception to the final order doctrine): Tells us an appeal is appropriate when there is an "interlocutory order granting, continuing, modifying, refusing or dissolving injunctions, or refusing to dissolve or modify injunctions…"

- Examples of why immediate appeals are allowed when there is a grant (or refusal to grant) of an injunction:
  - Immediate consequences for the parties
  - Avoiding irreparable harm

<!-- page: 8 -->

- Failure to rule on an injunction is **not** listed in 28 U.S.C. § 1292(a)(1) — failure to rule (even though it effectively has the same effect as a refusal to grant) on an injunction does not provide a basis for an appeal.

**28 U.S.C. § 1292(b) "Double discretion doctrine"** (exception to the final order doctrine): An interlocutory appeal is also appropriate when the district judge is of the opinion that an order not otherwise appealable involves a:
1. **Controlling question of law** as to which there is
2. **Substantial ground for difference of opinion**, and
3. That an **immediate appeal from the order may materially advance the ultimate termination of the litigation.**

The appealing party has only **10 days** to appeal, and the appellate court has discretion on whether to hear the appeal or not.

**"Cohen" or Collateral Order Doctrine**

In addition to FRCP 54(b), 28 U.S.C. § 1292(a)(1) and 28 U.S.C. § 1292(b), another exception to the final order doctrine is the *Cohen* or Collateral Order Doctrine, allowing appellate review of district court rulings that are:
- Separable from the merits of the case,
- Final,
- Urgent, and
- Involve an important, unsettled question of law.

Appellate court is very reluctant to say that a case would be effectively unreviewable later, so granting an appeal under this exception is difficult.

- **Not allowed:** Denial of motions of transfer under *forum non conveniens* doctrines
- **Not allowed:** Denials of motions to suppress information or motions asserted on the grounds of privilege
- **Allowed:** *Erie* "VCOL" issues
- **Allowed:** Abstention Doctrine stay

### Writs of Mandamus

The last grounds for an immediate appeal that appellate courts would seriously consider is a **Writ of Mandamus** under **28 U.S.C. § 1651** (extremely rare).

A petition for a writ is a last resort to get something in front of an appellate court when none of the other exceptions work. It is a new lawsuit in the court of appeals, as a new matter, but acts like an appeal, asking the appellate court to order a district judge (respondent) to do or not do something (reconsidering a decision that was made).

Appellate courts rarely grant petitions for writs of mandamus. When they do, circumstances are one of two situations:

1. **Regular writ** — When the petitioner is able to convince the court that:
   1. There is a **really big consequence** that will occur if the petition is not granted,
   2. That they have a **clear and indisputable** right to this relief, and
   3. That generally it would be a **good idea** for the appellate court to grant this petition.

2. **Supervisory Writ** — When the appellate court sees the district courts doing something that they do not like and therefore issues a writ for all of them to stop doing whatever that is (not just to the one respondent, but as a general writ to all lower district courts). (*La Buy*)

## Expanding the Lawsuit (Recap from Day 19)

We discussed expanding the lawsuit beyond a single plaintiff and a single defendant. We presume that if we have a lawsuit involving only a single plaintiff and a single defendant, we do not need special permission under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. We do still need Subject Matter Jurisdiction, Personal Jurisdiction, and Venue — **analyzed claim by claim.**

However, to expand the lawsuit beyond the single plaintiff and defendant, we need to ask two questions:

1. Do we have **special permission** under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure; and if so,
2. Is there **SMJ, PJ, and Venue** as to each of these claims.

Key points from that discussion:

- The lawsuit can be expanded by adding more than one party to either side (multiple plaintiffs can join against multiple defendants, etc.); anytime there is an arrow pointed one way, we would also analyze the compulsory counterclaim rule.
- Crossclaims are **always permissive**, as is party joinder itself.
- When claims are asserted by plaintiffs, even when permissible under the FRCP, they may cause problems when the independent basis of SMJ is diversity, and also when 28 U.S.C. § 1367(b) prevents supplemental jurisdiction over certain claims by plaintiffs.
- Bringing in third-party complaints does **not** invoke the T/O test; instead it involves the **shifting of liability** (most commonly to an insurance company).
- Under FRCP 18, once any arrow points from one party to another, the party who is suing may bring up **any claim whatsoever** (more efficient; parties can bargain and take care of everything at once).
- **FRCP 42** allows the district court to break up the case and take care of all of these in smaller pieces of litigation as it deems necessary.

## Summary Video: *United States v. Hayward-Robinson*

### *United States v. Hayward-Robinson*, 430 F.2d 1077 (2d Cir. 1970)

For a counterclaim to be compulsory it must have a logical relationship with the original claim.

<!-- page: 10 -->

**Facts**

- The Heyward-Robinson Company (Heyward) (defendant) hired D'Agostino Excavators, Inc. (D'Agostino) (plaintiff) as a subcontractor for two construction jobs.
- One job was the construction of Navy barracks for the federal government (Navy job); the other was a non-federal job for construction of a plant for Stelma, Inc. (Stelma job).
- The subcontracts were separate, but Heyward had the right to terminate both of them if D'Agostino breached either one.
- Progress payments made by Heyward were not allocated to one specific job, but were made as a lump sum for both jobs.
- D'Agostino brought a federal suit against Heyward, seeking to recover payments due on the Navy job.
- Heyward counterclaimed for alleged overpayments on both the Navy job and the Stelma job.
- D'Agostino then filed a reply counterclaim to recover payments allegedly due on the Stelma job.
- The jury found in favor of D'Agostino on both jobs.
- Heyward appealed on the grounds that the court had no jurisdiction over the counterclaims on the Stelma job because it was not a federal contract and there was no independent basis of federal jurisdiction.

**Holding**

- **A counterclaim is compulsory if it arises out of the transaction or occurrence that is the subject matter of the original claim.**
- **The counterclaim does not have to have the exact same factual background as the original claim to be compulsory; it only must have a logical relationship with the original claim.**
- The phrase "transaction or occurrence" is to be **interpreted broadly** in the name of judicial efficiency.
- The Stelma job counterclaims do have a sufficient logical relationship to the original Navy job claim to be considered compulsory counterclaims:
  - The subcontracts were for the same type of work at the same time.
  - Heyward's termination power for both contracts was linked.
  - Payments were made in a lump sum for both subcontracts and not allocated to one specific job.
  - The claims "were so interwoven at trial that they are now absolutely incapable of separation."
- Accordingly, the Stelma job counterclaims arose out of the same transaction and occurrence, and thus are compulsory. Judgment affirmed.

<!-- page: 11 -->

### The "Transaction or Occurrence" Test

In all of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (Rules 13, 14, 18, 20, 21, etc.), the **"transaction or occurrence" test** has a great deal of importance. Federal courts interpret this phrase quite broadly (not unlimited) to mean, generally:

- Any sort of logical relationship;
- Relationship in time and space;
- Similarity of the evidence; etc.

*Hayward-Robinson* illustrates this. The court considered two construction jobs at different physical sites to be from the "same transaction or occurrence" because:

- They were contracted for at the same time;
- They had cross-default and cross-termination clauses;
- Payments were not differentiated in the contracts;
- The parties did not see them as separate; and
- They had a single insurance policy covering both contracts.

When we begin discussing **claim preclusion**, this case will come back, because this is what "same claim" will essentially mean.

## Lecture Day 20

### Intervention

The last topic in terms of expanding the lawsuit is **Intervention**.

The other mechanisms for expanding the lawsuit (joinder, crossclaims, etc.) are often not temporal — parties come together and initiate a lawsuit at the same time. **Intervention**, however, typically does happen *after* a lawsuit has begun — when a person realizes a lawsuit has begun and wants to "intervene" as an additional plaintiff, or intervene as a defendant. The rule allows a stranger to interject him or herself into the lawsuit.

People do this because they might have some interest to protect (sometimes public interest organizations do this). This doctrine is governed by **FRCP 24**.

<!-- page: 12 -->

*(Full text of FRCP 24 as reproduced above.)*

FRCP 24 is not a rule that needs a lot of time parsing its phrases to extract the actual rule (unlike FRCP 50, for example).

### *Smuck v. Hobson*, 408 F.2d 175 (DC Cir. 1969)

Intervention is appropriate when:

1. The moving party has an **interest in the outcome of the case**,
2. That outcome may **impair the party's ability to protect its interests** if it does not intervene, and
3. The party is **not otherwise adequately represented** in the case.

<!-- page: 13 -->

**Facts**

- Parents (plaintiffs) of Black and poor students brought a class action discrimination suit against the District of Columbia School District Board of Education (Board) (defendant).
- The plaintiffs won in the district court.
- The Board voted not to appeal the judgment and ordered Dr. Carl Hansen, the superintendent, not to appeal.
- However, Hansen and Carl Smuck, a dissenting member of the Board, filed notices of appeal and motions to intervene.
- In addition, twenty parents who disagreed with the district court's decision also filed motions to intervene.
- Subsequently, Hansen left his position as superintendent.
- This court held the appeals in abeyance and remanded the motions to intervene to the district court. The district court granted the motions to intervene.

**Issue**

- Does the party who would like to intervene have a legally cognizable interest in the outcome of this case? And, to what extent is that legally cognizable interest already protected, or represented, by the party already in the lawsuit? To what extent are the parties not officially part of this litigation bound by its result?

**Holding**

- Under Rule 24, intervention is appropriate when **(1)** the moving party has an interest in the outcome of the case, **(2)** that outcome may impair the party's ability to protect its interests if it does not intervene, and **(3)** the party is not otherwise adequately represented in the case.
- The first prong should be read as a prerequisite to the other two prongs, not an otherwise determining factor.
- The court determines that Hansen's and Smuck's motions to intervene are **denied**, but the parents' motion is **granted**.
- Because Hansen left his position as superintendent, he no longer has an interest sufficient to warrant intervention.
- Similarly, Smuck, while still a member of the Board, has no interest either. The party to the case with the opportunity to appeal was the Board as a collective unit, and the Board voted not to appeal. Smuck may not intervene as an individual because he has no interest outside of his position on the Board.

<!-- page: 14 -->

- The parents, on the other hand, **(1)** do have a sufficient interest — seeking to protect their children's welfare — to reach the other two prongs of the intervention test.
- **(2)** Further, if not allowed to intervene, the parents' ability to protect their children's welfare in this matter would obviously be harmed.
- **(3)** Finally, the parents were not adequately represented by the Board's decision not to appeal. The Board represents all parents within the school district; these particular parents represent the specific interests of their children, which do not always — and certainly in this case do not — coincide with the interests of all of the other students in the district.
- Accordingly, the parents' motions to intervene are granted. The district court is reversed in part and affirmed in part.

**Discussion**

The basic dispute in *Smuck* involved a class action lawsuit where parents and students sued the Board of Education for being denied constitutional rights for equal educational opportunities, alleging the school district was operating on a racially and economically discriminatory basis (basically a school desegregation case).

Initially, the school board defended vigorously. Up until the district court judgment, no one tried to intervene — and normally, intervening after judgment is considered pretty late.

The would-be interveners were Hansen, Smuck, and the parents of white school children who liked the school system segregated as it was. They wanted to intervene in order to appeal the district court judgment.

The appeals court says that the former superintendent and the board member are unable to intervene, and since they cannot become part of the lawsuit, they could not appeal the judgment.

The court sets out to determine whether these would-be interveners have **standing (interests)** to appeal:

1. (a) The superintendent (Hansen), appealing in his former capacity, has no standing because he no longer works for the Board of Education and gave no indication that a reversal would result in him returning to his former job. He does **not** have an "interest in the property or transaction" sufficient for FRCP 24.

   (b) Mr. Smuck also has no appealable interest because he no longer works for the Board of Education. When he worked for the Board, the collective Board was the principal figure and Smuck could have worked with the Board in its decision not to appeal. Now as an individual, he has no separate interest in the litigation.

   (c) The appeal analysis by the parents is more complicated. The court said that these parents did have an interest in the lawsuit. However, in the context of intervention, **the question is not about whether a lawsuit should be begun, but whether already initiated litigation should be extended to include additional parties.** The appellate court exercised its discretion and let them become part of the case, but limited the types of arguments they could make — mostly because the court determines that their interests are more specific to their own children, and does not trust them to make arguments "for the interests of everyone." They could only assert arguments that were already made, and basically only defend the school board's decision-making power.

<!-- page: 15 -->

2. The court discusses where the interests of the Board and the interests of the parents may diverge. The interests of the parents are more focused on their own children, whereas the interests of the Board are supposed to be for the children in the district as a whole. Also, the school board in this case is likely welcoming direction from the appellate court so that it could be told what to do rather than use its own discretion, because it is likely being pulled in different directions by different parents.

3. Regarding whether the parents will unnecessarily complicate the lawsuit, the court decides that since intervention was late, and it did not want to revisit earlier arguments, it decided to limit its review only to the pieces that limit the discretion of the old or the new board (because the parents of the white children, according to the court, have an interest in **maximizing** the school board's discretion even if the school board does not want it).

**Key Takeaway for Class**

We are more interested in these three factors than in parsing each word of FRCP 24. Note that we did **not** analyze whether this is an intervention "as of right" under FRCP 24(a) or permissive intervention under FRCP 24(b). Some courts do emphasize that distinction, but in this class we will only focus on the **three factors** when considering an intervention motion.

## Claim Preclusion

We will cover the following topics in order:

1. **Claim preclusion** (beginning today)
2. **Issue preclusion**
3. **Preclusion among different types of adjudicatory systems**

### Introduction to Claim Preclusion

What happens after a court (or adjudicatory agency) issues a judgment — what is the **effect** of that judgment?

- One effect: someone might have to do something (or stop doing something), e.g., pay damages. (We discussed Lawsuit 1 and Lawsuit 2, where Lawsuit 2 was an effort to collect on money Lawsuit 1 granted.)
- Another potential effect: **repose** — preventing things that have been litigated in a lawsuit already from being litigated again in a subsequent lawsuit.

In claim preclusion or issue preclusion, it helps to draw pictures and firmly identify **Lawsuit #1** and **Lawsuit #2** — because it is in Lawsuit #2 that preclusion will kick in (we don't preclude people from filing the first lawsuit).

> ***For now, assume that both Lawsuit #1 and Lawsuit #2 are part of the same system (e.g., both in the federal district court, or both in state court even if two different states).***

The **policy** behind preclusion is basically an efficiency argument. We structure the preclusion system to induce parties to bring lawsuits when the court system feels they should bring them. Because the result involves extinguishing some rights, we want to keep an eye on the balance between efficiency considerations and justice considerations.

**Important:** The most common preclusion mistake on an exam is mixing and matching the elements/requirements of *claim preclusion* with those of *issue preclusion*. Exercise maximum discipline in going line by line of each element when trying to discern which doctrine fits. Also: **for claim preclusion, we do not care who wins.**

<!-- page: 17 -->

| Claim Preclusion | Issue Preclusion |
|---|---|
| 1) same claim | 1) same issue |
| 2) same party, **and** | 2) actually litigated, **and** |
| 3) a judgment that is both (a) final, **and** (b) "on the merits" | 3) necessary to decision |
| | 4) ??? mutuality ??? |
| | *plus other stuff: judgment of sufficient quality/finality; sufficient opportunity, incentive to litigate* |

If a party is not in, or fully represented in Lawsuit #1, then the court cannot preclude it in Lawsuit #2.

- We want everyone to be given **notice and the opportunity to be heard** (procedural due process).

### *Gonzalez v. Banco Central Corp.*, 27 F.3d 751 (1st Cir. 1994)

Claim preclusion bars a nonparty from suing the same defendant for similar claims where final judgment was entered in the earlier case, but only if the nonparty had **substantial control over** the earlier case.

**Three Elements of Claim Preclusion:**

1. Same claim
2. Same party, **and**
3. A judgment that is both
   - (a) Final, **and**
   - (b) On the merits

**Facts**

- Real estate developers sold lots of what turned out to be swampland to around 3,000 purchasers.
- A group of disgruntled purchasers (the Rodriguez plaintiffs) sued the sellers, banks, and others involved in the transactions for alleged violations of the Interstate Land Sales Full Disclosure Act (ILSFDA) and several other federal statutes.
- The Rodriguez plaintiffs attempted to form a class action but the court did not allow it.

<!-- page: 18 -->

- Gonzalez (plaintiff) represents a new group of plaintiffs (the Gonzalez plaintiffs) who sued the **same defendants, had the same lawyers, and alleged very similar claims** about five years after the Rodriguez plaintiffs filed their complaint.
- The **Rodriguez plaintiffs previously lost their suit at trial.**
- The court **dismissed the Gonzalez suit on the grounds that it was barred by claim preclusion under a theory of privity.**
- The Gonzalez plaintiffs appealed.
- Not only had the district court denied class certification for the Gonzalez plaintiffs (which would have brought them into the Rodriguez litigation), it also denied their motion to intervene. Both times the Gonzalez plaintiffs tried to enter the lawsuit, the district court denied their entry — which, had they been allowed to enter, would have bound them to the result.

**Holding and Reasoning (Selya, J.)**

- Claim preclusion mandates that once a final judgment has been made on the merits, all other claims by the parties based on the same cause of action are barred.
- **Claim preclusion bars a later suit when there is (1) a final judgment on the merits in the earlier suit, (2) sufficient subject matter identity between the earlier and later suits, and (3) sufficient identity between the parties in the earlier and later suits.**
- Neither party contests the first element (final judgment reached in the Rodriguez case).
- The second element is met because the claims in both suits pass the transactional test — both allege claims deriving from a single set of underlying facts (the sale of undevelopable lots by the same defendants).
- The third element requires more analysis.
- The Court holds that **claim preclusion can bar an action brought by one who was not a party to the earlier action if privity exists.**
- There is **privity if the nonparty either had substantial control** — meaning the ability to make decisions or exert power in the earlier case. The burden of proving substantial control is on the person who seeks to show privity.
- **No facts show that the Gonzalez plaintiffs controlled or had the ability to control any aspects of the Rodriguez litigation.**
- **Privity also exists if there was virtual representation.**
- **Virtual representation exists if (1) the nonparty has interests identical to parties in the earlier case, (2) the nonparty also had actual or constructive notice of the earlier case, and (3) the balance of equities favors barring the nonparty's later claim.**

<!-- page: 19 -->

- Although the Gonzalez plaintiffs have an identity of interests with the Rodriguez plaintiffs, that alone is not sufficient to say the Gonzalez plaintiffs were virtually represented.
- The equities weigh strongly against finding virtual representation because:
  - **There is no proof that the Gonzalez plaintiffs had notice of the earlier suit**;
  - There is no legal or special relationship between the groups that would suggest accountability;
  - The Gonzalez plaintiffs did not consent to be bound by the earlier judgment; and
  - The Gonzalez plaintiffs attempted to join the earlier suit, so there are no signs of bad faith or manipulation.
- **Since the Gonzalez plaintiffs were not parties in the earlier Rodriguez suit and were not in privity with parties in the earlier suit, claim preclusion does not bar their suit.** The district court's order dismissing the suit is reversed and the case is remanded.

**Discussion**

The Gonzalez court says that claim preclusion will not apply because the Gonzalez party and Rodriguez party were not the same party nor in privity.

Three elements required for claim preclusion to apply:

1. **Same party (or in privity)**
2. **Same claim**
3. **A judgment that is both**
   - a. **Final; AND**
   - b. **On the merits**

**Analyzing Element 1 — Same Party (or in Privity):**

None of the members of the Gonzalez party were named plaintiffs in the Rodriguez party. When analyzing the parties, start out very formalistic — if there is a board and a corporation, know that they are separate entities, etc.

Here, the parties in the Rodriguez litigation were formally different from the parties in the Gonzalez litigation. However, many factors did link them together:

- They had the same attorney;
- They shared discovery materials and other information gathered in the previous Rodriguez lawsuit;
- Their attorneys created a collective fund where all could contribute money to their joint legal endeavor;
- Some legal theories (fraud) involved in the two cases were similar.

The court nonetheless sees these two as different parties:

- The Gonzalez plaintiffs were not previously allowed to join the class action lawsuit;
- The Gonzalez plaintiffs did not get prior notice nor an opportunity to be heard on the matter;
- The court says there was no special relationship between the parties — merely two similarly situated parties sharing a lawyer. Basically they have **no privity**.

<!-- page: 20 -->

The court says the only times that they would allow formally nonparty litigants to be bound by claim preclusion is when they are in **privity** with someone who was formally in the litigation.

- **Privity** means more than just having the same general interests. It means the **same, exact interests** — for example, same ownership of the exact piece of land; or
- That the party being precluded had **substantial control over** the prior lawsuit.

Since we do not have these elements in the case at hand, the Gonzalez plaintiffs did not have privity over the prior Rodriguez lawsuit.

### Hypos and Examples: Two Types of Privity for Claim Preclusion

#### Two Types of Privity

| Substantial Control | Successor in Interest |
|---|---|
| Sole owner of a "close" corporation who assumes control of litigation against the firm | Credit card company sells debt to Debt Buyer 1 → Debt Buyer 2 → Debt Buyer 3; each successive holder is a successor in interest |

#### Example 1: Substantial Control

Privity here means that the party in Lawsuit 2 had substantial control — basically near complete control — of Lawsuit 1.

- Suppose that Party A creates a corporation and John Doe is the sole shareholder. There are some other people on the board, but John Doe is the sole shareholder.
- As the sole shareholder, John Doe makes all decisions on behalf of the corporation.
- John Doe, as the sole shareholder, decides that the corporation will enter into a contract to purchase paper.
- The other party breaches the contract with the corporation.

<!-- page: 21 -->

- John Doe, as the sole shareholder, decides to sue the opposing party for **breach of contract** on behalf of the corporation. Sole shareholder, in the capacity of CEO, hires a lawyer and sues on behalf of the corporation.
- Case goes to judgment. After judgment, John Doe files **Lawsuit #2** against the party that breached the contract, saying that by breaching the contract, the corporation reduced its value, and the value of his shares went down. This time, he sued as John Doe, harmed shareholder — not on behalf of the corporation.

*In this hypo, we are not worried about whether on the merits this person would have a legally cognizable cause of action. We only care about whether as a matter of preclusion he would be able to bring this claim at all as a result of Lawsuit #1.*

Because John Doe is the sole shareholder of the corporation, he would have privity with the corporation (**privity = substantial = near complete control**) — basically having enough power to direct the decision-making in Lawsuit #1:

1. Directing the lawsuit
2. Controlling what happens even in Lawsuit #1

#### Example 2: Successor in Interest

Privity here means that the interest being litigated in Lawsuit 2 really is the same interest, and the people have exactly the same legal interest as in Lawsuit 1.

- Suppose Jane Doe (consumer) got a credit card from a credit card company (signed a credit card contract).
- Jane Doe makes charges on the credit card and runs up the balance.
- Credit card company alleges that Jane Doe stops making payments.
- Credit card companies sometimes do not want to sue on their own, so they close the credit card account.

<!-- page: 22 -->

- Because there is still an amount Jane Doe allegedly owes, the credit company is able to sell this right to collect the ($700) debt.
- These "selling of rights to debt" contracts are done with **quitclaim language** (basically saying "the credit card company does not claim to have any rights to collect the debt, but whatever rights it has, it is selling them to the debt collecting company").
- So there's a quitclaim sale transaction to **Debt Buyer #1 (DB1)**, and DB1 begins to call the consumer Jane Doe.
- They might get tired of trying to collect and sell the debt to **Debt Buyer #2 (DB2)**.
- After a while, DB2 might get tired and file a lawsuit to get paid back.
- DB2 gets that lawsuit to go to judgment (remember: it does not matter who wins).
- Then, DB2 does something horrendous — it says: whatever rights I have in the *original right to collect contract* (*although not in the judgment, even if there were rights in the judgment*), I will sell to DB3 (thereby keeping the rights to collect in the judgment, but selling the rights to collect through the contract via quitclaim transfer, to DB3).
- DB3 sues Jane Doe again in **Lawsuit 2**.
- Jane Doe therefore will say that she was already sued once (claiming **claim preclusion**) — because it is the same debt.
- Assuming it is the same claim and the same debt, DB3 will claim that he/she was not a party in Lawsuit 1 (because in Lawsuit 1 it was DB2).
- In this situation, they would be in privity because one party is a **successor in interest**.

**Summary on privity types:**

- **Substantial control** — Non-parties had actual, or potential for, substantial control (involvement in decision making) over previous litigation.
  - Yes: owner of close corporation who assumes control of litigation against the firm
  - No: same attorney as prior party; financing earlier action

- **Successor in interest** — Two different parties who succeed one another in the interest of the subject matter of Lawsuit 1 in Lawsuit 2.

### Analyzing Element 2 — Same Claim

"Claim" for purposes of claim preclusion is different from "claim" for purposes of appellate jurisdiction. For appellate jurisdiction, "claim" means:

1. A theory of relief;
2. Tied to a specific set of facts;
3. Against a party.

**That is not what "claim" means for purposes of claim preclusion.**

Under the modern conceptualization of the "same claim" element, we mean **"same transaction or occurrence" (T/O)**. This is the same type of T/O discussed in the context of expanding the lawsuit.

- *Hayward-Robinson* provides a more extensive explanation of the definition of T/O, but is in the same legal context.
- In *Supplemental Jurisdiction*, T/O might be a little bit broader (uses the language "same case or controversy").

> ***NOTE:*** Claim preclusion will run **even if there are multiple theories of relief or multiple causes of action that were not asserted in Lawsuit 1.** This is the very implication of using the words "same transaction or occurrence," and the meaning of "claim" whether there is just one cause of action, or five, or even more. If many things happen in the same T/O, even if they are under multiple theories of relief, and only two of those theories of relief are brought before the court, **the rest of them will be lost forever under the doctrine of claim preclusion.**

For the Gonzalez court, it does not matter whether there is a securities fraud theory, a RICO theory, or common law fraud theory. What matters is whether or not they all were from the same T/O.

According to the Gonzalez court, the plaintiffs in both the Gonzalez case and the Rodriguez case were from the same claim (T/O). Reasons for same T/O:

- Same general area of land
- Same sellers
- Sellers were using the same kind of allegedly fraudulent scheme
- Roughly similar in time
- Roughly similar in space

Reasons they may **not** be from the same T/O:

- Although same general area of land, they were not exactly the same plots of land.
  - This is a bigger deal than the court actually realized, because if we say that general areas of land are the same T/O with respect to lawsuits, there is a possibility that we may mess with the market and the ability to sell those lots of land to different people.

### Claim Preclusion — Same T/O and Bonds and Coupons

#### *Cromwell v. County of Sac*, 94 U.S. (4 Otto) 351 (SCOTUS)(1876)

Where a second action is between the same parties and involves the same transaction or occurrence as an initial judgment, that judgment does not bar the second action if the second action is upon an issue that was not adjudicated in the initial action.

A **bond** is a promise to pay. A bond is a promise to pay when some contingent event has occurred. For bonds, we want a market where people can buy and sell bonds — if we stick bonds together, we might inadvertently prevent a market from growing.

Bonds have a certain maturity date. **Coupons** are basically little mini bonds. We want the bonds and coupons to be different claims, because if not, we may not be able to detach them later for purposes of avoiding claim preclusion.

**Hypo — Jill and Ben & Jerry's Ice Cream Bonds:**

- Jill is an investor who chooses to invest in Ben & Jerry's Ice Cream (B&J), which is issuing corporate bonds.
- B&J needs to raise capital in order to invent new ice cream, so they sell corporate bonds promising to pay later.
- They sell ten thousand $1,000 bonds.
- Jill buys 8 bonds. She might pay $6k for the eight thousand-dollar bonds.
- All 8 of the bonds mature and there is a legal obligation for B&J to pay $1k on each bond.
- Jill went to B&J to ask them to pay, but they refused.
- Jill **sues on only 2 of the 8 bonds** (Lawsuit 1).
- That case goes to judgment (doesn't matter who wins for claim preclusion).
- After suing on the 2 and going all the way to judgment (Lawsuit 1), Jill later decides to **sue on the other 6** (Lawsuit 2).
- Legal issue: Is Jill claim-precluded on Lawsuit 2?

**Analysis:**

1. **Same party (or in privity):** Yes. Jill and B&J are formally the same parties in Lawsuit 1 and Lawsuit 2. (No need to analyze privity in this specific hypo.)

2. **Same claim (same T/O):**
   - Related in time: yes
   - Related in space: yes
   - Are they similar issues? Yes
   - Will there be common evidence? Yes
   - What would be the policy implications if we interpret "same claim" this broadly? *(See alternate hypo below and applicable rule.)*

3. **A judgment that is both** (a) **Final** and (b) **On the merits.** (Stipulate this happened for now.)

<!-- page: 25 -->

**Alternate Hypo — If We Say These Are the Same T/O:**

- Jill hung on the two bonds from Lawsuit 1, sold the other 6 to Jack, then went to B&J and asked to get paid on her two bonds, and B&J refused.
- Jack subsequently goes to B&J and also asks to be paid on his new 6 bonds, and B&J also refuses.
- Jack now sues B&J on the 6 bonds (alternate Lawsuit 1), and that goes to judgment.
- B&J pleads claim preclusion on Jack on the theory that they are the same T/O as the previous lawsuit by Jill (B&J is the same party; Jack is the same party in privity as successor in interest; bonds were sold as part of the same T/O; judgment from Lawsuit 1 with Jill was both final and on the merits).

This cannot be right. It would not be fair (and therefore bad policy) for Jack to be claim-precluded because of Jill's previous Lawsuit 1. We can't say they were not the same parties (they were in privity, as explained above). We also know that the judgment in Lawsuit 1 was final and on the merits. **We therefore need to be able to say that these were not from the same T/O.**

> **BLL:** ***For this reason, bonds and coupons (by definition and by rule) are considered SEPARATE transactions or occurrences for claim preclusion purposes.***

Applying this to the Gonzalez case: we want there to be a real estate market in the different plots of land, and having interpreted all of the plots of land to be the same T/O might make the market hesitate to purchase real estate if they knew that a prior claim against it would preclude the new owner from asserting a new claim against the same lot of property.

<!-- page: 26 -->

We also have this rule for land as well, but maybe the Gonzalez court did not pay sufficient attention to it.

> ***After understanding the specific rule applicable for bonds and coupons:*** When encountering a new fact pattern involving a new determination that is not subject to the hard and fast rules above, one should consider not just (i) relationship in time; (ii) relationship in space; (iii) do they share common evidence; (iv) are they similar issues; but also **(v) the policy effect of saying that they are the same T/O for purposes of claim preclusion** (are we going to screw up a market?) — and if we are, then we would want to be careful.

### Claim Preclusion — Same T/O and Installment Payments

After bonds and coupons, now we need to discuss how the claim preclusion "same T/O" element works with **installment payments**.

**Installment payments** are a sum of money paid in small parts in a fixed period of time.

We sometimes define what is the same claim according to policy considerations, one of which is whether or not there is a market in that specific area. Another policy consideration: when do we want people to bring lawsuits together, and when do we not care.

**Hypo — Kermit's SUV:**

- Kermit bought a new SUV.
- He made a contract for purchase in January, so the first payment is due in February.
- Kermit makes the first 3 payments (Feb, Mar, Apr), but misses a payment in May and in June.
- The creditor (person owed the payments) wants to sue.
- Suppose that after missing the June payment, **Creditor only sues on the May payment**, rather than on May and June payments.
- The case goes to judgment in June (a superfast court — two weeks later in June).
- Later, after the judgment in the middle of June, because Kermit also missed the June payment, Creditor wants to sue Kermit again in **Lawsuit 2**.
- Kermit pleads claim preclusion.

**Analysis:**

1. **Same party (or in privity):** Yes — both plaintiff and defendants are the same.

2. **Same claim (same T/O):**
   - Related in time: yes
   - Related in space: yes
   - Are they similar issues? Yes
   - Will there be common evidence? Yes
   - Policy implications of interpreting "same claim" this broadly? In this situation, we **WOULD** want the car companies to bring these payment claims all together to be litigated all at once. We also in general do not cut installment payments up and pay them separately (unlike with bonds or coupons); so there is no market here that would be affected.

3. **A judgment that is both** (a) **Final** and (b) **On the merits:** Yes (stipulate this has happened).

**Conclusion:** The second lawsuit suing for the June payment of the SUV would be **claim precluded**.

However, if the car company were to sue in June before the July payment matures, *absent an acceleration clause*, ahead of when the payment is due they would not be able to sue because the person would not have breached the contract for payment — we would not want them to be able to bring suit before the payment is due unless there is an acceleration clause.

On the other hand, if they filed suit for both missed payments, went to judgment, and Kermit then missed the July payment, we would want them to sue for the July payment, because that debt would be part of what we would consider a **different claim** (a different T/O).

<!-- page: 28 -->

Agreement so far:

- The May payment and the June payment are part of the **same T/O** (for claim preclusion purposes), but
- The July payment is **not**.

**Alternate Hypo (same facts, except Kermit also misses July payment):**

- Kermit bought new SUV; first payment due in February.
- Kermit makes first 3 payments (Feb, Mar, Apr), but misses May and June.
- This time, Kermit also misses the **July** payment.
- The creditor files its lawsuit in July.
- But it only files a lawsuit for the **May** missed payment, and that claim reaches final judgment in Lawsuit 1.
- Later, the creditor sues for the **June and July** payments in Lawsuit 2.

In this case, they would be **claim precluded** because they had the opportunity to sue for all of them and they passed up the opportunity. So whether the missed payment in July is part of the same T/O for claim preclusion purposes depends on **when the lawsuit was filed**.

> **BLL:** *Whether a newly missed installment payment is part of the same T/O as the others for claim preclusion purposes (in installment payment situations) depends on when the lawsuit was filed.*

The policy behind this is efficiency gains, but we do not want efficiency so badly that we end up suing people before the installment payment is missed.

Things can change with the presence of certain clauses.

### *Jones v. Morris Plan Bank of Portsmouth*, 191 S.E. 608 (Va. 1937)

If the same evidence will support multiple actions arising from the same contract, there can only be one cause of action.

<!-- page: 30 -->

**Facts**

- William Jones (plaintiff) bought a car from J.A. Parker (Parker), the sale amount payable in 12 monthly installments.
- The note contained an **acceleration clause** stating: "The whole amount of this note (less any payments made hereon) becomes immediately due and payable in the event of nonpayment at maturity of any installment thereof."
- Parker assigned the contract to the Morris Plan Bank of Portsmouth (Bank) (defendant), which was to hold the title of the car until Jones paid in full.
- When Jones failed to pay in May and June, the Bank brought suit for those months' payments and Jones paid.
- Then, when Jones failed to pay again in July, the Bank brought suit again for that month's amount. In response, Jones filed a plea of res judicata which resulted in nonsuit.
- The Bank then took possession of the car from Jones without his consent and sold it.
- Jones then brought this suit against the Bank for **conversion** of his car.
- Jones based the suit on the theory that when he failed to make the May and June payments and the Bank elected to collect only those payments rather than the full amount, the Bank **waived** its chance to collect the remainder of the monthly installments.
- After Jones had presented all of his evidence, the Bank filed a motion to strike the evidence.
- The district court granted the motion and entered judgment for the Bank. Jones appealed.

**Holding and Reasoning (Gregory, J.)**

- A cause of action is based on facts.
- In determining whether a contract is divisible for purposes of maintaining multiple actions on the same contract, courts look at the facts that are needed to maintain each cause of action.
- **If the same evidence will support multiple actions arising from the contract, there can only be one cause of action.**
- Here, **Jones's failure to pay in May triggered all of the monthly installments becoming due**.
- Thus, the facts necessary to maintain the Bank's original action for the May and June payments — Jones's failure to pay in May — are the exact same as the facts that would have been necessary for the Bank to bring an action at that time for the entire remaining sale amount.
- The note and conditional sales contract allowing the Bank to keep the car's title until payment in full constitute **one, indivisible contract**.
- By seeking only the payments for May and June when it originally brought suit, **the Bank forfeited its right to the remainder of the payments when a judgment was reached in that proceeding**.
- At that time, the title of the car passed to Jones.
- The Bank's sale of the car was thus a **conversion**.

<!-- page: 31 -->

In this case, we no longer have the problem of knowing when the payment was missed. Due to the acceleration clause, all of the payments become due, so when the creditor brings a lawsuit only for a few of the payments, the rest of the payments due — which were triggered by the acceleration clause — will end up being **claim precluded**.

**Claim Preclusion Analysis (Jones):**

1. **Same party (or in privity):** Yes — both plaintiff and defendants are the same.

2. **Same claim (same T/O):**
   - Related in time: yes
   - Related in space: yes
   - Are they similar issues? Yes
   - Will there be common evidence? Yes
   - Policy implications of broad "same claim" interpretation? In this situation, we **WOULD** want the car companies to bring these payment claims all together to be litigated all at once. We also in general do not cut installment payments up and pay them separately (unlike with bonds or coupons); so there is no market here that would be affected.

3. **A judgment that is both** (a) **Final** and (b) **On the merits:** Yes (stipulate this has happened).
