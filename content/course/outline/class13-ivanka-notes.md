## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly*, 550 U.S. 544 (SCOTUS)(2007)**

  A complaint must allege sufficient facts that, if accepted as true, would "state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face" in order to defeat a motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim under Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(6).

- ***Ashcroft v. Iqbal*, 556 U.S. 662 (SCOTUS)(2009)**

  Under *Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly*, a complaint will only survive a motion to dismiss if it alleges nonconclusory facts that, taken as true, state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face.

- ***Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank*, 339 U.S. 306 (SCOTUS)(1950)**

  An elementary and fundamental requirement of due process…is notice reasonably calculated, under all the circumstances, to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections.

- ***Sniadach v. Family Finance Corp.*, 395 U.S. 337 (SCOTUS)(1969)**

  Under the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, a state government must provide notice and an opportunity to be heard prior to the taking of *wages*.

- ***Goldberg v. Kelly*, 397 U.S. 254 (SCOTUS)(1970)**

  When a state seeks to terminate welfare benefits, procedural due process requires the state to provide the recipient with a pre-termination evidentiary hearing for the purpose of determining the validity of discontinuing public assistance in order to protect the recipient against an erroneous termination of his benefits.

- ***Jones v. Flowers*, 547 U.S. 220 (SCOTUS)(2006)**

  When a state is attempting to serve a citizen with process, it must take **reasonable steps to serve the notice if service by mail is ineffective**.

## Lecture Recap for Day 12

Last time we discussed the level of specificity required to survive a Motion To Dismiss. After *Bell Atlantic* and *Iqbal*, the approach to a Motion to Dismiss under FRCP 12(b)(6) is:

### Analysis for MTD (FRCP 12(b)(6))

Make a careful note of the elements of the cause of action being asserted in the Complaint and break it down.

*Example — Negligence elements:*
1. **Duty** — to exercise due care
2. **Breach** — failure to exercise due care
3. **Causation** — that the breach of duty caused certain damages
4. **Damages** — that damages are sufficiently alleged

**Step 1:** Look at the complaint and physically cross out the portions that, according to the court, are conclusory or mere recitations of the elements of the cause of action (ignore whatever has been crossed out); then

**Step 2:** Look at what is left over, consider alternative explanations for it, and see if what is left over is sufficient to nudge the Complaint from merely possible to "plausible."

Under the *Iqbal* standard, you have to plead **the facts that give rise to an inference of a certain cause of action** — not merely the ultimate fact (e.g., "negligence") itself. *Bell Atlantic* similarly makes clear that one must allege the underlying facts that give rise to an inference of a certain cause of action.

We then moved on to relief pending litigation — the **Preliminary Injunction**.

A lot of major litigation, especially against government officials attempting to effect government policy, has involved preliminary injunctions. The grant or denial of a preliminary injunction gives rise to an appeal, which we will discuss later.

### BLL of Preliminary Injunctions

*(From the Thane Coat simulation: 4 key factors. Depending on the circuit, these can be balanced (9th Circuit) or treated as prerequisites (5th Circuit). SCOTUS has not resolved the split.)*

**1) Likelihood of success on the merits**
- The moving party must prove they are pretty likely to win on the merits if the case did go to court.
- Basically litigating the merits as fast as possible and persuading the court.
- Evidence; Documents.

**2) Irreparable Harm** (to the moving party if injunction does not issue)
- The assumption is there is a reason for urgency — the moving party will suffer some harm that cannot be repaired at judgment.
- **Can be repaired** (NOT irreparable): anything compensable with damages — business/economic harm, damages harm.
- **Cannot be repaired** (irreparable harm):
  - Loss of limb

<!-- page: 5 -->

  - Very serious sickness
  - Business harm that could be irreparable:
    - Where the entity cannot recover from damages (e.g., *Thane Coat* — when they could not sue the government later for damages)
    - Impossible to prove (or quantify) damages with enough certainty to support a damage award
    - Courts are generally suspicious of this argument; they would rather see what the business injury is.

**3) Balance of the equities** (harm to nonmoving party if injunction does issue)
- Courts consider not just the harm to the moving party if the injunction does not issue, but the **harm to the nonmoving party if the injunction does issue**.
- Court will bring it up and balance the two before deciding.

**4) The public interest**
- If **Government** wants an injunction (*Thane Coat*): They will argue that their interest reflects the public interest because they are the government.
- If **Private party** wants an injunction: Court will consider if the injunction is issued, will there be public harm in any way (messing with a regulatory scheme, etc.).
  - Example: patients seeking a PI to force pharmaceutical companies to continue providing medicine that under FDA regulations had failed. Court concerned that forcing companies to continue giving failed medicine would overstep bounds and deter drug companies from entering trials. Court decided issuing a PI was against the public interest.

### Procedural Due Process

The concept of relief prior to litigation is familiar from Quasi In Rem situations, where the plaintiff tries to induce the court to seize property while litigation is pending. Now we consider when it is permissible, and fair, for the court to do adverse things to a defendant prior to litigation, and what proof must be offered.

The concerns that arise in Procedural Due Process are similar to those the court considers when granting a Preliminary Injunction:
- The court considers whether the plaintiff actually has a case (similar to "likelihood of success on the merits").
- The court asks "what is the emergency here?" (In *Connecticut v. Doehr*, the court found no emergency and no particular reason to seize the property.)

**Procedural Due Process** involves two components:
- **Notice**
- **Opportunity to be Heard** (present facts and evidence to a decision maker)

---

#### The Due Process Clause

**Amendment XIV, § 1:** "nor shall any State deprive any person of life, liberty, or property without due process of law" — applies mainly to States.

**Amendment V:** Similar language, but applies only against the **Federal Government** (not the States).

<!-- page: 7 -->

**Two types of Due Process:**
- **Substantive due process:** Applies the Bill of Rights Amendments to the States (a ConLaw topic — not covered here).
- **Procedural due process** (what this class covers):
  - **Notice**; and
  - **Opportunity to be Heard**

If there is a legal proceeding that might affect the rights of the defendant, we want to give him **notice** that this is underway and an **opportunity for him to provide evidence to the court** of his side of the story.

---

### Notice — *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank*, 339 U.S. 306 (SCOTUS)(1950)

**RLL:** An elementary and fundamental requirement of due process…is notice reasonably calculated, under all the circumstances, to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections.

*Background — Wills and Trusts:*
- "Sort of rich" people (not rich enough to efficiently create their own individual trust) form a **common trust** — a pooled trust with other such persons.
- The trustee invests the pooled money and distributes profits to the beneficiaries — some during the settlor's life (inter vivos), some upon death (post-mortem).
- Beneficiaries may change over time; it may not always be clear who gets money or when.
- A sophisticated trustee entity might sue beneficiaries for a **declaratory judgment** ("I am not doing anything wrong") to seize the forum and use issue preclusion against later suits. To bind all beneficiaries, they must all be sued at once (class action).

<!-- page: 8 -->

**Facts:**
- Central Hanover Bank and Trust Company (defendant) established a common trust fund, pooling multiple small trusts into one large fund for easier administration.
- Central Hanover petitioned the court for settlement of its first account as trustee.
- Beneficiaries were given notice by **publication in a local newspaper** in compliance with the minimum requirements of New York banking law.
- The court appointed Mullane (plaintiff) as special guardian of beneficiaries who had not appeared. He objected that the notice was inadequate.
- The court nonetheless entered a final decree accepting the accounts. Mullane appealed.

**Issue:**
- Whether publication in a local newspaper is sufficient notice to comply with Constitutional standards.

**Holding and Reasoning (Jackson, J.):**
- No. The Due Process Clause requires that deprivation of life, liberty, or property by adjudication be preceded by **notice and opportunity for hearing**.
- This proceeding potentially deprives trust beneficiaries of property by cutting off their right to contest the administration of the trust.
- **Notice is constitutionally required to be reasonably calculated to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections.**
- Such notice must **reasonably convey the required information** and **offer any interested parties a reasonable time to come forward**.
- For parties whose identities and addresses were unknown to the trustee: notice by publication was sufficient under the circumstances. The number of beneficiaries and their identities were difficult or impossible to track; the cost of personal notice was enormous; Mullane adequately represented the interests of unidentifiable parties.
- However, **notice by publication does not satisfy due process where the beneficiaries' addresses and interests are known to the trustee.**
- **Here, the trustee has the names and addresses of the income beneficiaries represented by Mullane.**

<!-- page: 9 -->

- There is no reason why the trustee should be permitted to dispense with a serious effort to inform these parties personally.
- **Because the New York banking law permits notice by publication even to parties whose addresses and interests are known to the trustee, it is incompatible with the Fourteenth Amendment's due process provision.**

**Key distinction:** In *Mullane*, SCOTUS says that **actual notice is not required**, but **reasonable effort to provide notice is required**. If you know where they are, you must send them notice via mail to the place you know where they are (**reasonableness**).

**BLL:**
- Actual notice is not required.
- Reasonable effort to provide notice is required.
- If you know where they are, you must send notice via mail to that address.

The test is "**reasonableness**" — the court then has to develop this through subsequent case law.

---

### Subsequent Applications of *Mullane* Reasonableness Standard

<!-- page: 11 -->

**Government housing eviction** (*Green v. Lindsey*, 456 U.S. 444, SCOTUS, 1982):
- Posting notice on the door of a public housing unit is **not** reasonable because the notice could be stolen or interfered with by children or others.
- **BLL:** When using methods of notice that are subject to easy interference, one might be required by the court to investigate and use other forms of providing notice.

**Incarcerated individuals** (*Dusenbery v. U.S.*, 534 U.S. 161, SCOTUS, 2002):
- Mailing notice to an incarcerated individual is constitutionally sufficient, even if the prisoner may not actually receive it.

**Certified mail vs. regular mail:**
- Historically, certified mail to the home was considered "reasonable" (when someone was likely to be home to sign for it).
- Now, most people work outside the home and may not be home to sign for certified mail. Regular mail (which leaves notice without requiring a signature) may be more "reasonable" today.
- In a modern setting, electronic mail, phone numbers, text messages, or social media might also serve as notice — though no US court system has written electronic notice into its rules as permissible as of 2021.

### *Jones v. Flowers*, 547 U.S. 220 (SCOTUS)(2006)

**RLL:** When a state is attempting to serve a citizen with process, it must take reasonable steps to serve the notice if service by mail is ineffective.

**Facts:**
- Gary Jones (plaintiff) owned a house at 717 North Bryan Street in Little Rock, Arkansas.
- Beginning in 1997, he failed to pay property taxes.
- After three years of delinquency, Commissioner of State Lands Mark Wilcox (defendant) sent a letter via **certified mail to Jones' address**, informing him of his delinquency and that he had two years to pay or the property would be sold.
- **The letter was returned as "unclaimed."**
- Two years later, Wilcox published a notice of public sale in the Arkansas Democrat Gazette. No response received. A private sale was arranged with Linda Flowers (defendant).
- Prior to completion of the sale, **Wilcox sent another letter to Jones, which was also returned as "unclaimed."**
- **Flowers then purchased the house and personally served a detainer notice on the property, which was accepted by Jones' daughter.**
- Jones was notified and sued in Arkansas state court, arguing that the form of service employed by Wilcox was constitutionally inadequate.
- The trial court granted summary judgment in favor of Flowers and Wilcox; the Arkansas Supreme Court affirmed.

**Holding and Reasoning (Roberts, C.J.):**
- While reliance on service by mail is not unreasonable on its face, **there are instances when the state is required to enlist other forms of service.**
- **When the state is aware that service by mail has failed, it must take other reasonable steps to ensure that service is achieved.**
- When a certified letter is returned as "unclaimed," the situation is no different than if a state employee had seen a letter fall out of the postman's bag and into a storm drain.
- **The state is aware that service by mail completely failed.** That was the case here.
- The state was tasked with enlisting certain reasonable steps, particularly when it had nearly **two years** between learning that mail service failed and when the sale was to take place.
- **Examples of reasonable steps**: sending notice by regular mail; sending notice simply to "Occupant"; or posting notice on the front door of the house.
- What **would not** be considered reasonable: undertaking a search through the phonebook or other government records to locate Jones' new address.
- The newspaper advertisement was **not sufficient** to constitute notice.

<!-- page: 13 -->

**Key holding:** The duty to evaluate reasonable notice **does not stop when notice is first given.** There is a **continuing duty** — if you become aware of facts suggesting that notice was unsuccessful, there is an additional requirement to take further steps.

> *The court is not content with measuring reasonableness only as of the time that notice is first mailed out. Instead, the court continually evaluates what is reasonable, as time passes.*

**FRCP vs. the Constitution:** FRCP 4(e)(1) says that service of process may be satisfied by following state law for service of process. However, FRCP is a federal rule; the source of law for "Notice" as part of Procedural Due Process is the **Constitution**. The Constitution is the Supreme Law of the Land. If the FRCP articulates something that is insufficient per the Constitution, the Constitution controls. That is why there is case law to interpret what both the Constitution and the FRCP require.

> ***NOTE:** The relationship runs both ways. The Constitution sets a floor — the rules cannot go below it. But Federal Rules of Civil Procedure can set a **higher** standard than the constitutional minimum. If you fail to meet the FRCP's higher standard, you can still get a Motion to Dismiss for failure to comply with the Service of Process Rules.*

### Opportunity to be Heard — Introduction

We have now considered the basic rules with respect to "Notice." Now, we want to know the rules with respect to **"Opportunity to be Heard."**

The two cases that kick off this area of law are:
1. ***Sniadach v. Family Finance Corp. of Bay View*, 395 U.S. 337 (SCOTUS)(1969)** — "old property" (wage garnishment)
2. ***Goldberg v. Kelly*, 397 U.S. 254 (SCOTUS)(1970)** — "new property" (Social Security Disability benefits)

The court system struggled in these early cases to find standards, rules, and an analytical framework. *Sniadach* left open: (i) whether its rationale would apply to prejudgment seizure of property other than wages when hardship was less severe; and (ii) what extraordinary situations could justify seizure of property without a hearing.

*Goldberg v. Kelly* tells us what things we "might want to consider" as to what process is, but **it is not until *Mathews v. Eldridge* that we find out what the court actually wanted to consider.**

<!-- page: 15 -->

**Old Property vs. New Property:**
- **Old Property** — things traditionally called "property" (cell phones, bank accounts, house, real estate, retirement accounts, etc.) — sort of Lockean theory of property.
- **New Property** — additional things that were an uneasy fit under traditional views:
  - Government benefits: TANF, Social Security Benefits, Social Security Disability Benefits, Supplemental Social Security Income
  - Government permissions or licenses: business licenses, state IDs, driver licenses — things that were property, but not considered property in the traditional sense.

The courts felt there was enough of a property right in these "new property" things to fit within the language of the 5th and 14th Amendments' due process clauses.

- ***Sniadach*** — "old property" (wage garnishment): A plaintiff (think: credit card company) claimed unpaid credit bills and, before proving the debt, asked the court to garnish the defendant's wages — seizing a portion directly from the employer to pay the debt.
- ***Goldberg*** — "new property" (Social Security Disability benefits termination).

<!-- page: 16 -->

***Mathews v. Eldridge* is a "new property" case** that articulates a framework the court has stuck to ever since.

***Connecticut v. Doehr* is an "old property" case** (pre-judgment real estate attachment). The court also uses and adopts the *Mathews v. Eldridge* analysis, making it finally a single way to analyze both "old property" and "new property" situations.

---

### *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (SCOTUS)(1976) — New Property Case

Whether an administrative procedure meets the constitutional guarantees of the Due Process Clause requires a consideration of three factors:

1. The private interest at stake in the administrative action;
2. The risk of an erroneous deprivation of this interest through the procedures used, and the probable value, if any, of additional or substitute procedural safeguards; and
3. The government's interest, including the function involved and the fiscal and administrative burdens that additional or substitute procedural requirements would entail.

**Facts:**
- Eldridge was on social security disability benefits.
- Every year he had to initiate the renewal of the benefits.
- After a while, the state got additional information from him and decided he was no longer eligible and notified him they were thinking of terminating his benefits.
- He appealed. After reviewing his appeal, they still decided he no longer qualified and terminated them.

<!-- page: 17 -->

- After termination, he was allowed to send additional information for reconsideration, but had no right to a live hearing yet.
- If denied again after reconsideration, he could ask for a live hearing before an **Administrative Law Judge (ALJ)**.
  - He could call witnesses, introduce new documents, cross-examine opposing witnesses.
  - In these situations, there is no opposing counsel — only the judge to convince.
- If the ALJ denied the benefits, he could appeal to an appellate body within the agency, and if denied there, then to an Article III court.

Eldridge argued that there was insufficient due process — that he should have had a live hearing **before** the benefits were taken away (analogizing to *Goldberg v. Kelly*).

The court **distinguishes** this case from *Goldberg* because:
- This scheme relies on evidence of disability by a doctor (physical paperwork that could be mailed in).
- He was required to have worked prior to qualifying for this benefit.
- Social Security Disability's purpose is to **replace income** from wages no longer available due to disability — analogous to unemployment insurance — rather than to provide **sustenance for living** (as in *Goldberg v. Kelly*).
- Even if Eldridge did depend on these benefits as sustenance for living, it was the wrong scheme — he would need to apply for welfare benefits, not social security disability.

### The Mathews Three-Part Test

**1. Private interest of the party whose property is being taken:**
- Eldridge's private interest is the uninterrupted receipt of his benefits pending the final administrative decision on his claim.

**2. The risk of an erroneous deprivation of this interest through the procedures used, and the probable value of additional or substitute procedural safeguards (COMPARE):**
- Consider the governmental scheme in the fact pattern.
- Ask: what is the risk of erroneous determination under the **current** governmental scheme; and compare it to the risk of erroneous determination under the **scheme the plaintiff is proposing**.
- The procedure used (medical assessment of physical or mental condition) is straightforward, easily documented, and premised on a narrow amount of information. Additional procedural requirements would have **little value**.

<!-- page: 18 -->

**3. The government's interest, including the function involved and the fiscal and administrative burdens:**
- The administrative and societal costs of affording an evidentiary hearing prior to termination of disability benefits would be **high**. Affording such hearings would have a direct financial impact by increasing the number of hearings, and providing benefits to ineligible recipients pending decision would be expensive.

**In applying this when litigating:**
- Isolate the piece of the scheme one thinks is unconstitutional and provides insufficient process.
- Propose an alternative scheme.
- This allows the court to compare the risk of erroneous determination under the current scheme vs. the proposed scheme.

**Key distinction from *Goldberg v. Kelly*:**
- **Welfare benefits**: to make sure people have enough to eat or shelter — means-based, based upon poverty.
- **Social Security Disability**: to compensate for a disability keeping one from working — not means-based or based upon poverty.

The court concludes that the risk of erroneous determination here is less than in *Goldberg* or *Doehr*, because the decision can be made by reviewing medical evidence in the record, without requiring witnesses or a live hearing.

The final factor was government/public interest:
- Court looked at the administrative benefit and cost if the government awaited the live hearing vs. the benefit that the person would get.
- **Court was worried that money spent on process would be money not spent on benefits.**

Preview of *Connecticut v. Doehr* (Old Property Case — covered next):
- A bar fight case: plaintiff induced the court to file a pre-judgment "attachment" on the property records of the defendant's house (an encumbrance upon the title, such that when the owner tries to sell it, a title search will flag that someone else might own a piece of the house).
