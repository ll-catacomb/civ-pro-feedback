## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***Markman v. Westview Instruments, Inc.***, 517 U.S. 370 (SCOTUS) (1996)
  The court, because of its training in complex issues and knowledge of written documents, is more competent to correctly, fairly, and uniformly make decisions relating to the interpretation of a term of art.

- ***Kennedy v. Southern California Edison Co.***, 219 F.3d 988 (9th Cir. 2000)
  A district court has an obligation to correctly instruct the jury even after being presented with an instruction that is improper, but still directs the jury's attention to an important issue.

- ***Lansford-Coaldale Joint Water Authority v. Tonolli Corp.***, 4 F.3d 1209 (3d Cir. 1993)
  Findings and conclusions prepared by a party and adopted by the judge adequately serve the purposes of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 52(a).

- ***Aetna Casualty & Surety Co. v. Yeatts***, 122 F.2d 350 (4th Cir. 1941)
  The granting or refusing of a new trial is within the discretion of the trial judge and his action is not reviewable upon appeal, except for in the most exceptional circumstances.

- ***Unitherm Food Sys., Inc. v. Swift-Exkrich, Inc.***, 546 U.S. 394 (SCOTUS) (2006)
  Under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 50, a court of appeals may not review a verdict for sufficiency of the evidence unless the party moved the district court for post-verdict relief.

### Sources of Law: Federal Rules of Civil Procedure

<!-- page: 2 -->

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 50 – Judgement as a Matter of Law in Jury Trial; Related Motion for a New Trial; Conditional Ruling

**(a) Judgment as a Matter of Law.**

(1) In General. If a party has been fully heard on an issue during a jury trial and the court finds that a reasonable jury would not have a legally sufficient evidentiary basis to find for the party on that issue, the court may:

- (A) resolve the issue against the party; and
- (B) grant a motion for judgment as a matter of law against the party on a claim or defense that, under the controlling law, can be maintained or defeated only with a favorable finding on that issue.

(2) Motion. A motion for judgment as a matter of law may be made at any time before the case is submitted to the jury. The motion must specify the judgment sought and the law and facts that entitle the movant to the judgment.

**(b) Renewing the Motion After Trial; Alternative Motion for a New Trial.** If the court does not grant a motion for judgment as a matter of law made under Rule 50(a), the court is considered to have submitted the action to the jury subject to the court's later deciding the legal questions raised by the motion. No later than 28 days after the entry of judgment—or if the motion addresses a jury issue not decided by a verdict, no later than 28 days after the jury was discharged—the movant may file a renewed motion for judgment as a matter of law and may include an alternative or joint request for a new trial under Rule 59. In ruling on the renewed motion, the court may:

1. allow judgment on the verdict, if the jury returned a verdict;
2. order a new trial; or
3. direct the entry of judgment as a matter of law.

<!-- page: 3 -->

**(c) Granting the Renewed Motion; Conditional Ruling on a Motion for a New Trial.**

(1) In General. If the court grants a renewed motion for judgment as a matter of law, it must also conditionally rule on any motion for a new trial by determining whether a new trial should be granted if the judgment is later vacated or reversed. The court must state the grounds for conditionally granting or denying the motion for a new trial.

(2) Effect of a Conditional Ruling. Conditionally granting the motion for a new trial does not affect the judgment's finality; if the judgment is reversed, the new trial must proceed unless the appellate court orders otherwise. If the motion for a new trial is conditionally denied, the appellee may assert error in that denial; if the judgment is reversed, the case must proceed as the appellate court orders.

**(d) Time for a Losing Party's New-Trial Motion.** Any motion for a new trial under Rule 59 by a party against whom judgment as a matter of law is rendered must be filed no later than 28 days after the entry of the judgment.

**(e) Denying the Motion for Judgment as a Matter of Law; Reversal on Appeal.** If the court denies the motion for judgment as a matter of law, the prevailing party may, as appellee, assert grounds entitling it to a new trial should the appellate court conclude that the trial court erred in denying the motion. If the appellate court reverses the judgment, it may order a new trial, direct the trial court to determine whether a new trial should be granted, or direct the entry of judgment.

<!-- page: 4 -->

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 51 – Instructions to the Jury; Objections; Preserving a Claim of Error

**(a) Requests.**

(1) Before or at the Close of the Evidence. At the close of the evidence or at any earlier reasonable time that the court orders, a party may file and furnish to every other party written requests for the jury instructions it wants the court to give.

(2) After the Close of the Evidence. After the close of the evidence, a party may:

- (A) file requests for instructions on issues that could not reasonably have been anticipated by an earlier time that the court set for requests; and
- (B) with the court's permission, file untimely requests for instructions on any issue.

**(b) Instructions.** The court:

1. must inform the parties of its proposed instructions and proposed action on the requests before instructing the jury and before final jury arguments;
2. must give the parties an opportunity to object on the record and out of the jury's hearing before the instructions and arguments are delivered; and
3. may instruct the jury at any time before the jury is discharged.

**(c) Objections.**

(1) How to Make. A party who objects to an instruction or the failure to give an instruction must do so on the record, stating distinctly the matter objected to and the grounds for the objection.

(2) When to Make. An objection is timely if:

- (A) a party objects at the opportunity provided under Rule 51(b)(2); or
- (B) a party was not informed of an instruction or action on a request before that opportunity to object, and the party objects promptly after learning that the instruction or request will be, or has been, given or refused.

**(d) Assigning Error; Plain Error.**

(1) Assigning Error. A party may assign as error:

- (A) an error in an instruction actually given, if that party properly objected; or
- (B) a failure to give an instruction, if that party properly requested it and—unless the court rejected the request in a definitive ruling on the record—also properly objected.

(2) Plain Error. A court may consider a plain error in the instructions that has not been preserved as required by Rule 51(d)(1) if the error affects substantial rights.

<!-- page: 5 -->

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 52 – Findings and Conclusions by the Court; Judgement on Partial Findings

**(a) Findings and Conclusions.**

(1) In General. In an action tried on the facts without a jury or with an advisory jury, the court must find the facts specially and state its conclusions of law separately. The findings and conclusions may be stated on the record after the close of the evidence or may appear in an opinion or a memorandum of decision filed by the court. Judgment must be entered under Rule 58.

(2) For an Interlocutory Injunction. In granting or refusing an interlocutory injunction, the court must similarly state the findings and conclusions that support its action.

(3) For a Motion. The court is not required to state findings or conclusions when ruling on a motion under Rule 12 or 56 or, unless these rules provide otherwise, on any other motion.

(4) Effect of a Master's Findings. A master's findings, to the extent adopted by the court, must be considered the court's findings.

(5) Questioning the Evidentiary Support. A party may later question the sufficiency of the evidence supporting the findings, whether or not the party requested findings, objected to them, moved to amend them, or moved for partial findings.

(6) Setting Aside the Findings. Findings of fact, whether based on oral or other evidence, must not be set aside unless clearly erroneous, and the reviewing court must give due regard to the trial court's opportunity to judge the witnesses' credibility.

**(b) Amended or Additional Findings.** On a party's motion filed no later than 28 days after the entry of judgment, the court may amend its findings—or make additional findings—and may amend the judgment accordingly. The motion may accompany a motion for a new trial under Rule 59.

**(c) Judgment on Partial Findings.** If a party has been fully heard on an issue during a nonjury trial and the court finds against the party on that issue, the court may enter judgment against the party on a claim or defense that, under the controlling law, can be maintained or defeated only with a favorable finding on that issue. The court may, however, decline to render any judgment until the close of the evidence. A judgment on partial findings must be supported by findings of fact and conclusions of law as required by Rule 52(a).

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 59 – New Trial; Altering or Amending a Judgement

**(a) In General.**

(1) Grounds for New Trial. The court may, on motion, grant a new trial on all or some of the issues—and to any party—as follows:

- (A) after a jury trial, for any reason for which a new trial has heretofore been granted in an action at law in federal court; or
- (B) after a nonjury trial, for any reason for which a rehearing has heretofore been granted in a suit in equity in federal court.

(2) Further Action After a Nonjury Trial. After a nonjury trial, the court may, on motion for a new trial, open the judgment if one has been entered, take additional testimony, amend findings of fact and conclusions of law or make new ones, and direct the entry of a new judgment.

<!-- page: 6 -->

**(b) Time to File a Motion for a New Trial.** A motion for a new trial must be filed no later than 28 days after the entry of judgment.

**(c) Time to Serve Affidavits.** When a motion for a new trial is based on affidavits, they must be filed with the motion. The opposing party has 14 days after being served to file opposing affidavits. The court may permit reply affidavits.

**(d) New Trial on the Court's Initiative or for Reasons Not in the Motion.** No later than 28 days after the entry of judgment, the court, on its own, may order a new trial for any reason that would justify granting one on a party's motion. After giving the parties notice and an opportunity to be heard, the court may grant a timely motion for a new trial for a reason not stated in the motion. In either event, the court must specify the reasons in its order.

**(e) Motion to Alter or Amend a Judgment.** A motion to alter or amend a judgment must be filed no later than 28 days after the entry of the judgment.

## Lecture Recap for Day 16

Last time we discussed the division between law and equity. The primary point of that discussion was to start off with one of several doctrines in which we will explicitly recognize that there is a distribution of power between the judge and the jury. We had been doing this all along with the Motions to Dismiss and the Motions for Summary Judgement, but now we will consider it specifically within the context of trial mechanics.

Because of a historical distinction, the right to a jury trial was only available if a dispute was adjudicated in the King's Bench in the common law courts, whereas a jury was not available historically if the dispute was of a nature that was tried under a Chancellor.

That distinction was carried forward during the ratification of the Seventh Amendment, and even though in the federal law court system, we merged law and equity, the Seventh Amendment jury trial right continues to require a division between law and equity if one of the parties makes a Seventh Amendment jury trial demand, they may be entitled to one, and so we might have to honor it because of the Constitutional command.

Remember however that at least as of now, the Seventh Amendment applies **only** to the federal government, and not to the individual States.

<!-- page: 7 -->

In many cases there is no problem because the cause of action asserted was available at the time of the adoption of the 7th Amendment, so for those we understand whether there is a jury available or not, depending on whether it was a law or equity case. The issue arises when one of two circumstances happen:

1. **There is a newly created cause of action; and**
2. **There is a newly created form of decision making (adjudicators).**

### 1) Newly Created Cause of Action

Primary case comes from ***Chauffeurs v. Terry***

- Gives us a 2-part test to determine whether a newly created cause of action should be treated on the law side or the equity side:
  1. Find an analog to the historical cause of action itself
     - (Appears to require a focus on "issues" as opposed to "causes of action" — at least as it was administered in *Terry*, even though it was not how it was historically administered)
  2. Examine the remedy that the plaintiff is seeking
     - This second part of the test is more important than the first.

**Analysis:**

- **With respect to assessing the cause of action (1st factor of 2-factor test):**
  - Figure out the structure of the cause of action that the plaintiff is asserting
  - See whether it most resembles structurally, a cause of action that was either on the law side or the equity side of the divide.

- **With respect to assessing the remedy (2d factor of 2-factor test):**
  - We look to the remedy and compare it to the set of remedies:

| **Law** | **Equity** |
|---|---|
| compensatory damages | injunction |
| punitive damages | disgorgement of ill-gotten gains |
| ejectment | restitution |
| mandamus | constructive trust |
| | enforcement of a trust |
| | contract rescission |
| | contract reformation |
| | unjust enrichment |
| | quantum meruit |

  - The second factor is more important, and therefore weighed more heavily than the first.

- **If determined to be a "law" cause of action, then a right to a jury trial would be attached.**
- **If determined to be an "equity" cause of action, then no jury trial would attach.**

<!-- page: 8 -->

### 2) Newly Created Form of Decision Making (Adjudicators)

When Congress has created the power to adjudicate a cause of action in a different, specialized decision maker — not a federal court judge, but instead an administrative law judge before an agency:

- When this happens, we first need to go through the ***Chauffeurs v. Terry*** test above to assess if the cause of action being asserted is one that has a jury trial right attached to it at all.
  - If there **is** a jury trial right attached to it, then we ask whether Congress nevertheless and despite the attachment, can lodge decision making power in an agency, or does the case fall within the **Public Rights Exception.**

#### Public Rights Exception (2 components):

1. Whether the government is involved at all in the case as a party; and
2. Is the government involved as a regulator attempting to enforce some sort of regulatory component, and **not** as a purely economic actor.

If both of these (1 and 2 above) are true, then the public rights exception probably applies and there is no right to a jury trial. If one or both are not true, then there may be a right to a jury trial.

- Primary case for this concept was ***Atlas Roofing***, in which OSHA promulgated regulations governing workplace safety, which allegedly a company was violating. OSHA imposed fines upon the company and the company demanded a jury trial. SCOTUS said that this case fell under the *public rights exception* because the government was involved as a regulator enforcing workplace safety rules.

The reason why we go through the *Terry* analysis first even when we are in situation 2 (a newly created decision maker) is simply because the cause of action being asserted before the "newly created adjudicator" may be one that already did not carry a right to a jury trial historically, in which case we could reject the demand to a jury trial on that basis alone, without having to reach the public rights exception analysis.

<!-- page: 9 -->

In *Terry*, the court rejected an analogy that was very similar to the cause of action, and in practice, it may be difficult to know what the court will determine. One should certainly keep that in mind, but courts do not like it at all when someone points out that they did something different than what they said. As such, one can say that the court in *Terry* looked at issues and not just at causes of action.

> ***Exam tip:*** In an exam or in an assignment, it might help to say: "If the court determines the newly created cause of action analogous to 'x,' then the court will decide 'y'. If the court rejects the analogy and finds the cause of action more analogous to 'a,' instead, then the court will decide 'b.'"

Today we will continue our discussion of the distribution of power between the judge and the jury, and also discuss along the way what happens when a case is tried to the court, meaning when the judge is the decision maker (FRCP 52).

Now that we have finished understanding the historical distinction, we need to go over the law's assumptions regarding what judges and juries are each good at, as if we were trying to distribute the power in accordance to their respective areas of function, to tell us based on functional considerations, who would be the better decision maker. We also need to take these differences into account when trying to understand the legal rules built around judges and juries, particularly FRCP 50.

After discussing our primary case as our source for functional considerations (*Markman*), we will discuss things like jury instructions, findings of facts and conclusions of law, motions for new trial, motions for judgement notwithstanding the verdict, and motions for leave notwithstanding judgement (essentially, motions to reopen cases).

## Law v. Fact

### Functional Considerations of Adjudicators (Judges v. Juries)

**Judges are better at:**

- Construing or interpreting instruments (*Markman*)
- Applying (and interpreting) laws
  - Note that judges are not required to have graduated law school or be lawyers (particularly in small claims courts). But, the theory says that even though some of them are not lawyers and have not gone to law school, they deal with law every day, so they are more competent at understanding the law.
- Applying precedent to new fact patterns
- Understanding statutes
- Overseeing long-running disputes (which sometimes can go on for decades)

<!-- page: 10 -->

**Juries are better at:**

- Resolving disputes of fact, particularly deception detection
- Sense of what "due care" is generally accepted and expected within the community
  - Example 1: what is considered negligent conduct
  - Example 2: what is considered to be obscene in the community
  - Although judges may be from the community, there may be benefits to having a jury composed of varying experiences by having more than one person available, thus getting a better sense through a more representative body.

### *Markman v. Westview Instruments, Inc.*, 517 U.S. 370 (SCOTUS) (1996)

> The court, because of its training in complex issues and knowledge of written documents, is more competent to correctly, fairly, and uniformly make decisions relating to the interpretation of a term of art.

**Facts:**

- Markman (plaintiff) sued Westview (defendant) for infringing its patent to an inventory control system for use by dry cleaners.
- A key issue was the construction of the claim term "inventory."
- After a jury verdict, Westview moved for judgment as a matter of law (JMOL).
- The district court held that claim construction is a matter of law for the court and found non-infringement.
- Markman appealed to the court of appeals for the federal circuit, which upheld the district court decision en banc.
- Markman petitioned for a writ of certiorari, which was granted.

**Holding and Reasoning (Souter, J.):**

- In a patent case, **the interpretation of a term of art is a question reserved for the judge**, and **will not be subject to the Seventh Amendment guarantee to a jury trial**.
- The Seventh Amendment states that in suits at common law, where the value in controversy shall exceed $20, the right of trial by jury shall be preserved.
- The wording of the amendment makes it clear that **in a patent infringement case, the parties are entitled to a jury trial if either demands one**.
- However, **in technical cases, additional questions often arise in relation to the jury trial that might not be best resolved by the jury.**

<!-- page: 11 -->

- These **issues require difficult application of law to fact that the court is in the best position to make.**
- Here, the **issue is regarding the construction of written instruments**.
- The **court, because of its training in complex issues and knowledge of written documents, is more competent to correctly, fairly, and uniformly make decisions relating to the interpretation of a term of art**.
- The judge, not the jury, should decide the interpretation of the term "inventory."
- The holding below is affirmed.

*Markman* involved the issue of understanding and interpreting a written instrument. The court mentions that there will likely be few cases which involve a dispute where an expert is saying that a written document or instrument means something and another expert saying the same document or instrument means something else. Usually they (the two experts) will both agree on what the written document or instrument is and means. In this case then, the court was making a sheer determination of credibility.

The instrument at issue in the case is an application for a patent which had been filed by one company which created an invention and wanted to patent their invention. Another company sued saying that the company's invention and application for patent was too similar to the invention they already had a patent for, and that their patent was broad enough to cover the second company's invention.

- Issue is how **broad was the meaning of the term** included in the first company's patent.
- One party makes the jury trial demand, and the remedy being requested was damages.
  - It is clear then that when looking at the law v. equity divide, we are on the law side, and there will be a jury.
  - The issue here therefore is **not** whether or not there will be a jury.
  - Instead, the issue is **whether or not this particular question will go to the jury for interpretation determination** (Law/Fact distinction). The question is who gets to decide whether the patent is broad enough to cover the type of machine that the defendant in the case wants to sell and market.
  - Court has to decide whether this is an issue of law (more like applying and interpreting a statute), or whether this is an issue of fact (more like a dispute in which there needs to be some deception detection and determination as to which party is telling the truth).
    - If legal issue → judge will resolve it
    - If on the fact side → jury will resolve it

SCOTUS says this is a legal issue because it says that judges are "functionally better" at construing instruments and writings:

- They write opinions
- They create precedent
- They can therefore remain more consistent
  - Juries may have differing and inconsistent standards of interpretation
  - We want consistency with respect to patents because it can affect investments
- They have education and experience in construing writings, whereas the juries may not as much

<!-- page: 12 -->

So we discussed through this case and in class, the functional areas in which judges or juries are respectively better at according to the legal system, and in doing so, we have discussed the **doctrinal distinction of law vs. fact**.

### BLL: Law v. Fact Distinction

*(Determined functionally, as well as by using historical precedent)*

**Analysis:**

1. **Determine whether the issue is one of law or equity.**
   - If issue is one of **fact** (in the law v. equity divide), then a jury (if available) will decide.
     - Judges do adjudicate issues of fact if the issue is on the **equity** side of the law v. equity divide.
   - If issue is one of **law** (in the law v. equity divide), then proceed to step 2.

2. **Decide whether the issue is one of law or one of fact** (in the law v. fact distinction doctrine):
   - If the issue is one of **law** (in the law v. fact distinction) → the judge will decide.
   - If the issue is one of **fact** (in the law v. fact distinction) → the jury will decide.

Notice that we are using the word "law" in a couple of different ways that seem inconsistent:

**In the Law v. Equity divide:**
- Law = Jury.
- Equity = Judge.

**In the Law v. Fact divide:**
- Law = Judge.
- Fact = Jury.

In some limited set of cases, we resolve whether an issue is on the law or fact side by looking at several sources of information:

- **Functional distinctions** between a judge or a jury
- **Historical precedent** in making the decision on whether the issue is on the law side of the law v. equity divide
- Congressional explanations

<!-- page: 13 -->

Most of the time these will be clear, such as when they involve interpreting a statute, or applying prior case law — we know that it must be a legal issue. But, when it involves determining whether one of the parties is telling the truth, the jury will decide that.

In this case, construing patents was not resolved by the time *Markman* was adjudicated, so the SCOTUS treated it like a new situation.

This is a bit unusual because this means that judges will need to swear in witnesses to find out what something means, especially in this case, where they needed witnesses to find out what something means so that they can construe their legal interpretation.

Another way that we distribute power between juries and judges is when we think we are ready to have a jury consider an issue.

## Jury Instructions (FRCP 51)

After there has been a trial and **all** the evidence has been introduced, the jury will go and consider the issue, but before that, they will be given "**jury instructions**."

For purposes of understanding the jury instructions, you will need to anticipate some appellate litigation and know the four standards of review in when there is an appeal, because what happens with the jury instructions is affected by these "standards of review."

Usually an appeal happens when the trial is almost over and allegation is that the trial judge has made some error or mistake in his or her ruling(s). Ordinarily, this will happen when the trial court has issued a final judgment.

- There are rare circumstances in which the federal court system, at least, there will be interlocutory appeals (appeals in the middle of the trial during litigation), but ordinarily the appeals will be done after the final judgement has been entered by the trial court judge.

These standards of review govern "levels of deference" that the appeal court will give to the trial court in its process or ruling during the appeal.

- Part of the analysis will need to include what kind of issues are better adjudicated by the trial judge versus the appeal judge.

<!-- page: 14 -->

- The appeal court basically asks themselves whether they are in the same position as the trial court to assess the legal question or whether the trial court was in a better position to adjudicate it, even if they don't necessarily agree with the ultimate outcome as decided by the trial court. In the latter case, they will only get involved and take the appeal if something was horrendously wrong.

### Standards of Review Chart

| **Standard of Review** | **Level of Deference Given to Trial Court** | **Types of Issues** | **Examples of Issues/Decisions** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **De Novo** | No deference whatsoever | Issues of law (in law v. fact divide) | 1. Statutory interpretation; 2. Review precedent; 3. Apply the law; 4. Interpreting case law, etc. |
| **Clearly Erroneous** | Some deference | Issues of fact (in law v. fact divide) | 1. When judge has sat as the trier of fact and has issued findings of fact and conclusions of law |
| **Abuse of Discretion** | Deference (but not utter deference) | Procedural issues (motions, etc.) | 1. Whether or not to give jury instructions; 2. Evidentiary rulings |
| **Plain Error** | Enormous amount of deference | Extremely obvious and important (even though no one requested or objected, appellate court needs to fix it) | 1. Whether or not the trial court had subject matter jurisdiction to begin with |

### 1) De Novo

*(Latin word for "starting over")*

- **Issues of "Law"** (as opposed to issues of fact)
- No deference whatsoever given to the trial court.
  - The appellate court will look at everything (all the evidence) all over again, and give its own ruling.
  - Treat any writing by the trial judge, if available, as a legal brief.
    - If writing by the trial judge is persuasive, they might use some of the reasoning; if not, they will disregard.

<!-- page: 15 -->

The next two standards, "Clearly Erroneous" and "Abuse of Discretion," seem very similar, and have not been distinguished well by anyone to date. They both mean that the appellate court gives "some" deference to the trial court's ruling, even if the appellate court would have done things differently. It will nevertheless affirm what the trial judge did, either because they feel the trial judge is in a better position to decide that specific issue of fact, or because it is an issue that they do not want to continue litigating. Ordinarily this will be a type of issue that the appellate court feels is not significant enough to keep litigating over, and prefers a quick decision in this case, rather than a consistently correct decision.

### 2) Clearly Erroneous

- **Issues of Fact**
- A ruling is clearly erroneous if the appellate court is left with a definite and firm impression that a legal mistake has been made by the trial judge.
- It is not a discretionary thing that the judge could exercise his discretion on.
  - When the judge has sat as the trier of fact and there was an issue with that now being appealed:
    - For example, when the issue is on the law side of the law v. equity divide and there is a jury trial, the judge sometimes has to make decisions on factual issues.
      - If there is for instance a dispute as to whether or not there is subject matter jurisdiction, we might have a question about where someone is domiciled for diversity purposes; the trial judge (and not the jury) would decide that issue and hear testimony — and the judge will make his findings of fact and conclusions of law, and those findings of fact themselves will involve factual issues, and will be subject to this **Clearly Erroneous** standard of review.
        - For example, if there are two witnesses, one says the light is red and the other that the light is green, and the judge arbitrarily decides that the one that said the light was green was telling the truth — the appellate court will almost certainly not reverse that because the judge was present and the appellate court will feel like since the trial judge saw the witness testify, they will only review that issue if there is a **clear error** in the process. (But will not necessarily reverse the ruling, even if they themselves would have reached a different outcome.)
- Some deference to what the trial judge did.
  - Maybe a type of issue that they generally do not want in the appeal courts.
  - Maybe an issue that is not significant enough, and a quick decision is needed.

<!-- page: 16 -->

### 3) Abuse of Discretion

- **Decision by the Judge on a Motion**
- Basically the court system wants the trial judge to control the way the litigation proceeds. Therefore, the judges will need to make a lot of decisions:
  - Scheduling on dockets
  - Whether or not to give jury instructions
  - Discovery disputes
  - Scope of discovery
  - Evidentiary rulings (whether a piece of evidence should be allowed or not into the case for consideration)
  - When Summary Judgement Motions will be granted
  - What order do we hear cases or issues in
  - Examples of issues include many of the rules for civil procedure
- Deference but not utter deference given to the trial court.
- Presumption is that the issue was the trial judge's call but appellant feels the trial judge made the wrong call.

In trials, we know that parties fight. Since we have an adversarial system and we know that parties fight, and we try to find out what side is right, there are several things that should be required of opposing parties to do when going to trial:

We require parties to put evidence forward that is in favor of their side and try to persuade the judges that they are each correct. Therefore, generally, we require parties to do the following in a trial court:

- We want a party to ask for what they want (i.e., enter a motion).
- If the opposing party does not like it, we want them to say so (object to it).
- If the party who did not like the request fails to object to something that the trial court did, or failed to ask for something they wanted that the trial court did not end up doing, the appellate court will be reluctant to hear that issue and will show that reluctance in one of two ways:
  - It will refuse to hear the issue (*Unitherm*, FRCP 59); or
  - The court will review it under the **plain error** standard.

In *Unitherm*, a party wanted a new trial, never filed a motion for new trial in trial court, and then appealed saying that they wanted a new trial. SCOTUS said no, because they failed to ask for one during the trial court.

<!-- page: 17 -->

### 4) Plain Error

- **Decision by the Judge**
- This standard still gives an enormous amount of deference to the trial judge.
- Even though there was no objection, and even though there was no request by the parties, the trial court should have done it anyway.

This is different from the **Clearly Erroneous** standard because in the Clearly Erroneous standard of review, they will give the trial some deference. They basically say that they will reverse a judge's findings of fact only if they are left with a definite and firm impression that a mistake was made.

In the **Plain Error** standard of review, they refer to a type of error that is so screamingly obvious, and so important, that even though there was no objection made to it by the parties, the appellate court still needs to fix it. They will be extremely reluctant to do this, but if they do end up doing it, they will still give the trial judge an enormous amount of deference.

---

### Back to Jury Instructions: Application of the Standards

**Hypo:**

- We have had a trial and a bunch of evidence was introduced.
- Evidence is now done and closed out.
- Ordinarily at this point the court will ask the parties to submit jury instructions that they each want the judge to read to the jury.
- Lawyers for each party will propose some jury instructions:
  - Each lawyer will write them as favorable as possible to his side, but not so favorable that the judge would reject them for being too slanted.
- Court will typically have some model jury instructions that are circuit-specific created by the appeal courts.
- **Once the judge compiles all the specific requests along with the model instructions, the court will give the parties the opportunity to object to any instructions they deem necessary.**
  - If a party does not object to a particular jury instruction, and that jury instruction is read to the jury, and the jury finds against that party, and the judge enters judgement on the jury verdict, and then that party appeals based on the jury instruction on the grounds that the jury instruction was wrong on the law:
    - **What kind of standard of review would this situation get? Plain Error.**

**Jury instructions normally fall under the Abuse of Discretion standard of review, UNLESS a party did not object after the judge reads back the instructions to the parties prior to reading them to the jury.** In this case, if the parties do not object when they should have, and the judge reads the instructions to the jury, then it would be reviewed under the **Plain Error** standard.

<!-- page: 18 -->

If, in the same hypo, the jury instructions that a party wanted given to the jury were not given to the jury by the court, and were not requested by the party:

- **What standard of review would that be given? Plain Error.**

However, if they requested the instruction and the court did not give it to the jury, and the party objected to the court's omission, then it would be reviewed under **Abuse of Discretion**.

---

### *Kennedy v. Southern California Edison Co.*, 219 F.3d 988 (9th Cir. 2000)

> A district court has an obligation to correctly instruct the jury even after being presented with an instruction that is improper, but still directs the jury's attention to an important issue.

**Facts:**

- Kennedy (plaintiff) brought a wrongful death suit against Southern California Edison Co. (SCEC) (defendant), seeking to recover for the death of his wife.
- Kennedy claimed that her death was due to cancer caused by nuclear radiation from nuclear rods that SCEC manufactured.
- In such cases, the **proper jury instructions must state that a plaintiff must show that exposure to radiation from the defendant's product in reasonable medical probability was a substantial factor contributing to the decedent's risk of developing cancer.**
- However, the jury instructions that Kennedy proposed and that the court adopted stated that **Kennedy must show that exposure to radiation from SCEC's product in reasonable medical probability contributed** to his wife's risk of developing cancer.
- The jury found in favor of SCEC. Kennedy appealed based on the jury instructions.

**Holding and Reasoning (Hawkins, J.):**

- A **district court has an obligation to correctly instruct the jury even after being presented with an improper instruction that nonetheless directs the jury's attention to an important issue.**
- Here, Kennedy's jury instructions that the court adopted stated that radiation from the nuclear rods only had to have "contributed to" to his wife's risk of developing cancer. (*imposes a somewhat low burden of persuasion*)

<!-- page: 19 -->

- The **proper jury instructions should have stated (by law) that the nuclear rods were "a substantial factor in contributing" to the risk of cancer.** (*imposes a higher burden of persuasion, and was what the law said at the time*)
- This is a small, but consequential difference, altering Kennedy's burden of proof.
- Although the jury instructions that Kennedy proposed and the court adopted raised an important issue of law, the court was not free to accept the instructions because there is clear law outlining the specific jury instructions that are on point in this type of case.
- An error in jury instructions that relates to the parties' burdens of proof is ordinarily not harmless error.
- Therefore, the judgment was reversed, and the case is remanded for a new trial.

The judge's duty, in a situation when the party gives an instruction on an important issue that is close to right but not fully correct, is to correct the instruction as determined by the law and disregard the party's incorrect instruction proposal.

In *Kennedy* the instruction given was not legally correct. It was on an important issue close to legally correct, so the court had a duty to correct the instruction prior to giving it.

**BLL:** A judge therefore is under no obligation to read a jury instruction that is erroneous. But, the court says that if the issue is important, and the instruction given by the requesting party is close to being right, the judge is supposed to correct it, and give the instruction.

When the legal instruction is confusing, a party may want to rewrite it so that it is less confusing and more simplified, but judges get nervous when reading those because they feel like something might get missed that would cause the appellate court to reverse the judgement later at some point, so they really hesitate to allow simplified instructions to be read. However, with the assistance of lawyers, the court's job is to find out what the applicable law is for each specific jury instruction, so there really is no need for them to come up with instructions on their own.

## Harmless Error

Appeal court will not necessarily reverse every judgement in which a trial court made certain mistakes.

**Harmless error** — the appellate court, in any mistake or appeal, will look to see if there is error:

- Will need to apply the correct standard of review to determine whether there is error or not.
  - After applying the right standard of review, is the mistake harmless?
    - If yes, no change to the judgement or ruling.
      - Maybe dicta, but would not reverse the judgment.
    - If not harmless, they will overturn the judgment.

The appellate courts will not automatically reverse any time that a trial court has reached a judgement at the end of the trial, even if a party has persuaded the appellate court, under whatever is the applicable standard of review, that the trial court made a mistake, due to the **Harmless Error** doctrine.

<!-- page: 20 -->

### Harmless Error: Analysis

Appellate courts analyze the situation:

1. They will apply the standard of review appropriate for the issue.
2. Look at the alleged error under that applied standard of review, and:
   - If there is a mistake found after applying the appropriate standard of review:
     - Appellate court will admit that there was a mistake found, but will further ask whether it was "harmless," basically asking whether the error really affected the judgement below, or whether it was something that would not have made a difference.
       - If the error found was "harmless," they might issue dicta to explain their reasoning but would not reverse the judgment.
       - If the error was **not** "harmless," they would reverse the judgment.
   - If there was no mistake found after applying the appropriate standard of review, then court will affirm the lower court's judgement.

Appellate judges use this doctrine to avoid reversing judgments and effectively kill appeals. Appellate judges like declaring that errors are harmless so as to not force trial court judges to retry cases. This can happen on both procedural or substantive issues, and regardless of what the issue is. All is subject to a declaration of "harmless error" at the appellate courts.

The appellate courts only use this doctrine after declaring that there was error in the first place. However, there are instances that the court might reach the conclusion that if there was an error, it would be harmless first, before declaring that there was actually an error in a specific case.

## Findings of Fact and Conclusions of Law (FRCP 52)

- What happens if there is an issue in the **equity** side of the **law v. equity divide**, and the **judge is sitting as the trier of fact**?
- Or, if this is a preliminary fact, such as where one of the parties is domiciled, so that the judge could figure out whether the court has subject matter jurisdiction?
- Or, maybe there are facts that are being tried in the context of a preliminary injunction motion?

We generally require the judge, under **FRCP 52**, to either orally or in writing, give us the findings of fact and conclusions of law. They are allowed to give them orally, although many times they give them in writing. Many times, at the end of the hearing, the judge will ask the parties to propose the writings of fact and conclusions of law, and the judge will simply synthesize them and make any edits.

<!-- page: 21 -->

The three purposes served by requiring the parties to propose their own findings of fact and conclusions of law for the judge are:

1. To improve the quality of the reasoning in their adjudication and decision making.
2. Helps appellate court understand what is happening and facilitates good review proceedings.
3. Provides exactly what issues were adjudicated for purposes of clean application of preclusion in appellate review.

**FRCP 52** however, only applies to judges. We do not give juries the requirement to give us findings of fact because we do not necessarily want to find out their reasoning behind their verdicts — if we do so, it could throw off the entire balance of our justice system, as many times we would want to disregard what they came up with if we disagreed with the reasoning behind it.

So generally, when we give things to juries it's because we are willing to have inconsistencies. If a rational jury (trier of fact) could have only found in one direction, we would have granted Summary Judgement earlier in the process, so therefore, when we give juries some case to decide, it is really because it could be decided either way. We are willing to tolerate some inconsistencies when we give things to juries, but we are basically terrified to find out their reasonings behind their judgements, so that is why we do not force them to state their findings of fact or conclusions of law.

Announcing the findings of fact and conclusions of law therefore generally are supposed to help to apply the standards of preclusion later on in the appeal process.

When the judge simply adopts the findings of facts and conclusions of law as proposed by one of the parties, without editing and stating their own, the appellate court will defer to the trial court's findings of fact and conclusions of law.

### *Lansford-Coaldale Joint Water Authority v. Tonolli Corp.*, 4 F.3d 1209 (3d Cir. 1993)

> Findings of fact and conclusions of law prepared by a party and adopted by the judge adequately serve the purposes of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 52(a).

**Facts:**

- Lansford-Coaldale Joint Water Authority (the authority) (plaintiff) provided public water.
- Tonolli Corp. (defendant) owned a smelting site near the authority's groundwater production and supply wells.
- The authority brought suit against Tonolli, claiming that hazardous substances from Tonolli's facility had contaminated its wells.
- At the conclusion of the trial, the district court made oral findings of fact and conclusions of law denying the authority recovery on all claims.

<!-- page: 22 -->

- The court directed counsel for Tonolli to prepare and submit findings of fact, conclusions of law, and a form of judgment.
- The judge's order did not include an opinion setting out the facts and legal conclusions that led him to his decision.
- The authority appealed, contending that the court's factual findings did not satisfy the purposes of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 52(A).
- The authority complained that the court made its findings within a few hours after the trial ended, instead of waiting for a transcript and taking the time to write an opinion.
- The authority also objected to the court's having requested proposed findings from the parties only before, and not after, trial.
- Additionally, the authority argued that the court's findings were not entitled to deference because most of the findings were adopted verbatim from Tonolli's proposed findings of fact.
- The case was heard by the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit.

**Issue:**

- Do findings and conclusions prepared by a party and adopted by the judge adequately serve the purposes of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 52(a)?

**Holding:**

- Yes. Findings and conclusions **prepared by a party and adopted by the judge adequately serve the purposes of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 52(a)**.
- However, such findings and conclusions are **likely to be looked at by appellate courts more narrowly and given less weight on review if they are adopted verbatim by the judge**.
- Rule 52(a) states that a trial court must state its findings of fact and conclusions of law separately.
- Rule 52(a) expressly authorizes oral findings.
- The Supreme Court has held that a trial court's findings should receive no less deferential review when the court announces its decision to the parties first and then asks the prevailing party to prepare findings of fact.
- **The Court also stated that even when the trial judge adopts proposed findings verbatim, the findings are those of the court and may be reversed only if clearly erroneous.**
- In this case, by issuing oral findings on the day the trial ended, the court served the interests of the parties and the legal system in speedy adjudication.
- Additionally, the trial court's findings here are not clearly erroneous.
- There is no indication that the court was unfamiliar with the testimony and exhibits, or that it was using Tonolli's proposed findings as a crutch.
- The record suggests that the trial court's findings reflected its informed and considered views.
- However, judges should be aware that findings and conclusions adopted verbatim from parties will be looked at more narrowly than if they are the work product of the judge himself, or at least bear evidence that he has given them careful study and revision.
- Thus, the trial court's conclusion that the authority did not sufficiently demonstrate that Tonolli's site posed any threat of contamination to the authority's water supply is upheld.

## Judgement as a Matter of Law in Jury Trial; Related Motion for a New Trial; Conditional Ruling (FRCP 50)

Now that we know what happens when the judge decides the facts, we want to go back and find out what happens when the jury decides the facts.

**Hypo:**

- We have had a Motion to Dismiss.
- Some claims have survived:
  - We have had discovery on the claims that have survived.
  - We have had Summary Judgement on the claims that have survived.
    - Remember that Summary Judgement was based on the "record" that the parties had compiled at discovery:
      - Statements by the parties
      - Depositions
      - Affidavits, etc.
  - We have denied the Summary Judgements, so we are going to trial.
  - Posture: We are on the **law** side of the **law v. equity** divide (so jury trial).
  - We are having issues of **fact** on the **law v. fact divide** (so jury decides).
  - Jury hears testimony.

Now that we are having a live jury trial, the record now will be made of the trial documents, rather than the discovery record, and the previous discovery record will no longer be relevant, as witnesses might have testified differently, and some documents may not have been entered into evidence, etc. We will now have a "**trial record:**"

- Live Witness testimony
- Evidence
- Physical exhibits
- Documents

We will have the jury issue a **verdict** on the **trial record** after the instructions are read:

- Who should win?
  - If Plaintiff wins, then how much money should the Plaintiff get?

The question now is whether the judge has to listen to the jury and enter judgement on that verdict, and it turns out that the **judge, as a matter of law, does not always have to do what the jury renders in its verdict.**

<!-- page: 24 -->

By operation of law, when the jury renders a verdict, that does not decide the case. There are different types of verdicts:

- **Special verdict**: jury verdict that makes specified findings of fact but does not apply the law or make a final determination as to liability or guilt.
- **General verdict**: the most common form of verdict. It is a comprehensive decision on an issue. In civil cases the jury makes a decision in favor of the plaintiff or the defendant, determining liability and the amount of money damages.

After receiving whatever type of verdict from the jury, the judge will need to decide whether or not to enter judgment on that verdict. Instead we give the power to the judge to upset, review and revise the jury's verdict. He can either:

1. Order a new trial (FRCP 59); or
2. Enter a judgement "notwithstanding" the Jury's verdict (officially called in the rules, **Judgement as a Matter of Law "JMOL"**) (FRCP 50).

---

## Judgments as a Matter of Law (FRCP Rule 50(a))

We will first discuss when a judge is allowed to enter a Judgment as a Matter of Law, despite the jury's verdict.

- The judge must find that **a reasonable jury would not have a legally sufficient evidentiary basis to find for the party on the specific issue** in order to enter a Judgement as a Matter of Law.

<!-- page: 25 -->

### *Aetna Casualty & Surety Co. v. Yeatts*, 122 F.2d 350 (4th Cir. 1941)

> If there is substantial evidence to support a party's case, the judge cannot direct a verdict against them.

If a reasonable jury, construing the trial record evidence in light most favorable to the nonmoving party, could reasonably find for the party not moving for the Judgement as a Matter of Law, then **the judge should not** issue a Judgement as a Matter of Law notwithstanding the jury's verdict.

### JMOL Analysis

When there has been a verdict issued by the jury, and one party says: "despite the fact that the jury has issued its verdict saying that the other party should win, and I think I should win" (filed a motion for a Judgement Notwithstanding the Jury's Verdict):

1. Judge should consider the facts disclosed in trial (in the trial record) in the **light most favorable to the non-moving party**.
2. If a reasonable trier-of-fact (jury) could have found in the same way as this jury did, then the court should **deny** the motion.
   - If the court should find that no reasonable jury could have found in the same way this one did, then the judge would **grant** the Judgement as a Matter of Law, despite the jury's verdict.

Functionally, if a party has been fully heard on an issue and has introduced all its evidence on some issue, the court does not need to wait until after the jury considers the issue and issues its verdict in order to grant a Judgement as a Matter of Law, so long as the court goes through the analysis above and finds, reasonably construing the evidence in the light most favorable to the non-moving party, that no rational jury could have found in the same way this one did.

Sometimes the Judge might want to issue a Judgement as a matter of law in the middle of a trial, after a party has been fully heard on an issue, if it knows that, after construing the evidence in light most favorable to the nonmoving party, no rational jury could find in favor of the nonmoving party. Sometimes however, the judge will want to wait and see what the jury says, even though the judge knows (hypothetically) that if the defendant moves for JMOL, and the jury finds in favor of the Plaintiff, that no rational jury could have reasonably found in favor of the plaintiff, and thus he/she will have to grant the motion for JMOL — in order to see whether the jury decided in the same way as the judge would so as to avoid a reversal later on, during the appeal, if any.

For example, if a judge enters judgement as a matter of law granting the hypothetical defendant's motion, and the plaintiff later appeals, on the grounds that the jury had decided in the plaintiff's favor, and nonetheless the JMOL was granted, and the appeals court agrees with the Plaintiff, the appeal court could just remand and force the trial judge to enter judgement in accordance with the jury's verdict. This way, by waiting for the jury to issue its verdict anyways, it can save time later on by not having to hold the second trial.

<!-- page: 26 -->

When a trial judge is deciding whether to grant a motion of Judgement as a Matter of Law:

**Analysis:**

1. Construes the evidence reasonably in the light most favorable to the nonmoving party.
2. Asks: could a rational jury find in favor of the non-moving party?
   - If yes, then deny motion for Judgement as a Matter of Law (regardless of when it was made).
   - If no, then judge may or may not want to grant the motion for Judgement as a Matter of Law.
     - If motion for JMOL was made in the middle of trial, before the judge issued its verdict on that issue, the judge must further analyze:
       - If grant the motion for JMOL now, trial might still continue on other issues, and resources would be wasted.
       - Or the judge might have sufficient doubt and might be worried that an appellate court could issue a reversal from the beginning.
         - There would be a brand new jury at that point, which would need to hear all of the evidence that the current sitting jury would have already heard, and so then all current resources would be wasted if everything needs to be heard again.

The Judge in this case might reserve the ruling on the party's motion for JMOL (if in the middle of the trial), but will tell the party that he/she can renew the motion after the jury issues its verdict **(FRCP 50(b))**. It's up to that party to choose to renew the motion once the jury has issued its verdict on that issue.

### FRCP 50(b): Renewing the Motion After Trial

**FRCP 50(a)** allows a party to make a motion for a Judgment as a Matter of Law, during the trial, after the opposing party has been fully heard with respect to the issue and has entered all its evidence on that issue.

- A court could grant the motion for JMOL right then and risk the chance of having its ruling reversed on appeal, or,
- If the court does not grant the motion for JMOL, according to the rule, it does not necessarily deny it. Instead, it merely **defers** it until after the jury issues its verdict.

However, in order to put this issue in front of the judge again, the party will need to **renew** the motion.

**If the party who would want to move for JMOL does not motion for JMOL before the jury issues its verdict, the party forfeits the opportunity to move for JMOL after the jury issues its verdict.** We require the party to file twice for two reasons:

1. If there was no requirement to file a motion for JMOL prior to the jury's issuance of the verdict, all the parties who lose their case would file for JMOL after the jury issues the verdict finding in favor of the opposing party; and
2. It gives the judge the opportunity to use his discretion and go back to the nonmoving party and ask whether there is any more evidence that would weigh in their favor (puts the party and the judge on notice that this might happen).

<!-- page: 27 -->

Therefore, if a motion for Judgement as a Matter of Law was submitted by a party after the jury issued its verdict (for the first time) and was **not filed before the jury issued its verdict, that motion for Judgement as a Matter of Law should be denied**, without considering whether a reasonable jury could find in favor of the nonmoving party.

In the event that the party has previously requested and filed a motion for JMOL prior to the jury issuing its verdict, and after the jury issues the verdict, the party renews its motion for JMOL, then the standard we apply is the same standard and analysis as above:

1. Judge should consider the facts disclosed in trial (in the trial record) in the light most favorable to the non-moving party.
2. If a reasonable trier-of-fact (jury) could have found in the same way as this jury did, then the court should deny the motion.
   - If the court should find that no reasonable jury could have found in the same way this one did, then the judge would grant the Judgement as a Matter of Law, despite the jury's verdict.

This is very much like a Summary Judgement. In fact, there have even been cases that declare that the FRCP 50 (Judgement as a Matter of Law) and FRCP 56 (Summary Judgement) standards are the same thing (*Reed*). There are two main differences:

<!-- page: 28 -->

1. Summary Judgement (FRCP 56) is done after discovery, using the discovery "record," whereas Judgement as a Matter of Law (FRCP 50) is done after the party has been heard on all its evidence for a certain issue in trial, using the trial record; and
2. JMOL does not include any burden shifting in its analysis, unlike the Summary Judgement analysis.

## Motion for New Trial (FRCP 59)

Now, suppose the court says that the evidence is such that a court could not grant a motion for a Judgement as a Matter of Law. In other words, that it is possible for a jury, construing the evidence in the light most favorable to the nonmoving party, to find in the way this one did. Despite this, **the judge can nevertheless grant a new trial (FRCP 59)** on the grounds that a reasonable jury could find for the party who prevailed. There are three different grounds in which this could be granted which will be discussed later, but one of them is when the verdict issued by the jury is **against the great weight of the evidence**.

**BLL:** If a party may move for a New Trial after a jury verdict, and say that the verdict was against the great weight of the evidence, and the court finds that the verdict was in fact against the great weight of the evidence, it can grant a ruling for New Trial.

This does not make any sense, because if the court finds that no rational jury could have found in the way this one did, when reasonably construing the evidence in the light most favorable to the nonmoving party, the court could grant Judgement as a Matter of Law.

- By refusing to grant the motion for Judgement as a Matter of Law, the court has found that this jury's verdict was in fact reasonable.
  - BUT, although it was reasonable, it was against the "great weight of the evidence."
  - **This is very confusing.**

The law thinks that there are some class of cases, as to which it would be reasonable for the jury to find the way it did, but nevertheless that the verdict is against the great weight of the evidence, and therefore, the court will do a new trial and do everything all over again with a new jury, even if it turns out later that the new jury hears the same exact evidence.
