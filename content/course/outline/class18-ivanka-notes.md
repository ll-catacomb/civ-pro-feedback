## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***Aetna Casualty & Surety Co. v. Yeatts*, 122 F.2d 350 (4th Cir. 1941)**
  The granting or refusing of a new trial is within the discretion of the trial judge and his action is not reviewable upon appeal, except for in the most exceptional circumstances.

- ***Fisch v. Manger*, 130 A.2d 815 (N.J. S. Ct., 1957)**
  A court may condition the granting of a new trial on the defendant agreeing or not agreeing to an **additur** (the power of a court to increase an excessively low damage award, reserved for cases in which the jury is influenced by prejudice or mistake).

- ***Powers v. Allstate Insurance Co.*, 102 N.W.2d 393 (1960)**
  A court may condition the granting of a new trial on the plaintiff agreeing or not agreeing to a **remittitur** (a reduction, or proposed reduction, of a damages award).

- ***Unitherm Food Systems v. Swift-Eckrich*, 546 U.S. 394 (2006)**
  Under **Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 50**, a court of appeals may not review a verdict for sufficiency of the evidence unless the party moved the district court for postverdict relief.

- ***Liberty Mutual Insurance Co. v. Wetzel*, 424 U.S. 737 (SCOTUS)(1976)**
  A finding of liability that does not finally dispose of the plaintiff's prayers for relief is not appealable to the court of appeals. (In other words, in order to be able to file an appeal, there must be a final order (judgment) from the trial court.)

### Federal Rules of Civil Procedure

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 50 – Judgment as a Matter of Law in Jury Trial; Related Motion for a New Trial; Conditional Ruling

**(a) Judgment as a Matter of Law.**

> (1) In General. If a party has been fully heard on an issue during a jury trial and the court finds that a reasonable jury would not have a legally sufficient evidentiary basis to find for the party on that issue, the court may:
>
> > (A) resolve the issue against the party; and
> >
> > (B) grant a motion for judgment as a matter of law against the party on a claim or defense that, under the controlling law, can be maintained or defeated only with a favorable finding on that issue.

<!-- page: 3 -->

> (2) Motion. A motion for judgment as a matter of law may be made at any time before the case is submitted to the jury. The motion must specify the judgment sought and the law and facts that entitle the movant to the judgment.

**(b) Renewing the Motion After Trial; Alternative Motion for a New Trial.** If the court does not grant a motion for judgment as a matter of law made under Rule 50(a), the court is considered to have submitted the action to the jury subject to the court's later deciding the legal questions raised by the motion. No later than 28 days after the entry of judgment—or if the motion addresses a jury issue not decided by a verdict, no later than 28 days after the jury was discharged—the movant may file a renewed motion for judgment as a matter of law and may include an alternative or joint request for a new trial under Rule 59. In ruling on the renewed motion, the court may:

> (1) allow judgment on the verdict, if the jury returned a verdict;
>
> (2) order a new trial; or
>
> (3) direct the entry of judgment as a matter of law.

**(c) Granting the Renewed Motion; Conditional Ruling on a Motion for a New Trial.**

> (1) In General. If the court grants a renewed motion for judgment as a matter of law, it must also conditionally rule on any motion for a new trial by determining whether a new trial should be granted if the judgment is later vacated or reversed. The court must state the grounds for conditionally granting or denying the motion for a new trial.
>
> (2) Effect of a Conditional Ruling. Conditionally granting the motion for a new trial does not affect the judgment's finality; if the judgment is reversed, the new trial must proceed unless the appellate court orders otherwise. If the motion for a new trial is conditionally denied, the appellee may assert error in that denial; if the judgment is reversed, the case must proceed as the appellate court orders.

**(d) Time for a Losing Party's New-Trial Motion.** Any motion for a new trial under Rule 59 by a party against whom judgment as a matter of law is rendered must be filed no later than 28 days after the entry of the judgment.

**(e) Denying the Motion for Judgment as a Matter of Law; Reversal on Appeal.** If the court denies the motion for judgment as a matter of law, the prevailing party may, as appellee, assert grounds entitling it to a new trial should the appellate court conclude that the trial court erred in denying the motion. If the appellate court reverses the judgment, it may

<!-- page: 4 -->

order a new trial, direct the trial court to determine whether a new trial should be granted, or direct the entry of judgment.

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 54(b) – Judgment; Costs

**(b) Judgment on Multiple Claims or Involving Multiple Parties.** When an action presents more than one claim for relief—whether as a claim, counterclaim, crossclaim, or third-party claim—or when multiple parties are involved, the court may direct entry of a final judgment as to one or more, but fewer than all, claims or parties only if the court expressly determines that there is no just reason for delay. Otherwise, any order or other decision, however designated, that adjudicates fewer than all the claims or the rights and liabilities of fewer than all the parties does not end the action as to any of the claims or parties and may be revised at any time before the entry of a judgment adjudicating all the claims and all the parties' rights and liabilities.

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 59 – New Trial; Altering or Amending a Judgment

**(a) In General.**

> (1) Grounds for New Trial. The court may, on motion, grant a new trial on all or some of the issues—and to any party—as follows:
>
> > (A) after a jury trial, for any reason for which a new trial has heretofore been granted in an action at law in federal court; or
> >
> > (B) after a nonjury trial, for any reason for which a rehearing has heretofore been granted in a suit in equity in federal court.
>
> (2) Further Action After a Nonjury Trial. After a nonjury trial, the court may, on motion for a new trial, open the judgment if one has been entered, take additional testimony, amend findings of fact and conclusions of law or make new ones, and direct the entry of a new judgment.

**(b) Time to File a Motion for a New Trial.** A motion for a new trial must be filed no later than 28 days after the entry of judgment.

**(c) Time to Serve Affidavits.** When a motion for a new trial is based on affidavits, they must be filed with the motion. The opposing party has 14 days after being served to file opposing affidavits. The court may permit reply affidavits.

**(d) New Trial on the Court's Initiative or for Reasons Not in the Motion.** No later than 28 days after the entry of judgment, the court, on its own, may order a new trial for any reason that would justify granting one on a party's motion. After giving the parties notice and an opportunity to be heard, the court may grant a timely motion for a new trial for a

<!-- page: 5 -->

reason not stated in the motion. In either event, the court must specify the reasons in its order.

**(e) Motion to Alter or Amend a Judgment.** A motion to alter or amend a judgment must be filed no later than 28 days after the entry of the judgment.

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 60 – Relief from a Judgment Order

**(a) Corrections Based on Clerical Mistakes; Oversights and Omissions.** The court may correct a clerical mistake or a mistake arising from oversight or omission whenever one is found in a judgment, order, or other part of the record. The court may do so on motion or on its own, with or without notice. But after an appeal has been docketed in the appellate court and while it is pending, such a mistake may be corrected only with the appellate court's leave.

**(b) Grounds for Relief from a Final Judgment, Order, or Proceeding.** On motion and just terms, the court may relieve a party or its legal representative from a final judgment, order, or proceeding for the following reasons:

> (1) mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect;
>
> (2) newly discovered evidence that, with reasonable diligence, could not have been discovered in time to move for a new trial under Rule 59(b);
>
> (3) fraud (whether previously called intrinsic or extrinsic), misrepresentation, or misconduct by an opposing party;
>
> (4) the judgment is void;
>
> (5) the judgment has been satisfied, released, or discharged; it is based on an earlier judgment that has been reversed or vacated; or applying it prospectively is no longer equitable; or
>
> (6) any other reason that justifies relief.

**(c) Timing and Effect of the Motion.**

> (1) Timing. A motion under Rule 60(b) must be made within a reasonable time—and for reasons (1), (2), and (3) no more than a year after the entry of the judgment or order or the date of the proceeding.
>
> (2) Effect on Finality. The motion does not affect the judgment's finality or suspend its operation.

<!-- page: 6 -->

**(d) Other Powers to Grant Relief.** This rule does not limit a court's power to:

> (1) entertain an independent action to relieve a party from a judgment, order, or proceeding;
>
> (2) grant relief under 28 U.S.C. § 1655 to a defendant who was not personally notified of the action; or
>
> (3) set aside a judgment for fraud on the court.

**(e) Bills and Writs Abolished.** The following are abolished: bills of review, bills in the nature of bills of review, and writs of coram nobis, coram vobis, and audita querela.

## Lecture Recap for Day 17

We decided that according to the law, judges are generally better at applying law, construing instruments, and overseeing long-running disputes.

According to the law, juries are good at deception detection, resolving testimony when there is a disputed fact, and providing community views and standards on things like negligence and obscenity.

This concept is built into the law at the Summary Judgment and Judgment as a Matter of Law stages of litigation: when there is conflicting testimony, and we do not have a *Scott v. Harris* situation in which we can look at the evidence that no rational person could contradict, the law says that it will be a jury issue.

It is also built in when we make the law versus fact determination that has not been previously resolved as to be law or fact.

Ordinarily, precedent and common practices will have decided whether an issue should be of law or fact, and we will just follow it. Sometimes though, as in *Markman*, the court will encounter an issue that involves novel questions. We have a good idea of what historic precedent legal issues are, but every now and then something new comes up, and in those cases the court will take account of functional considerations provided in the chart.

We looked at the different ways trials are conducted, and the distribution of power between the judge and jury and what happens when a case is tried to the courts.

If a case is tried to the courts, meaning that the judge is sitting as the fact finder:

### Judge (Bench Trial)

- We will have the court issue findings of fact and conclusions of law
  - Can be written
  - Can be orally recited
  - Can be delegated to the attorneys to propose findings and conclusions from the parties and sign those rulings
    - To improve the quality of decision making
      - Articulated reasoning is better!
    - Make clear what has been decided vs. not, for appellate purposes (to understand judge's reasoning)
    - Allow clear issue preclusion and claim preclusion analysis
      - If judge merely announces these as proposed by a party, they are accepted just as if the judge would have written these himself (for purposes of appellate review)

### Standards of Review

- **DE NOVO**
  - Least deferential to trial rulings
    - Disregard what the judge said in terms of deference; appellate court only reads the judge's reasoning for persuasive purposes

- **CLEARLY ERRONEOUS**
  - Fair amount of deference given to trial court
    - Feels the trial court is in a better position to adjudicate the issue; or
    - More important to have quick proceedings and final rulings on an issue than to get 100% accuracy and correctness
  - Applied to findings of fact and conclusions of law issued by a judge

- **ABUSE OF DISCRETION**
  - Fair amount of deference given to trial court
    - Applied to Discovery rulings;
    - Rulings on FRCPs; or
    - Evidentiary rulings

- **PLAIN ERROR**
  - Party below did not request or did not object and now is appealing and saying the trial judge committed error

We also discussed jury instructions and how they are requested, prepared, and given:
- Must be requested
- Must be legally correct or the judge is under no obligation to give it; and

  - **BUT (*Kennedy*):** If instruction is on an important or key issue, and as proposed by the party is almost correct, the judge may be under obligation to correct and issue corrected instruction.
- A party must object if it does not like the instruction given to the jury (or if it appeals later, will get plain error standard of review by the appellate court).

### How FRCP 50 Works (JMOL – *Unitherm*)

**If a party wants to move for a Judgment as a Matter of Law**, it needs to:

1. File its motion once **BEFORE** the jury issues its verdict, and
   - If the judge does not grant the motion:
     - By rule of law, it is considered to have deferred the ruling on it (as opposed to having denied it) until after the jury's verdict.
   - If the jury issues its verdict and the movant loses in front of the jury:
     - Party should "renew" its motion to the judge for Judgment as a Matter of Law.

2. **In order to determine whether or not to grant either the pre-verdict or the post-verdict filings of Motions for Judgments as a Matter of Law, the judge will apply the following standard or analysis:**
   - Reasonably construe the evidence in light most favorable to the non-moving party and see whether a rational jury could find for the non-moving party with respect to this issue.
     - If yes, then we should deny the motion.
     - If not, then we should grant the motion.

It is also important to understand the standard of review that an appellate court will apply to a denial of a **FRCP 50 (JMOL)** motion.

**HYPO:**
- We are in the midst of a trial
- Plaintiff has entered all its evidence on an issue and has been fully heard
- Defendant moves for Judgment as a Matter of Law
- Trial court refuses to grant (and thereby defers) a pre-verdict Motion for Judgment as a Matter of Law
- Jury returns a verdict for the Plaintiff
- Defendant then renews its Motion for Judgment as a Matter of Law
- Trial court denies it and enters judgment on the jury's verdict for the Plaintiff
- Defendant appeals, saying the Trial court erroneously denied the Motion for Judgment as a Matter of Law

Appellate court would apply a **DE NOVO** standard of review to this situation, because since it is a "reasonableness" standard, it believes it is in just as good a position as the trial court to understand what was reasonable, in terms of construing the evidence in the light most favorable to the non-moving party, whether a rational jury could find the way the trial court jury did.

<!-- page: 9 -->

In this situation, no credibility determinations are involved, so the appellate court feels it's just as good in a position as the trial court is.

## Lecture for Day 18

Today we will discuss the importance of finality.

About this time in the course it is good to take a step back and consider what are the very broad policies that we are trying to effectuate in constructing rules of civil procedure:

- Effectuate efficiency (efficiently resolve disputes)
  - There is a value to ending litigation; even if we don't get to the right answer, there needs to be a time when we stop the fighting (FRCP 6(b)(2))
- Preserve federalism and separation of powers
- Arrive at the legally correct ruling on disputes
- Preserve access to justice

We will begin today's discussion at the point we left off yesterday — with a motion for a New Trial.

We had finished **FRCP 50 – Motion for a Judgment as a Matter of Law**, where we decided that:
- If a judge does not enter a motion for JMOL, which a party must make if there is any reasonable chance that it could be granted,
- The judge has found that a rational jury could have found in the way this one did, so the judge denies the motion for Judgment as a Matter of Law.

Now we want to discuss the three different circumstances under which a motion for a New Trial may be granted.

### FRCP 59 – Motion for New Trial

(a) In General.

> (1) Grounds for New Trial. The court may, on motion, grant a new trial on all or some of the issues—and to any party—as follows:
>
> > (A) after a jury trial, for any reason for which a new trial has heretofore been granted in an action at law in federal court; or
> >
> > (B) after a nonjury trial, for any reason for which a rehearing has heretofore been granted in a suit in equity in federal court.
>
> (2) Further Action After a Nonjury Trial. After a nonjury trial, the court may, on motion for a new trial, open the judgment if one has been entered, take additional testimony, amend findings of fact and conclusions of law or make new ones, and direct the entry of a new judgment.

### FRCP 59 – Grounds for Granting a Motion for New Trial (3 Bases)

1. If a moving party moves for a motion for a new trial on the grounds that **there was some evidentiary ruling, or some legal ruling by the judge, which the moving party can now convince the judge was so obviously wrong that, if appealed, would for sure be reversed** by the appellate court and the appellate court will order the trial court to grant a new trial (**not harmless error**):
   - Judge may grant this, if he agrees that he made a mistake earlier that would not be considered harmless error.
     - This is pretty rare.
     - Judges will be reluctant to admit their prior errors.

2. The verdict is **"against the great weight of the evidence"** (*Yeatts*); or

3. **Additur/Remittitur.**

We will be discussing the last two theories for motions for a New Trial (FRCP 59):

---

#### Basis 2: Verdict "Against the Great Weight of the Evidence" (*Yeatts*)

***Aetna Casualty & Surety Co. v. Yeatts*, 122 F.2d 350 (4th Cir. 1941)**

The granting or refusing of a new trial is within the discretion of the trial judge and his action is not reviewable upon appeal, except for in the most exceptional circumstances.

**Facts**
- Yeatts (defendant) had an indemnity insurance policy with Aetna Casualty & Surety Co. (Aetna) (plaintiff).
- When Yeatts was sued, he sought to recover on his policy from Aetna.
- Aetna brought suit against Yeatts, seeking a declaratory judgment that Yeatts was performing a criminal abortion when he incurred his liability, an act that was expressly excluded from his coverage under the Aetna policy.
- At trial, Yeatts testified that he was not engaged in a criminal act.
- The jury found that Yeatts was not performing a criminal abortion when he incurred his liability and therefore found in his favor.
- Aetna filed motions for a judgment non obstante veredicto (JNOV) (notwithstanding the verdict) and a new trial.
- The trial judge denied the motions.
- Aetna appealed.

**Holding and Reasoning (Parker, J.)**
- The granting or refusing of a new trial is within the discretion of the trial judge and his action is not reviewable upon appeal, except for the most exceptional circumstances.
- **A trial judge may grant a new trial if the jury's verdict is against the clear weight of the evidence, or is based on evidence that is false or will result in the miscarriage of justice.**

<!-- page: 11 -->

- Otherwise, the Seventh Amendment requires that the jury makes the decision based on its review of the evidence.
- In the present case, while it is entirely possible that the trial judge could have granted Aetna a new trial based on the evidence, his refusal to do so did not constitute an abuse of discretion.
- This was not an exceptional circumstance where the jury's verdict is against the clear weight of the evidence.
- The fact that Yeatts testified on his own behalf that he was not engaged in a criminal abortion is sufficient evidence to support the trial judge's decision to deny Aetna's motion for new trial.
- Here the jury found for the doctor.
- The trial judge could **NOT** have legitimately issued JMOL for the insurance company.
  - Because the jury could have reasonably believed the doctor based on his testimony, the court could NOT have granted the JMOL.

**BLL:** Nonetheless, the trial judge could have still granted a motion for a new trial, and the basis that the new trial could have been granted under is that ***the jury verdict issued was "contrary to great weight of the evidence,"*** which in such a case the trial judge is entitled to grant a motion for new trial.
- This usually comes up when judges believe someone is lying.

Judges believe that they can tell when someone is lying (even though scientific evidence says that is incorrect). When judges decide that someone is lying, and they cannot grant a motion for Judgment as a Matter of Law because that would be for the jury to credit, nevertheless, they could make another jury, and re-hear the evidence, and render a new verdict.

#### Basis 3: Additur / Remittitur

- **Additur:** The power of a court to increase an excessively low damage award, reserved for cases in which the jury is influenced by prejudice or mistake.
- **Remittitur:** A reduction, or proposed reduction, of a damages award.

**Additur (*Fisch*):**
- There is a trial for damages
- Plaintiff prevails
- Jury award of damages is for a certain amount
- Plaintiff believes jury amount for damages is insufficient
- Court turns to the Defendant and gives him a choice:
  - Accept a higher amount of damages that the court specifies; or
  - Court will order a new trial (at least on the issue of damages)

<!-- page: 12 -->

**Remittitur (*Powers*):**
- There is a trial for damages
- Plaintiff prevails
- Jury award of damages is for a certain amount
- Defendant believes jury amount for damages is excessive
- Court turns to the Plaintiff and gives him a choice:
  - Accept the new, reduced damages as specified by the court; or
  - Court will order a new trial (at least on the issue of damages)

The law on this is quite simple.

**Remittitur is consistent with the right to a jury trial**, and therefore **permissible**.
- The theory behind this is that if a jury has awarded for example $10K, then by default the jury is saying that $5K is okay too (because $5K is less than $10K, and so it is included in $10K).

**Additur**, on the other hand and at least in the federal courts, **is considered to violate the right to a jury trial**, and so it **is not permissible (unconstitutional)**.
- The theory behind this is that if a jury has awarded for example $10K, the jury did not think that $15K was okay.

In *Fisch*, the court was saying that Additur was okay, and that is because the case was adjudicated in state court, and the Seventh Amendment, as we discussed before, does not apply to the individual states.

- Permissible for state courts to use **Additur**
- Only **Remittitur** is permissible in the federal court system
- Some states allow both
- Some states allow only Remittitur
- No states allow only Additur

Many people think that a Remittitur is a way of doing bad things to the Plaintiff. However, the Plaintiff is empowered by the choice because if they choose not to accept the lower damages award, they can get a whole new trial. On the flipside, there is an argument that additur empowers the defendant in the same way.

The alternative to the award adjustment (either additur or remittitur) is a new trial. That is why these two are considered species of motions for a new trial. The court is basically conditioning granting the motion for a new trial if the party does not accept the adjusted damage award.

### HYPO: Timeliness of Post-Verdict Motions

- Plaintiff prevails on a case at judgment
- Defendant had previously filed for JMOL prior to the jury's verdict
- Judge deferred ruling on the JMOL
- After a status conference held the day after the jury's verdict, the Defendant's attorney tells the plaintiff's lawyer and the court that he has another trial starting tomorrow that will take 5 weeks, and that he is planning to submit the renewed motion for JMOL in 40 days. Is that okay?
- Court says yes, that the Defendant could file his renewed motion for JMOL in 40 days, and the Plaintiff could file his response 60 days from now; hearing to be set on the motion at day 75.
- Plaintiff's lawyer says that the timeline also works for him because he will also have a 5-week trial coming up.
- Everyone agrees.
- When the motion is filed 40 days later, the Plaintiff looks at the Rule and says that the judge has to dismiss the renewed motion for JMOL because the motion was filed untimely.
- **What should the court do?**

- Motion for Judgment as a Matter of Law shall be filed no later than **28 days** after the entry of judgment.
- It does not matter if all parties agreed that the motion could be filed later; the Rule says it needs to be filed no later than 28 days after the entry of judgment.
  - Rule 6(b)(2) says "the court **MUST NOT** extend the time." (*Hulson*)
- It does not matter if the judgment could be entered a bit later.

**Why the harsh time periods?**
- Efficiency and cost savings.
  - There is an interest in finality.
  - There needs to be a time that we all stop fighting.
    - Even if we do not get the 100% right answer.

  - A motion for a new trial must be filed no later than 28 days after the entry of judgment.

    - (5) the judgment has been satisfied, released, or discharged; it is based on an earlier judgment that has been reversed or vacated; or applying it prospectively is no longer equitable; or
    - (6) any other reason that justifies relief.

### FRCP 60 – Discussion

**FRCP 60** is basically a motion for reopening the judgment. On its face, it looks like there may be a lot of reasons why a court could issue a relief from judgment and open a case, and that the court does it a lot. However, it hardly ever happens. Even though FRCP lists all these reasons, it is virtually impossible to get a judge to reopen the case, and mostly it is because of effecting the concept of finality.
- Later on during the course we will discuss preclusion, which may be an exception to this, but the big picture rule is that FRCP 60 is almost never granted by a judge, and hardly ever happens.

### HYPO: *Title v. US* and the Limits of FRCP 60

In *Title v. US*, the United States proceeded against a person claiming that person committed fraud in his naturalization application, and therefore wanted to take away the person's naturalization (denaturalize them).

**HYPO (built on the *Title* case):**
- There is a denaturalization litigation
- Case depends on the meaning of a particular phrase within the naturalization statutes (legal issue on the law vs. fact divide), on whether Ms. Title should be denaturalized
- Case turns on who is correct based on the interpretation of the statute
- Plaintiff Title offers one interpretation; US government offers a different interpretation
- District court agrees with Ms. Title's interpretation and US appeals to the 9th Circuit
- 9th Circuit reverses and finds in favor of the US, orders Ms. Title denaturalized
- Ms. Title petitions SCOTUS for cert
- SCOTUS refuses to hear the case
- Later comes another litigant, Ms. Idon'tknow
- US also files denaturalization proceedings against Ms. Idon'tknow in Richmond, VA (4th Circuit), which would turn on exactly the same legal issue
- District court rules in favor of Ms. Idon'tknow
- US appeals to the 4th Circuit and Ms. Idon'tknow's argument prevails, so now there is a circuit split
- The US petitions for cert to the SCOTUS to resolve the circuit split, which gets granted
- SCOTUS affirms Ms. Idon'tknow's case and says that her interpretation (which was the same as the previous Ms. Title's interpretation) is correct
- Now we know Ms. Title back in the 9th Circuit was right all along, but it's more than 28 days since her judgment, so she cannot motion for new trial, or JMOL, nothing. Therefore, she files for a Motion under Rule 60 to relieve her from judgment because she was right all along.
  - SCOTUS will not grant her motion to reopen that case because the federal courts cannot afford to open up every single case.
  - Falls in line with the consideration of finality.
  - Can have harsh consequences, but nonetheless courts will not reopen judgments easily.

## Appellate Review Process

### Final Order Policies

Now we will discuss the terms by which a party can appeal to the higher court a ruling that the party thinks is wrong and which can affect the conduct of the litigation.

<!-- page: 17 -->

In this class we will not discuss how to do appeals well, or write appellate briefs (that can be done in LRW and in another course called the Appellate Process), but we will specifically discuss when it is **permissible** to take the appeal. As usual, we will focus on the federal rules, but in case you are wondering about the states, they differ on this process.

One thing to keep in mind is that there are federal rules of appellate procedure, just like there are FRCP that are used in the trial court system.

Under the Federal Rules of Appeal, a party who wants to appeal a ruling **MUST** file what is called a **Notice of Appeal** in the United States District Court that issued the ruling that the party is appealing.

There are also time limits associated with that. If the Notice of Appeal is not filed within the time limit, then the appeal typically will be dismissed for failing to be filed in a timely manner.

- Ordinarily the time limit for **private parties** is **30 days** after the ruling/judgment.
- **The United States itself gets 60 days** (due to the process involved within the DoJ).

Our primary teaching case on this is ***Liberty Mutual***.

---

### *Liberty Mutual Insurance Co. v. Wetzel*, 424 U.S. 737 (SCOTUS)(1976)

A finding of liability that does not finally dispose of the plaintiff's prayers for relief is not appealable to the court of appeals. (In other words, in order to be able to file an appeal, there must be a final order (judgment) from the trial court.)

**Facts**
- Wetzel (plaintiff) brought suit against Liberty Mutual Insurance Co. (Liberty) (defendant) in the United States District Court for the Western District of Pennsylvania, alleging that Liberty's insurance benefits and maternity leave regulations violated Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964.
- Wetzel requested injunctive relief ordering Liberty to cease its discriminatory practices and establish non-discriminatory practices and damages including back pay and exemplary damages.
- The district court found for Wetzel but granted none of the requested relief.
- Liberty appealed to the court of appeals, which affirmed the district court's judgment, and then petitioned the United States Supreme Court for certiorari.

<!-- page: 18 -->

**Holding and Reasoning (Rehnquist, J.)**
- **The district court's decision is not appealable to the court of appeals, because the district court's decision does not finally dispose of any of Wetzel's requests for relief.**
- Wetzel set forth a single claim, and **while the district court found for her, it left unresolved what relief would be granted.**

<!-- page: 19 -->

- The district court's order was merely a grant of partial summary judgment on the issue of Liberty's liability.
- Grants of partial summary judgment are interlocutory, not final.
- The only appealable interlocutory judgments are grants of injunctive relief.
- Since **the district court made no ruling on the request for injunctive relief, this was not a basis for appeal.**
- The district court and the court of appeals also found that the decision was appealable as a final judgment under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 54(b), which allows a court to enter judgment on fewer than all of the claims in a multiple-claim action.
- This, however, was not a multiple-claim action.
- Rather, there was a single claim and prayers for several types of relief.
- The judgment of the court of appeals is vacated and is remanded with instructions to dismiss the appeal.

**28 U.S.C. § 1291** and **28 U.S.C. § 1292** tell us when it is permissible to appeal.

Both parties agreed that the SCOTUS could hear this case, but the SCOTUS said that it was not appealable because there was no final order. The court granted just the liability, without addressing the relief requested, so there was no basis for Liberty Mutual to appeal yet.

In this case, the court that lacked jurisdiction was the appeals court, because there was no final order. Because the appellate court did not have jurisdiction, the SCOTUS also did not have jurisdiction to hear the case.

**BLL:**

**In order for an appeal to be proper, two things must be true:**

1. **District court MUST have SMJ** (same as *Mottley*, because neither party raised it, but the court still has an independent duty to raise the issue and make sure that they have jurisdiction — both as to subject matter of the district court, and as to its own appellate jurisdiction).

<!-- page: 20 -->

2. **Appeal needs to be proper** (meaning that the appeal court must have appellate jurisdiction, and in order to do that, they **need a final order or a judgment, on both the liability, as well as any relief requested**).

We can see from *Liberty Mutual* that the appellate courts have, just like when we studied Subject Matter Jurisdiction, an independent duty to inquire whether the lower level court had subject matter jurisdiction and whether it has subject matter jurisdiction itself, even if the parties did not raise the issue, or if the parties agreed to have a certain appellate court review the case.

Usually what happens is that a party who thinks that the appellate court does not have jurisdiction should file a **Motion to Dismiss the Appeal**. This usually notifies the court that there may be an issue with the jurisdiction over the appeal. Nonetheless, even if neither party flags it, the appellate court will also do a check of its own.

Most of the appeals — the overwhelming majority — are on the basis of what is called the ***final order/decision doctrine*** (**28 U.S.C. § 1291**). The appeals courts will have appellate jurisdiction over all final decisions of the district courts, which means that they need a final decision, which means that the district court **MUST enter judgment**. Then, any decision that led up to the entry of judgment which could materially affect the district court judgment could be appealed — but we must wait until the end.

**The reasons or advantages for 28 U.S.C. § 1291 (waiting for final judgment before appeal) are the following:**
- The trial court may correct its ruling if the ruling is wrong, before the appeal is taken
- Even if the district court issues a ruling, even if it is erroneous, it may not matter to the appellate court's ruling (*harmless error doctrine*)
- Statute seems to express a policy which suggests that most trial decisions are correct in the eyes of the appellate courts

By law, when an issue in a case is taken for appeal, the trial court still can and is supposed to continue, but sometimes they prefer to stop because it wants to wait to see what the appeal court will say before it continues, just in case the appellate court reverses, because it can affect how the trial court continues to conduct the case. Because of this, litigation can be costly. In the event that the trial court judgment is wrong, but we do not allow the appeal until the end of the case, there may also be a cost because they would have to do the trial over again in the event there is a new trial granted — so there is a balancing consideration that needs to happen. The federal court system feels that if there will be a cost incurred either way, they prefer that it wait until the end, because they believe that most judges' decisions are correct.

### What Is a "Final Order"?

<!-- page: 21 -->

A final order is when a trial court enters judgment over all the claims and defenses, and if there is a ruling for the plaintiff, the court awards the plaintiff some relief.

**Samples of final orders:**

- If the court grants a Summary Judgment for one party or the other, it is **NOT** in itself a final order.

- Suppose there is an order granting final judgment, and there is a renewed motion for JMOL after the verdict. The court grants or denies that motion.
  - **NOT A FINAL ORDER.**
    - That would have to be accompanied by a separate order that says **the judge directs final order to be entered** for summary judgment on behalf of whatever party.

- Suppose there is an order granting final judgment and there is a renewed motion for a JMOL (there was a motion for JMOL previously filed prior to the jury's verdict). The court grants or denies that motion.
  - **FINAL ORDER.**
    - The judge would have had to **defer ruling** on the first JMOL, and after the jury issued its verdict, the court **entered judgment** on the jury's verdict. After the motion for JMOL is renewed, when the court rules on it, **that would be a final judgment**.

- Suppose there was a jury verdict, a trial judgment, and then we have a timely motion for a new trial within the time limits. The judge grants the motion for a new trial.
  - **NOT A FINAL ORDER.**
    - Need to conduct the new trial, therefore, not appealable. (Denial of a motion for a new trial would be a final order and therefore, appealable).

### Exceptions to 28 U.S.C. § 1291

Exceptions to the final order rule (which are all reviewed — although rejected — in *Liberty Mutual* itself):

**1) FRCP 54(b) (Judgment; Costs) – Judgment on Multiple Claims or Involving Multiple Parties:** If there is a final order on one claim, but not on the others, that final order on that one claim is appealable.

- **(b) Judgment on Multiple Claims or Involving Multiple Parties.** When an action presents more than one claim for relief—whether as a claim, counterclaim, crossclaim, or third-party claim—or when multiple parties are involved, the court may direct entry of a final judgment as to one or more, but fewer than all, claims or parties only if the court expressly determines that there is no just reason for delay. Otherwise, any order or other decision, however designated, that

<!-- page: 22 -->

adjudicates fewer than all the claims or the rights and liabilities of fewer than all the parties does not end the action as to any of the claims or parties and may be revised at any time before the entry of a judgment adjudicating all the claims and all the parties' rights and liabilities.

- **When can we invoke Rule 54(b)?**
  - There has to be more than one "claim," as opposed to more than one theory of relief.

**Claim** (in accordance with *Liberty Mutual*) is a theory of relief as applied to a particular set of facts.

Greiner prefers to define a *claim* to be: 1) a theory of relief; 2) as applied to a particular set of facts; 3) against a particular party.

In *Liberty Mutual*, we did not have more than one claim. Instead, we had:
- One theory of relief, which was the violation of Title VII (Employment discrimination statute),
- Against a set of facts, which was the maternity policies of this particular insurance company; and
- There was only one defendant (the insurance company).

Because at *Liberty Mutual* we only had one claim, **FRCP 54** (multiclaim exception) did not apply.

Furthermore, and not at issue in *Liberty Mutual*, if there are multiple claims that are closely related, and a district judge enters final judgment on some but not all claims, the more closely related the claims are, the more likelihood that on appeal the trial judge's judgment would be reviewed for *abuse of discretion*.

**When should FRCP 54(b) be used?**
- A district court should generally **not** use this rule (FRCP 54(b)) when the claims they are finally adjudicating and the ones that they are still trying to finish are closely related and involve the same facts.
  - It is better for them to allow the appellate courts to deal with them all at the same time.
  - However, if they are unrelated to each other, then the trial court can do this, but there is no legal duty for the trial court to do so (discretion).
  - If the district court decides to grant final judgment on one or more of the multiple claims, then the 30-day clock for the parties to file the Notice of Appeal with the Appellate Court will begin at that point as to those claims which received an entry of partial judgment.

<!-- page: 23 -->

**What is the procedure that must be followed in order to use FRCP 54(b)?**
- When the district judge wants to use FRCP 54(b), it must follow all of the rules stated above, and must be when the court **expressly** (judge must announce what he is doing — not necessarily in writing, orally is fine) determines that there is no reason for the delay; and
- There needs to be a judgment entered on the claim.

This requires the judge to announce this because the appeal clock starts and we need the parties to be put on notice so that they do not accidentally miss their right to appeal.
