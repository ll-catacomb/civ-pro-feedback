## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***Gulf Oil Corp. v. Gilbert*, 330 U.S. 501 (SCOTUS) (1947)**

  A court may resist imposition upon its jurisdiction even when jurisdiction is authorized by the letter of a general venue statute.

- ***Piper Aircraft Co. v. Reyno*, 454 U.S. 235 (SCOTUS) (1981)**

  A plaintiff may not defeat a motion to dismiss on grounds of forum non conveniens merely by showing that the substantive law that would be applied in the alternative forum is less favorable to the plaintiffs than that of the present forum.

- ***Hoffman v. Blaski*, 363 U.S. 335 (SCOTUS) (1960) — Transfer of venue in federal courts**

  The power of a district court to transfer an action to another district depends not upon the wish or waiver of the defendant but, rather, upon whether the transferee district was one in which the action might have been brought initially by the plaintiff.

- ***Van Dusen v. Barrack*, 376 U.S. 612 (SCOTUS) (1964)**

  The new subsection requires the court to determine that the transfer is necessary for convenience of the parties and witnesses, and in the interest of justice.

- ***Ferens v. John Deere Co.*, 494 U.S. 516 (SCOTUS) (1990)**

  When a plaintiff transfers a case from one venue to another under 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a), the choice of law rules of the transferor jurisdiction apply.

- ***Goldlawr, Inc. v. Heiman*, 369 U.S. 463 (SCOTUS) (1962)**

  28 U.S.C. § 1406 authorizes the transfer of an action even if the transferor court lacks personal jurisdiction.

- ***Atlantic Marine Construction Co. v. U.S. District Court*, 571 U.S. 49 (SCOTUS) (2013)**

  When parties have entered into a valid forum-selection clause that designates a federal venue, the case should be transferred to the designated district unless extraordinary circumstances exist that are unrelated to the convenience of the parties.

- ***Swift v. Tyson*, 41 U.S. (16 Pet.) 1 (SCOTUS) (1842)**

  Interprets the Rules of Decision Act.

### Statutes

#### 28 U.S.C. § 1391 — Venue Generally

**(a) Applicability of Section.** Except as otherwise provided by law—

> (1) this section shall govern the venue of all civil actions brought in district courts of the United States; and
>
> (2) the proper venue for a civil action shall be determined without regard to whether the action is local or transitory in nature.

**(b) Venue in General.** A civil action may be brought in—

> (1) a judicial district in which any defendant resides, if all defendants are residents of the State in which the district is located;
>
> (2) a judicial district in which a substantial part of the events or omissions giving rise to the claim occurred, or a substantial part of property that is the subject of the action is situated; or
>
> (3) if there is no district in which an action may otherwise be brought as provided in this section, any judicial district in which any defendant is subject to the court's personal jurisdiction with respect to such action.

**(c) Residency.** For all venue purposes—

> (1) a natural person, including an alien lawfully admitted for permanent residence in the United States, shall be deemed to reside in the judicial district in which that person is domiciled;
>
> (2) an entity with the capacity to sue and be sued in its common name under applicable law, whether or not incorporated, shall be deemed to reside, if a defendant, in any judicial district in which such defendant is subject to the court's personal jurisdiction with respect to the civil action in question and, if a plaintiff, only in the judicial district in which it maintains its principal place of business; and
>
> (3) a defendant not resident in the United States may be sued in any judicial district, and the joinder of such a defendant shall be disregarded in determining where the action may be brought with respect to other defendants.

<!-- page: 3 -->

**(d) Residency of Corporations in States With Multiple Districts.**

For purposes of venue under this chapter, in a State which has more than one judicial district and in which a defendant that is a corporation is subject to personal jurisdiction at the time an action is commenced, such corporation shall be deemed to reside in any district in that State within which its contacts would be sufficient to subject it to personal jurisdiction if that district were a separate State, and, if there is no such district, the corporation shall be deemed to reside in the district within which it has the most significant contacts.

---

#### 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a) — Change of Venue

**(a)** For the convenience of parties and witnesses, in the interest of justice, a district court may transfer any civil action to any other district or division where it might have been brought or to any district or division to which all parties have consented.

---

#### 28 U.S.C. § 1406(a) — Cure or Waiver of Defects

**(a)** The district court of a district in which is filed a case laying venue in the wrong division or district shall dismiss, or if it be in the interest of justice, transfer such case to any district or division in which it could have been brought.

---

#### 28 U.S.C. § 1631 — Transfer to Cure Want of Jurisdiction

Whenever a civil action is filed in a court as defined in section 610 of this title or an appeal, including a petition for review of administrative action, is noticed for or filed with such a court and that court finds that there is a want of jurisdiction, the court shall, if it is in the interest of justice, transfer such action or appeal to any other such court in which the action or appeal could have been brought at the time it was filed or noticed, and the action or appeal shall proceed as if it had been filed in or noticed for the court to which it is transferred on the date upon which it was actually filed in or noticed for the court from which it is transferred.

---

#### Rules of Decision Act (28 U.S.C. § 1652)

The laws of the several states, except where the Constitution or treaties of the United States or Acts of Congress otherwise require or provide, shall be regarded as rules of decision in civil actions in the courts of the United States, in cases where they apply.

## Lecture for Day 9

### Venue

- **Venue is entirely a statutory concept.** There is nothing in the Constitution about it. It is a doctrine about geography, determining where the court has power to adjudicate a case.
- **Verbiage to use when discussing Venue:**
  - Where can a plaintiff "**lay**" venue
  - Where is "**venue proper**"
  - We do **not** say "exercising venue"
- Even though made by statutory law only, **if a defendant objects that venue is not proper, a court must take action.**
  - Defendant can "**waive**" venue if he so desires.
- Venue is mostly about **convenience and efficient resolution.**
- **Venue Statute (28 U.S.C. § 1391):** Applies only to civil cases, not criminal cases.

---

### Analyzing 28 U.S.C. § 1391(b)

- **§ 1391(b)(1)** will give you one way in which a plaintiff could lay venue.
- **§ 1391(b)(2)** will give you an alternate way to lay venue.

> ***NOTE: You do not need both. If you can meet either § 1391(b)(1) or § 1391(b)(2), then you can say that Venue is proper. ONLY IF NEITHER (b1) nor (b2) apply does § 1391(b)(3) have to be analyzed, but there is no guarantee that it will be applicable.***

**"Resides"** in the venue statute is defined in 28 U.S.C. § 1391(c).
**Resides = "domicile" analysis**

**§ 1391(b)(1):** For (b1) to apply, we either need all defendants to reside in a single state, or we need to have only a single defendant.
- If we have this, then the **district** in which the defendant (person) "resides" will provide proper venue.
  - Pay attention — a "person" can sometimes mean a corporation or unincorporated entity.
    - If a corporation is incorporated in a specific state, its venue is the **district in which the principal place of business is located**.
      - For specific jurisdiction of the corporation for which we are trying to find proper venue, we still need to do:
        - Minimal contacts analysis
        - Fair and reasonable analysis

**§ 1391(b)(2):** The place in which a substantial part of events or omissions giving rise to the claim occurred.

<!-- page: 7 -->

**§ 1391(b)(3): Only applicable if neither (b1) nor (b2) is applicable.**
- This subsection is only available against a single defendant who would be a foreign person or concern; or
- Only available when the event occurred outside of the United States.
  - In this event, you would use the court where the defendant (or any defendant) is subject to personal jurisdiction.

### Venue vs. Personal Jurisdiction — The 2×2 Grid

**One can have Personal Jurisdiction without Venue, and vice versa — one can have Venue without Personal Jurisdiction.**

| | **PJ: Yes** | **PJ: No** |
|---|---|---|
| **Venue: Yes** | Suit in N.D. Texas against Dallas domiciliary, facts occurring in Dallas | Suppose *Nicastro* facts, suit in D. New Jersey |
| **Venue: No** | Suit in E.D. Texas against Dallas domiciliary, facts occurring in Dallas | Suppose *Nicastro* facts, suit in E.D. Texas |

**Scenario 1 — Suit in Northern District of Texas against a Dallas Domiciliary (Top Left: Yes/Yes):**
- **Venue:** § 1391(b)(1) — Venue is proper because the domiciliary is residing within the judicial district.
- **Personal Jurisdiction:** General jurisdiction under domicile theory, since he is a domiciliary of the state of Texas.
  - *(Note: Representing that Dallas is in the Northern District of Texas for federal court jurisdiction purposes.)*

**Scenario 2 — Plaintiff injured in NJ, defendant from UK, suit in Eastern District of Texas (Bottom Right: No/No):**
- **Personal Jurisdiction:** Lacking.
  - Specific: Events did not occur in Texas — no minimum contacts.
  - General: Defendant was not a domiciliary of Texas and no minimal contacts.
- **Venue:** Improper.
  - § 1391(b)(1): No. Defendant does not reside in E.D. of Texas.

<!-- page: 9 -->

  - § 1391(b)(2): No. Events did not occur in the E.D. of Texas.
  - § 1391(b)(3): McIntyre is not ordinarily under jurisdiction in Texas.
    - However, there is another district where we could lay venue — the state of New Jersey, where there are substantial events that occurred (the accident), which means (b)(2) is available, so (b)(3) is not.
      - The problem is that we would not be able to use a theory of Personal Jurisdiction in a federal district court in New Jersey over McIntyre UK on those facts.
        - No, because they did not avail themselves in any specific state, but specifically in NJ.

> ***Even though federal court is a national court, and if we did a minimal contacts with the US as a whole there might be minimal contacts, the federal court could still not exercise personal jurisdiction over McIntyre UK because of FRCP 4(k)(1)(A), which states that federal court piggy-backs on state court. NJ state court cannot exercise jurisdiction over the Nicastro facts. Since the state court cannot, due to FRCP 4(k)(1)(A), federal courts also cannot exercise personal jurisdiction.***

**Scenario 3 — Suit in E.D. Texas, against a Dallas domiciliary, facts occurring in Dallas (Top Right: Venue Yes, PJ No):**
- **Venue:** Would be proper because the accident occurred there — § 1391(b)(2).
- **Personal Jurisdiction:**
  - General jurisdiction because PJ is analyzed as to the whole state — so yes, PJ is available.
  - **Venue:** Person would **not** be able to lay venue:
    - (b1) — Defendant does not reside in the Eastern district.
    - (b2) — No events happened in the Eastern district of Texas.
    - (b3) — No, because we could lay venue elsewhere, in the Northern District of Texas.

**Scenario 4 — Suit in E.D. Texas, Dallas domiciliary, facts in Dallas (Bottom Left: PJ Yes, Venue No):**
- **Personal Jurisdiction:** General personal jurisdiction over the defendant (PJ applies to the entire state).
- **Venue:**
  - (b1) No — Defendant is not resident in that **District**.
  - (b2) No — Events did not occur in that district.
  - (b3) No — Because it could be adjudicated somewhere in the US.

### Moving the Case Around — Forum Non-Conveniens and Transfer

The fun starts when we think about ways to move the case around, even if venue is proper and the court can exercise personal jurisdiction.

#### Forum Non-Conveniens

**When to use:**
- Defendant filing a motion to dismiss citing the doctrine of ***forum non-conveniens*** for any kind of move of a case, **EXCEPT** a federal court to federal court "transfer."
  - (i.e., case is pending in a state court and want to move it to another state court, or abroad; or case is pending in federal court and want to relocate it abroad.)
  - If case is pending in a state court and we want to move it for convenience reasons, we would remove — but if a case does not fit under removal statutes, it would also not fit under the venue statute. Definitively not for convenience.
  - Federal court system is also reluctant to relocate a case from federal court to a state court for convenience — it would instead transfer to another federal court.
    - **(Except: only if there is a forum selection clause.)**

---

<!-- page: 11 -->

### *Gulf Oil Corp. v. Gilbert*, 330 U.S. 501 (SCOTUS) (1947)

**BLL:** In considering the public and private factors for purposes of a motion to dismiss for *forum non-conveniens*, the court needs to start with the **presumption that plaintiff's choice of forum is correct** (venue is proper and court can exercise PJ over defendant). If not, we would have to take some action to remedy. But, assuming both of those things are right, and we are considering to dismiss for *forum non-conveniens*, **only if the public and private interest factors overwhelmingly overcome that presumption** would we so dismiss.

Court set out a division of "**private-interest factors**" and "**public-interest factors**" affecting the convenience of the forum:

**Private interest factors:**
- Relative ease of access to sources of proof
- Availability of compulsory process for attendance of unwilling witnesses
- Cost of obtaining attendance of willing witnesses
- Possibility of view of premises, if view would be appropriate to take action
- All other practical problems that make a trial case easy, expeditious, and inexpensive

**Public interest factors:**
- Administrative difficulties when litigation is piled up in congested courts
- Jury duty is a burden not to be placed on the people that have no relation to the litigation
- In cases which touch the affairs of many people, there is reason to hold trial within their reach
- Local interest in having localized controversies decided at home

---

### *Piper Aircraft Co. v. Reyno*, 454 U.S. 235 (SCOTUS) (1981)

**BLL:** In considering the public and private factors for purposes of a motion to dismiss for *forum non-conveniens*, the court needs to start with the **presumption that plaintiff's choice of forum is correct** (venue is proper and court can exercise PJ over defendant). **If plaintiff is from abroad, he does not get that initial presumption.** When considering to dismiss for *forum non-conveniens*, only if the public and private interest factors overwhelmingly overcome that presumption would we so dismiss. **Defendant can waive those factors** which would otherwise prevent a case from being able to be litigated there.

Also, with respect to choice of law analysis, it is okay for the court to approve dismissal of a case for its belief in the **difficulty of applying the other state's law**, even if the other state would run into the same problem in applying this state's law.

<!-- page: 12 -->

**Facts:**
- In 1976, a small commercial aircraft crashed in Scotland, killing the pilot and five passengers.
- The deceased were all Scottish citizens and residents.
- The plane was manufactured in Pennsylvania by Piper Aircraft Co. (defendant), and the propellers were manufactured in Ohio by Hartzell Propeller, Inc. (defendant).
- Gaynell Reyno (plaintiff), the administratrix of the passengers' estates, filed wrongful-death actions against Piper and Hartzell in California state court, alleging negligence and strict liability.
- Reyno admitted to filing the action in the United States because its laws were more favorable to her case than those of Scotland.
- Piper and Hartzell removed to federal district court in California and then sought a transfer to the Middle District of Pennsylvania.
- The California district court granted the motions.
- After both cases were moved to federal district court in Pennsylvania, Piper and Hartzell sought to dismiss the case on grounds of forum non conveniens.
- The Pennsylvania district court granted the motions, citing the discretion courts have to dismiss a case if an alternative forum has jurisdiction to hear the case and if trial in the chosen forum would be burdensome to the defendant.
- The United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit reversed on the ground that dismissal for forum non conveniens is not appropriate if the law of the alternative forum is less favorable to the plaintiff. The United States Supreme Court granted certiorari.

**Holding and Reasoning:**
- A plaintiff may not defeat a motion to dismiss on grounds of forum non conveniens merely by showing that the substantive law that would be applied in the alternative forum is less favorable to the plaintiffs than that of the present forum.
- In general, the possibility of an unfavorable change in substantive law should not be a substantial consideration in a forum non conveniens inquiry.
- If conclusive or substantial weight were given to the possibility of a change in law, then forum non conveniens doctrine would be rendered useless. American courts would become even more attractive to foreign plaintiffs, which would increase court congestion in the United States.
- Despite these concerns, the possibility of an unfavorable change in law may be given substantial weight in a forum non conveniens inquiry if the remedy available in the alternative forum is so clearly inadequate that it is effectively no remedy at all.
- The central purpose of a forum non conveniens inquiry is to ensure that the trial is convenient.

<!-- page: 13 -->

- A resident or citizen plaintiff is entitled to greater deference if it has chosen the home forum because it is reasonable to assume the choice is convenient. The same cannot be said for a foreign plaintiff.
- The forum non conveniens determination is at the trial court's discretion and may be reversed only for a clear abuse of discretion.
- To guide trial-court discretion, in *Gulf Oil Corp. v. Gilbert*, 330 U.S. 501 (1947), the Court set out "private-interest factors" and "public-interest factors" affecting the convenience of the forum.
- In this case, although the potential theories of recovery and the remedies available to the decedents' families may be more restricted in a Scottish court than in the United States, it cannot be said that the available remedies are so limited as to be effectively nonexistent.
- Accordingly, the appellate court incorrectly concluded that the possibility of an unfavorable change of law should control the forum non conveniens inquiry.
- Furthermore, regarding the private-interest factors, although records concerning the design, manufacture, and testing of the propeller and plane are located in the United States, a large proportion of the relevant evidence is located in Great Britain.
- The district court was correct to conclude that Piper and Hartzell's inability to implead potential third-party defendants supports holding the trial in Scotland.
- As to public-interest factors, the district court's choice-of-law analysis concluded that if the case were tried in Pennsylvania, then Pennsylvania law would apply to Piper and Scottish law to Hartzell — a trial involving two sets of laws would be confusing to the jury.
- Further, Scotland has a strong interest in this matter given that the accident occurred in Scottish airspace, all the decedents were Scottish, and all of the potential plaintiffs and defendants besides Piper and Hartzell are either Scottish or English.
- Therefore, the district court did not abuse its discretion in deciding that public and private interests support moving the trial to Scotland.

**Analyzing *Piper* under standard doctrine:**

1. **Presume that plaintiff's choice of forum is correct:**
   - In this case, the presumption was **not** applicable because the plaintiff itself was not from the United States. (Foreigners do not get the same presumption because they could be forum shopping.)

2. **Court looks at private and public factors:**
   - Access to witnesses (not very easy here).
   - Access to compelling unwilling witnesses (not easy for a court here).
     - Can argue that really important witnesses and evidence can come from that state.
     - But judge cannot tell if the really important witnesses are where they are unless he can litigate the case on the merits in order to find out.

<!-- page: 14 -->

**Hypo** (same situation as *Piper*):
- Maybe defendant can promise that his employees testify live wherever they need to.
- Maybe defendant promises to turn all evidence over to wherever they need to be.
  - Affirmative Defense could be that the statute of limitations passed.
    - Defendant would promise to the court that no one will assert limitations period.
    - But then, personal jurisdiction: If Scotland uses personal jurisdiction, then Scotland will not have Personal Jurisdiction (see *Nicastro*).
    - Defendant can promise not to raise Personal Jurisdiction objections.
    - Can also promise not to raise Venue objections.

**Using these waivers of affirmative defenses, a defendant can create a proper forum in order to get out of the United States' court system.**

Court decides that this can happen and that there would be value in litigating this in Scotland. But it's terrified that they would have to apply Scottish law and the court says that the jury might get confused.
- But they do not present what difficulties would be.
- And they also overlook that Scotland might have to apply US law (which potentially could be even more complicated).
  - Court does not consider (or care) this issue that the Scottish court might run into.

### Transfer Statutes (Federal Court to Federal Court)

*Forum non-conveniens* is when we try to relocate a case being litigated, except for a move between federal court to federal court. For a fed-to-fed move, we have other statutes.

> ***NOTE: Do not spend any time in this class picking which of these statutes (§ 1404, § 1406, § 1631) allow what. You can refer to all of these as "the transfer statutes."***

The rationale for using transfer statutes rather than *forum non-conveniens* for fed-to-fed moves is that it's **administratively easier** to transfer the case than dismiss and force plaintiff to re-file elsewhere:
- No paperwork
- No process of serving
- No additional or duplicate filing fees, etc.

<!-- page: 15 -->

#### Three Questions to Analyze When Deciding Whether to Transfer (Fed to Fed)

**1. Must I transfer?**

Must have a "**Happy Court**."
- **Happy courts** are courts that can exercise PJ over the defendant **and** in which venue is proper.
- **Must transfer** if you are in an "**unhappy court**."
- Can **only** transfer to a **Happy Court** (*Hoffman v. Blaski*).
- If the court in which the case is pending is unhappy, **IT MUST** transfer, or dismiss.
  - Unless defendant waives either of those two requirements.
- **Unlike *Piper***, and unlike *forum non-conveniens*, in federal court, a defendant could **not** waive these requirements and create their own forum if the plaintiff could not have originally brought the case in that court in the first place (Happy Court).
  - Unless a plaintiff consents, since it normally has choice in where to file.

```
Hoffman Diagram:
  N.D. Ill. (Chicago) ◄──────────────────── red line (transfer back denied)
         │
         │
  N.D. Tex. (Dallas) ────────────────────► green line (transfer allowed)
```

**2. Even if I don't have to transfer, should I transfer? May I transfer?**

Even though the current court is a happy court, and one of the parties moves to transfer the case, how does the court analyze?
- Apply ***forum non-conveniens* reasoning:**
  - Presume plaintiff was correct in filing (Venue and PJ); **AND**
  - Consider private and public interest factors.

We may wonder why have these statutes if we would allow *forum non-conveniens* anyway — but the rationale is that it's administratively easier to transfer the case than dismiss and force plaintiff to refile elsewhere.

<!-- page: 16 -->

**3. What happens if I do transfer? *(Most difficult of the three questions)***

The difficulty presents itself in the **Choice of Law analysis.**

- Federal court gets a case sitting in **diversity**, and that means, under *Erie*, that it would apply **state substantive law**.
- Because it will be conducting state substantive law, it will apply a choice of law analysis (just as any state would) in order to figure which state's substantive law will apply and govern a particular issue.
- The federal court sitting in diversity will try to figure out whether it will use its own choice of law rules, or use a state's choice of law rules.
- So now as an *Erie* matter:
  - The federal court sitting in a diversity case will use the **state choice of law rules of the state in which it is sitting** (the forum).
  - It therefore considers the COL analysis a **substantive** analysis.
  - This is an *Erie* ruling for transfer purposes (not compelled by FRCP 4(k)(1)(A), which is only used for federal jurisdiction).

> **SO — Anytime there is a fed court to fed court transfer, the HORIZONTAL CHOICE OF LAW analysis will follow the transfer ONLY IF the transferor (originating) court was a happy court.**
>
> **If the transferor (originating) court was an unhappy court, then CHOICE OF LAW ANALYSIS will not follow, in which case, the transferee court should use its forum choice of law analysis and dismiss the case.**

This is the **Greiner Happy Court Rule.**

<!-- page: 17 -->

So if a case gets transferred, we would ask: which state's choice of law analysis will be applied to that case?
- If a case gets transferred from MS to PA, should the PA court use MS choice of law analysis, or PA choice of law analysis?
- The structure we have is that federal courts sitting in diversity apply the state's choice of law analysis of the state in which the federal court is sitting. But if the case moves from one state to another, then we have to understand which state's choice of law analysis to use.

### *Ferens v. John Deere Co.*, 494 U.S. 516 (SCOTUS) (1990)

**Rule:** In a diversity suit, the **transferee forum is required to apply the law of the transferor court, including its choice of law rule, regardless of who initiates the transfer.**

#### *Ferens* Diagram

```
W. Dist. Pa. (Pennsylvania):
  SOL Torts: 2 years | SOL Contracts: Long
  ─────────────────────────────────────────
  Breach of Contract Lawsuit (filed here)
  │
  │ Transfer under § 1404(a) (Plaintiff's motion)
  ▼
D. Miss. (Mississippi):
  SOL Torts: 6 years | SOL Contracts: Long
  ─────────────────────────────────────────
  Tort Lawsuit filed here at Year 3
  (after PA 2-year SOL expired; before MS 6-year SOL expired)
```

**Key distinction:**
- For **horizontal choice of law analysis**: Statutes of limitations are **PROCEDURAL** (use forum law).
- For **vertical choice of law purposes** (*Erie*): Statute of limitations is **SUBSTANTIVE** (*York*).

> ***\*\*\*\*Note that there is a big difference here and that there are two different concepts being discussed — do not let them confuse you:***
>
> - **"HORIZONTAL CHOICE OF LAW ANALYSIS"** is **NOT THE SAME THING** as **"CHOICE OF LAW ANALYSIS FOR TRANSFER PURPOSES"**
>   - **Horizontal Choice of Law analysis** applies to state court cases, determines which state's law will be applied to each claim, and depends on whether the issue is procedural or substantive and IF SUBSTANTIVE, then whether the state is a first restatement or second restatement state.
>   - **For a federal case sitting in diversity**, there will also need to be a choice of law analysis to determine which state's law will be applied in that federal court.
>   - **The court sitting in diversity will use the choice of law of the state in which it sits (*Claxon*).**
>   - A question that arises is which state's choice of law framework to use when a diversity case is transferred from one state to another — and this is when we need to apply the **Greiner Happy Court Rule.**

<!-- page: 18 -->

**Facts:**
- In the following case, there are two different theories by which the plaintiff is suing: Torts and Breach of Contract.
- Limitations periods vary between Tort and Contract cases. Breach of contract SOLs are usually long; Torts SOLs are usually shorter. Also, limitations periods vary for each of these theories in each state.
- In this case, the **tort claim**:
  - SOL in **Pennsylvania**: 2 years long.
  - SOL in **Mississippi**: 6 years long.
- For the breach of contracts claim, the SOL is long for both states.
- Deere is incorporated in MS and has PPOB in MS.
- Accident occurred in Pennsylvania.
- Plaintiffs filed the tort lawsuit **after** the SOL for the tort claim had expired in Pennsylvania.
  - They filed in Mississippi.
- They filed the Breach of Contract Claim in Pennsylvania.
  - The breach of contract lawsuit is irrelevant for understanding the BLL, except that when the plaintiffs tried to transfer their tort lawsuit, part of their argument was that they already had a breach of contract lawsuit pending in Pennsylvania, and so the other case might as well move there too.

Had the plaintiffs filed the torts case in PA, the court would have had to dismiss because the SOL for the torts case would have expired.

**Analysis:**
- Federal courts sitting in diversity have to apply the **choice of law analysis of the state in which it is sitting** (in this case, Pennsylvania).
  - In other words, it will use Pennsylvania state law to analyze the rules for what state law should apply to this case.
    - Like for example, depending on whether Pennsylvania is a first restatement or second restatement state.
  - **WE DO NOT NEED TO KNOW THAT, because for HORIZONTAL CHOICE OF LAW ANALYSIS, Statutes of Limitations are PROCEDURAL.**
    - Additionally, under the analysis for the HORIZONTAL choice of law, statute of limitations is PROCEDURAL, which means we would apply the **law of the forum (also Pennsylvania)**.
    - **\*\*\* NOTE: For VERTICAL choice of law purposes, Statute of limitations is SUBSTANTIVE.**

<!-- page: 19 -->

**Holding:**
- Yes. Under 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a), a court is permitted to transfer a case to another federal court if the interest of justice or the convenience of the parties merits such a transfer.
- The Court in *Van Dusen v. Barrack*, 376 U.S. 612 (1964), held that when a defendant moves for such a transfer, the **choice of law statute of the transferor court is applied in the transferee court**.
- The rationale for this rule is that it:
  - (1) preserves state law advantages,
  - (2) discourages forum shopping, and
  - (3) focuses on the convenience of the parties and the interests of justice in decisions regarding transfer, rather than gamesmanship.
- The concerns that fueled the Court's decision in *Van Dusen* remain relevant when a plaintiff makes a motion for transfer.
- The first rationale applies in this set of facts because no one is disadvantaged from a substantive law perspective when transferor law is used.
- The case instead simply continues using the same law as it would have used without the transfer, which is consistent with the principles of ***Erie R. Co. v. Tompkins***, 304 U.S. 64 (1938).

<!-- page: 20 -->

- In addition, following transferee law would change the substantive law of a case, which contradicts the housekeeping purpose of § 1404(a).
- The second rationale similarly applies, because applying transferor law does not encourage forum shopping. Rather, it simply recognizes the forum shopping that occurs whenever a plaintiff chooses a venue in which to sue.
- That fundamental decision of the plaintiff should not be easily disturbed.
- Finally, the purpose of transfer is for convenience and the interests of justice.
- Performing an examination of the transferee law to ensure the propriety of the transfer would paralyze the transfer system.
- Though this may appear to be rewarding the Ferens' for bad behavior, a different rule would not create a more just result.
- The decision of the court of appeals is reversed and remanded.

### Recap of Question 3 — The Greiner Happy Court Rule

> **Anytime there is a fed court to fed court transfer, the HORIZONTAL CHOICE OF LAW analysis will follow the transfer ONLY IF the transferor (originating) court was a happy court.**
>
> **If the transferor (originating) court was an unhappy court, then CHOICE OF LAW ANALYSIS will not follow, in which case, the transferee court should use its forum choice of law analysis and dismiss the case.**

**Because MS was a happy court, it was able to stay there because the MS choice of law analysis followed.**

**But if the plaintiff filed in an unhappy court, then they will not get the benefit of having that state law apply when they transfer.**

### *Atlantic Marine Construction Co. v. U.S. District Court*, 571 U.S. 49 (SCOTUS) (2013) — Forum Selection Clauses

What happens if all of these things are happening, and the parties suing have a contract with a forum selection clause?

- Venue and PJ are proper, but the Plaintiff filed elsewhere other than where they agreed to in the forum selection clause.

**Three modifications to the standard transfer analysis:**

1. We **no longer presume** that the Plaintiff's choice of forum is correct.
2. When analyzing the factors, we **do not consider them** anymore because the parties have contracted.
3. If we transfer — **Greiner's happy court rules no longer apply.**
   - Instead, if we transfer, will use the **transferee court's state law** to do its choice of law analysis.
