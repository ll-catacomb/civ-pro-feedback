*Solid lines = appeal as of right; dashed lines = discretionary review (petition required)*

- The US government structure is different than that of many other countries.
- The US has state sovereigns and an overall federal sovereign; states are supposed to be sovereign within their spheres, and the federal government is supposed to be sovereign within its spheres of defined power.
- The Supremacy Clause (from the US Constitution) states that when there is a conflict between state law and federal law, the federal law wins because it is supreme.
- The original idea was that the states do most of the governing for their states, even though it hasn't quite worked out that way.
- We have a system of two sovereigns (states and federal government), and you are subject of the state in which you are operating, as well as subject to the federal law.
- Additionally, there are two types of court systems within our country: the state court system and the federal court system.
- This chart shows a little of the relationship between the two court systems.

### Federal Courts

<!-- page: 2 -->

- **US District Courts for the \_\_\_\_\_ of the \_\_\_\_\_\_\_**
  - These are Federal trial courts, analogous to state trial courts
  - Can hear evidence
  - Can sit juries
  - Defined within regions
  - None cross state lines
  - May have more than one in each state

- **US Court of Appeals for the \_\_\_\_th Circuit**
  - Federal intermediate appellate courts, analogous to State Intermediate Appellate Courts
  - Regionally defined
  - Ordinarily encompass more than one state (except for DC, which is treated like a state for these purposes)
  - Circuits are intermediate appellate courts:
    - Cannot hear testimony
    - Cannot try cases

- **United States Supreme Court**
  - Highest court in the United States

### State Courts

- **State Trial Courts**
  - Frequently has subdivisions within it (Housing, Criminal, Family, etc.)
  - Some can sit juries, some cannot:
    - No jury in family courts
    - No jury in housing courts
    - Some juries available in some criminal courts
  - Geographically defined within the state

- **State Intermediate Appellate Courts**
  - Geographically defined within the state
  - Not trial courts, only appellate review
    - No hearing evidence
    - No sitting juries

- **State High Courts**
  - Geographically defined within the state
  - Not trial courts, only appellate review
    - No hearing evidence
    - No sitting juries
  - Frequently, though not always, referred to as the State Supreme Courts

- **United States Supreme Court**
  - Highest court in the United States
  - Sits above both state and federal court systems

<!-- page: 3 -->

- Just like there are federal and states' court systems, there is both federal law and state law (in fact, 50 different states' laws).
- The Supreme Court sits above both state and federal court systems because federal law issues can begin in either the state or federal court systems.
- Being in the FEDERAL COURT SYSTEM tells one nothing about the issues being decided in that court, or whether they are federal law issues or state law issues.
- Federal law issues that began in a state court system may be appealed all the way up to the Supreme Court (dotted/dashed line); however, state law issues that are appealed typically get the final word by the State High Court (and do not reach the US Supreme Court, because the US Supreme Court is a federal court, and typically does not have authority over state law (*Erie*)).
- If a case begins in the federal court system, however, and involves state law issues, it still stays in the federal court system.

**Solid lines v. dashed lines**
- Solid lines indicate that an appeal is "as of right" — if a party loses in the relevant court system, it has the right to appeal (if it believes there was an error of law) to the next higher court, and the next higher court has to adjudicate on that case.
- Dashed lines indicate that the appeal is not "as of right" — in order to get the case heard or adjudicated in that higher court, it must first ask the court to hear the appeal, and the higher court can either agree to or decline to hear (a Petition to hear the case).
  - Same situation on the federal side.
  - For the Supreme Court, it is called a Petition for Certiorari (or Cert Petition).

- The chart above is about the identity of the court system, not about the law that the relevant system is trying, as both federal and state laws are tried in state and federal courts.

### Background Cases, Statutes, and References

<!-- page: 4 -->

#### Cases

- ***Grable & Sons Metal Products, Inc. v. Darue Engineering & Manufacturing*, 545 U.S. 308 (2005) (SCOTUS) and *Smith* — Federal Jurisdiction over State Cause of Action Exception**

  A federal court may have jurisdiction over a state cause of action, if the action has a substantial federal component in actual controversy (in the cause of action), and federal jurisdiction would not disrupt the balance of labor between state and federal courts.

  In other words, a state-law cause of action may give rise to federal question jurisdiction, if the case relies on the application and analysis of a federal law. A state-law cause of action may give rise to a federal question, and thereby give the federal court "arising under" jurisdiction, if the right to relief is based in the application of federal law. In order for "arising under" jurisdiction to be proper, the federal law must actually be in controversy, and this controversy must be substantial. Furthermore, the court must consider whether taking the case would upset the balance of labor between state and federal courts. **(Smith-Grable Exception)**

- ***Strawbridge v. Curtiss*, 7 U.S. (3 Cranch) 267 (1806) (SCOTUS) — Complete Diversity Requirement**

  All defendants must be completely diverse from all plaintiffs in order to obtain federal jurisdiction based on diversity of citizenship. Parties similarly situated have joint interests. Therefore, parties are not diverse if at least one defendant and plaintiff are residents of the same state.

- ***Mas v. Perry*, 489 F.2d 1396 (5th Cir. 1974) — Establishing Domicile**

  To **change** where one is domiciled, a party must both 1) take up residence in a different location; AND 2) intend to stay at the new location.

  ***NOTE:** Do not look at* Mas v. Perry *for establishment of domicile requirements, as they are not correct, but do cite this case for CHANGE of domicile requirements.*

- ***United Mine Workers of America v. Gibbs*, 383 U.S. 715 (1966) (SCOTUS) — Pendent Jurisdiction (now known as Supplemental Jurisdiction, since codified as 28 U.S.C. § 1367)**

  A state claim may proceed in federal court when it accompanies a federal claim that arises from a common nucleus of operative facts.

  In other words, a state-law cause of action is justiciable in federal court alongside a federal claim if one would expect that the two claims be tried in one judicial proceeding.

  ***NOTE:** Supplemental (fka Pendent) Jurisdiction applies only in "arising under" situations. In cases premised on Diversity Jurisdiction, pendent jurisdiction does not apply.*

<!-- page: 5 -->

  Also, if the court dismisses all the federal claims, it also loses jurisdiction over the state claims. A lawsuit based on "arising under" jurisdiction that is left with only state claims may not proceed in federal court.

  Last, if a lawsuit is predominantly based on state claims, dismissing the suit without prejudice may be advisable, so that the plaintiff can re-file in state court (remember there is no other way for the federal court to send the case back to state court).

- **STEP 1:** Do we have a **federal issue embedded in a state law cause of action**?
  - If **no** → must throw out! (*Mottley*) — no § 1331 jurisdiction
  - If **yes** → proceed to STEP 2:

- **STEP 2:** Now we have to consider if they **SHOULD** exercise jurisdiction:

### Declaratory Judgment — 28 U.S.C. § 2201

<!-- page: 14 -->

Derived from *First Federal Savings*:

- In the hypothetical, we have a party anticipating that a lawsuit is coming, and that the bank would be a defendant in it, by anticipating that Schmoe would sue in state court for a breach of contract (state law cause of action) claim.
- For that reason, the Bank decided to file suit (as a plaintiff) in federal court, asking for a declaratory judgment, prior to Schmoe suing them for the breach of contract (state law cause of action), sort of to have an advantage over where the case is filed (picking a more favorable court to the plaintiff usually) — **"seizing the forum"**
- Although not legally, functionally, a quiet title is the same thing and works the same way as a declaratory judgment:
  - In a quiet title (like in *Grable*), they seek a declaration that determines what the title is and who it legally belongs to
  - In the hypo, the Bank wants a declaration that allows them to fire Schmoe without paying the money
- In the hypo, the Bank's tactic does not work because the court interprets that Congress did not enact 28 U.S.C. § 2201 with the intent to create causes of action for the purpose of obtaining declaratory judgments; instead, it creates "remedies."
- If the hypo was changed so that Schmoe, being an African American, would sue for being fired for discrimination, then there would be federal subject matter jurisdiction.

**Declaratory Judgment does not create a cause of action. It creates a remedy!**

So, how do you determine whether there is federal subject matter jurisdiction in a declaratory judgment?

**ANALYSIS OF COERCIVE ACTION RULE** (bka **"Greiner imaginary lawsuit rule"**): Way to figure out and apply whether there is arising under or diversity jurisdiction (SMJ) over the 28 U.S.C. § 2201 lawsuit, in a **declaratory judgment situation:**

- **STEP 1:** Imagine the lawsuit that would have been filed if 28 U.S.C. § 2201 had not been passed (imagine what that lawsuit would look like independently); **and**
- **STEP 2:** See whether there would be independent basis for federal SMJ in that lawsuit (either using arising under or diversity jurisdiction) — and that would give you an answer of whether there is SMJ over the 28 U.S.C. § 2201 lawsuit.

## Diversity Jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. § 1332)

**Big Question:** When can a plaintiff initiate a lawsuit in federal court?
- One way: Subject Matter Jurisdiction (notes above)
- Another way: Diversity Jurisdiction (notes below)

**YOU DON'T NEED BOTH; ONLY ONE WILL DO!**

Most state law causes of action can be brought to federal court if they have "complete Diversity." Although in some cases there may be both, in order to be in federal court, we only need one.

### Basic Terminology

**Has nothing to do with where the court is located; only the parties on each side of the lawsuit.**

- **Minimal Diversity:** (Some states on both sides of the lawsuit) — at least one plaintiff is diverse from at least one defendant AND at least one plaintiff is from the same state as at least one defendant

  ```
  P1 (SC) ──────────────────→ D1 (VA)
  P2 (SC) ─────────────────╲→ D2 (SC)
                             ╲
  ```
  *(Arrows cross: P1(SC) and P2(SC) sue D1(VA) and D2(SC) — some diversity, but not complete because P2 and D2 share SC)*

- **Complete Diversity:** (No state on both sides of the lawsuit) — ALL plaintiffs are diverse from ALL defendants

  ```
  P1 (SC) ──────────────────→ D1 (VA)
  P2 (SC) ─────────────────╲→ D2 (NC)
  ```
  *(All plaintiffs (SC) diverse from all defendants (VA, NC))*

<!-- page: 16 -->

For Diversity Jurisdiction, just like for Arising Under Jurisdiction, there is also a Constitutional grant of power, and also a statute; and just like with Arising Under Jurisdiction, the statute for Diversity Jurisdiction is also **narrower** than the Constitutional provision addressing the diversity power.

**Constitutional provision** (U.S. Const. Art. III, § 2, cl. 1):

> "The judicial power shall extend to all cases, in law and equity, arising under this Constitution, the laws of the United States, and treaties made, or which shall be made, under their authority;--to all cases affecting ambassadors, other public ministers and consuls;--to all cases of admiralty and maritime jurisdiction;--to controversies to which the United States shall be a party;--to controversies between two or more states;--**between a state and citizens of another state;--between citizens of different states;**--between citizens of the same state claiming lands under grants of different states, and between a state, or the citizens thereof, and foreign states, citizens or subjects."

*(Highlighted language = the diversity-relevant provision; Constitution requires only minimal diversity)*

**Statutory comparison — 28 U.S.C. § 1332(a) — COMPLETE DIVERSITY REQUIREMENT:**

> "(a) The district courts shall have original jurisdiction of all civil actions where the matter in controversy (AIC) exceeds the sum or value of $75,000, exclusive of interest and costs, and is **between**—
> > (1) *citizens of different States;*
> > (2) citizens of a State and citizens or subjects of a foreign state, except that the district courts shall not have original jurisdiction under this subsection of an action between citizens of a State and citizens or subjects of a foreign state who are lawfully admitted for permanent residence in the United States and are domiciled in the same State;

<!-- page: 17 -->

> > (3) citizens of different States and in which citizens or subjects of a foreign state are additional parties; and
> > (4) a foreign state, defined in section 1603(a) of this title, as plaintiff and citizens of a State or of different States."

Just like with Arising Under Jurisdiction, the language in the Constitution matches that in the statute, but the statutory scope of diversity power is **narrower** than that of the Constitution. The Constitution also authorizes Congress to enact statutes that only require minimal diversity. However, the judicial branch interpreted the statute to be narrower in scope than the Constitutional provision.

The citation for this is *Strawbridge v. Curtiss*.

**Why do people care about complete versus minimal diversity?**
- Supreme Court interpreted the statute to mean that there needs to be complete diversity on both sides of the lawsuit. (*Strawbridge v. Curtiss*)
- The US Constitution requires minimal diversity only, and Congress previously passed a statute requiring only minimal diversity.
- The statute (28 U.S.C. § 1332) does not state that there is a need for "complete" diversity; nevertheless, it has been interpreted by the SCOTUS to require it.
  - Harder to achieve; but
  - It also might protect against any out-of-state bias (**"home-cooking"**) (rationale for complete diversity)
- *Strawbridge* likely narrows the scope to require complete diversity to keep better control of the cases brought into federal court.

**The Diversity statute (28 U.S.C. § 1332) has two main requirements:**
1. Amount in controversy (AIC): $75,000; and
2. Complete diversity (as interpreted in *Strawbridge*)

- Sometimes the "homecooking" rationale can look funny, and there is currently an idea that this issue should be revisited by Congress:
  - What happens if there is a plaintiff from SC and a defendant from NC (AIC $80K), and files a suit in federal court located in SC?
    - There is SMJ (but do not know at this point if there is "Personal Jurisdiction" — to be studied in later classes)

**28 U.S.C. § 1332(b) — AMOUNT IN CONTROVERSY (AIC) REQUIREMENT**

> "(b) Except when express provision therefor is otherwise made in a statute of the United States, where the plaintiff who files the case originally in the Federal courts is finally adjudged to be entitled to recover less than the sum or value of $75,000, computed without regard to any setoff or counterclaim to which the defendant may be adjudged to

<!-- page: 18 -->

> be entitled, and exclusive of interest and costs, the district court may deny costs to the plaintiff and, in addition, may impose costs on the plaintiff."

One should assume the AIC is whatever the plaintiff says, unless it's obviously not right.

**28 U.S.C. § 1332(c) — CITIZENSHIP REQUIREMENT**

> "(1) a corporation shall be deemed to be a citizen of every State and foreign state by which it has been incorporated and of the State or foreign state where it has its principal place of business, except that in any direct action against the insurer of a policy or contract of liability insurance, whether incorporated or unincorporated, to which action the insured is not joined as a party-defendant, such insurer shall be deemed a citizen of—
> > (A) every State and foreign state of which the insured is a citizen;
> > (B) every State and foreign state by which the insurer has been incorporated; and
> > (C) the State or foreign state where the insurer has its principal place of business; and
>
> (2) the legal representative of the estate of a decedent shall be deemed to be a citizen only of the same State as the decedent, and the legal representative of an infant or incompetent shall be deemed to be a citizen only of the same State as the infant or incompetent."

**In other words:**

- **Corporations** (can be citizen of multiple states — makes it harder to get "complete" diversity):
  - Citizens of the state where they are incorporated; **AND**
  - The state where their Principal Place of Business is located (i.e., its corporate headquarters; where main decisions are made and board meets, *not* where major corporate activity takes place) (*Hertz* — case not read because it was sooo boring)
  - Also, corporations are not "domiciled"; that word is reserved for human beings only. Corporations are instead referred to as "citizens" of a certain state.

- **Incorporeal entities that are not corporations** (business partnerships, labor unions, charities):
  - Citizen of every state in which their members are (for international unions, for example, all 50 states)
  - If you're from "outside of the United States" on both sides, you do not have complete diversity.

- **Human beings** (see Professor Greiner's Life assignment below):
  - State in which they are "**domiciled**" (see *Mas v. Perry* for CHANGE of domicile)
