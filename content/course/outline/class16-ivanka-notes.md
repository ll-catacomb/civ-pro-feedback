## Background Cases, Statutes, and References

### Cases

- ***Scott v. Harris*, 477 U.S. 317 (1986)**

  In deciding a summary judgment motion, all facts are viewed in the light most favorable to the non-moving party unless no reasonable jury would be able to accept that version of the facts.

- ***McCants v. Ford Motor Co.*, 781 F.2d 855 (11th Cir. 1986)**

  (1) In most cases, a voluntary dismissal should be granted unless the defendant will suffer clear legal prejudice, other than the mere prospect of a subsequent lawsuit; and, (2) when the defendant has been put to considerable expense, then dismissal is not warranted except on condition that plaintiff reimburse defendant for a portion of his expenses, including attorneys' fees.

- ***Wojtas v. Capital Guardian Trust Co.*, 477 F.3d 924 (7th Cir. 2007)**

  Plaintiff's failure to respond to the statute of limitations argument constitutes a waiver of the same, and, it would be an abuse of the District Court's discretion to permit voluntary dismissal when "the defendant would suffer 'plain legal prejudice' as a result."

- ***Atlas Roofing Co. v. OSHA*, 430 U.S. 442 (SCOTUS)(1977)**

  At least in cases where public rights are being litigated (for example, cases in which the government sues in its sovereign capacity to enforce public rights created by statutes within the power of Congress to enact) the Seventh Amendment does not prohibit Congress from assigning the factfinding function and initial adjudication to an administrative forum with which the jury would be incompatible.

- ***Granfinanciera, S.A. v. Nordberg* (SCOTUS)(1989)**

  Bankruptcy courts, like administrative agencies, are not necessarily outside of the Seventh Amendment and therefore may sometimes hold trials by jury.

- ***Chauffers, Teamsters and Helpers Local 391 v. Terry*, 494 U.S. 558 (SCOTUS)(1990)**

  To determine whether a particular action will resolve *legal* rights, we examine both the nature of the issues involved and the remedy sought, focusing more on the remedy sought.

### Sources of Law

#### U.S. Constitution — Amendment VII

In suits at common law, where the value in controversy shall exceed twenty dollars, the right of trial by jury shall be preserved, and no fact tried by a jury, shall be otherwise reexamined in any court of the United States, than according to the rules of the common law.

<!-- page: 2 -->

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 38 — Right to a Jury Trial; Demand

**(a) Right Preserved.** The right of trial by jury as declared by the Seventh Amendment to the Constitution — or as provided by a federal statute — is preserved to the parties inviolate.

**(b) Demand.** On any issue triable of right by a jury, a party may demand a jury trial by:

> (1) serving the other parties with a written demand — which may be included in a pleading — no later than 14 days after the last pleading directed to the issue is served; and
>
> (2) filing the demand in accordance with Rule 5(d).

**(c) Specifying Issues.** In its demand, a party may specify the issues that it wishes to have tried by a jury; otherwise, it is considered to have demanded a jury trial on all the issues so triable. If the party has demanded a jury trial on only some issues, any other party may — within 14 days after being served with the demand or within a shorter time ordered by the court — serve a demand for a jury trial on any other or all factual issues triable by jury.

**(d) Waiver; Withdrawal.** A party waives a jury trial unless its demand is properly served and filed. A proper demand may be withdrawn only if the parties consent.

**(e) Admiralty and Maritime Claims.** These rules do not create a right to a jury trial on issues in a claim that is an admiralty or maritime claim under Rule 9(h).

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 39 — Trial by Jury or by the Court

**(a) When a Demand Is Made.** When a jury trial has been demanded under Rule 38, the action must be designated on the docket as a jury action. The trial on all issues so demanded must be by jury unless:

> (1) the parties or their attorneys file a stipulation to a nonjury trial or so stipulate on the record; or
>
> (2) the court, on motion or on its own, finds that on some or all of those issues there is no federal right to a jury trial.

**(b) When No Demand Is Made.** Issues on which a jury trial is not properly demanded are to be tried by the court. But the court may, on motion, order a jury trial on any issue for which a jury might have been demanded.

**(c) Advisory Jury; Jury Trial by Consent.** In an action not triable of right by a jury, the court, on motion or on its own:

> (1) may try any issue with an advisory jury; or
>
> (2) may, with the parties' consent, try any issue by a jury whose verdict has the same effect as if a jury trial had been a matter of right, unless the action is against the United States and a federal statute provides for a nonjury trial.

<!-- page: 3 -->

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 41 — Voluntary Dismissal

**(1) By the Plaintiff.**

**(A) Without a Court Order.** Subject to Rules 23(e), 23.1(c), 23.2, and 66 and any applicable federal statute, the plaintiff may dismiss an action without a court order by filing:

> (i) a notice of dismissal before the opposing party serves either an answer or a motion for summary judgment; or
>
> (ii) a stipulation of dismissal signed by all parties who have appeared.

**(B) Effect.** Unless the notice or stipulation states otherwise, the dismissal is without prejudice. But if the plaintiff previously dismissed any federal- or state-court action based on or including the same claim, a notice of dismissal operates as an adjudication on the merits.

**(2) By Court Order; Effect.** Except as provided in Rule 41(a)(1), an action may be dismissed at the plaintiff's request only by court order, on terms that the court considers proper. If a defendant has pleaded a counterclaim before being served with the plaintiff's motion to dismiss, the action may be dismissed over the defendant's objection only if the counterclaim can remain pending for independent adjudication. Unless the order states otherwise, a dismissal under this paragraph (2) is without prejudice.

*(This specific rule written immediately below applies the facts from the Jones v. Clinton case to the law, assuming that we are in J. Brennan's second option)*

**BLL: If the moving party that does not bear the burden of persuasion at trial can discharge (fail to meet) its initial burden of production in the summary judgment context:**

- If the **moving party** fails to provide evidence to affirmatively knock out an element of the non-moving party's claim or defense, or
- If the **moving party** fails to demonstrate sufficiently that there is no evidence to support a key element of the opposing party's claim, then,

**The judge does not need to analytically go any further. He will deny the summary judgment motion and go to trial (presumption always is to go to trial).**

- If the **moving party** meets that **burden of production**, then the **burden of production** would shift to the **nonmoving party**.
  - The **burden of production** for the **nonmoving party** would be to show that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to resolve.

<!-- page: 6 -->

  - If the **nonmoving party** would be able to show (meets its **burden of production**) that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to resolve, then we go to trial; but
  - If the **nonmoving party** fails to meet its **burden of production** that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to resolve, then we will grant the summary judgment motion and not go to trial.

**In deciding a summary judgment motion, all facts are viewed in the light most favorable to the non-moving party unless no reasonable trier of fact would be able to accept that version of the facts.**

### *Scott v. Harris*, 550 U.S. 372 (2007) — Application

In *Scott v. Harris*, there was a dispute as to whether the police used excessive force, when, in a high-speed chase, the defendant was moved off the road by the police officers.

When the deputy attempted to pull Harris over, Harris fled. The original deputy and several others gave chase over ten miles at significant speeds. A camera in Deputy Timothy Scott's (defendant) cruiser captured the chase. The video showed Harris driving at high speeds and swerving on the motorway as well as driving through red lights. During the chase, Scott hit Harris' back bumper causing Harris' car to leave the roadway and hit a tree in a nearby embankment. As a result of the crash, Harris became a quadriplegic. Scott filed a motion for summary judgment, arguing that he enjoyed qualified immunity as a police officer. The district court denied his motion, holding that there were disagreements as to material facts.

In a motion for summary judgment, the court views all facts in the light most favorable to the non-moving party. Furthermore, a motion for summary judgment can only be granted if there is no genuine dispute of material fact. **When one view of the facts is directly contradicted by evidence such that no reasonable jury could agree with the former version, then there is no dispute.**

In *Scott v. Harris*, both the district court and the court of appeals held that Harris was not a danger to bystanders on the road. However, the video of the chase demanded a contrary conclusion; that Harris was driving dangerously and posed a threat to those around him. **The video was determined to be sufficiently compelling that no jury could accept Harris' version of events in light of the tape.**

### Consolidated BLL — J. Brennan's Analysis to Summary Judgment Motions

Notice that in Motions for Summary Judgment, we begin at the same place as we have to with Motions to Dismiss, under FRCP 12(b)(6) "*Twiqbal*" type analysis:

- A clear understanding of what are the elements of the cause of action
- Narrow the issues
  - Pick the issue that is easier to show proof of (or lack of proof, depending on who you are representing)
- Then hone in and show whether there was not sufficient evidence to prove one of the elements

**BLL — J. Brennan's Analysis to Summary Judgment Motions:**

**1) Brennan's dissent instructs us to first ask the question:**

On the **issue that is the subject of the summary judgment motion**, who has the **burden of persuasion at trial**?

- Is it the moving party? Or,
- Is it the nonmoving party?

**2) The ANALYSIS depends on the answer to this question.**

**If the moving party** (could be either the Plaintiff or the Defendant) **has the burden of persuasion** for a specific element, as regards to the issue that is the subject of the summary judgment at trial, then there is **one mode of analysis** we use:

> The burden of production is to produce evidence which if uncontradicted, would compel a rational trier of fact in its favor, even if drawing all reasonable inferences in favor of the non-moving party.
>
> - If this party meets its **burden of production** then the burden of production shifts to the nonmoving party to articulate evidence in argument that would demonstrate that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to solve.
>   - We don't require an amount of evidence that would compel a verdict in its favor — just that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to solve.

**If the non-moving party** (could be either the Plaintiff or the Defendant) **has the burden of persuasion** for a specific element at trial, then there are **two modes of analysis** we use:

<!-- page: 12 -->

> **1) Produce evidence, which if uncontradicted, would affirmatively negate one of the moving party's essential elements of the claim**, such that if uncontradicted, the evidence would compel a trier of fact to find in its favor;
>
> - If this party meets its burden of production then the burden of production shifts to the moving party to articulate evidence in argument that would demonstrate that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to solve.
>   - We don't require an amount of evidence that would compel a verdict in its favor — just that there is a genuine issue of fact that requires a trial to solve.
>
> **or**
>
> **2) Claim that the moving party's evidence is in perfect equipoise**, in other words, did not show enough evidence to meet its burden (to tip the scale in its favor), such that:
>
> - That means looking at the record and demonstrating that there is a lack of evidence from which a trier of fact could find or infer, (the proof by the preponderance of the evidence of a key element the cause of action), in the nonmoving party's favor.
> - If this party meets its burden of production then the burden of production shifts to the nonmoving party to articulate evidence in argument that would demonstrate that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to solve.
>   - We don't require an amount of evidence that would compel a verdict in its favor — just that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to solve.

The whole point of summary judgment is to find out whether we need to go to trial in this case or not after discovery.

### Distinction Between Law v. Equity

The lecture today discusses the distribution of power between a judge and a jury, and what a judge must do to exercise the power vis-à-vis the jury, or, when the judge exercises fact-finding power when there is no jury present.

Summary Judgment poses the issue of distribution of power between a judge and jury, because the Motion to Dismiss and the Motion for Summary Judgment effectively prevent us from getting to a jury.

<!-- page: 13 -->

The first point to discuss goes to the issue of whether there is a jury available at all, or whether the issues of fact will be decided by the judge and not a jury.

- For example, the judge will hear live testimony
- The judge will decide who is telling the truth vs. not
- The judge will decide the implications of documents

Our discussion right now is not functional and will not make too much sense nor does it involve a lot of rationality; **instead, it involves history.**

### Remedies: Law Versus Equity

In order to understand the difference between law and equity, we need to understand some basics about remedies:

**LAW:**

- **Compensatory damages** are monetary compensation for actual injury or economic loss, in efforts to make the Plaintiff whole.
- **Punitive damages** are monetary damages awarded to the Plaintiff over and above just compensation in order to punish the defendant and serve as a deterrent.
- **Ejectment** is an action to recover possession of real property.
- **Mandamus** (as in writ of mandamus) is a writ ordering a public agency, or governmental official to perform an act required by law.

**EQUITY:**

- **Injunction** is a form of equitable relief in which a party is ordered to perform or refrain from performing a certain act. The order typically comes in the form to "cease and desist" from doing something; even when the order "to-do" something, it usually comes in the form of a double negative, for example "you shall cease and desist from not doing X…," which would effectively be an order "to do X."
- **Disgorgement of ill-gotten gains** is a remedy in a breach of contract action allowing a plaintiff to recover the profits realized by the breaching party.
- **Restitution**, generally, is the return or recompense of something wrongfully taken from its rightful owner. May also refer to a specific remedy in equity, which generally requires a person to give up unjustly acquired gain.
- **Constructive Trust** is a legal fiction employed by courts of equity to compel the owner of property to convey that property to the victims of that owner's fraud. Generally, the goal of a constructive trust is to prevent unjust enrichment of a party who receives title to property but is not legally entitled to that property.
- **Enforcement of a Trust** is when courts use their authority to ensure that the Trust's covenants are followed by the Trustees.
- **Contract Rescission** is a judicial order declaring a contract a nullity and returning all parties to the same positions they were in before entering into the contract.
- **Contract Reformation** is an equitable remedy of reformation of a contract based on mistake, fraud, or other equitable grounds.
- **Unjust Enrichment** is a quasi-contract theory that prevents a party from retaining a benefit conferred on that party to the detriment of the opposing party, if it would be unjust for the enriched party to retain that benefit.
- **Quantum Meruit** is a remedy entitling the provider of services to be compensated for the value thereof.

<!-- page: 14 -->

In looking at the chart above, it does not appear that there is a clean line or distinction between the remedies — for example, the ones that require a monetary exchange versus a demand to perform or not perform an action. For example, ejectment does not involve an exchange of money and unjust enrichment, as well as Quantum Meruit, usually do.

Therefore, we need to figure out the history of this distinction and why we have this.

### Historical Origins of Law and Equity — A Cartoon History

Approximately about 1000 years ago, there was a king, and everyone thought that all rights, responsibilities, and anything stemming from law, stemmed from the king and his power.

As time evolved, kings had better things to do than to resolve private disputes between parties. For example, they need to go fight wars, dispute the power of religious authorities, etc.

As a result, they created a system of courts called a **King's Bench**. The King's Bench proceeded to create a system to pursue what are the precursor to the modern day causes of action — various forms of writs that people paid for to be issued that initiated court proceedings — and so their Kings' Bench begins to administer laws and grow the system.

Their procedures were nonsensical, difficult to follow, etc. They eventually made it so that people did not go to the court because there is no remedy, or they have gone to the court and could not get a remedy even though they thought they deserved it.

They therefore go to the king and let him know that the courts were not working. The king had better things to do than to deal with individual petitions, so he decided the **Chancellor** could take care of it. During this time, the Chancellor could read, write, was learned, could read the law according to whatever sources of law there were at that time, and had control of the king's seal.

The Chancellor would take a petition and investigate it. He knew he was not superior to the King's Bench, which was recognized as a court system, so he decided then to issue subpoenas — formal written documents directing a party to appear in court or produce documents — when he needed to make inquiries about individual disputes.

One of the things the Chancellor could do was find there was some great injustice by a previous proceeding in the King's Bench. If he indeed found a great injustice, he did not want to outright quash a decision made by the King's Bench. Instead, he issued an order to the appropriate party saying that they will be incarcerated if they try to enforce that King's Bench order.

<!-- page: 15 -->

When the King's Bench would hear about that, they did not particularly like it. But, the Chancellor would justify the action by saying that he did not directly kill the King's Bench judgment, but that instead was only using the powers of the office he holds.

At the same time, the Chancellor was not just effectively overruling the King's Bench, but also starts to develop a limited independent law that the King's Bench did not recognize. This was essentially creating a law of trust.

We have studied trusts before when we discussed *Hanson v. Denkla* and *Mullane*.

Trusts were thought to be a useful way to organize money and to have money and property disposed of in ways that would be impossible to do otherwise. But, the King rendered them useless.

Ultimately, the King's Bench was creating common law by deciding cases, and the Chancellor was also creating law of trust by deciding cases, common law style.

A high official in the King's Bench picks a fight with the Chancellor, and thinking he would get a good judgment from the King, asks the Chancellor to stop, and asks the King to tell the Chancellor to stop. But, the high official misjudged how much he was in favor of the King — ultimately the King issues a final order saying that the Chancellor will continue to operate as he had been operating.

By this time there was too much business for an individual Chancellor to dispose of, so there were many other mini Chancellors adjudicating these petitions, effectively creating a separate legal system. This is called **equity** because the Chancellors were free to issue judgments that were less in line with the strictness of the law, but more in line with fairness, equity and justice.

The equity court system was careful, despite its recent victory, not to encroach so much upon the power of the King's Bench system, so its remedies were limited. In fact, one of its policies was that in order to invoke equity, unless there was **no adequate remedy at law** — which is, for example, effectively embedded in the preliminary injunction jurisprudence that we have read, which requires there to be **irreparable harm**, meaning that the law system is unable to remedy, at least quickly enough, the situation that the moving party is facing.

Even though this all took place a long time ago and may seem irrelevant now, because we adopted the English legal system when the colonies formed here after immigrating from England. Some of the colonies immediately abolished the English legal system and the rule of the king right away, some others dispensed with this dual system after becoming states, but many others continued with this system making a distinction between law and equity.

<!-- page: 16 -->

Also at this time, during the colonial era of the US and the early foundings of the US government, they had separate judges in separate buildings for these separate dual legal systems.

Petitioners had to decide whether they wanted to file suit, either in the court of equity, or the court of law.

To make matters more complex, at the time of the founding we also get the **Seventh Amendment** of the US Constitution:

> **Amendment VII:** In suits at common law *(discussing the law side of the law v. equity distinction)*, where the value in controversy shall exceed twenty dollars *(literally means $20, even today, as it was not indexed for inflation)*, the right of trial by jury shall be preserved *(this is where we run into the issue, because it turns out that the King's Bench, all of this time, had been adjudicating factual disputes with juries, whereas the Chancellor and his successors were not using juries, but nonetheless, due to this Constitutional Amendment, the jury component had to be preserved the right to a jury trial in suits of common law)*, and no fact tried by a jury, shall be otherwise reexamined in any court of the United States, than according to the rules of the common law.

It was not an issue when even the federal courts maintained the differences between law and equity, but when we created the lower courts, we did not maintain or create separate law and equity judges. At that point, there were innovations by the states that instead of creating separate judges, they would just separate the dockets and filings into cases of equity or cases of law.

But, once we created these two systems of power and placed them under a single judge, we began to merge the two systems together. The merger in the federal court system was essentially completed when we enacted the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, specifically **FRCP 1**, which states that there is "one form of civil action." That meant that there was no longer a distinction between law and equity.

The issue is that the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure **cannot overrule the Constitution**, so we still have the Seventh Amendment, causing trouble and confusion moving forward.

So why does it cause a problem? **Two reasons:** newly created causes of action and newly created adjudicators.

<!-- page: 17 -->

We unfortunately cannot say "this is a cause of action that would have been on the law side of the house and therefore preserves the right to a jury trial, and that was a cause of action on the equity side of the house so there is no right a jury trial," because Congress and even judges in some cases began creating **new causes of action**, which were not available before under either of the two previously used systems.

These new causes of action were not just new, they were created to effectuate rights by certain organizations where the existence of such organizations themselves would have been illegal at the time of the founding of the US Constitution, in particular **Labor Unions**, which were essentially illegal horizontal restraints of trade *(because what they do is fix prices and wages, and raise them above levels which market forces would have them)*.

Therefore, suits by labor unions were by definition, causes of action that were not recognized at the time of the Constitution, which causes trouble when there are jury trial demands in a dispute between a Labor Union and another party, because we have to figure out whether the Seventh Amendment compels us to honor them.

Secondly, there is also a second issue called the **Administrative State**, which begins to introduce us to new forms of decision makers which are not recognized as traditional judges.

So the fundamental problem is that we have to figure out how to deal with these two issues given the fact that we have the Seventh Amendment, demanding the right to trial by jury, which is in some way defined historically.

Another detail is that the Seventh Amendment applies to the federal courts. The SCOTUS has **never held** that the Seventh Amendment applies to the States; in fact, it has held to the contrary. Therefore, many states do not offer rights to a jury trial in civil suits, whereas the federal government might sometimes be compelled to do so.

**What is the situation now in the United States with respect to law and equity?**

- Many states have dispensed with this distinction.
- Some have merged law and equity by maintaining a single judge but separate dockets.
- Some states continue to maintain not just separate dockets, but also separate judiciaries.
  - They have a system of Chancellors; and
  - They have a system of Judges and courts.
  - One of these states, for example, is **Delaware**.

There are many corporations created and incorporated in Delaware, and as a result, there is tremendous amounts of commercial law developed by the **Delaware Chancery court**, which is now the successor of the first Chancellor in administering equity law. But, because everyone incorporates in Delaware, even though it is a tiny state, it almost governs all of the commercial law jurisprudence, at least in the entire northeast of the United States.

<!-- page: 18 -->

With that said, we will first go over how we are supposed to analyze newly created causes of action (*Chauffeurs v. Terry*), and the second thing will be to figure out what to do with new adjudicators (*Atlas Roofing v. OSHA*).

## Newly Created Causes of Action — *Chauffeurs v. Terry*

### *Chauffers, Teamsters and Helpers Local 391 v. Terry*, 494 U.S. 558 (SCOTUS)(1990)

**BLL:** To determine whether a particular action will resolve *legal* rights, we examine both the nature of the issues involved and the remedy sought, focusing more on the remedy sought.

**Simplified and Hypothesized Facts:**

- A group of truck drivers are not happy with the wages that they receive so they get together and form a Union.
- The union negotiates a collective bargaining agreement with an employer.
- Part of the collective bargaining agreement includes two things that should affect the wages received by the truck drivers:
  - One is that the long-haul routes should be paid a bit more (which are also more desirable because one can quickly get the required driving quota for the week out of the way)
  - The second thing is that seniority should carry its benefits and that senior drivers should get paid more.
- Another part of the collective bargaining agreement includes an **arbitration committee**, which has some representatives of the union and some representatives of the employer, which get together to arbitrate disputes arising from the collective bargaining agreement.
- Additionally, the collective bargaining agreement says that more senior truckers should have priority in being assigned to the longer-haul routes that are getting the higher wages.
  - The result of this is that the employers would have to pay more money because the more senior truckers get more pay, and the longer routes get more pay.
- The employer might realize that and decide that if he assigned some subordinate truckers, he could save some money.
- The senior truckers were upset that the employer was not assigning routes in accordance with the collective bargaining agreement, so they complained to the union.
- The union brings the dispute to the arbitration committee.
- The arbitration committee decides that the employer did not violate the collective bargaining agreement.
- Some of the truckers realize that although action was taken by the arbitration committee, the resulting decision was still not fair, thus requesting the union to look into the matter, but the union says that the issues being brought up are very similar to the ones prior, which have been adjudicated by the arbitration committee, and so the union refuses to bring the matter to them again.
- The truckers therefore **sued the Union itself for the "breach in its duty to provide fair representation."** (newly created cause of action)
  - This is a lawsuit by the represented union members, against the Union itself, for a breach of a duty of fair representation, in which the nature of breach was the union's failure to invoke the collective bargaining agreement and raise this matter with the grievance committee again.
  - Very analogous to a breach of contract suit, basically saying that the employer is violating the contract, by not assigning the long-haul routes to the senior employees.
- Someone in the lawsuit demands a jury trial.

<!-- page: 19 -->

**Holding (BLL when analyzing whether a cause of action involves law or equity):**

**To determine whether a particular action will resolve *legal* rights, we examine both the nature of the issues involved and the remedy sought.**

1. **First, compare the statutory action to 18th century actions brought in the courts of England prior to the merger of the courts of law and equity.**
2. **Second, examine the remedy sought** (which also involves a historical comparison, but more limited) **and determine whether it is legal or equitable in nature.**
   - **The second inquiry is the more important in the analysis.**

**APPLICATION of the law to the facts in *Terry*:**

#### Step One — Compare to 18th Century Actions

Breach of duty of representation was an **unknown cause of action** in the 18th century. The Court discusses three possible analogies to 18th century causes of action:

**(a) Suit to vacate arbitration award:**

Union contends that this resembles a suit to vacate arbitration award because truckers seek to set aside the result of the grievance process, which historically was **equitable** in nature.

Court decides that if it is true that this cause of action mostly resembles a suit to vacate an arbitration agreement, then it would have been an equitable cause of action, and therefore would not grant a right to a jury trial.

However, the specific claim in this case is that the **Union breached its duty of fair representation, by its failure to invoke the collective bargaining agreement against the employer** — not that the arbitration committee sat and ruled. Therefore, the court decides that these two claims are not analogous at all.

<!-- page: 20 -->

**(b) Action by a trust beneficiary against a trustee for breach of fiduciary duty:**

Union next argues that truckers' duty of fair representation lawsuit resembles an action by a trust beneficiary against a trustee for breach of fiduciary duty, which historically was **equitable** in nature.

Structural comparison of Chauffeurs to Hanson trust relationship:

| Trust (*Hanson v. Denkla*) | Union (*Chauffeurs v. Terry*) |
|---|---|
| Rich person gives money to trust | Truckers give up right to act alone; form collective group |
| Trust has a contract with trustee; trustee has fiduciary duty | Union granted power to negotiate employment rights and benefits with employer, in exchange for fiduciary duty of fair representation |
| Trustee administers the trust and gives the money that the trust earns to the beneficiaries | Union administers the truckers' collective agreement and represents their collective best interests against the employer |
| Rich person can be a lifetime beneficiary (life estate), but can no longer control the trust | Truckers are themselves beneficiaries of the Union's collective bargaining agreement, though they gave up individual exercise of employment rights |
| Trustee makes contracts on behalf of trust with other entities | Union created the collective bargaining agreement with the employers |
| If another entity breaches its contract with the trust, the trustee might bring a lawsuit against that entity for breach of contract | Here the employer breached its collective bargaining contract; the truckers wanted the Union therefore to bring grievance to the arbitration committee against the employer for breach of contract |
| If beneficiaries think the trustee erroneously refused to sue another business for breach of contract, they sue the trustee for breach of fiduciary duty | Here the truckers think the Union erroneously refused to bring a grievance; they cannot sue the employer directly for breach of contract (they gave up that right); so instead they are suing the Union for breach of fiduciary duty |

<!-- page: 21 -->

Court decides that as far as the first factor is concerned, this is more comparable to a trust action, which historically was an equitable action in nature. However, in this case, they decide that they are not persuaded enough to characterize this specific cause of action as wholly equitable.

They decide that the application of the Seventh Amendment depends on the **nature of the issue to be tried** rather than the character of the overall action.

Court points out that while a regular trust action has one prong that the beneficiaries need to prove (that the trustee violated his fiduciary duty to trust, and therefore to the beneficiaries), this action has **two prongs** the truckers would have to prove:

1. That the **employer** violated its Collective Bargaining Agreement against the Union;
   *(analogous to a breach of contract — typical **legal** claim)*

   **and**

2. That the **Union** breached its fiduciary duty of fair representation.
   *(analogous to a breach of fiduciary duty — typical **equitable** claim)*

The court explains that because this specific claim involves two separate issues, one of law and one of equity, the Seventh Amendment historical analysis leaves them in equipoise as to whether truckers are entitled to a jury trial.

Due to the **Cleanup Doctrine**, which states that the courts recognize that there are certain categories of cases which involve both law and equity components, this entire lawsuit would have likely been litigated in front of the Chancellor, because in order to litigate those cleanly, they would be dealt with by the same adjudicator, which they previously decided would be one of equity.

In attempt to interpret the policy intent of the SCOTUS' decision here, it seems that they were beginning to show a **preference for trial by jury** cases, any time it is possible to do so.

**(c) Attorney malpractice suit:**

Truckers argue that their lawsuit is less like a trust action and most comparable to an attorney malpractice action, which historically was a **law** (legal) cause of action, which would have entitled a right to a jury trial.

Truckers claim that the Union in this case would be analogous to the Attorney, and that they would be analogous to the client.

<!-- page: 22 -->

SCOTUS claims that this comparison is no good, for the following structural distinctions:

- Unlike Clients, employees represented by the Union **cannot control** the significant decisions by the Union regarding their individual representation
- Unlike Clients, who can fire their attorney if dissatisfied with the attorney's performance, truckers **cannot fire the Union**
- Unlike clients, who are the decision-makers when deciding to settle a case, truckers are **not the decision-makers** when deciding to settle a claim; instead, the Union would be the final decision-maker when deciding to settle claims

After the first part of the analysis, the court here recognizes that the stronger of the three analogies was option (b), which is the comparison to the trust action (which is more analogous to a historical issue of equity). However, it also mentions that the issue at hand is also analogous to a breach of contract claim (which is analogous to a historical issue of law). It concludes that the first part of the analysis has left it **in equipoise**.

#### Step Two — Examine the Remedy Sought (More Important)

**The second inquiry is the more important in the analysis.**

If the remedy is more analogous to **damages**, we would say that the issue is one of law, and there would be an entitlement to a jury trial. However, if it is more analogous to **disgorgement of ill-gotten gains** or **restitution**, we would say that the issue is one of equity and there would be no entitlement to a jury trial whatsoever.

What we need to figure out is whether the remedy sought by the truckers here is more analogous to a suit for **damages** or a suit for **restitution**, because both of them involve the transfer of money.

**\*\*\* Key factor is to figure out who has been unjustly enriched. \*\*\***

According to the theory that the senior truckers did not get the higher wages because they did not get assigned the long-haul routes, the party that has the money that according to the truckers' theory belongs to the truckers, is the employer. In other words, the employer is the one unjustly enriched.

But, the defendant in the lawsuit is not the employer — it's the Union.

Therefore, this **cannot** be a suit for **restitution** because that would require the employer to return or recompense the truckers of the salary wrongfully withheld from its rightful owners, the truckers. However, this lawsuit was not against the employer. Instead, it's against the Union, but the Union was not the party which was unjustly enriched.

Instead, the Union should have to pay **compensatory damages**, which would be on the **law** side of the house, and which would entitle the truckers to have a right to demand a **trial by jury**.

<!-- page: 23 -->

Because the rule says that the second factor is the most important, and therefore holds more weight than the first, the SCOTUS here decided that this case required a remedy which was **legal in nature**, and therefore concludes that the truckers have a right to demand a trial by jury.

#### Review of BLL — *Terry* Two-Step Test

**When we have a newly created cause of action, and we are trying to determine whether it is legal or equitable in nature, we need to do a two-step analysis:**

1. **First, compare the statutory action to 18th century actions brought in the courts of England prior to the merger of the courts of law and equity.**
   - Look at the nature of the lawsuit, its legal structure, try to find an analogy to a cause of action that did exist in the 18th century, and look to see whether it was on the law or equity side of the house.

2. **Second, examine the remedy sought** (which also involves a historical comparison, but more limited) **and determine whether it is legal or equitable in nature.**
   - Look at the nature of the remedy sought, its legal structure, try to find an analogy to a remedy that existed in the 18th century, and look to see whether it was on the law or equity side of the house.

3. **The second inquiry is the more important in the analysis.**

<!-- page: 24 -->

The only way to understand this is that we will put more weight towards the scale that includes a right to a jury trial because part one of the analysis seems fairly tortured, and seems to be a way for the court to try to reach the conclusion that there is a right to a jury trial.

## New Adjudicators — *Atlas Roofing v. OSHA*

After learning how to resolve the first problem (how to analyze new causes of action), we now need to focus on the second problem: **how to analyze new adjudicators** to determine whether the issue is one of law or one of equity.

Congress wants to grant decision-making power to the administrative agencies (adjudicatory decision-making power, which is analogous to court proceedings), which is how we have Administrative Law judges (like the ones we discussed in the *Mathews v. Eldridge* context when we learned about Procedural Due Process).

The problem again is that the parties involved in this type of administrative proceedings might demand a trial by jury. But, the administrative state is not arranged or set up to hold trials by jury. The entire theory behind using administrative law judges is due to their expertise in the specific agency.

So, we need to figure out **when it is permissible for Congress to lodge power to the Administrative Agency.**

### *Atlas Roofing Co. v. OSHA*, 430 U.S. 442 (SCOTUS)(1977)

**BLL:** At least in cases where public rights are being litigated (for example, cases in which the government sues in its sovereign capacity to enforce public rights created by statutes within the power of Congress to enact) the Seventh Amendment does not prohibit Congress from assigning the factfinding function and initial adjudication to an administrative forum with which the jury would be incompatible.

**BLL: This requires one of two things in order for the Agency to have adjudicatory power and therefore no entitlement to a trial by jury:**

1. **Need the US Government, either itself or as one of its agencies (as a regulator, or sovereign), to be a party to the case, or**
2. **When the US Government acts as a pure commercial actor.**

<!-- page: 25 -->

**Facts:**

- In 1970, Congress determined that state trial courts were not sufficiently protecting the rights of workers against unsafe conditions at places of employment.
- With this in mind, Congress passed the OSHA of 1970 (the Act).
- The Act empowered the Secretary of Labor to conduct health and safety inspections of employers and issue fines up to $10,000 against employers.
- Employers are permitted to challenge the fine in an evidentiary hearing chaired by an administrative law judge.
- In the hearing, the burden is on the government to show that the fine is proper.
- The decision of the judge can then either be appealed to the full OSHA Review Commission at the request of a commissioner or to a court if the Commission does not review it.

**Holding and Reasoning (White, J.):**

- The Seventh Amendment to the Constitution preserves the right to a jury trial as it existed at the time of the amendment's writing.
- This right, as it stood at the time, applied to **private causes of action in tort, contract, and property**. However, **it did not apply to public rights that were created by statute**. This included such actions as those in equity or admiralty, all of which refrained from using a jury as the fact-finder.
- A lack of a jury requirement in suits adjudicating public rights has been reaffirmed in various contexts since the 1930s.
- **It is not the purpose of the Seventh Amendment to require Congress to force adjudication of every right it creates into the federal courts.**
- Rather, Congress has the ability to shape methods of adjudication of rights that it creates in order to provide the best method of resolving claims.
- It is particularly important to note that parties who litigate their claims before the Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission may still appeal their claims to a federal court.
- The ability to have the ultimate outcome of a claim be determined by a court supports the constitutionality of the Act.

The key here is that Congress could not create a massive administrative agency to adjudicate what are considered "**private rights**," (such as torts, contracts, property rights), where one private person could sue another private person, enact it by statute, and declare that they would have to adjudicate that lawsuit in the newly created administrative agency which would grant no entitlement to a trial by jury.

Under the two-factor prong, in private proceedings, we would not have the government as a party, acting as a regulator, or acting in its sovereign capacity.
