### Cases

- ***Payne v. S.S. Nabob*, 302 F.2d 803 (3d Cir. 1962)**

  A court's pretrial order is not merely procedural, but instead holds weight as to what a party may argue and what non-rebuttal witnesses a party may call at trial. The pretrial order basically wipes the pretrial slate clean.   A party making a motion for summary judgment does not need to provide affirmative evidence in the form of affidavits to support its motion.

- ***Scott v. Harris*, 477 U.S. 317 (1986)**

  In deciding a summary judgment motion, all facts are viewed in the light most favorable to the non-moving party unless no reasonable jury would be able to accept that version of the facts.

### Sources of Law

#### Fed. R. Civ. P. 56 — Summary Judgment

**(a) Motion for Summary Judgment or Partial Summary Judgment.** A party may move for summary judgment, identifying each claim or defense — or the part of each claim or defense — on which summary judgment is sought. The court shall grant summary judgment if the movant shows that there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact and the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. The court should state on the record the reasons for granting or denying the motion.

**(b) Time to File a Motion.** Unless a different time is set by local rule or the court orders otherwise, a party may file a motion for summary judgment at any time until 30 days after the close of all discovery.

**(c) Procedures.**

> **(1) Supporting Factual Positions.** A party asserting that a fact cannot be or is genuinely disputed must support the assertion by:
>
> > **(A)** citing to particular parts of materials in the record, including depositions, documents, electronically stored information, affidavits or declarations, stipulations (including those made for purposes of the motion only), admissions, interrogatory answers, or other materials; or
> >
> > **(B)** showing that the materials cited do not establish the absence or presence of a genuine dispute, or that an adverse party cannot produce admissible evidence to support the fact.
>
> **(2) Objection That a Fact Is Not Supported by Admissible Evidence.** A party may object that the material cited to support or dispute a fact cannot be presented in a form that would be admissible in evidence.
>
> **(3) Materials Not Cited.** The court need consider only the cited materials, but it may consider other materials in the record.
>
> **(4) Affidavits or Declarations.** An affidavit or declaration used to support or oppose a motion must be made on personal knowledge, set out facts that would be admissible in evidence, and show that the affiant or declarant is competent to testify on the matters stated.

<!-- page: 2 -->

**(d) When Facts Are Unavailable to the Nonmovant.** If a nonmovant shows by affidavit or declaration that, for specified reasons, it cannot present facts essential to justify its opposition, the court may:

> (1) defer considering the motion or deny it;
>
> (2) allow time to obtain affidavits or declarations or to take discovery; or
>
> (3) issue any other appropriate order.

**(e) Failing to Properly Support or Address a Fact.** If a party fails to properly support an assertion of fact or fails to properly address another party's assertion of fact as required by Rule 56(c), the court may:

> (1) give an opportunity to properly support or address the fact;
>
> (2) consider the fact undisputed for purposes of the motion;
>
> (3) grant summary judgment if the motion and supporting materials — including the facts considered undisputed — show that the movant is entitled to it; or
>
> (4) issue any other appropriate order.

<!-- page: 3 -->

**(f) Judgment Independent of the Motion.** After giving notice and a reasonable time to respond, the court may:

> (1) grant summary judgment for a nonmovant;
>
> (2) grant the motion on grounds not raised by a party; or
>
> (3) consider summary judgment on its own after identifying for the parties material facts that may not be genuinely in dispute.

**(g) Failing to Grant All the Requested Relief.** If the court does not grant all the relief requested by the motion, it may enter an order stating any material fact — including an item of damages or other relief — that is not genuinely in dispute and treating the fact as established in the case.

**(h) Affidavit or Declaration Submitted in Bad Faith.** If satisfied that an affidavit or declaration under this rule is submitted in bad faith or solely for delay, the court — after notice and a reasonable time to respond — may order the submitting party to pay the other party the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, it incurred as a result. An offending party or attorney may also be held in contempt or subjected to other appropriate sanctions.

## Lecture Recap for Day 14

Last week we got close to finishing our Procedural Due Process lesson. We discussed the Opportunity to be Heard test which comes from *Mathews v. Eldridge*.

The framework that the court wants to use is a three-part framework:

1. **Strength and nature of the private interest of the party whose property is being taken**
   - If it is a private dispute between two parties (or sometimes there are more), **BOTH private interests would be considered**:
     - (i) the private interests of the party having the "property" taken (A's Property), and
     - (ii) the other party's (B's) interests if the property is not taken from A

2. **The risk of an erroneous deprivation of this interest through the procedures currently used by the agency, as compared to the risk of erroneous deprivation of the scheme that the party is proposing to be adopted**
   - This is more of a counterfactual determination. We look at:

<!-- page: 4 -->

   - (i) the nature of the process in place and see what kind of risk that poses to erroneous determination, and
   - (ii) then we think of the alternative process of the person who is alleging that the scheme is insufficient under PDP requirements proposes to us, and we assess in some way the risk of erroneous determination as between those two schemes

3. **The government's interest, including the function involved and the fiscal and administrative burdens that additional or substitute procedural requirements would entail.**
   - Government always has an interest in minimizing the cost of adjudication
   - We look for any other government interests at stake

We continued discussing things that were not included in the *Mathews* test and that took us to examine *Lassiter*, which we did not get to discuss in much detail, but which we will again discuss later in the course in our access to justice module. For now, it teaches us about some procedural due process norms and requirements embedded in the Constitution, especially after the *Mathews* test.

The majority opinion has no concern as to whether litigants are treated with dignity and respect, or the fact that judges are adjudicating actual human lives, or whether the process is fair or not, except that an unfair process can get us to the right result. The question is, whether we still want a fair process even if an unfair process could get us to the right results with a sufficiently high frequency to which we'd say that the risk of erroneous determination is fairly low?

Reasons we might want to do that can be in two different categories:

1. It's the right thing to do and an important value independent of what it gives us. We think that when a government acts, a government should be fair in some way, and we have to define what "fair" means (moral reason)
   - It's probably a stretch to say that what happened in *Lassiter* was in any serious way "fair," given what we know about it from the opinion and from some additional outside research

2. Might help us persuade other litigants involved in the case, or other people in society (instrumental reason)
   - The court ruling in a case should be voluntarily complied with
   - No piece of the government can successfully function without overwhelming amount of voluntary adherence by its society members
     - There is no way to coercively enforce every edict that it issues
   - Process is what the courts can provide to legitimacy and to persuade the population to do what it says
     - If not, people will refuse to follow, honor or lose faith in the process

The point about using this and pointing it out is that none of it seems at issue with the majority in *Lassiter*. The majority seems to be interested simply in the interests of the parties in getting to the right result (assuming there is one) with the application of the law to the facts.

<!-- page: 5 -->

We also spent a large amount of time attempting to figure out how to operationalize the *Mathews* test factors. We decided for example, that when we assess a scheme for prejudgment seizure of property, or some wages, etc., we could look to see if it has a bonding requirement and a counter-bonding option. If so, we could say that that is a factor that could weigh in favor of the conclusion that the scheme passes procedural due process scrutiny.

Why?

- Party initiating the seizure posts a bond to protect (1) the damages that the other party might incur as a result of the seizure, as well as (2) the litigation costs.
  - (i) This step reduces the opposing party's interests in avoiding the prejudgment seizure (because we know that there is money available to remedy any damages caused)
- The bonding requirement also provides a market signal for the likelihood of success on the merits of the party filing litigation
- The availability of a counter-bonding requirement decreases the concern of the party opposing the seizures' interest
  - (i) By putting up a certain amount of money to buy a counter-bond, this party opposing the seizure can re-gain possession of the property (or wages, etc.) that would have otherwise been seized

This is an example of a particular court procedure operation that can be cast in terms of the *Mathews* factors, and can protect a scheme from Procedural Due Process attack if present, or can be pointed out as something defective or missing in a scheme, if it's not there.

Similarly, we might also ask who is the decision maker in issuing the writ of garnishment, lis pendens, or attachment?

- **If it is a judge:** Judges will assume that other judges do not make mistakes. Risk of erroneous determination is low or nonexistent.
- **If it is a mere clerk:** Clerks sometimes do make mistakes because unlike judges, clerks are mere humans. Risk of erroneous determination is higher.

Other factors might be:

- What kind of case is this?
  - (i) **Documents case?** (*Mathews*)
    - Risk of erroneous determination is lower, so the court could do more things quickly, maybe even prejudgment than in a factual dispute involving historical facts and requiring testimony
  - (ii) **Fact-specific testimony type case** (*Doehr*)
    - Where deeply ingrained in the legal system is, that in order to resolve issues of historical fact, especially with respect to factual disputes that require testimony, the scientifically erroneous assumption that the best way to resolve that is by live trial so that the judge or jury could assess witnesses' character while testifying and judge whether they are telling the truth

We discussed the possibility of multiple parties having a property interest in the same piece of property and how that has the ability to affect the calculation by effecting the nature of the interests that would be asserted in applying the *Mathews* test.

## Lecture Day 15

### *Vitek v. Jones* — Procedural Due Process Continued

*Vitek* was convicted of a serious felony, armed robbery, and is sentenced to 3-9 years in prison. While there, there are some actions that suggest that he might have some mental challenges. He may be placed in solitary confinement, but then he set his bed on fire while he was still in it. The prison warden transfers him to a secured mental institution as opposed to the prison where he is incarcerated.

#### *Vitek Correctional Director, et al. v. Jones*, 445 U.S. 480 (SCOTUS)(1980)

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires notice and opportunity for a hearing, even under conditions of confinement, when they amount to an additional "grievous loss" of liberty.

**Facts:**

- A Nebraska statute authorized the Director of Correctional Services (Vitek) to designate any available, suitable residence facility or institution as a place of confinement for any state prisoner and to transfer a prisoner from one place of confinement to another.
- The statute further provides that when a designated physician or psychologist finds that a prisoner suffers from mental disease and cannot be given proper treatment in that facility, the director may transfer the prisoner for examination, study or treatment within or without the Department of Correctional Services.
- Jones was convicted of robbery and sentenced to 3-9 years in state prison.
- He was transferred to the state penitentiary hospital in January 1975.
- He later was put in solitary confinement where he set his mattress on fire, burning himself severely.
- Upon the release from the burn unit of a private hospital, and based on findings required by the statute he was found to be suffering from a mental illness or defect and could not receive proper treatment in the penal complex.

<!-- page: 7 -->

- He was transferred to the security unit of the state mental hospital under the jurisdiction of the Department of Public Institutions.

**Issue:**

- Whether the involuntary transfer of a Nebraska state prisoner to a mental hospital implicates a liberty interest that is protected by the Due Process Clause?

**Holding:**

- Yes.
- First, a liberty interest is rooted in the state statute itself, under which a prisoner could reasonably expect that he would not be transferred to a mental hospital without a finding that he was suffering from a mental illness for which he could not secure adequate treatment in the correctional facility.
- Second, the District Court was convinced that characterizing Jones a mentally-ill patient and transferring him to the Lincoln Regional Center had "some stigmatizing" consequences which, together with the mandatory behavior modification treatment to which Jones would be subject, constitute a major change in the condition of confinement amounting to a "grievous loss" that should not be imposed without the opportunity for notice and an adequate hearing.
- Undoubtedly, a valid criminal conviction and prison sentence extinguish a defendant's right to freedom from confinement.
- Such a conviction and sentence sufficiently extinguish a defendant's liberty "to empower the State to confine him in any of its prisons."
- It is also true that changes in the conditions of confinement having a substantial adverse impact on the prisoner are not alone sufficient to invoke the protections of the Due Process Clause "[as] long as the conditions or degree of confinement to which the prisoner is subjected is within the sentence imposed upon him."
- In light of the findings made by the District Court, **Jones' involuntary transfer to the Lincoln Regional Center . . . for the purpose of psychiatric treatment, implicated a liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause.**
- Many of the restrictions on the prisoner's freedom of action at the Lincoln Regional Center by themselves might not constitute the deprivation of a liberty interest retained by a prisoner.
- But here, **the stigmatizing consequences of a transfer to a mental hospital for involuntary psychiatric treatment, coupled with the subjection of the prisoner to mandatory behavior modification as a treatment for mental illness, constitute the kind of deprivations of liberty that requires procedural protections.**
- The District Court held that to afford sufficient protection to the liberty interest it had identified, the State was required to observe the following minimum procedures before transferring a prisoner to a mental hospital:
  - A. **Written notice** to the prisoner that a transfer to a mental hospital is being considered;
  - B. **A hearing**, sufficiently after the notice to permit the prisoner to prepare, at which disclosure to the prisoner is made of the evidence being relied upon for the transfer and at which an opportunity to be heard in person and to present documentary evidence is given;

<!-- page: 8 -->

  - C. **An opportunity at the hearing to present testimony of witnesses** by the defense and to confront and cross-examine witnesses called by the state, except upon a finding, not arbitrarily made, of good cause for not permitting such presentation, confrontation, or cross-examination;
  - D. **An independent decisionmaker**;
  - E. **A written statement by the factfinder as to the evidence relied on** and the reasons for transferring the inmate;
  - F. **Availability of legal counsel, furnished by the state**, if the inmate is financially unable to furnish his own; and
  - G. **Effective and timely notice of all the foregoing rights.**

- The court applied the *Mathews* factors:
  - Concededly the **interest of the State** in segregating and treating mentally ill patients is strong.
  - The **interest of the prisoner** in not being arbitrarily classified as mentally ill and subjected to unwelcome treatment is also powerful;
  - however, as the District Court found, **the risk of error in making the determinations required by [the statute] is substantial enough to warrant appropriate procedural safeguards against error.**

Under the current scheme in the case, there was apparently no process given to Vitek. He did not get notice or an opportunity to a hearing. Someone basically looked in his files and decided that he needed to be transferred to the mental institution.

He did not get any type of process, so if there was in fact any interest of his at stake that was Constitutionally cognizable, such that would require any process at all, this would be an easy case to adjudicate, because there was no process at all.

*Vitek*'s interests did not lie in taking away his physical liberty, since he was already in prison. However, his interest was in the **substantive** component of the due process clause, which we do not study here, and which you will study in a course called Constitutional Law. (In this class we focus on procedural due process, but in order to understand the procedural due process issue in this case, you have to know that it involved a substantive due process (or lack thereof) claim.)

Some of the substantive due process doctrine involves the right to privacy, which include the right to decide to have an abortion with counsel of a doctor at certain times of the pregnancy, other rights, etc., as well as a right to certain liberty interests, which in this case consists of two things:

- State law in the form of the prison's regulations themselves creating something that the SCOTUS recognizes as a liberty interest; and
- A liberty interest against stigma from government actions (which will get more complex in your future Con Law class because the SCOTUS has refused to acknowledge this type of liberty interests involving stigma resulting from government actions in different contexts, but in this context, they recognized it)

<!-- page: 9 -->

SCOTUS says the transfer to the mental facility in this case arguably triggers the rights guaranteed by state law against such a transfer without sufficient process, as well as the inflicted stigma against the plaintiff.

The substantive liberty interest at issue in *Vitek* was his service in jail versus what he would be subjected to in the mental institution:

- Forcing him to participate in behavior modification programs
  - Euphemisms for forced treatment that we now may characterize as abuse and torture
- Stigma
  - That he is not just a criminal, but that he is mentally unstable

The interesting part of this case is not so much the court's application of the *Mathews* test because the court basically says that the interest was significant against the stigma, and the State Law, and once they reached that conclusion, they are basically done with the *Mathews* test, since there was no additional process. As such, the risk of erroneous determination is necessarily high, because we have not heard from the person subject to the transfer. What is interesting here is the remedy, which has two aspects to it:

1. It proposes a list of factors that are good to have when assessing procedural due process:
   - **Written notice** to the prisoner that a transfer to a mental hospital is being considered;
   - **A hearing**, sufficiently after the notice to permit the prisoner to prepare, at which disclosure to the prisoner is made of the evidence being relied upon for the transfer and at which an opportunity to be heard in person and to present documentary evidence is given;
   - **An opportunity at the hearing to present testimony of witnesses** by the defense and to confront and cross-examine witnesses called by the state, except upon a finding, not arbitrarily made, of good cause for not permitting such presentation, confrontation, or cross-examination;
   - **An independent decisionmaker**;
   - **A written statement by the factfinder as to the evidence relied on** and the reasons for transferring the inmate;
   - **Availability of legal counsel, furnished by the state**, if the inmate is financially unable to furnish his own; and
   - **Effective and timely notice of all the foregoing rights**

It seems the courts will never be able to list everything that they would possibly need, but SCOTUS feel this list seems to be a good start in terms of constructing a scheme from scratch. The SCOTUS uses this list as a remedy.

<!-- page: 10 -->

2. However, there are several things that they don't discuss as being an essential part of procedural due process analysis:
   - Discovery;
   - Appeals; or
   - Legal counsel, etc.

Legal counsel, in this opinion, only received 4 votes from SCOTUS, and in the case, they say that the person should get some kind of assistance but not necessarily a lawyer.

The SCOTUS says that lawyers dissolve disputes by formalizing them, which tend to make the character and nature of the dispute more formal than needed, and therefore, more confrontational and adversarial. Justice Powell says that they do need some type of assistance, but not necessarily a lawyer, and that an expert should also permissible because he or she can also analyze the subject matter of the dispute, as the issue may be more factual and technical, rather than legal.

It is a bit ironic in this case that J. Powell says that in some cases, we would not want a lawyer, and that we may want a subject matter expert to do the investigating, and he himself is a classic lawyer. He spent decades and decades in civil litigation prior to getting nominated to the SCOTUS. He is therefore on board saying that the person would need assistance, but that they would not necessarily need a lawyer, and that there are many times in which we may use inquisitorial style investigations. Powell seems to think that a combination of this would be better, because if we are talking about someone who is mentally ill, there may be a need for some paternalism.

## Pretrial Orders and the Road to Summary Judgment

Now we go back to the standard litigation case walking forward in federal court. We have had a Complaint, and we have had a "*twiqbal*" Motion To Dismiss which has partially been denied, so there are claims that are still going forward. We have passed the discovery stage, if applicable (some cases like challenges to administrative actions, do not have discovery).

Today for the most part we will discuss **Summary Judgment**, but to pick up something from last period: if summary judgment has happened in the process, they have been at least in part denied, so this next concept begins as if we are marching towards a civil trial.

There will be in almost all circumstances, should be at least, in all, a **Pretrial Order.**

**Rules of pretrial orders:**

<!-- page: 11 -->

- There have to have been Pleadings:
  - Parties have exchanged information:
    - Where the Plaintiff says "this is what I am alleging"
    - Defendant might have denied certain pieces of it
- There has to have been Discovery
  - Took place in light of the pleadings
- There have been some Summary Judgments
  - Parties have moved for Summary Judgment with respect to certain claims and defenses and not others that have attempted to define the issues

Now we are at the **Pretrial Order** stage, and our case for that doctrine is:

### *Payne v. S.S. Nabob*, 302 F.2d 803 (3d Cir. 1962)

**BLL:** A court's pretrial order is not merely procedural, but instead holds weight as to what a party may argue and what non-rebuttal witnesses a party may call at trial. The pretrial order basically wipes the pretrial slate clean. If something was not in the pretrial order, even if it was in the complaint and prior summary judgment litigations, it will not be able to be brought up on trial. On the flipside, if something was included in the pretrial order, even if it was not in the Complaint, it could be litigated in the trial.

**Facts:**

- Payne (plaintiff) brought a personal injury admiralty action against the S.S. Nabob (defendant).
- Payne filed a pretrial memorandum indicating that he was relying on a defective winch to make his case.
- The court's pretrial order — which the court termed a "pretrial report" — outlined this issue and listed the witnesses that Payne's pretrial memorandum indicated he would be calling.
- Payne did not object to the order's outline of his argument.
- At trial, Payne's attorney argued as part of his case that the loading of the ship had been handled improperly, thus helping to make the winch "unseaworthy."
- The attorney for the S.S. Nabob objected, arguing that the loading issue was outside the pretrial memorandum and report.
- The court upheld the objection.
- The court also refused to allow two witnesses for Payne testify because Payne had not included them in his pretrial memorandum.
- Payne appealed, arguing that a "pretrial report" is just that — a report — and not a binding order.

**Holding and Reasoning (McLaughlin, J.):**

- A court's pretrial order is not merely procedural, but holds weight as to what a party may argue and what non-rebuttal witnesses a party may call at trial.
- Regardless of whether a pretrial order is labeled a "report," it encompasses the basis of a plaintiff's claim and any arguments outside its scope may not be brought at trial.

<!-- page: 12 -->

- Attorneys have a duty to the court and the opposing party to make their views of the issues of a case known at the pretrial stage.
- Here, Payne did not include the handling of the loading in his pretrial memorandum.
- He also did not include the two witnesses in question.
- Nor did Payne object to the pretrial report when it was issued.
- The report was based on Payne's voluntary statement regarding the basis of his claim.
- In order to maintain the fairness of the judicial process, any issue or non-rebuttal witness not included in the pretrial memorandum (and resulting pretrial report) should not be permitted at trial.
- The district court was correct in sustaining the S.S. Nabob's objection.

The terms in this case are maritime law terms. This particular case is a straight-forward negligence case. In this case, there were two separate theories of negligence claims:

1. Some operation of the port winch (some mechanism that is involved in loading and unloading things on to and off of the ship) (Theory A);
2. The way the loading was done other than the port winch (Theory B).

The plaintiff's attorney asked for Theory A and its corresponding witnesses to be put in the pretrial order. The defendant gave a list of defenses and witnesses for Theory A only. The judge then wrote the order asserting the plan for trial, the issues to be litigated, and the witnesses to be called.

At trial, the Plaintiff's attorney tried to introduce Theory B by contending certain things in the opening statement that relate to Theory B and not Theory A, and by calling witnesses that pertain to Theory B and not Theory A.

The judge then said that because they did not assert anything about Theory B in the Pretrial Order and they did not object to the Pretrial Order after the judge had written it knowing that it did not include Theory B, it would not be fair for them to bring it up in the trial because the Defense would not have had adequate time to prepare a defense for it, and since it's not in the Pretrial Order, it cannot be brought up in the case. Higher court affirmed.

**The pretrial order basically wipes the pretrial slate clean.** If something was **not in the pretrial order**, even if it was in the complaint and prior summary judgment litigations, **it will not be able to be brought up on trial**. On the flipside, if something was **included in the pretrial order**, even if it was not in the Complaint, **it could be litigated in the trial**. (*Payne*)

## Summary Judgment — Rule 56

Now we will discuss **Rule 56 — Summary Judgment** (see entire Rule above).

<!-- page: 13 -->

Pre-*Twiqbal*, Greiner thought that Summary Judgment was by far the most important stage in many pieces of civil litigation in the trial court system. Summary Judgment is still one of the most important stages in the civil trial process. Most cases do not end up in trial anymore. What frequently happens is that Summary Judgment decides whether the defendant will pay the plaintiff off, and if the defendant cannot get summary judgment, then he or she will try to settle at this stage; but, he or she will try hard to get Summary Judgment before engaging in serious settlement negotiations.

In order to understand how **Summary Judgment** works, we need to understand "**burdens**."

**Burden of production:** The obligation for a party to **produce sufficient evidence** to the court on an issue to avoid a dismissal or directed verdict.

**Burden of persuasion:** The requirement that the party with the burden to convince the judge or jury of the validity of his/her case.

- Can be thought of as: who bears the risk that evidence is in perfect equipoise (perfect balance)?
  - **Whatever party who loses when the evidence is in equipoise, is the one that bears the burden of persuasion, and therefore, has the burden of tipping the evidence to one side or the other.**
    - If there is no evidence on something, that means we have no evidence as to whether something is true or something is false. In this case, the evidence would be in equipoise (perfect balance).
    - If we do have evidence on something, but we are left looking at the evidence and we cannot tell whether something is true or something is false, the evidence is again in equipoise.

**BLL: The plaintiff bears the burden of persuasion on all of the elements of the cause of action(s) asserted.**

- For example, on a **breach of contract** cause of action, the elements are as follows:
  - There was a contract
  - There was a breach of that contract
  - That the breach caused some harm
  - What are the damages/Injury
- The Plaintiff bears the **burden of persuasion** as to these elements.

- Another example: If it is a **negligence tort** cause of action, like the one in *Payne*, the elements are as follows:
  - There was a duty owed by the defendant to the Plaintiff of due care
  - There was conduct below the level of due care by the defendant (breach of that duty)
  - That the conduct below the level of due care by the defendant caused harm
  - That the plaintiff suffered damages due to that harm caused by the defendant

<!-- page: 14 -->

> *Note that if your contracts professor or torts professor tell you differently, please listen to them in their class. This above is sort of "cartoon law" for purposes of illustrating the applicable civil procedure.*

Using the elements of the cause of action (whatever cause of action it may be in a specific case), the plaintiff bears the **burden of persuasion** in proving each and all of the elements of the cause(s) of action.

- Meaning that if the evidence is in perfect equipoise as to any of the elements in either of the example causes of action above, the Plaintiff loses.
  - (**Plaintiff has the burden of tipping the evidence scale in his or her favor, IN EACH ELEMENT**)
  - In typical civil litigation trials, the burden of persuasion level is called "**preponderance of the evidence**" standard, which means the plaintiff has to show that it "more likely than not" happened.
  - We value the status quo so we don't do anything unless the plaintiff can meet that burden of persuasion, and only then do we offer a remedy.

We don't conceptually place a high burden on the plaintiff, but we do place some level of burden.

**BLL: The defendant bears the burden of persuasion on all of the affirmative defenses asserted.**

- For example, one may encounter in torts the defense of "contributory negligence," and even we did here in class when we studied *Babcock*, where we explored the defense of the "guest statute"
  - *Babcock* was the case where the two people got in the car in NY, drove to Ontario and got into a car accident. If the defendant in *Babcock* wanted to interpose an **affirmative defense** of the guest statute, that means that the defendant would bear the burden of persuasion and therefore would have to prove that the passenger in the car was a free passenger, and that it was not a taxi service —
    - If the defendant could not show evidence that the passenger was riding for free, then we might say that the defendant did not meet his burden of persuasion in his **affirmative defense** for that element, because he would have failed to give us any evidence that tip the balance scale in his favor, towards the conclusion that the passenger was in the car as a free passenger.

So, to be clear:

**BLL: The plaintiff bears the burden of persuasion on all of the elements of the cause of action(s) asserted.**

**BLL: The defendant bears the burden of persuasion on all of the affirmative defenses asserted.**

<!-- page: 15 -->

Now then, the **burden of production** is the burden to produce evidence sufficient to accomplish something.

**Examples:**

- We might require a plaintiff in the beginning stages to produce evidence that would be sufficient, if unrebutted, to meet a burden of persuasion; or,
- We might require the defendant to produce evidence to create a material issue of fact.

The last thing we need to understand in order to proceed:

- The parties at this stage might be gathering (discovery) information from each other:
  - **Interrogatories** — Written questionnaires which require responses from the opposing party
  - **Depositions** — Lawyer asks opposing party many questions and a court reporter creates written transcripts — now it's recorded and done with software
  - **Requests for production of documents** — Other party required to either produce the documents or object to producing the documents
    - They're really burdensome
    - They're outside the scope of proper discovery
- As you can see there may be many documents that are part of the "record", and the parties might have witnesses, which might generate affidavits about what the witnesses might say.
- A court therefore will not want to see all of that voluminous material, but they will be interested in seeing the most relevant material, which is decided by the parties themselves.

So, when we talk about the "**record**" in a Summary Judgment proceeding we are referring to the most relevant pieces of all of the voluminous documents exchanged, which are put together for the judge to analyze, and not the whole of that record.

**HYPO:**

- A party (hypothetically, the defendant) will **move** (submit a Motion) for something in a 2-3 page motion.
  - Example: "Comes now the defendant Jim Greiner, who hereby moves for Summary Judgment as to these issues, or these causes of actions, or these contentions…"
    - You don't need to move with respect to the whole case
    - For example, "there are seven causes of action here, first three were eliminated based on the court's prior ruling on Motions to Dismiss, out of the four remaining, there are **two that I am moving for Summary Judgment** with respect to"

<!-- page: 16 -->

- There will also be a **Memorandum in Support of SJ.**
  - This document will include a recitation of the facts, which according to the defendant have been discovered through discovery, and that the defendant has found, say through affidavits
    - Each of the statements of fact must be accompanied by citations to "the record" but since the record is not in front of the judge, the relevant parts of the record need to be "attached" to the record as exhibits
      - Back in the day this would be done by attaching tabs with excerpts as exhibits which would directly support the factual assertions
      - Now it can be done by adding electronic tabs or hyperlinks
  - There will also be legal arguments in the Memorandum, with case citations (the brief) about why the court should grant the summary judgment

This is the beginning of the record that the judge will be later looking at and analyzing.

- Then, the party opposing the summary judgment, let's say the plaintiff, will file the following:
  - A **Memorandum in Opposition to the Motion for Summary Judgment**
    - This document will also include a recitation of the facts, which according to the plaintiff have been discovered through discovery, and that the plaintiff has found, say through affidavits
      - Each of the statements of fact must again be accompanied by citations to "the record" but since the record is not in front of the judge, the relevant parts of the record need to be "attached" to the record as exhibits

- Then there may be a way to do a reply brief, if the court allows it, from the initial moving party, but this is case specific and governed by the different local jurisdictions.

- Additionally, the parties may stipulate on some items that both parties agree that are facts.

The court thus far has not been looking at any documents. They have not read the initial motion or anything; instead, it will wait for all the motions to be fully filed and complete before it reviews all of the papers at the same time. When people refer to "the record" then, they are referring to the set of attachments in support of the briefs, and the judge's job is to analyze it.

One thing to keep in mind is that all of the judge's analysis happens at the same time, and while he is reviewing the record. We do not have a time process, where one party submits something and the judge looks at it, and then the other party responds, etc. Both parties have submitted everything, and the conceptual process by the judge is more analytical.

### *Celotex Corp. v. Catrett*, 477 U.S. 317 (1986) — The Brennan Framework

The conceptual framework that we use to analyze whether a Summary Motion (for each element and for entire causes of action) should be granted or not, comes to us from Justice Brennan's dissenting opinion in *Celotex*.

**BLL:** A party making a motion for summary judgment does not need to provide affirmative evidence in the form of affidavits to support its motion.

**Facts:**

- Catrett (plaintiff) sued a number of asbestos manufacturers including Celotex Corp. (defendant) in district court, claiming that her husband died from exposure to the manufacturers' asbestos.
- Celotex moved for summary judgment on the ground that Catrett failed to present any evidence showing that her husband had been exposed to Celotex's products.
- In objection to the summary judgment motion, Catrett submitted three documents that suggested the decedent had been exposed to Celotex's products.
- The district court granted summary judgment, because Catrett lacked sufficient evidence to show her husband had been exposed to Celotex asbestos in the District of Columbia or anywhere else.
- The court of appeals reversed on the ground that Celotex had not offered any evidence to support its motion.
- The United States Supreme Court granted certiorari.

**Holding and Reasoning (Rehnquist, J.):**

- Rule 56(c) directs the entry of summary judgment, after adequate time for discovery and upon motion, against a party who fails to establish the existence of an element essential to that party's case, and bears the burden of persuasion of demonstrating that element at trial.
- Summary judgment is designed to afford the parties a just, speedy and inexpensive resolution to their controversies.
- The party seeking summary judgment carries the initial responsibility of informing the court of the reason why the motion should be granted by identifying those parts of the lawsuit lacking a genuine issue of material fact.
- However, there is no specific requirement under Rule 56 that the movant provide affidavits to support its motion.
- A party making a motion for summary judgment does not need to provide affirmative evidence (in the form of affidavits) to support its motion.
- The court of appeals incorrectly assumed that the moving party was expressly or impliedly required to provide affidavits or other evidence disproving the adverse party's claim. No such requirement exists under Rule 56(c).
- **The summary judgment motion should be used to identify factually insufficient claims or defenses.**
- Although the moving party does not need to present affirmative evidence, the nonmoving party is expected to present some form of affirmative evidence to overcome the challenge.

<!-- page: 18 -->

- The court of appeals should have looked at Catrett's evidence produced in the trial court to see if her claim was warranted.
- The court of appeals' judgment is reversed, and the matter is remanded.

**Dissent (Brennan, J.):**

- The majority's legal analysis is correct, but incorrectly applied.
- **If the burden of persuasion (on the specific issue at trial) is with the nonmoving party, the moving party may request summary judgment by either submitting affirmative evidence disproving an essential element of the claim or demonstrating to the court that the evidence on record is insufficient.**
- Celotex has elected to take the second option, but its motion cannot succeed with conclusory assertions.
- While affirmative evidence is not required under the second option, an affirmative showing of the absence of evidence is necessary.
- Celotex did not meet its burden of production in showing that Catrett did not possess any evidence supporting her claim.

Ordinarily, we do not cite dissenting opinions because they are not the law. But, in *Celotex*, Justice Brennan agreed with the majority as to the framework of analysis for a Summary Judgment, but his opinion everyone thinks explained that framework more clearly than the majority did. Now, in every Summary Judgment motion that is made, the very first citation you will see as citing the law in Summary Judgment is Justice Brennan's dissent in *Celotex*. (***and, it is the first citation that should be made when analyzing Summary Judgment motions in any assignments or exams***)

> *Note that if any or all summary judgment motions get denied, case simply goes to trial, either on the causes of action that did not get dismissed through summary judgment, or on all causes of action.*

## BLL — Analysis to Summary Judgment Motions

**1) Brennan's dissent instructs us to first ask the question:**

On the **issue that is the subject of the summary judgment motion**, who has the **burden of persuasion at trial**?

- Is it the moving party? Or,
- Is it the nonmoving party?

**2) The ANALYSIS depends on the answer to this question.**

- **If the moving party** (could be either the Plaintiff or the Defendant) **has the burden of persuasion** for a specific element at trial, then there is **one mode of analysis** we use.

- **If the non-moving party** (could be either the Plaintiff or the Defendant) **has the burden of persuasion** for a specific element at trial, then there are **two modes of analysis** we use.

<!-- page: 19 -->

> **Two modes when the non-moving party bears the burden of persuasion:**
>
> 1. Produce evidence that affirmatively negates one of the moving party's essential elements of the claim; or
> 2. Claim that the moving party's evidence is in perfect equipoise (claiming that the moving party does not have enough evidence to meet its burden (to tip the scale in its favor))

### Example 2a — Moving Party Has Burden of Persuasion at Trial

**HYPO:** Plaintiff is moving for summary judgment on the element of causation (remember that a cause of action has 3-4 elements; we are just looking at granting (or not) summary judgment on one of the elements now; causation):

- We know from above that with this motion, the burden of persuasion at trial for this element would be on the plaintiff, so therefore:
- **Burden of production at trial** (using the hypo above) **would also be on Plaintiff:**
  - Plaintiff would have the "burden to produce" evidence to show that the defendant caused the harm, such that if uncontradicted or contravened by the defendant in the record, a rational trier of fact can only find in favor of the plaintiff.
- If the moving party (in this hypo, the Plaintiff) meets this burden of production, then the **burden of production shifts to the nonmoving party** (in this hypo, the defendant):
  - Using the hypo above, the defendant in this case either:
    1. agrees that the facts as presented by the plaintiff are correct, or
    2. claims that we need a trial to determine that (asserting that there is a genuine issue of material fact)
  - The nonmoving party's burden of production is to produce evidence sufficient to show that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to resolve.
    1. If he fails to produce evidence showing that there is a genuine issue of material fact, then he would not have met his burden of production, and the summary judgment would be granted as to that element, meaning this element would not go to trial.
    2. If the defendant meets this burden of production, and shows that there is a genuine issue of material fact, then the burden might shift again to the moving party (in this hypo, the plaintiff) to require him or her to show the court why the evidence produced by the nonmoving party is not sufficient to create a trial issue (genuine issue of material fact).

> ***Note:*** This process is done by the judge and is an analytical type of framework, not a framework that is done in time. It is an analytical framework that the court uses to apply to the evidence that is already compiled in the briefing. All the documents are in front of the judge and he or she will look for this production evidence throughout the record and go back and forth through the record deciding whether each party has met each required burden. This just describes the process in the judge's mind of the steps they take to consider the evidence provided in the record, and not a reflection of what happens in time.

<!-- page: 20 -->

### Example 2b — Non-Moving Party Has Burden of Persuasion at Trial

**If the non-moving party (could be either the Plaintiff or the Defendant) has the burden of persuasion for a specific element at trial, then there are two modes of analysis we use.**

1. **Produce evidence that affirmatively negates one of the moving party's essential elements of the claim; or**

2. **Claim that the moving party's evidence is in perfect equipoise (claiming that the moving party does not have enough evidence to meet its burden (to tip the scale in its favor))**

In order to operationalize the **first option**, we could get evidence that directly negates at least one of the moving party's essential elements of the claim.

In order to operationalize the **second option** under Justice Brennan's framework, we can obtain information and evidence gathered by the interrogatories, depositions, and any testimony found within the record. If this happens in the evidence acquired during discovery, the documentation would already be in the record. So for the second option, if there was no evidence acquired during the discovery phase that would prove the elements of the claim, then the evidence would be in perfect equipoise, because there would not be evidence for that element.

The way to do this is to go find out the opposing party's best case, and then highlight in the brief that there is an absence of evidence to prove that element.

**We discussed having two different ways that the moving party that does not bear the burden of persuasion at trial can discharge (fail to meet) its initial burden of production in the summary judgment context:**

<!-- page: 21 -->

- If the **moving party** fails to provide evidence to affirmatively knock out an element of the non-moving party's claim or defense, or
- If the **moving party** fails to demonstrate sufficiently that there is no evidence to support a key element of the opposing party's claim, then,

The judge does not need to analytically go any further. He will deny the summary judgment motion and go to trial.

- If the **moving party** meets that **burden of production**, then the **burden of production** would shift to the **nonmoving party**.
  - The **burden of production** for the **nonmoving party** would be to show that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to resolve.
  - If the **nonmoving party** would be able to show (meets its **burden of production**) that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to resolve, then we go to trial.
  - If the **nonmoving party** fails to meet its **burden of production** that there is a genuine issue of material fact that requires a trial to resolve, then we will grant the summary judgment motion and not go to trial.

Basically, in a Summary Judgment we are trying to see whether we need to go to trial or not, and this analytical framework is what will allow us to analyze it.

***Remember that the important part to start with is figuring out what is the moving party's position, relative to the party who has the burden of persuasion at trial?***

- Is the moving party the party who bears the burden of persuasion at trial? Or,
- Is the moving party NOT the party who bears the burden of persuasion at trial?

Our key standard in this entire thing is **whether or not there is a genuine issue of material fact?**

The verbiage to use (on an assignment, or an exam, for example) is:

- Is there a genuine issue of material fact?; and
- Is the moving party entitled to summary judgment as a matter of law.

The analytical framework you would use to analyze this is Justice Brennan's analytical framework we just outlined.

In the real world, no one uses this analytical framework. In the real world, the judges basically determine whether, after looking at all the evidence, a rational jury could find in favor of the nonmoving party.

<!-- page: 22 -->

Thinking of it this way, the rules make more sense. For instance, if there are conflicts in testimony between the parties, they would be construed in favor of the nonmoving party. Because the presumption is to go to trial, all evidence is construed in light of the nonmoving party.

But in a formal brief, or on the exam for this class, or on a civil procedure assignment, you have to do it the way outlined above.
