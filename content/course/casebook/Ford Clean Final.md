## *Ford Motor Co. v. Montana Eighth Judicial District Court* (2021)

In *Ford*, the United States Supreme Court established that a state may exercise specific personal jurisdiction over a company that (i) sold products of the type that allegedly caused the plaintiff's injury in the forum state (as it did in all 50 states), and (ii) took steps, successful and profitable steps, to create a market for used versions of those products, but (iii) did not sell the particular allegedly defective product that caused the plaintiff's injury to the plaintiff, nor (iv) sell that particular product to the consumer who did buy it in the forum. Instead, the plaintiff bought the product in used form from a prior consumer, who had purchased it (from Ford) in a different state. The Court held that because Ford had systematically served the market (primary and used) in Montana and Minnesota (the two fora at issue) for the vehicle types that the plaintiffs alleged had malfunctioned in those States, there were sufficiently strong contacts among the defendant, the forum, and the subject matter of litigation for the state to exercise specific personal jurisdiction.

Two plaintiffs — one the estate of a Montana resident (Ms. Gullett) and one a Minnesota resident (Mr. Bandemer) — brought separate tort claims (products liability, failure to warn, and negligence) in their respective states against Ford. Ms. Gullett's suit alleged that a malfunction in her Ford Explorer resulted in a fatal accident in Montana. Mr. Bandemer's suit alleged that a Crown Victoria's defective airbags caused him serious injury during a collision in Minnesota. Ford designed both vehicles in Michigan and manufactured them in Kentucky or Canada. Ford originally sold the cars at issue outside the forum states — the Explorer in Washington, the Crown Victoria in North Dakota. Later resales and relocations in the used car market brought the vehicles to Montana and Minnesota.

Ford moved to dismiss both suits for lack of personal jurisdiction. Ford admitted it had "purposefully availed itself of the privilege of conducting activities" in both states but claimed that, for the plaintiffs to exercise specific personal jurisdiction, a causal link occurring in or relating to the forum was required between Ford and the incident at issue. Ford asserted that such a link only existed "if the company had designed, manufactured, or sold in the State the particular vehicle involved in the accident." *Ford Motor*, 592 U.S. 351, 356–357. Both states' supreme courts rejected Ford's argument. The Montana court pointed to the 36 Ford dealerships within the state. The Minnesota court pointed to 2,000 Crown Victorias that had been sold in Minnesota and Ford's 84 dealerships as evidence of Ford's targeted marketing.

Ford appealed these holdings to the United States Supreme Court.

The Supreme Court affirmed both decisions, finding that "[w]hen a company like Ford serves a market for a product in a State and that product causes injury in the State to one of its residents, the State's courts may entertain the resulting suit." 592 U.S. at 355. The Court held that the phrases "arise out of" and "relate to" from its previous SIPJ cases were alternative pathways to establish SIPJ. A claim could therefore relate to the defendant's contacts with the

<!-- page: 2 -->

forum, even absent causation, if a defendant "serve[d] a market for a product in the forum State and the product malfunctions there." *Id.* at 352.

Ford served "the market" in Montana and Minnesota by encouraging people to drive their cars into those states. Ford, by advertising to Montanans and Minnesotans to persuade them buy its vehicles (including its used vehicles), served the market and thus purposefully availed themselves of the right to do business in the states, despite there (allegedly) being no direct causal relationship among Ford, the forum states, and the specific accidents at hand. Further, the court found that it would make less jurisdictional sense to channel the suits to the states of first sale — Washington and South Dakota — as neither of the accidents occurred there and neither of the plaintiffs were citizens of those states.

**Questions:**

1. What distinguishes this case from the stream of commerce theory that the *Nicastro* plurality tried so hard to discredit? Ford sells to a consumer in State A, who then resells to another consumer in State B, who suffers an injury and sues in the state in which the injury occurred. SCOTUS holds that there is IPJ. McIntyre UK sells to McIntyre USA in country A, who then resells to a consuming company state B, whose employee suffers an injury and sues in the state in which the injury occurred. Both are products liability cases (meaning the same causes of action are asserted). SCOTUS holds that there is no IPJ. What distinguishes the two cases?

2. Similarly, which facts distinguish *Ford* from *World-Wide Volkswagen* (which, you'll remember, held that the Oklahoma court could not exercise personal jurisdiction over a northeastern automobile retailer and its wholesale distributor with respect to a car that the consumer drove to the forum state)?

3. In his concurrence in *Ford*, Justice Gorsuch suggests any state court should be able to exercise personal jurisdiction over any nationwide corporation regardless of any "case-links." The majority rejects this assertion, claiming that under Gorsuch's view a California court could hear a claim against Ford brought by an Ohio plaintiff based on an accident occurring in Ohio involving a car purchased in Ohio. If personal jurisdiction is about satisfying due process through (usually fictional) "consent," does this hypothetical case violate Ford's rights? What if personal jurisdiction is about "traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice?" What about Ford's ability to reasonably anticipate being hauled into court in California? (Surely there is no argument that a company like Ford is non-trivially inconvenienced by the prospect of defending a lawsuit anywhere in the United States?)

## Lower Court Split: Different Markets Theory

In the past few years global manufacturers have argued an exception to *Ford*. They claim that, even where all the *Ford* conditions are met, their contacts with the forum state are not

<!-- page: 3 -->

'related' to the suit (and thus the state may not exercise SIPJ) if the company took steps to limit its sales to particular buyers within the state's market. This "different-markets theory" involves splitting the forum state's market for a product into submarkets, then identifying which submarket is the right one in which to conduct the personal jurisdiction inquiry. *See Ethridge v. Samsung SDI Co., Ltd.*, 137 F.4th 309, 318 (5th Cir.), *opinion withdrawn and superseded on reh'g*, 163 F.4th 136 (5th Cir. 2025).

For example, in *Yamashita v. LG Chem*, the Plaintiff brought a products-liability suit in Hawaii against LGC claiming that it negligently manufactured and distributed a 18650 lithium-ion battery that the Plaintiff used to power an electronic cigarette that subsequently exploded in his mouth. 62 F.4th 496, 508 (9th Cir. 2023). Plaintiff had bought the 18650 battery through an unidentified third-party reseller. LGC employees denied that the firm authorized any third-party retailer or distributor to sell stand-alone batteries in Hawaii. Nonetheless, the Plaintiff claimed the accident sufficiently related to LGC's conduct in Hawaii because LGC had shipped lithium-ion batteries and the raw materials to build 18650 batteries to and through Hawaii. LGC alleged, however, that these shipments did not contain any 18650 batteries. Further, LGC argued it sought to limit its sales in Hawaii to residential solar batteries, which were large batteries installed in solar-power systems and were different than the small, portable stand-alone 18650 battery involved in the Plaintiff's accident. LGC argued there was no relatedness between their forum contact (through shipments and solar batteries) and the Plaintiff's injury.

The 9th Circuit held that because the LGC had sought to limits its sales to a submarket other than the one that had resulted in the plaintiff's purchase, LGC contacts did not sufficiently "relate to" the plaintiff's injuries to exert SIPJ over LGC. The 9th Circuit held that "*Ford* gives little reason to think that the relatedness prong would have been satisfied if, for example, Ford had sold Crown Victorias only to police departments in Minnesota, had not marketed them to consumers, and had not serviced them at all. Such contacts would not typically cause, and could not be foreseen to cause, injuries resulting from consumer ownership of Crown Victorias, especially if most consumer-owned Crown Victorias were acquired out-of-state. Similarly, even if LGC sold 18650 batteries to manufacturers for incorporation in consumer products sold in Hawaii, these sales would not be related to purchases of stand-alone batteries by Hawaii consumers." *Yamashita*, 62 F.4th at 508.

In a similar, battery-related case, the Seventh Circuit found an Indiana district court could not exercise SIPJ over a manufacturer who sold to third parties that then sold to customers in Indiana. The court pointed to evidence that the company had taken steps to ensure its customers used the batteries only for approved purposes and tried to prevent sales to individual consumers like the plaintiff. *B.D. by & through Myers v. Samsung Sdi Co.*, 143 F.4th 757 (7th Cir. 2025).

To date, the Sixth Circuit, the Texas Supreme Court, the Mississippi Supreme Court, and an intermediate appellate court in Minnesota have rejected the different-markets theory. *See Sullivan v. LG Chem, Ltd.*, 79 F.4th 651 (6th Cir. 2023) (holding that a Michigan district court could exercise personal jurisdiction over a company in a suit by a plaintiff whose vape exploded because the company directly shipped batteries into Michigan even though the company never

<!-- page: 4 -->

directly sold to vape stores or individual consumers in Michigan); *LG Chem Am., Inc. v. Morgan*, 670 S.W.3d 341 (Tex. 2023) (holding that a consumer's claim was sufficiently related to manufacturer's and distributor's contacts with Texas to satisfy due process for exercise of personal jurisdiction); *Dilworth v. LG Chem, Ltd.*, 355 So. 3d 201 (Miss. 2022) (holding the battery manufacturer purposefully availed itself of the market for lithium-ion batteries in Mississippi, such that the exercise of personal jurisdiction was fundamentally reasonable and fair); *Peters v. Samsung SDI Co.*, No. A25-0195, 2025 WL 2902144 (Minn. Ct. App. Oct. 13, 2025), *review denied* (Dec. 31, 2025); *LG Chem, Ltd. v. Lemmerman*, 863 S.E.2d 514 (2021).

The Fifth, Seventh, and Ninth Circuits, the Nevada Supreme Court, and an intermediate appellate court in California have accepted the different markets theory. *See Ethridge v. Samsung SDI Co., Ltd.*, 163 F.4th 136 (5th Cir. 2025); *Franceschi v. LG Chem, Ltd.*, 580 P.3d 1279 (2025) (upholding the lower court's dismissal of the lawsuit for lack of personal jurisdiction because, while the company purposefully availed itself the Nevada market through certain contacts, the harm alleged by plaintiff did not arise out of or relate to those contacts); *Chem, Ltd. v. Superior Ct.*, 295 Cal. Rptr. 3d 661 (2022) (holding that personal jurisdiction could not be exerted over the battery company because the plaintiff's personal injury claims did not arise out of or relate to the company's sale of car batteries to California).

The Fifth Circuit landed on the opposite side of the split from the Texas and Mississippi state Supreme Courts within it, creating a particularly untenable problem: a suit might proceed in state court with SIPJ, but if it were removed to the federal court, where Fifth Circuit precedent governs, it would be dismissed for lack of personal jurisdiction. That should trouble you, as FRCP 4(k)(1)(A) generally seeks to ensure coherence in the personal jurisdiction analyses of federal courts and the state courts in which they sit. Expect more on the extent to which we want uniformity between state and federal courts when we discuss the *Erie* doctrine. It is likely that the Supreme Court will soon grant certiorari in a different markets case to resolve the emerging within-circuit conflicts.

**Questions:**

1. Putting all of the analysis you just read aside, if you had tried to apply the rule we've learned from *International Shoe* which requires (i) a long arm statute, (ii) minimal contacts linking defendant, subject matter of litigation, forum, and (iii) fair and reasonable considerations, would you have ruled the forum could exercise SIPJ over *Ford*? What about *Yamashita*?

2. Do you find the "different-markets theory" persuasive? As a judge, what evidence would you demand when determining if a corporation targeted one market within a forum but not another?

3. The 9th Circuit suggests the "relatedness" prong for SIPJ would not have been met if Ford had sold Crown Victorias only to police departments in Minnesota, had not marketed them to consumers, and had not serviced them. Do you agree? Do you think all

<!-- page: 5 -->

*three* prongs should be necessary conditions for Minnesota to exercise SIPJ over Ford? Is one prong more or less critical to this analysis than the others?

4. Let's say one of these potential different markets cases is filed in a Texas state court and the defendant removes to the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Texas. Think back to when we learned removal (28 U.S.C. § 1441). Does it say anything about remanding to the lower court over *personal* jurisdiction? Assuming the federal court does not remand, should they apply the precedent of the 5th Circuit (no personal jurisdiction) or Texas law (court has personal jurisdiction)? You will soon learn about what to do when there is a conflict between federal and state laws with *Erie*, but how do you think courts should handle this?
