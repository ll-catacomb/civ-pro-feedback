## Day 1: Overview, SMJ p.1 — AUJ

The United States has both federal and state judicial systems. For either a federal or state court to hear a case, it must have *jurisdiction*. Actually, there are several kinds of jurisdiction. In this course, you will learn about two of them, subject matter jurisdiction and personal jurisdiction. The first four class periods deal with subject matter jurisdiction, which governs the type of case that a court can hear.

Let's start with subject matter jurisdiction in the state courts. For the purposes of this class, state courts are courts of *general jurisdiction*, meaning that as far as subject matter jurisdiction goes, they have the power to hear any type of case. In other words, the state courts are ready and willing to hear cases about anything and everything. Sometimes, the federal government (specifically, Congress) says that the state courts cannot hear a case involving a certain subject matter. When that happens, we say that there is *exclusive federal jurisdiction*, meaning only the federal courts can hear that type of case. That's a federal government decision, though, it's not the state courts refusing to hear something. And by the way, exclusive federal jurisdiction is rare. For your purposes, it applies only to lawsuits involving federal antitrust statutes, intellectual property (copyright, trademark, patent), and the United States as a party.

Federal court are different. For a federal court to hear a case, it must have *subject matter jurisdiction* (SMJ). If a federal court lacks SMJ, the plaintiff must file in state court. What happens if both a state and federal court have jurisdiction (*concurrent jurisdiction*)? The plaintiff has a choice! In that case, which court should they file in? What factors should they consider in deciding where to file? What consequences could arise from this decision?

For the next few days, we will be discussing how a federal court can obtain SMJ. One way is through *arising under jurisdiction*, which means that the case *arises under* federal law (although . . . it's complicated). Today we will consider: How does a federal court obtain arising under jurisdiction? Where does the federal court's power to hear cases "arising under" federal law come from? How broad is the "arising under" power? Can a party get a case into federal court under the "arising under" power through something called a declaratory judgement action?

## Day 2: SMJ p.2 — Diversity Jurisdiction, begin SuppJ

Yesterday, you learned that a federal court can obtain SMJ through arising under jurisdiction, or when a case *arises under* federal law. Today, you will learn a second way a federal court can obtain SMJ: diversity jurisdiction. Diversity jurisdiction refers to cases "between Citizens of different States." We will consider: What does it mean to be "Citizens of different States"? Where does the federal court's power to hear cases "between Citizens of different States" come from? What are the justifications for and criticisms of diversity jurisdiction? Should the scope of diversity jurisdiction be curtailed or expanded?

For a federal court to have diversity jurisdiction, two equally important requirements must be met. If either of these requirements are not met, there can be no diversity jurisdiction.

<!-- page: 2 -->

First, the parties to the case must have *complete diversity*. Complete diversity means **all** plaintiffs are diverse from all defendants. It does not matter if multiple parties on the same side of the *v.* have the same citizenship, so long as all parties on the left of the *v.* are diverse from all parties on the right of the v. You will also learn the rules of citizenship: when a human being, a corporation, or an unincorporated association (a labor union, a charity, a partnership) is a citizens of a particular state.

Second, the amount in controversy must exceed $75,000. For the purposes of our class, this can only be met if one plaintiff sues one defendant with one claim exceeding $75k, OR if one plaintiff sues the same defendant with multiple claims that add up to exceed $75k. The latter circumstances is called "stacking."

We may also begin our discussion of the third way a federal court can obtain SMJ: supplemental jurisdiction.

## Day 3: SMJ p.3 — SuppJ and Removal Jurisdiction

So far, you have learned two ways a federal court can obtain SMJ: arising under jurisdiction and diversity jurisdiction. Today we will discuss the third way a federal court can hear a claim: supplemental jurisdiction. Supplemental jurisdiction applies when there are multiple claims in a single federal court lawsuit. For at least one of those claims, either arising under or diversity provides a bases of SMJ. We say that for those claims for which arising under or diversity is available, there is "original" SMJ or an "independent" basis of SMJ. In class, we will refer to this claim (or these claims) as the "anchoring claim(s)." But for other claims, there is neither arising under or diversity. So for these claims, there is no original SMJ or independent basis of SMJ. Note: If federal law creates a claim, there is arising under definition under 1331. For that reason, we often say that supplemental SMJ might be necessary for a "state law claim." Don't get confused. All diversity claims are state law claims. So the "state law claim" that might need supplemental SMJ is one that either (i) lacks complete diversity, or (ii) is AI deficient.

Federal courts have discretion to deny supplemental jurisdiction. That's unusual!

We will also consider: Are there different considerations if the anchoring claim created SMJ under arising under jurisdiction or diversity jurisdiction? What factors does a federal court consider when exercising their discretion to deny supplemental jurisdiction?

We may also begin removal jurisdiction, which allows a party (typically the defendant) to transfer a case from a state court to federal court. What are the justifications for removal jurisdiction? Why would a defendant want to remove their case to federal court? What are the rules for removal? When will a case be remanded (sent back to state court)?

## Day 4: SMJ p.4 — Abstention

So far, we have discussed the ways a federal court can obtain SMJ over a claim: arising under jurisdiction, diversity jurisdiction, and supplemental jurisdiction. Are there any occasions on which a federal court will refuse to hear a case, despite having SMJ? This is known as abstention. Abstention is pretty rare and only occurs for a few reasons.

<!-- page: 3 -->

First, even if the requirements for diversity jurisdiction are met, a federal court will not hear marital (divorce) or probate cases. These are called the martial and probate exceptions to diversity jurisdiction.

Second, a federal court might abstain from hearing a case if there is simultaneous ongoing federal and state court litigation among substantially the same parties on substantially the same issues. Why would a federal court wish to abstain in this case? How often does the court do this?

## Day 5: HCOL (completed by BEN & SUNNY)

Sometimes, the transaction giving rise to a lawsuit occurs across state boundaries. Perhaps a party in State A makes a contract in State B, and the party in State A later alleges that the other party breached. Perhaps a set of events giving rise to a tort begins in State A and finishes in State B. If the laws of State A and State B are the same, there is no problem about how to decide a case. But, if the law of State A differs from the law of State B, which governs the case? That is the subject for today.

## Day 6: IPJ p.2

Up until this point, we have spent our time discussing federal court subject matter jurisdiction, which is all about what type of case a federal court can hear. Today, we will be moving on to personal jurisdiction. Personal jurisdiction answers a different question: what are the geographic limits on a court's power? In other words, where, geographically, can you file a lawsuit?

Consider: if a plaintiff from South Carolina was in a car accident in Nebraska with a Defendant from North Carolina, we would all logically agree she cannot sue in Hawaii. But why, if she has SMJ in federal court through diversity jurisdiction? The answer is that no Hawaii court, state or federal, would have personal jurisdiction over the defendant.

For the purposes of our class, federal courts and state courts are the same when it comes to personal jurisdiction. That's a big difference from SMJ. Remember that class periods 1–4 concerned SMJ in the federal courts; state courts were willing to hear cases about anything, and Congress only rarely lodged exclusive SMJ in the federal courts.

You will learn about three types of personal jurisdiction: in personam, in rem, and quasi in rem. Let's start with in personam jurisdiction (IPJ). In personam personal jurisdiction is a basis for jurisdiction over a person. How do you get it? There are two primary ways: general and specific. Today, you will learn the answers to the following: What are the ways to obtain general IPJ? What is the specific IPJ test? What options does a defendant have when a plaintiff files a lawsuit?

## Day 7: IPJ p.3

Yesterday, you learned several bases for general in personam jurisdiction (the defendant is really at home; the defendant is *Daimler* "at home;" the defendant consents via failure to

<!-- page: 4 -->

object or a forum selection clause; or (maybe?) the defendant is physically tagged.) You also learned that specific in-personam jurisdiction requires a long arm statute; minimal contacts linking the defendant, subject matter of litigation, and forum; and the *McGee* fair and reasonable factors.

When determining whether there are minimal contacts for specific IPJ, you might look towards whether the defendant purposefully availed themselves of the privileges of the forum state. This is relatively easy to determine when dealing with intentional torts or contracts cases — for example, if you sell cars in Georgia, you have likely availed yourself of the privilege of doing business in Georgia.

But what about in the case of unintentional torts? And what about outside the commercial context, such as cases involve family law and child support payments? These cases are harder to determine.

We will also move on to our discussion on quasi-in rem and in-rem jurisdiction today. In-rem and quasi-in-rem are asserting personal jurisdiction over a piece of property. This piece of property must be in the forum state. We will run into the same problem we saw on Day 2, when we discussed locating incorporeal entities for purposes of diversity jurisdiction. For example, how can we locate personal debt? Bonds? Stocks? Also, does the exercise of in-rem or quasi-in rem jurisdiction require an analysis of minimum contacts, as required for in-personam jurisdiction?

## Day 8: IPJ p.4

As we conclude our study of jurisdiction over person and property, we will focus on three unclear areas of law related to personal jurisdiction.

1. As you learned yesterday, there are two ways to think about quasi-in-rem jurisdiction following *Schaffer*. One option is that quasi-in-rem jurisdiction lives on. The second option is that a court cannot exercise quasi-in-rem jurisdiction unless the property had a relationship to the subject matter of the dispute. This view practically eliminates the entire point of quasi-in-rem jurisdiction. Which is correct?

Are all traditional bases for exercising IPJ unconstitutional if they do not provide minimal contacts? Is an in-state tag sufficient for a court to exercise personal jurisdiction?
