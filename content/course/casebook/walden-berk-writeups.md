## *Walden v. Fiore*

A pair of Nevada residents were traveling home from Puerto Rico. They had a layover in Atlanta. TSA searched their bags at the Puerto Rico airport and found $97,000 in cash. The Nevadans informed TSA that they were professional gamblers, and the money was their starter stake and winnings from the trip. TSA allowed them to board the flight to Atlanta with their cash, but they alerted the DEA. At the Atlanta airport, Walden, a Georgia DEA agent, stopped the Nevadans and seized their money on the grounds that it constituted the proceeds of illegal activity. According to the Nevadans' subsequent lawsuit (you will later learn that because of the procedural posture of the case, the court had to assume that the following facts were true), Walden assisted in drafting a false probable cause affidavit that would enable the government to "forfeit," meaning to seek a court order to allowing the government to become the owner of, the seized funds. Walden sent that affidavit to the US Attorney's Office in Georgia, but it was never acted upon, meaning the U.S. Attorney never filed a forfeiture lawsuit. Plaintiffs' funds were returned eight-to-nine months afterwards. Plaintiffs filed a tort claim against Walden in the District Court of Nevada based on Walden's search, seizure, and false affidavit. The district court found that it lacked personal jurisdiction over Walden in Nevada, but the Ninth Circuit reversed. The Ninth Circuit noted that Walden knew that the affidavit would injure the Nevada plaintiffs in Nevada, and that some of the cash originated from Nevada. According to the Circuit, these facts were enough to establish a basis for personal jurisdiction.

The Supreme Court reversed, concluding that the Nevada District Court for Nevada did not have personal jurisdiction over Walden. The Court held: "[T]he plaintiff cannot be the only link between the defendant and the forum. Rather, it is the defendant's conduct that must form the necessary connection with the forum State." There will be times when the defendant's contacts with the forum are wrapped up with their relationship to the plaintiff, but that relationship, absent those contacts with the forum, is insufficient to establish personal jurisdiction on its own. Applying those principles to the case, Justice Thomas for a unanimous Court concluded that "none of [Walden's] challenged conduct had anything to do with Nevada itself." Nor did the injury suffered by the plaintiffs (the seizure of funds) have anything to do with Nevada itself, because the State was only implicated by the plaintiffs' independent choice to return there. There was thus no personal jurisdiction over Walden in Nevada.

But wait...what about *Calder v. Jones*? Didn't that case allow personal jurisdiction where the defendant knew that their actions would cause injury to a plaintiff in the forum state? Not so, said Justice Thomas. He reasoned that the reputational injury in *Calder* would not have occurred but for the fact that the defendant wrote an article for publication in the forum state that was subsequently read by other citizens of the forum about events that occurred in the forum. In this way, the reputational injury was tied up with the forum itself, not just the plaintiff. Here, however, the plaintiffs would have suffered the same loss of funds no matter where they were, Nevada or otherwise. Is this convincing?

## *Berk v. Choy*

Harold Berk, on a seemingly innocuous trip to Delaware, broke his ankle after falling out of bed. His resulting hospital stay would kickstart the Court's latest chapter in the *Erie* saga. While the details do not matter, the upshot is that Berk sued his physician, Dr. Choy, and the hospital for medical malpractice (a state law cause of action) in federal court, alleging diversity jurisdiction. Under Delaware law, a complaint for medical malpractice must include an affidavit of merit signed by a medical practitioner in which the practitioner affirms under penalty of perjury that there were reasonable grounds to believe that the defendant committed malpractice. (The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, as you know, contain no such requirement.) Berk did not file the affidavit, and the District Court dismissed the lawsuit. The Third Circuit affirmed, holding that the Delaware law was substantive and therefore applied in federal court.

The Supreme Court reversed, holding that the Delaware law did not apply in federal court. Justice Barrett, writing for eight justices, held that the Delaware law requiring an affidavit at the complaint stage conflicted with FRCPs 8(a) and 12(b). Both provisions addressed what information a plaintiff must provide about their claim at the first step of the litigation. Rule 8, she reasoned, established both the minimum and maximum pleading standards in federal court. Rule 8 requires only a short plain statement showing entitlement to relief. Delaware law therefore conflicted with the Federal Rules because the state provision required both a pleading and evidence of a malpractice claim (the affidavit) for the lawsuit to proceed. Rule 12(b)(6), Justice Barrett further reasoned, listed the grounds for which a federal court complaint could be dismissed for inadequacy, and it made no mention of an affidavit. As interpreted by the Delaware Supreme Court, failure to file an affidavit of merit in a malpractice claim constitutes grounds for dismissal of the case, implying that a federal court must look beyond the four corners of the complaint in ruling on a motion to dismiss. You know from *Twombly*, however, that motions to dismiss under the Federal Rules are evaluated only on the basis of the factual allegations in the complaint itself. Delaware law thus conflicted with the Federal Rules.

Justice Barrett then considered whether Rule 8 "really regulate[d] procedure" under *Sibbach*. Like all the federal rules examined in *Erie* cases, Rule 8 did, in fact, regulate procedure. Despite Rule 8's indirect effects on substantive rights, it regulated process, not "the rights themselves, the available remedies, or rules of decision." The Court also held that Rule 8 applied to the issue at hand because it specified what a plaintiff had to provide at the complaint stage, namely, a short and plain statement showing entitlement to relief and nothing else. The Court ended its opinion with a terse rejection of Justice Stevens's reliance on the Rules Enabling Act in *Shady Grove*, noting in dicta that it was irrelevant whether the displaced state law was procedural.

On its face, the *Berk* opinion is unremarkable. The news in the case is the dog that did not bark, or perhaps, the dog that did not consider barking. At no point did Justice Barrett consider narrowly interpreting the federal rules to accommodate Delaware's interest in protecting its medical services community from certain kinds of malpractice lawsuits. Couldn't the Court, had it so desired,

have held that Rule 8 specified that a federal court complaint must include a short plain statement showing entitlement to relief but was silent on whether anything more was required? Given the long-running drumbeat from the medical community that defensive medicine drives up health care costs, couldn't Delaware (and the many states like it that have similar laws) have pointed to powerful concerns about primary conduct that supported its law? Why didn't Justice Barrett at least consider reading Rules 8 and 12 "with sensitivity to important state interests," as five justices had insisted be done in *Shady Grove*?
