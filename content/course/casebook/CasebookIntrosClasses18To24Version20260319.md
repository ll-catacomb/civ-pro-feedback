## Day 18: Appellate Litigation

A trial court judgment is not necessarily the end of parties' legal conflict. Parties can appeal any decision a trial judge makes to a higher court. A key question: when in a proceeding may a party who does not like a trial court order appeal it? Should a party be able to appeal any trial court order immediately after the trial court issues it? Or should the party have to wait, and if so, until when?

We will study only the federal system's answers to these questions. State systems have different answers, but you are not responsible for them in this class.

In the federal system, the doctrine that determines when a party may appeal a trial court decision is called "appellate jurisdiction." Like a trial court, a court of appeals must have jurisdiction over a proceeding to hear it. For the purposes of our class, the source of the appellate court's jurisdiction turns on the nature of the decision below. In most instances, we need to know whether the lower court decision was a "final order." The court system is busy, and we assume that the trial court usually decides things correctly, so we only allow for appeals at the end. Today, we will discuss what constitutes a final order, as well as a few key exceptions to the rule that a litigant must wait until a trial court issues a final order to appeal any decision that led up to the final order. These exceptions allow litigants to seek appellate review in the middle of a case (prior to the trial court's final judgment). We call such appeals "interlocutory."

## Day 19: Expanding the Lawsuit

Lawsuits are long and expensive. It is more efficient for the court system when parties sue everyone related to a given controversy at once on as many possible theories of relief as once. Until now, we've focused almost entirely on suits between one plaintiff and one defendant. Today, we will be expanding the lawsuit. One limit on who parties can add to a suit comes from what we've learned earlier in class: courts must have subject matter jurisdiction and personal jurisdiction, and venue must be proper over each of the claims they hear. We will also discuss rules for who can — and sometimes, who must — be part of the expanded lawsuit, and what sorts of claims parties can add against one another. Those rules are literally rules: the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

To repeat: for each claim and each party a litigant wants to add to the basic structure of single plaintiff sues single defendant on a single cause of action, the addition of the claim or party must be copacetic under (i) the FRCP, (ii) SMJ doctrine, and (iii) IPJ doctrine. In addition, venue must be proper with respect to the first set of claims that plaintiff(s) file(s) against defendant(s).

## Day 20: Claim Preclusion

As we learned last class, the court system wants to encourage efficiency by allowing, sometimes requiring, the addition of parties and claims to a lawsuit. One way courts enforce this desire for efficiency is through claim preclusion. Under claim preclusion, a party is barred from bringing a future suit on a "claim" that has already been litigated. Beware: the word "claim" in this context has a meaning totally and completely different from what it meant in the previous class period.

For claim preclusion to apply, three key elements must be present: (1) same claim, (2) same party, (3) a judgement that is final and on the merits. Today, we will discuss what each of these elements actually entails, as well as a few stealth factors that impact whether a party is able to bring their claim.

## Day 21: Issue Preclusion

Last class we learned about claim preclusion. Issue preclusion is a totally different doctrine, and it is critically important to keep the two separated in your head, your notes, and (eventually) your outline.

Under issue preclusion (sometimes called collateral estoppel), a party can bar another party from relitigating an issue that was litigated previously; for issue preclusion to work, the party to be barred had to have lost on that issue in the previous litigation. An issue is much narrower than a claim and is usually factual. Issue preclusion requires four elements to run: (1) same issue, (2) actually litigated, (3) necessary to the decision, (4) mutuality. Today, we will discuss each of these elements, as well as a few stealth factors that impact whether a party is able to invoke issue preclusion.

## Day 22: Intersystem Preclusion, Agency Preclusion, Class Actions 1

Until today, we have discussed examples where Lawsuit 1 and Lawsuit 2 happen in the same court system. But what happens for claim and issue preclusion when they happen in different court systems? We have learned several instances where preclusion law may differ by jurisdiction.

Article IV Section 1 of the Constitution tells us that states must give full faith and credit to judicial decisions in other states but is silent as to what happens when L1 or L2 happens in federal court. 28 USC § 1738 extends full faith and credit to situations where L1 occurs in state court and L2 is brought in federal court, and federal common law dictates the rest. Today, we will learn how to apply these different sources of law to determine whose preclusion law to apply when Lawsuit 1 and Lawsuit 2 occur in different jurisdictions. And please remember, this may sound like an *Erie* or HCOL issue, but it is not! Don't be the person who confuses them.

Lastly, what happens if the rendering adjudicator is an agency? When must a subsequent adjudicator (like a state or federal court, or another agency) give preclusive effect to the agency decision? For an agency decision to have preclusive effect, the agency must provide due process protections discussed earlier in the course.

We will also begin our discussion of class action lawsuits today. As we've learned throughout class, lawsuits are extremely costly and our legal system is incredibly complex, making it inaccessible to most people. When small wrongs have happened to lots of people, they face a collective action problem. Class actions provide one solution. Is a class action lawsuit a procedural aggregation device or the creation of a new, single legal entity? We're not really sure. Today, we will discuss these two different treatments as it results to SMJ, IPJ, and venue for class action lawsuits. We will also discuss the prerequisites to forming a class: numerosity, commonality, typicality, and representativeness, as well as a few other requirements that may attach depending on the type of class action you are filing.
