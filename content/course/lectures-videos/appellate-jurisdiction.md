## § 1292(a)(1): Interlocutory Appeals of Injunctions

We examined 28 U.S.C. § 1292(a)(1), which provides appellate jurisdiction for orders granting, modifying, refusing to grant, or refusing to continue an injunction.

The underlying logic has two sides:

- **Defendant-side**: If a court is going to impose affirmative obligations on a defendant, the defendant should have an immediate appeal to test whether that ruling is legally correct.
- **Plaintiff-side**: If a plaintiff has alleged irreparable harm and the injunction is denied, there is a presumptive emergency that warrants immediate appellate review to determine whether that harm should be prevented.

---

## § 1292(b): The Double Discretion Doctrine

[00:05:00] Section 1292(b) establishes a two-step discretionary mechanism for interlocutory appeals.

**Step 1 — District court certification.** The district judge must enter a written order finding that:
1. The prior order decided a controlling issue of law;
2. There is substantial ground for difference of opinion on that issue; and
3. An immediate appeal would materially advance the ultimate resolution of the litigation.

**Step 2 — Circuit court acceptance.** Within 10 days of that certification, the losing party must petition the Court of Appeals. The court of appeals has complete discretion to accept or decline. Factors include the procedural posture of the case, the costs the parties are incurring, and the court's own docket. The circuits exercise very wide discretion here.

---

## The Cohen Collateral Order Doctrine

The collateral order doctrine provides appellate jurisdiction for certain interlocutory orders. An order qualifies when it:
1. **Finally decides** the particular issue;
2. Is **collateral to the merits** of the litigation; and
3. Is **effectively unreviewable later**.

The third element is where most collateral order claims fail. The Supreme Court is very reluctant to find that issues are effectively unreviewable on later appeal. Examples the Court has rejected include denials of motions to transfer or dismiss on forum non-convenience grounds, and denials of protective orders based on privilege.

Examples the Court has accepted include the issue in *Cohen* itself — whether a litigation bond is required — and abstention-based stays.

## Petition for Writ of Mandamus

[00:15:00] A writ of mandamus is technically a new legal proceeding in the Court of Appeals — a lawsuit against the district judge — asking the appellate court to order the judge to take a specific action. It functions like an appeal but is not technically one.

**Two categories:**

1. **Supervisory mandamus** — illustrated by the *LaBuy* litigation, where the Court of Appeals observed a district court repeatedly referring cases for trial to special masters and directed the district judge to stop, holding that the judge must personally hear cases.

2. **Emergency mandamus** — far more common of the two. The petitioner must show:
   - Irreparable harm will result if the court waits for a traditional appeal;
   - A clear and indisputable right to relief; and
   - That issuing the writ would be a wise exercise of the court's discretion given all relevant circumstances.

These factors closely track the preliminary injunction standard, and courts asked to issue emergency mandamus often ask: Is this effectively reviewable later? What are the consequences? How certain is the right to relief?

## Expanding the Lawsuit Beyond a Single Plaintiff vs. Single Defendant

[00:30:00] With appellate jurisdiction concluded, we turned to expanding the lawsuit. The most important structural point: **to move beyond a single plaintiff versus a single defendant, you must run a two-step analysis.**

1. **Federal rules permission**: Is it permissible under the Federal Rules to accumulate additional claims or additional parties?
2. **Jurisdictional analysis**: Is there subject matter jurisdiction, personal jurisdiction, venue, and any other required predicate — analyzed claim by claim and party by party?

### Multiple Plaintiffs and Defendants

Under Rule 20, multiple plaintiffs may join if their claims arise from the same transaction or occurrence and share a common issue of law or fact — a low threshold.

### Counterclaims: Rule 13

Whenever there is an arrow pointing one way (a claim from one party to another), Rule 13 applies:
- **Compulsory counterclaim**: If the claim arises from the same transaction or occurrence as the original claim, the defending party *must* assert it or forfeit it once the first case goes to judgment.
- **Permissive counterclaim**: Any other claim the defending party holds against the opposing party; it need not be asserted.

The loss of a compulsory counterclaim triggers when the first case reaches trial court judgment — that is when preclusion rules attach.

### Cross-Claims

Cross-claims (Rule 13(g)) are always permissive and require the same transaction or occurrence. Claims by plaintiffs invoking supplemental jurisdiction warrant particular attention: § 1367(b) restricts supplemental jurisdiction for certain plaintiff-side claims even when the federal rules would permit them.

### Third-Party Complaints: Rule 14

Third-party practice does not invoke the transaction-or-occurrence test. Instead, it operates on a theory of **shifted liability** — most commonly when a defendant brings in its insurer, saying: "If I have to pay, you have to pay." This is the one arrow that does not depend on transaction or occurrence.

### Rule 18: Claim Joinder

Once any claim exists between two parties, Rule 18 allows the asserting party to bring any additional claim against the opposing party whatsoever — even claims completely unrelated to the original. This promotes efficiency and allows parties to bargain all their claims against each other at once. Rule 42 then empowers the district court to restructure the litigation into coherent pieces as it sees fit.

## The Transaction-or-Occurrence Test: *Heyward-Robinson*

[00:45:00] A quick walkthrough of *Heyward-Robinson* <!-- corrected: "Hayward Robinson" → "Heyward-Robinson" --> illustrates how broadly federal courts interpret the transaction-or-occurrence test.

Because of a quirk in pre-§ 1367 supplemental jurisdiction law (which you need not know), the court had to determine whether two separate construction contracts and jobsites arose from the same transaction or occurrence. Despite being geographically separated, the court found they did because:

- The contracts were negotiated at roughly the same time;
- They contained **cross-termination** and **cross-default clauses** — a default on one gave the non-breaching party the right to cancel both;
- Payments were not differentiated between the two contracts, showing the parties treated them as a single arrangement;
- Work on both jobs was ongoing simultaneously; and
- A single insurance policy guaranteed performance under both contracts.

The general trend in federal courts is to read "transaction or occurrence" broadly to encompass any logical relationship or relationship in time, space, or evidentiary overlap — not without limit, but generously.

This test will be central when we turn to claim preclusion, where "same claim" is essentially coextensive with "same transaction or occurrence."
