## Overview

This is the summary for the first day of Civil Procedure (Sections 2 and 3). We covered the rules governing when a case can begin in the federal court system.

---

## The Federal Court System and the Choice of Forum

[00:00:00] We began with the basic structural chart: the state court system on the left (trial court → appellate court → state high court) and the federal court system on the right (U.S. District Court → U.S. Court of Appeals → U.S. Supreme Court). The state court system is always willing to hear any case. The question is when the plaintiff also has the option to file in federal district court.

A plaintiff has a choice of federal court only when the **United States District Court has subject matter jurisdiction** over the dispute.

---

## The Constitutional and Statutory Framework

The Constitution grants Congress the power to confer jurisdiction on the lower federal courts. That constitutional power is broad. But unless and until Congress actually enacts a statute conferring jurisdiction, the lower federal courts cannot exercise it.

Visually: there is a large oval representing the outer limit of constitutional power, and a smaller oval inside it representing the actual statutory grants Congress has made. For a case to begin in federal court, it must fall within the smaller, statutory oval — not just the constitutional one.

---

## § 1331: Arising Under Jurisdiction

[00:15:00] The first statutory basis is **28 U.S.C. § 1331**, which grants jurisdiction over cases "arising under the laws of the United States."

Despite the fact that identical "arising under" language in the Constitution is read broadly, the Supreme Court in *Motley* interprets § 1331 narrowly: arising under jurisdiction exists only when the plaintiff's **cause of action** is federal — meaning the right to sue was created by federal law.

**Key rule from *Motley* — the well-pleaded complaint rule**: The federal question must appear on the face of the plaintiff's well-pleaded complaint. Federal issues that arise only as anticipated defenses or in response to defenses do not suffice.

In *Motley* itself, the plaintiffs brought a **state law breach of contract** claim against a railroad that had refused to honor free-pass contracts. There were federal issues lurking in the case, but they arose only as defenses — not as part of the cause of action itself. The contract existed, the railroad breached it, and the harm was clear — all without reference to any federal issue. The Supreme Court dismissed for lack of subject matter jurisdiction, even though neither party had raised the issue and the case had already proceeded to the Supreme Court.

**Structural rules from *Motley*:**
1. The constitutional grant of judicial power is a grant to Congress, not directly to the courts.
2. The lower federal courts can only hear cases within the statutory grant.
3. A federal court has an **independent duty** to assess its own subject matter jurisdiction — it must dismiss *sua sponte* even if both parties argue that jurisdiction exists. This obligation never expires; it persists on appeal.

**Exclusive federal jurisdiction**: A subset of arising-under cases — primarily federal antitrust, patents and copyrights, and suits against the United States — must be filed in federal court. Congress has designated these as exclusive.

*Historical note*: Until 1875, there was no general arising under statute. Federal causes of action created between 1790 and 1875 had to be initiated in state court.

---

## The Smith-Grable Exception

[00:35:00] *Motley* would be clean law if it were the end of the story. It is not. Even when a plaintiff's cause of action is created by state law, there may still be arising under jurisdiction under § 1331 if a federal issue is necessarily embedded in the state law claim.

We began discussion of *Smith*. There, shareholders used a **state law shareholder's derivative action** — a state-created cause of action — alleging that the corporation's directors and officers had damaged the corporation by investing in United States government bonds that were allegedly unconstitutional and therefore worthless.

The structure is:
- **Cause of action**: State law (shareholder's derivative suit alleging breach of fiduciary duty)
- **Federal issue**: Whether Congress had the power to issue the bonds — i.e., whether those bonds were valid under the Constitution

The federal issue is not incidental. It is embedded within the state law claim: there is no way to prove that the directors and officers did something wrong without first establishing that the bonds are worthless, which requires resolving the federal constitutional question.

This contrasts directly with *Motley*, where the breach-of-contract cause of action — contract formed, contract breached, harm suffered — could be fully proved without ever encountering the federal issue. The federal issue entered *Motley* only through the railroad's defense.
