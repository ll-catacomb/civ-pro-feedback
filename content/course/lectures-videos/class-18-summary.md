## Overview

This is a summary for Class 18. We finished some material from Class 17 on trial mechanics and post-trial practice, then turned to appellate jurisdiction.

## Rule 6(b): Non-Extendable Time Limits

Rule 6(b) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure provides that certain time limits specified in the rules — particularly those associated with motions for a new trial and motions for judgment as a matter of law — cannot be extended. Even if all parties agree to an extension, and even if the district court purports to enter an order extending the time, the order has no effect. The reason is a strong policy in favor of finality: the trial should be a final event, and we need finality in the resolution of litigation.

---

## Rule 60(b): Relief from Judgment

Rule 60 is a motion for relief from a prior judgment. The rule's language is broad, suggesting the court system would be quite willing to revisit prior judgments — but in practice, courts are extremely reluctant to do so, and it almost never happens.

Even if a party exhausts all appeals and it later turns out the party was legally correct, the fact that a court previously ruled incorrectly is generally insufficient to reopen the judgment under Rule 60(b). Being right is not enough. The finality policy is very strong. For right or wrong, once litigation is resolved, it stays resolved.

## Appellate Jurisdiction: General Principles

We used *Liberty Mutual Insurance Co. v. Wetzel* <!-- corrected: "Liberty Mutual case" → Liberty Mutual Insurance Co. v. Wetzel --> to lay out the black letter law of appellate jurisdiction. Appellate jurisdiction governs when an appeal may be taken.

Two principles parallel subject matter jurisdiction in the district courts:

1. **Appellate courts have an independent duty to notice a potential defect in their own appellate jurisdiction**, even if neither party raises it. If such a defect is found, the court must immediately dismiss the appeal and return the case to the district court. The parties cannot by agreement create appellate jurisdiction.
2. **Only Congress can grant appellate jurisdiction.** Appellate courts have no jurisdiction without a congressional grant. Congress controls the appellate courts through the appellate jurisdiction statutes just as it controls district courts through the subject matter jurisdiction statutes.

There is also a strong policy in the federal system against **interlocutory appeals** — appeals filed before the district court's final judgment. If a party believes the district court made a mistake (in denying a motion to dismiss, in a discovery ruling, in an evidentiary ruling), the party must generally wait until the final judgment and then appeal everything at once. The policy reflects a judgment that most district court decisions are correct, most errors will be corrected before final judgment, and most that are not will be harmless in any event.

---

## 28 U.S.C. § 1291: The Final Judgment Rule

The workhorse appellate jurisdiction statute is 28 U.S.C. § 1291, which grants the circuit courts appellate jurisdiction over **final decisions** of the district courts. "Final decision" means the district court's final judgment — the definitive resolution of the case. Everything up to that point must ordinarily await that final judgment before it can be appealed.

---

## Rule 54(b): Partial Final Judgment

Rule 54(b) is not an independent basis for appellate jurisdiction. It is an interpretation of what "final judgment" means under § 1291, available only when:

1. The case involves **more than one claim**. For Rule 54(b) purposes, "claim" means a legal theory + a set of facts + a party. Any variation in one of those three components creates a different claim. (*Liberty Mutual* involved only one claim and therefore could not use Rule 54(b).)
2. The district court has **finally decided some but not all claims**.
3. The district court **expressly finds**, either orally on the record or in writing, that there is **no just reason for delay**, and enters judgment on those adjudicated claims.

When these conditions are met, the losing party on the adjudicated claims may take an immediate appeal. A 30-day clock starts running from the entry of judgment; failure to file a notice of appeal within 30 days forfeits the right to appeal.

The key consideration in finding "no just reason for delay" is whether the adjudicated claims are sufficiently unrelated — factually, legally, and logically — from the remaining claims that the appellate court can hear the appeal now without inefficiency, rather than being forced to revisit the same subject matter again when the remaining claims are later decided.

## The Cohen Exception (Collateral Order Doctrine)

The *Cohen* exception is another interpretation of "final decision" under § 1291. Appellate jurisdiction still derives from § 1291, but *Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp.* <!-- corrected: "Cohen exception" origin case → Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp. --> defines a category of orders that are "final" for purposes of § 1291 even though the case as a whole is not over.

Three requirements must be met (with a possible fourth):

1. **Finality as to the issue**: The district court's ruling on that particular issue is final — the district court does not anticipate revisiting it. (A summary judgment ruling is not final in this sense because Rule 56 itself says such orders can be reconsidered. But most orders satisfy this criterion.)
2. **Collateral to the merits**: The issue must not be intertwined with the merits of the case. We do not want an appellate court resolving issues closely tied to the merits while merits litigation continues in the district court.
3. **Effectively unreviewable later**: A later appeal at the end of the case can provide no practical remedy. This is the hardest criterion to meet.
4. **(Stealth criterion)**: Courts are reluctant to apply the *Cohen* exception to highly discretionary rulings that require extensive familiarity with the record. They prefer to apply it to clean, binary legal issues — up or down, this way or that.

**Examples from the cases:**

*In Cohen itself*: Whether a plaintiff in a shareholder derivative action had to post a litigation bond was immediately appealable. If the district court ruled (as an Erie matter) that no bond was required, there was no effective way to challenge that ruling after the case ended — by then, no insurer would sell the plaintiff a bond at a reasonable price.

*Not effectively unreviewable (no Cohen exception)*:
- A district court order to disclose allegedly privileged material — reviewable after final judgment, when the court can assess whether the material was used at trial and whether any error was harmless.
- A forum selection clause issue — if the clause should have been enforced, the appellate court can simply direct dismissal and invite the parties to relitigate at the proper forum.

*Cohen exception present*: A *Colorado River* stay order (discussed in the prior class period on Colorado River abstention).

## 28 U.S.C. § 1292(a)(1): Injunction Appeals

28 U.S.C. § 1292(a)(1) provides appellate jurisdiction over district court orders:

- Granting or refusing a preliminary injunction
- Modifying or refusing to modify an injunction
- Dissolving or refusing to dissolve an injunction

The rationale: either the enjoined party is suffering adverse consequences and needs appellate review, or an emergency has been alleged and the district court has declined to act. In either case, we want the appellate court to be able to take a look.

**Important limitation**: A district court's refusal to *rule* on a motion for an injunction is not immediately appealable under § 1292(a)(1). If the district court is simply sitting on the motion, your only possible avenue is to petition the appellate court for a writ of mandamus ordering the district court to issue a ruling. That is strategically dangerous — it tends to annoy the district court — and will be addressed in the next class.
