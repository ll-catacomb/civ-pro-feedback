## Notice: The Reasonableness Standard

In terms of black letter law, the Court articulates in *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust* <!-- corrected: "the lane" → Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust --> a general reasonableness standard: efforts to provide notice, in order to be constitutionally sufficient, must be reasonable under all the circumstances.

Several principles help give content to that reasonableness standard:

**First**, in order to judge reasonableness, it often helps to put yourself in the position of someone who actually cared whether the defendant — or whoever is subject to some legal consequence — actually received notice. If you pretended you cared, what would you do?

**Second**, under *Jones v. Flowers*, the duty to act reasonably to provide notice does not stop after the first attempt. If a person or court system attempting to provide notice discovers that the first attempt was unsuccessful, and there is something reasonable that could be done to follow up, that person or institution may be under a continuing duty to try something else.

**Third**, the court system sometimes takes into account changing social and socioeconomic circumstances. For example, courts have generally shifted away from requiring certified mail and have not constitutionalized that requirement. *Jones v. Flowers* suggests that regular mail may sometimes be sufficient — or even preferable — in modern settings. The courts have not generally kept pace with the digitalization of our society and the information age, and we discussed some of the consequences of that.

**Fourth** — and perhaps most important as a black letter rule — actual notice is never the constitutional standard. It is never the case that the Constitution requires actual notice before a party's rights may be extinguished. A court system can extinguish rights, and consider them adjudicated, even if the individual whose rights are being extinguished did not receive actual notice. The standard is reasonableness of the efforts to provide notice.

We worked through specific examples and particular fact patterns, and that is where we left the notice discussion.

## Opportunity to Be Heard: The *Mathews v. Eldridge* Test

We then turned to opportunity to be heard — what kind of opportunity must be provided before a consequence is imposed. This covers both pre-judgment actions (such as seizure of property prior to judgment) and actions in the administrative state (such as termination of benefits before a hearing).

The governing black letter law here is the *Mathews v. Eldridge* <!-- corrected: "Matthews versus Eldritch" → Mathews v. Eldridge --> test. The test asks courts to consider three factors in determining whether a procedural scheme meets constitutional minimums for opportunity to be heard:

**Factor One: The private interest** at stake — the interest of all private litigants involved in the adjudicatory proceeding.

**Factor Two: The risk of erroneous determination** under the current scheme, compared to the risk of erroneous determination under the scheme that the challenger proposes. In *Mathews v. Eldridge*, the claimant for SSDI benefits argued that the existing scheme — which allowed benefits to be cut off prior to a live hearing, subject to later reconsideration and appeal — was constitutionally deficient, and that benefits could not be cut off until after a live hearing. The second *Mathews* factor demands a comparison between the world we live in and the alternative world the challenger proposes. What is the difference in risk of erroneous determination between those two schemes?

**Factor Three: The government's interest** — in minimizing the cost and delay of adjudication, and sometimes in avoiding payment of benefits that are not ultimately owed.

### Application in *Mathews v. Eldridge*

The Court distinguished *Mathews v. Eldridge* from *Goldberg v. Kelly* — which involved welfare benefits — on two grounds:

First, on the private interest: the SSDI benefits at issue in *Mathews* did not implicate quite the same magnitude of private interest as welfare benefits. SSDI's purpose is not subsistence — not to allow people to eat, have a roof over their heads, or meet basic human needs. It is instead income maintenance for a period in which a previously employed person becomes unable to work due to disability. It is a form of workers' insurance. Because eligibility is based on work history rather than need or destitution, and because income and assets are irrelevant to eligibility, the Court inferred that subsistence is not the purpose of the scheme.

Second, on the risk of erroneous determination: the type of decision in *Mathews* can generally be made on documents — specifically medical records — whereas the determination in *Goldberg*, according to the Court, requires live testimony. If you are applying this framework to a new fact pattern, focus on the nature of the interest at stake and the kind of decision involved: Can it be made on documents alone, or does it require live testimony? If the latter, it may be more difficult to impose consequences before a live hearing, because the risk of erroneous determination would be too high.

### Other Factors Discussed

We also discussed the risk of erroneous determination that can arise when a clerk — rather than a judge — makes a decision, such as whether to issue a garnishment order or allow seizure of property. Courts have recognized that judges, because they adjudicate more often, are likely to make better decisions. A clerk's involvement might therefore increase the risk of erroneous determination, bearing on the second *Mathews* factor.

We also discussed the difference between schemes that require only conclusory allegations to obtain relief (such as the scheme in *Sniadach* <!-- corrected: "Schneidach" → Sniadach -->, where a clerk could issue a garnishment order upon the filing of a complaint-like document with no evidence required) versus schemes that require the requesting party to submit actual evidence. If evidence is required, that requirement reduces the risk of erroneous determination.
