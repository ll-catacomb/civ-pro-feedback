## The Problem Trusts Solve

[00:00:00] Rich people who do not want to spend their time managing their own money can create a separate legal entity — a **trust** — transfer money into it, and then hire someone to manage the money on the trust's behalf.

The trust is a separate legal entity, somewhat like a corporation. It holds assets and requires a human agent to direct its decisions. That agent is the **trustee**, engaged by contract — analogous to the way a corporation engages directors and officers.

---

## Structure of a Trust

[00:05:00] The basic arrangement:

1. **Settlor** (the rich person who creates the trust) transfers money or assets into the trust.
2. The trust hires a **trustee** to manage the assets — to invest, to make decisions about the portfolio.
3. The trust generates income through those investments.
4. The trustee distributes income to **beneficiaries** according to the settlor's instructions.

**Two types of beneficiaries:**
- **Life beneficiary**: Receives income during some period, often the settlor's lifetime.
- **Death beneficiary**: Receives the remaining trust assets (or ongoing income) upon the settlor's death.

Settlors often name themselves as the life beneficiary — essentially directing the trustee to pay them the investment returns while alive — and name children, grandchildren, or other heirs as the death beneficiaries. This is exactly what the wealthy woman in *Hanson v. Denckla* did: she named herself as life beneficiary and designated some (but not all) of her descendants as death beneficiaries.

---

## Why Use a Trust? (Tax Reasons)

[00:15:00] The primary reason is tax treatment. Assets transferred to a trust before death are no longer the settlor's property — they belong to a separate legal entity. They therefore fall outside the **estate** at death and receive more favorable tax treatment than assets that pass through probate. The estate consists only of assets still legally owned by the decedent at death.

A key condition: for the trust to receive this favorable treatment, the settlor must genuinely give up control. The settlor can direct *who* receives income (by designating beneficiaries), but **cannot direct how the trustee invests** — cannot instruct the trustee to buy specific bonds, stocks, or real estate, and certainly cannot instruct the trustee to purchase personal property for the settlor's own use. If the settlor retains effective control over the trust corpus, a court may find the trust invalidly created, collapsing the supposed separation between the person and the money.

---

## Wills and Probate: Contrast

[00:22:00] A will directs what happens to a decedent's **estate** — the assets legally owned at death. Persons who benefit under a will are called **legatees**. The estate goes through **probate**: a court-supervised process for identifying assets, paying debts, and distributing the remainder per the will's instructions.

Assets that passed into a trust before death are **not part of the estate** and do not go through probate. This is often the tax and estate-planning point of using a trust.

---

## Litigation Risks: Undue Influence and Trustee Misconduct

[00:28:00] Because large sums of money are involved and the arrangements are complex, trust structures generate a great deal of litigation:

**Undue influence**: When a settlor — especially an elderly person — creates a trust or designates beneficiaries, there is a risk that someone exercised undue influence over the settlor's decisions. In *Hanson v. Denckla*, the question is whether someone exercised undue influence either in the creation of the trust or in the designation of death beneficiaries.

**Trustee misconduct**: A trustee sits on someone else's money. The risk is self-dealing — for example, directing the trust to invest in the trustee's own business, putting the trustee in a conflict of interest. The trustee's duty is to act in the best interests of the beneficiaries; making favorable loans to one's own business at the trust's expense breaches that duty.

## Relevance to Personal Jurisdiction

The specific legal issue that makes trust arrangements important in a civil procedure course is **personal jurisdiction over the trustee**. In *Hanson v. Denckla*, the central question is whether Florida courts can exercise personal jurisdiction over a Delaware trustee to resolve a dispute about the trust's beneficiaries. The trust was created in Delaware; the settlor later moved to Florida. The trustee never acted in Florida and never had any contact with Florida — the trustee only had contacts with Delaware, where it was incorporated and conducted all of its trust administration.

Terry* <!-- corrected: "Chauffeur versus Terry" → *Chauffeurs, Teamsters & Helpers Local No. 391 v. Terry* --> also involve analogies to the trustee-beneficiary relationship.
