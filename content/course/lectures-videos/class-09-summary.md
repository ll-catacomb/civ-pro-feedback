## Venue: Basic Doctrine

[00:00:00] Venue is a **convenience doctrine** only. It has nothing to do with constitutional power, nothing to do with a court's authority to hear a particular type of case. It is governed entirely by federal statute and concerns which **federal district** (not which state) a case may be filed in.

Like personal jurisdiction, improper venue requires dismissal or transfer if the defendant objects. Like personal jurisdiction, venue may be waived by failing to object promptly.

### Federal Venue Statute § 1391

- **B1**: Venue is proper in any district where any defendant resides, if all defendants reside in the same state. Residency means domicile for individuals; for corporations and unincorporated associations, any district in which the entity is subject to personal jurisdiction.
- **B2**: Venue is proper where a substantial part of the events or omissions giving rise to the claim occurred, or where relevant property is located.
- **B3**: An outlet provision — available only when neither B1 nor B2 provides any available district. Essentially: if events occurred entirely outside the United States, or if defendants reside in multiple states with no overlapping B1 district and no substantial events in any US district.

## Personal Jurisdiction vs. Venue: How They Differ

[00:15:00] To illustrate with a hypothetical: suppose *Calder* <!-- corrected: "Castro" → *Calder* --> had been filed in the District of New Jersey. There would have been **venue** (the injury occurred there), but no **personal jurisdiction** (the federal court is limited by Rule 4(k)(1)(A) to what the New Jersey state courts could do, and those courts could not exercise personal jurisdiction on those facts).

Conversely, personal jurisdiction can exist without proper venue: if a state allows personal jurisdiction over a defendant, and the plaintiff files in a district within that state where no substantial events occurred, venue under B2 is improper even though personal jurisdiction would be valid.

## Forum Non-Convenience

[00:22:00] Forum non-convenience doctrine applies when:
1. A case is in **state court** and the suggestion is to send it to another state or abroad; or
2. A case is in **federal court** and the suggestion is to send it **abroad**.

It does **not** apply to move a case from one federal court to another federal court — use the transfer statutes for that. It does not apply to move a case from federal court to state court (absent a forum selection clause).

### Standard Analysis

In a garden-variety case without a forum selection clause:
1. **Presume** the plaintiff's choice of forum is correct (except when the plaintiff is from abroad — *Piper Aircraft* holds that presumption does not apply).
2. Consider the **private interest factors** from *Gulf Oil*: ease of access to evidence, compellability of witnesses, practical problems of the litigation.
3. Consider the **public interest factors** from *Gulf Oil*: court congestion, relationship between the community and the litigation, appropriateness of having a local jury sit on the case.
4. If those factors, taken together, overwhelm the presumption, dismiss and invite the plaintiff to refile elsewhere.

**Defendant's option to manufacture an alternative forum**: The defendant may promise not to raise defenses — including personal jurisdiction, venue, and limitations — in the alternative forum, thereby creating a viable forum that the court can recognize in its dismissal order.

**Note**: Forum non-convenience and the transfer statutes differ on this point. Under the transfer statutes, a defendant **cannot** manufacture a happy transferee court by promising to waive defenses there. The transferee court must independently qualify as a place where the plaintiff could have brought the case.

## The Transfer Statutes: §§ 1404, 1406, 1631

[00:35:00] For this course, treat these statutes collectively. The key rule: a case may be transferred only to a court in which it "**could have been brought**" — interpreted in *Hoffman v. Blaski* to mean a **happy court**, one with both personal jurisdiction and proper venue.

### Three Questions

**1. Must I transfer?**
If the court lacks personal jurisdiction or venue over the defendant (and the defendant is objecting), the court must transfer or dismiss.

**2. May I (and should I) transfer?**
May: only to a happy court under *Hoffman*.
Should: a forum non-convenience analysis — presume the plaintiff's choice is correct; weigh private and public *Gulf Oil* factors; transfer if those factors overcome the presumption.

**3. What happens if I do?** — The **Greiner Happy Court Rule**

Because a federal court sitting in diversity uses the choice-of-law rules of its home state (*Klaxon*), a transfer raises the question: which state's choice-of-law rules does the transferee court apply?

- **Transferor court was happy**: The transferee court uses the transferor state's choice-of-law rules, including the transferor state's limitations periods. Limitations periods here are treated as procedural by the forum state — so the transferor forum's limitations period travels with the case. The transfer is just a change of courtroom.
- **Transferor court was unhappy**: The Greiner Happy Court rule does not apply. The transferee court uses its own state's choice-of-law rules. The plaintiff filed in the wrong place and will not benefit from that choice.

### Forum Selection Clauses: *Atlantic Marine Construction Co. v. U.S. District Court* <!-- corrected: "Atlantic Marine" → *Atlantic Marine Construction Co. v. U.S. District Court* -->

When a defendant moves to transfer pursuant to a forum selection clause, three things change:

1. The **plaintiff's choice of forum is not presumed correct**. Instead, the court presumes in favor of the forum named in the clause.
2. **Private interest factors are not considered** — they were addressed at the time of contracting. Only public interest factors can overcome the presumption.
3. **The Greiner Happy Court rule does not apply**. The transferee court's choice-of-law rules govern.

The result: cases governed by forum selection clauses almost always transfer to the designated forum.
