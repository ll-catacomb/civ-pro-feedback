## Overview and Course Context

This video explains a doctrine from commercial transactions law called the Holder in Due Course Doctrine. We will encounter it again later in the semester — in particular for class period nine, in the form of *Swift v. Tyson*, which is a precursor case to *Erie*.

## Historical Background: The Problem with Hard Currency

To understand the Holder in Due Course Doctrine, consider the world 150 to 200 years ago. Banks were not common, and traveling to one — especially if you lived outside a major city — was a significant ordeal. Hard currency (dollar bills and coins) was equally difficult to come by. Much of the economy still relied on barter and trade, which are inefficient and make transacting business difficult.

To develop a functioning economy, you need a mechanism that allows people to transact without barter. That mechanism often requires credit: the idea that you can have something now and pay for it later. Around this time, the concept of memorializing credit in writing began to develop — promises to pay, and eventually, as banks emerged, what we now call checks.

## How Checks Work

A check is a piece of paper you write out to someone. Notably, it says "pay to the order of" the named person — not simply "pay this person." That phrasing is intentional. It gives the person receiving the check the right to order you (the check writer) to pay someone. And critically, that right can be passed along.

Instead of having to go to a bank to convert the check into cash, the person receiving it can endorse it over to someone else — essentially ordering the check writer to pay a new person. That new person can do the same, and so on. The check itself functions as currency, circulating from hand to hand without ever being deposited.

This was highly practical in an era when getting to a bank was difficult. Under commercial law, a check doesn't even need to be written on paper — technically, writing one on a shirt would still be valid. The point is that checks were designed to move as freely as money.

## The Swindle Problem

The difficulty arises as a check moves further from the original transaction. Suppose the person who originally received the check obtained it through fraud — by swindling the check writer. If the check goes no further and the check writer discovers the fraud, the writer can go to their bank and issue a stop payment order, blocking the fraudulent transaction.

But once the swindler endorses the check over to a third party in exchange for something of value, the problem compounds. That third party had no knowledge of the original fraud. If people downstream worry that any given check might be subject to a stop payment order, they will either refuse to accept checks or discount their value. A $10 check might only be worth $7.50 to someone who has to factor in the risk that it won't be honored. This undermines the entire purpose of allowing checks to circulate as currency.

## The Holder in Due Course Doctrine

To solve this problem, the law — developed primarily in the United Kingdom — created the **Holder in Due Course Doctrine**. The doctrine provides that if you are a downstream recipient of a check (the third, fourth, or tenth person to receive it, not the original alleged swindler), you can require the check writer to honor the check. You can override the stop payment order and demand payment.

The reasoning is straightforward: you had no knowledge of the original swindle, and you gave value in exchange for the check. The check writer, who did have direct dealings with the swindler, is in a far better position to identify and pursue the swindler through litigation. So the law says: you (the check writer) must pay the downstream holder, and then you go find and sue the swindler yourself.

This doctrine makes downstream parties more willing to accept checks at full face value, which is essential to making checks function as a reliable medium of exchange.

## Illustrative Example: Batgirl, the Joker, Thor, and Black Widow

Consider the following transaction chain:

- **Batgirl** (domiciled in South Carolina) writes a check "pay to the order of" the **Joker** in exchange for land — which, as it turns out, does not exist. The Joker has swindled her.
- Before Batgirl discovers the fraud, the **Joker** endorses the check over to **Thor** in exchange for a hammer.
- **Thor** endorses the check over to **Black Widow** in exchange for the Tesseract.

At the point Black Widow receives the check, Batgirl discovers the fraud. She contacts her bank and issues a stop payment order. When Black Widow tries to deposit the check, payment is refused.

Black Widow (domiciled in Massachusetts) then sues Batgirl in federal court — a diversity case. The question becomes: what law applies?

## Connection to *Swift v. Tyson* and the Erie Question

Some states at the time *Swift v. Tyson* was decided did not recognize the Holder in Due Course Doctrine. Those states held that the original fraud — the failure of consideration in the transaction between the swindler and the check writer — relieved the check writer of any obligation to honor the check, regardless of who held it downstream.

In *Swift v. Tyson*, the underlying facts follow this same pattern:

- **Swindlers** obtain a loan from a **creditor** (Swift).
- As part of the transaction, the swindlers convey nonexistent land to a **guarantor**, who in exchange guarantees repayment of the loan.
- The swindlers then pay the creditor via a check drawn on the guarantor.
- The swindlers disappear. The guarantor discovers the fraud, stops payment on the check, and refuses to honor the guarantee.
- The creditor sues the guarantor.

The legal question: Does New York state law — which did not recognize the Holder in Due Course Doctrine — apply? Or does some body of federal common law apply, which might recognize the doctrine?

More fundamentally: is there such a thing as federal common law that a federal court sitting in diversity can apply? That is the question at the heart of *Swift v. Tyson*, and ultimately the question that *Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins* will answer.
