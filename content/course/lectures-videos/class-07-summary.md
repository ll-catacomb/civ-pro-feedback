## Overview

Class 7 completed the personal jurisdiction framework by working through a large number of cases — *Hanson v. Denckla*, *World-Wide Volkswagen*, and *J. McIntyre Machinery* — to articulate the two-phase specific jurisdiction test and explore its implications.

---

## Federal Long-Arm Statute: Rule 4(k)(1)(A)

[00:00:00] Last class we discussed state long-arm statutes. Federal long-arm statutes work differently.

**Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 4(k)(1)(A)**: A federal court may exercise personal jurisdiction over a defendant whenever a court of the state in which the federal court is sitting could do so. It is precisely that simple — and that limiting. If the relevant state court could not exercise personal jurisdiction (either because the state long-arm statute does not reach the defendant, or because the Constitution blocks it), the federal court under 4(k)(1)(A) cannot exercise jurisdiction either.

## Building the Two-Phase Framework

[00:08:00] *McGee* expanded the analysis beyond the defendant's contacts alone. *Hanson v. Denckla* then imposed a constraint, and *World-Wide Volkswagen* confirmed the two-phase structure.

### Phase 1: Minimum Contacts (2A on the Framework)

Ask whether there are **minimum contacts** linking:
- the defendant,
- the subject matter of the litigation, and
- the forum.

The key element within minimum contacts is **purposeful availment**: did the defendant deliberately direct its activities toward the forum, seek to benefit from the forum, and avail itself of the forum's law and protections?

If minimum contacts are **absent**, the analysis ends. It does not matter how strong the plaintiff's interest is, how convenient the forum is for the state, or how logical it would be to litigate there. No minimum contacts = no jurisdiction.

### Phase 2: Fair and Reasonable (2B on the Framework)

If minimum contacts are present, the court then considers whether exercise of jurisdiction would be **fair and reasonable**, weighing:
- Extent of the defendant's contacts (beyond the threshold minimum)
- Defendant's convenience (the burden of litigating in the forum)
- Plaintiff's interest and convenience
- The forum state's interest in providing a forum and applying its own law
- The interest of the interstate judicial system, including location of witnesses and evidence, and which state's law is likely to apply (presumption: a state applies its own law better than any other court would)

## *J. McIntyre Machinery v. Nicastro*

[00:30:00] A UK manufacturer sold industrial machinery through a US distributor. A worker was injured in New Jersey by a McIntyre machine. The plurality (four justices) found no specific jurisdiction in New Jersey.

**Notable features of the plurality opinion:**

### Sovereign-Based Analysis

The plurality frames the question in terms of **sovereignty** rather than defendants' rights. The relevant sovereign for minimum contacts purposes is the specific state asserting jurisdiction — New Jersey — not the United States as a whole. J. McIntyre UK had attended trade shows and advertised in the United States, but not specifically in New Jersey.

The plurality notes that had Congress enacted a long-arm statute authorizing federal courts to exercise jurisdiction based on contacts with the United States as a whole, the constitutional analysis might be different. Under current law (Rule 4(k)(1)(A)), federal courts are tied to state long-arm limits, and those contacts did not extend to New Jersey specifically.

### Revival of Implied Consent Fictions

The plurality seems interested in reviving the implied-consent fiction cast aside after *Pennoyer* — the idea, familiar from *Hess v. Pawloski*, <!-- corrected: "Pawloski" → *Hess v. Pawloski* --> that a defendant who enters a state impliedly appoints a state official as agent for service of process. This fiction is not analytically clarifying — it just restates the question (what conduct suffices to imply consent?) in different terms. The plurality appears to favor it regardless. Given that the plurality reflects the views of at least four justices and a sixth highly conservative vote has since joined the Court, this framing may become important.

**The concurrence** (Justice Breyer, joined by Justice Alito) declines to articulate a broader rule and simply applies prior precedent to reach the same result. Lower courts generally treat the concurrence as controlling because it is narrower, but it offers little guidance for future cases.

## *Kulko v. Superior Court* (Family Law Context)

[00:45:00] <!-- corrected: "Colco" → *Kulko v. Superior Court* --> Married in New York; children; one spouse eventually relocates with the children to California. The court attempts to apply the purposeful availment language in a family law context, which is awkward — moving to California for personal reasons does not map naturally onto voluntary economic activity directed at a forum. Nevertheless, the Court finds insufficient minimum contacts linking the defendant, the subject matter of the litigation, and California, so the analysis stops at Phase 1.

The lesson: the purposeful availment language is the Court's chosen vocabulary even when it does not fit naturally.
