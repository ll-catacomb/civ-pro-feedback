## Erie Doctrine: Review via Shady Grove

### The Four-Part Framework

When you encounter an Erie issue on an exam, run through all four Erie tests:

1. The York test
2. The Hanna majority test — Scalia version
3. The Hanna majority test — Ginsburg version
4. Justice Harlan's concurrence in *Hanna v. Plumer*

In terms of the actual black-letter law that courts apply after this course, the Court appears to have settled 9-0 on what we call the **Hanna majority framework**, though it is not settled which version governs.

### The Hanna Majority Framework

**Step 1 — Identify the source of federal law.** If the source is constitutional law, statutory law, or a Rule of Civil Procedure, apply the **valid and applicable** test: if the federal law is valid and applies to the situation, it governs under the Supremacy Clause regardless of contrary state law.

- **Constitutional provisions:** Validity is presumed; they are generally not interpreted narrowly for applicability purposes.
- **Federal statutes:** Validity is governed by whether there is a constitutional provision empowering Congress to enact the statute. For statutes affecting federal court procedure, the power to create the lower federal courts combined with the Necessary and Proper Clause empowers Congress to make housekeeping or procedural rules applicable in federal courts. Congress may also use its substantive powers (e.g., Commerce Clause) to provide rules of decision for substantive matters. As to applicability, courts are generally not eager to construe a federal statute narrowly to get out of the way of a state rule of decision — though Justice Ginsburg would do so more readily than others.
- **Rules of Civil Procedure:** Validity is governed by the Rules Enabling Act, specifically 28 U.S.C. § 2072, which provides that a rule "shall not abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right." The Court appears thoroughly settled on the *Sibbach v. Wilson & Co.* <!-- corrected: "SIVAC" → Sibbach v. Wilson & Co. --> test for interpreting that language — an atextual interpretation that asks whether the rule "really regulates procedure." Because all rules do regulate procedure, as long as you cite § 2072 and the *Sibbach* test and conclude the rule is valid, you will be fine.

**Step 2 — Applicability (where Scalia and Ginsburg diverge):**

- **Scalia's version:** Consult only the federal rule itself. If the rule applies according to its plain language, apply it.
- **Ginsburg's version (supported by four other Justices, a total of five in *Shady Grove*):** Interpret the rule with sensitivity to state interests. This may mean construing the rule narrowly or in a way that allows the state policy to be effectuated.

In *Shady Grove*, Justice Ginsburg was unable to persuade Justice Stevens to join her in narrowly construing Rule 23. Justice Stevens agreed with Ginsburg's interpretive approach but disagreed with its application, and thereby provided the fifth vote for the result that Rule 23 governs in a situation where a New York statute purported to function as a class-action bar in certain circumstances.

**Step 3 — Federal common law (if no constitutional, statutory, or Rules source applies):** Use the twin aims of Erie: (1) discouraging forum shopping and (2) avoiding inequitable administration of the laws. The aim that does the real work is forum shopping — what causes plaintiffs' lawyers to forum shop is the opportunity to get a different and better result in one court versus another. That is the York test: whether there is an outcome-determinative difference between the federal and state approaches.

## Access to Justice

### Black-Letter Law from Lassiter and Turner

**From *Lassiter v. Department of Social Services*:** <!-- corrected: "LASTER" → Lassiter v. Department of Social Services --> The presumption that there is no right to state-funded counsel in civil cases applies to **all** civil cases — not just cases where incarceration is not at issue. The presumption applies equally when incarceration is at issue.

**From *Turner v. Rogers*:** When very serious interests are at stake (and incarceration would certainly qualify), the state faces a choice:

1. Provide counsel at state expense, or
2. Simplify, rationalize, or otherwise make its procedures and adjudicatory processes generally more accessible.

This is a new statement from the Court. In reaching it, the Court hints — without fully acknowledging — that the procedural protections that emerged from the "due process revolution" of the 1960s and 1970s (cases like *Goldberg v. Kelly*, *Mathews v. Eldridge*, <!-- corrected: "Matthews versus Eldridge" → Mathews v. Eldridge --> and what the textbook calls the *Sniadach* line of cases <!-- corrected: "Schneidack" → Sniadach --> — *Sniadach*, *Fuentes v. Shevin*, <!-- corrected: "Quintez" → *Fuentes v. Shevin* --> *North Georgia Finishing, Inc. v. Di-Chem*, <!-- corrected: "Dikem" → *North Georgia Finishing, Inc. v. Di-Chem* --> *Connecticut v. Doehr*, <!-- corrected: "Dorr, Connecticut versus Dorr" → *Connecticut v. Doehr* --> and others) have generated robust procedural protections but may have simultaneously made the process more complex and thereby created new access-to-justice barriers. The Court in *Turner v. Rogers* appears to hold (by a five-justice majority) that when serious interests are at stake, the state has a duty to remedy or address those access-to-justice problems.

### Broader Policy Questions

The bigger-picture questions in the access-to-justice module are more policy-oriented:

- If the federal (and much of the state) court system is effectively unusable by ordinary individuals without substantial resources — accessible primarily to wealthy individuals and to institutions that can marshal the necessary resources — what is its legitimacy?
- If legitimacy rests only on the threat of government enforcement rather than on genuine accessibility and the rule of law, is that satisfactory, and if not, what should be done?

Some have advocated for overturning or limiting *Turner* and *Lassiter* and having the court system recognize a civil right to counsel. Severe challenges to that approach are discussed in the readings, including the fact that the constitutional right to counsel in criminal cases — where the stakes are arguably at least as high, especially in capital cases — has not produced reliably high-quality representation or adequate funding. There also appears to be limited political will to extend such a right to civil cases.

What other measures short of a civil right to counsel might address these access-to-justice problems? Even for those who believe a civil right to counsel is an appropriate aspiration, it is worth thinking about what should be done in the meantime.
