## Motion to Dismiss: The *Iqbal*/*Twombly* Two-Step

In terms of black letter law for the motion to dismiss, under *Iqbal* and *Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly* <!-- corrected: "Bell Atlantic" → Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly --> — but most clearly articulated in *Iqbal* — the Court tells us that we need to engage in a two-step analysis.

In the first step, when reading a complaint, the court should ignore any conclusory statements, any recitation of the elements of the cause of action, and any assertions of an ultimate fact that the jury will have to find — such as negligence for a negligence cause of action, agreement for a Sherman Antitrust Act claim alleging restraint of trade, or discrimination of any kind. We ignore all of those statements. We do not accept them as true.

We then look to what is left over in the complaint: the pleading of the underlying facts that might give rise to an inference of discrimination or an inference of an agreement. We ask whether what remains is sufficient to nudge the complaint across the line from merely possible to plausible, taking into account possible alternative legally innocent explanations.

In *Iqbal*, the Court ignored the complaint's assertion that Ashcroft and Mueller had been acting discriminatorily — or had been deliberately indifferent to the discriminatory conditions of incarceration of Muslim men — and asked what was there in the complaint factually, beyond that assertion, to suggest deliberate indifference or active, animus-based discrimination against Muslim detainees. The Court found the facts insufficient to nudge the complaint from merely possible to plausible, given what it viewed as an innocent alternative explanation: that the conditions of confinement were due to the post-September 11 investigation the FBI was conducting under the Attorney General's leadership.

We saw how the same analysis applied in the *Twombly* context — an allegation of agreement by large telephone companies to restrain trade after the AT&T breakup — and in a simple slip-and-fall case in diversity, sitting in a Virginia federal court. In that case, the basic issue was that under Virginia law, the plaintiff would eventually have to show at trial that the puddle on which she slipped had been there too long, or that the store had known about it in some way. She could not plead the facts she intended to prove because she did not yet know them — she would have to find them in discovery — but she could not reach discovery because she could not plead them in the complaint.

We discussed some of the policy tensions here: how much we will allow complaints to survive motions to dismiss and impose costs on the parties, the court system, and taxpayers; but also the costs of a strict rule — that many meritorious complaints may be dismissed at the motion-to-dismiss stage and never reach discovery.

## Preliminary Injunctions

We then conducted a simulated oral argument on a motion for a preliminary injunction. The four factors courts consider are:

1. Likelihood of success on the merits
2. Irreparable harm
3. Balance of the equities
4. The public interest

In some circuits, these are prerequisites — the moving party must prevail on all four to be entitled to a preliminary injunction. In other circuits, a strong showing on one factor can compensate for a less than fully persuasive showing on another.

### Irreparable Harm

Anything that can be fully compensated by monetary damages after the fact is not irreparable harm. Business loss that can be reasonably quantified does not qualify. Loss of money in some sort of tort does not qualify. Loss of life or limb is irreparable harm, even though we attempt to compensate for it after the fact — money is recognized as inadequate there.

It is also irreparable harm if the loss is monetary but cannot be recovered due to some doctrine like sovereign immunity — for instance, where the government cannot be sued for money damages, or where damages are insufficiently quantifiable. Courts are reluctant to reach that last conclusion, but in some cases they will find that damages would be too hard to quantify after the fact, making the harm irreparable.

### Public Interest

Sometimes there is a statutory scheme in the background — a framework that Congress has enacted. If the plaintiff's request for a preliminary injunction would undermine or run contrary to that statutory scheme, that is a strong argument that granting the injunction would not be in the public interest.

We saw this in the FDA case on the reading. Congress has established a scheme whereby ordinary people cannot access pharmaceuticals unless both the FDA and the prescribing physician agree. The plaintiffs sought a ruling that only the prescribing physician's approval is required. The court held that this was contrary to the statutory scheme, and therefore not in the public interest.
