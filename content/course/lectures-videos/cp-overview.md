## Introduction

This recording provides a broad overview of the topics covered in Civil Procedure across the semester, organized by class day. Rather than taking notes, watch it once now to orient yourself, and then return to it midway through the semester and again at the end as a review of how the doctrines fit together.

---

## Days 1–4: Subject Matter Jurisdiction of the Federal Courts

[00:00:00] State courts are, as a baseline, always willing to hear any type of dispute. The question we address in days one through four is: even though a case can almost always begin in state court, when is it *also* permissible for it to begin in federal court?

The doctrine that answers this question is **subject matter jurisdiction** — specifically, the subject matter jurisdiction of the lower federal courts (the federal district courts).

### Day 1 — Arising Under Jurisdiction (§ 1331)

The first basis for federal subject matter jurisdiction is **arising under jurisdiction**, governed by 28 U.S.C. § 1331. A federal court has jurisdiction when the plaintiff's cause of action was created by federal law. Our principal case is *Motley*, which establishes this narrow construction and introduces the **well-pleaded complaint rule**. We then examine the *Smith-Grable* exception, under which a state law cause of action may still support arising-under jurisdiction when a federal issue is necessarily embedded within it. *Smith* and *Grable* illustrate this exception.

Congress also designates certain case types as **exclusive federal jurisdiction**, meaning they must be filed in federal court — primarily federal antitrust, intellectual property (patents and copyrights), and suits against the United States.

### Day 2 — Diversity Jurisdiction (§ 1332)

The second basis is **diversity jurisdiction**, which turns not on the subject matter of the dispute but on the citizenship of the parties. The parties must be citizens of different states. For purposes of § 1332, citizenship means **domicile**, not formal state citizenship. Rules differ for corporations (principal place of business and state of incorporation) and unincorporated associations.

### Day 3 — Supplemental Jurisdiction (§ 1367)

When a plaintiff wants to assert multiple claims in federal court but only some have an independent basis of subject matter jurisdiction (the "anchoring claims"), **supplemental jurisdiction** under § 1367 allows the court to hear the remaining "would-be supplemental" claims — provided they form part of the same constitutional case or controversy. Section 1367(b) imposes limits on plaintiff-side claims in diversity cases.

### Day 4 — Removal and Abstention

**Removal** is the doctrine by which a defendant moves a case filed in state court to federal district court. Only defendants may remove; there is no mechanism to move a case from federal back to state court. We also address **abstention** — circumstances in which a federal court, despite having subject matter jurisdiction, declines to exercise it.

## Day 5: Horizontal Choice of Law

[00:20:00] When a legal transaction or tort crosses a state boundary, which state's law governs the dispute? This is **horizontal choice of law** — horizontal because it involves a choice between states, as opposed to vertical choice of law (between state and federal law).

*Example*: A Massachusetts resident drives into New Hampshire and injures someone's dog. When the resulting lawsuit is filed, does New Hampshire law or Massachusetts law govern the negligence claim?

---

## Days 6–8: Personal Jurisdiction

Days six through eight address geography — specifically, in which court, located where, can a lawsuit properly be brought?

**Personal jurisdiction** answers the question: can *this* court, in *this* place, exercise power over this defendant? A court in Alaska has no personal jurisdiction over a Massachusetts resident for conduct that occurred entirely in New Hampshire and has no connection to Alaska.

We will trace personal jurisdiction doctrine from its territorial origins through the modern minimum-contacts framework established in *International Shoe*.

---

## Day 9: Venue and Forum Non-Convenience

[00:35:00] **Venue** is another geographic doctrine, operating entirely within the federal court system. It specifies which federal district a case must be filed in. More importantly, we explore how cases can be moved:

- **Transfers** move cases between federal courts within the United States.
- **Forum non-convenience** is used when the suggestion is to dismiss a state or federal court case so the plaintiff may refile in a court in another state or abroad. There is no mechanism to transfer directly from one state court to another.

---

## Days 10–11: The Erie Doctrine — Vertical Choice of Law

After a case called *Erie Railroad v. Tompkins*, the Erie doctrine governs the **vertical choice of law** question: when a case is in federal court, which law does the court apply — state law or federal law?

This question arises constantly in diversity cases. The framework for answering it — the York test, the Hanna majority test, and Justice Harlan's concurrence — will occupy much of days ten and eleven.

---

## Days 11–12: The Complaint, Pleading Standards, and Preliminary Injunctions

[00:50:00] Once we know where a lawsuit will proceed and what law applies, we walk the lawsuit through its lifecycle.

The first document is the **complaint**. How specific must it be? How much factual detail must a plaintiff provide before having the opportunity to gather evidence from the defendant through discovery? We will work through notice pleading, code pleading, and the modern Supreme Court's plausibility pleading standard from *Twombly* and *Iqbal*.

Day 12 introduces **preliminary injunctions** — orders a plaintiff seeks before winning the case, asking the court to prevent irreparable harm while the litigation is pending. Courts are reluctant to grant relief before the merits have been established, so they require the plaintiff to satisfy a demanding four-factor test.

---

## Days 13–14: Service of Process, Procedural Due Process, and Discovery

**Day 13** addresses two related questions: What notice must a defendant receive of the lawsuit? And what opportunity to be heard must the court provide before taking action against the defendant? These questions arise both in civil litigation and in the administrative state — for example, under what circumstances may the Social Security Administration terminate disability benefits, and what process must it offer? This falls under the doctrine of **procedural due process**.

**Day 14** introduces **discovery** — the set of tools (interrogatories, depositions, requests for production and admission) that allow parties to compel each other and third parties to produce information after a complaint has been filed.

---

## Day 15: Summary Judgment

After discovery, the question is: do we need a trial? If the facts are sufficiently clear and there is no genuine issue of material fact, the court can resolve the litigation without trial through a **motion for summary judgment**.

---

## Days 16–17: The Jury, the Judge, and Allocation of Decision-Making Power

[01:05:00] **Day 16** takes up which cases entitle a party to a jury trial. There is a historical division between actions at law (right to jury) and actions in equity (no right to jury). Even when a jury is available, issues of fact are for the jury while issues of law are for the judge.

**Day 17** examines the tools by which a judge can intervene even after a jury has spoken — including motions for judgment as a matter of law (JMOL) and motions for a new trial.

---

## Day 18: Appellate Jurisdiction

When does a right to appeal arise? The federal system follows a **final judgment rule** under 28 U.S.C. § 1291 — the case must proceed essentially to final judgment before appeal is available. This contrasts with many state systems (such as New York's) that permit broad interlocutory appeals. We examine the exceptions in the federal system: § 1292(a), § 1292(b), the collateral order doctrine, and mandamus.

---

## Days 19–22: Multiple Parties and Preclusion

**Day 19** asks: when is it permissible to have multiple plaintiffs and defendants in a single lawsuit? We also begin **preclusion**.

**Preclusion** doctrine governs when a concluded lawsuit bars re-litigation in a subsequent suit. There are two types:

- **Claim preclusion** (res judicata): prevents re-litigation of the entire claim that was or could have been litigated.
- **Issue preclusion** (collateral estoppel): prevents re-litigation of a specific factual issue that was actually litigated and decided.

*Example*: I drive into New Hampshire and hit both a person and their dog. The dog owner sues for damage to the dog, loses, and then files a second suit for personal injuries. Can that second suit proceed? Can the value of the dog be re-litigated in a later case?

We also address what happens when the preclusion law of the rendering court differs from that of the subsequent court — inter-jurisdictional preclusion problems.

---

## Days 22–23: Class Actions

[01:20:00] **Class actions** are aggregation devices: a single plaintiff represents thousands or millions of similarly situated individuals in a single lawsuit. We cover the requirements of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 and the policy debates surrounding aggregate litigation.

At the end of day 23 we begin three capstone cases — *Exxon*, *Beacon Theaters*, and *Shady Grove* — that sit at the intersection of multiple doctrines we have studied, allowing us to review and synthesize the course as a whole.

---

## Day 24: Access to Justice

The final module asks a frank question: after an entire semester of civil procedure, are you ready to represent your first client? If not, why not? And what does that say about the ability of the overwhelming majority of people who interact with American courts — those who do so without any lawyer at all? These are **pro se litigants**. What does it mean that the court system is effectively inaccessible to most of the people it is supposed to serve? What can we do about it?
