## Overview

Class 21 focused mostly on issue preclusion and began introducing the challenges of inter-system preclusion.

## Elements of Issue Preclusion

Issue preclusion has three essential elements: same issue, necessary to the decision, and actually litigated. The elements "same issue" and "actually litigated" will sometimes tend to merge a little, which is fine, because we need all three elements in order for issue preclusion to run. There are also a few stealth elements, just as there are with claim preclusion.

With those elements in mind, issue preclusion and claim preclusion are fairly different doctrines:

- In **claim preclusion**, we do not care who won, and we preclude theories of relief whether or not they were actually raised in lawsuit one.
- In **issue preclusion**, we only preclude losers — people who lost in litigation one — and we only preclude things that were actually litigated.

Walk through the elements of issue preclusion to decide whether it applies; do not mix and match the two doctrines.

### Same Issue

As illustrated in *Cromwell v. County of Sacramento*, <!-- corrected: "Cromwell versus County of SAC" → Cromwell v. County of Sacramento --> issues are fairly narrow and they are factual. Issue preclusion does not preclude relitigation of purely legal issues — for that, we have stare decisis. Issue preclusion applies only to factual issues, and they must be fairly narrow. Not "can somebody recover in this lawsuit" or "are all the elements of this cause of action the same as in litigation one," but rather something like whether a party is a holder in due course — which is what was at issue in *Cromwell* (did the party buy the bonds without knowledge of the fraud prior to the maturity date?). Or: was there fraud in a particular transaction? Were statements made in the context of a transaction fraudulent or misleading? Was a proxy statement materially misleading? Those are the sorts of issues for which we use issue preclusion.

### Actually Litigated

We only use issue preclusion for something that the parties did raise and fight about. There is no rule requiring parties to raise everything they could have raised. That means, for example, that even though in *Cromwell v. County of Sacramento* it was arguably Cromwell's fault that he did not raise the question of holder-in-due-course status, we still allow him to litigate it for the first time in lawsuit two. We think the incentives to raise issues in lawsuit one are sufficient on their own — you want to win lawsuit one — so we do not need the additional incentive of precluding unraised issues in lawsuit two.

### Necessary to the Decision

The fact pattern of *Rios v. Davis* <!-- corrected: "Rios versus Davis" → Rios v. Davis --> illustrates what "necessary to the decision" means: the finding must matter for the judgment the court issues. If a finding has no effect on the judgment, then it is not necessary to the decision.

In *Rios v. Davis*, the finding of negligence against Rios was not necessary to the court's decision that Davis recover nothing from Rios, because once Davis is found negligent, the inquiry ends — it does not matter whether Rios was also negligent.

A more challenging scenario arises when there are two independent grounds for a jury verdict and the jury returns a general verdict that does not distinguish between them:

- Some jurisdictions (including the rule articulated by the U.S. Supreme Court in *Russell v. Place*) hold that because we do not know which of those grounds the general verdict rested on, we cannot say that any one of them was necessary to the decision, and therefore issue preclusion does not apply.
- Other jurisdictions take the opposite view: if there are multiple independent grounds for a general jury verdict, they will assume the jury ruled in favor of the prevailing party on all of them, and thus treat each as necessary to the decision.

## Mutuality

Mutuality may or may not be required, and if required it may have different meanings. There are four views:

1. **No mutuality required (New York view):** Issue preclusion runs regardless of whether there is mutuality.

2. **Strict mutuality:** Same parties in lawsuit one and lawsuit two, and they must be in the same procedural posture — same plaintiff, same defendant.

3. **Same parties, different posture:** The same two parties must have participated in the first lawsuit in an adversarial posture, but they need not be in the same procedural posture. A party that was a defendant in lawsuit one may become a plaintiff in lawsuit two, and vice versa.

4. **Federal common law view (*Parklane Hosiery*):** <!-- corrected: "Park Lane hosiery" → Parklane Hosiery -->
   - **Defensive non-mutual collateral estoppel** — where the plaintiff is the same in both lawsuits and lost on an issue against defendant one, and defendant two seeks to use that loss to preclude the plaintiff from relitigating that issue — is generally **allowed** under federal common law.
   - **Offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel** — where the defendant is common to both lawsuits and a second plaintiff wants to use the defendant's loss in lawsuit one to prevent the defendant from relitigating that issue — is generally **not allowed** under federal common law.

The reason for the distinction is incentive structure:

- Allowing defensive non-mutual collateral estoppel incentivizes the common plaintiff to sue all defendants at once, since losing against one will be used by others.
- Allowing offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel would incentivize a second plaintiff to sit out of lawsuit one — if plaintiff one wins, plaintiff two can use the ruling offensively; if plaintiff one loses, plaintiff two is still not precluded because preclusion cannot run against a party who has not had their day in court. That wait-and-see incentive is bad.

However, the Supreme Court in *Parklane Hosiery* noted that this reasoning depends on plaintiff two having been able to join the first lawsuit. If there was a legal impediment to joining, the rationale for disallowing offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel disappears, and we might as well allow it to save the costs of relitigating issues already decided. In *Parklane Hosiery* itself, that exception applied and the Court allowed offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel.

## General Rules Applicable in All Issue Preclusion Situations

- We do not preclude parties who have not had their day in court — each party is entitled to one day in court.
- The concept of privity from claim preclusion applies to issue preclusion as well: preclusion runs against parties or their privies.
- Issue preclusion generally does not run when the party to be precluded in lawsuit two did not have a full and fair opportunity to litigate in lawsuit one. This might occur because of unusual discovery rules severely limiting discovery, inability to obtain witness testimony, or lack of access to documents.
- Issue preclusion can sometimes operate in unexpected ways: different sources of law may require litigation of the same facts. Even if the source of law in lawsuit one differs from the source of law in lawsuit two, if there are factual issues in common that are truly the same, we may allow issue preclusion to run in lawsuit two. The facts of *Marrese v. American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons* <!-- corrected: "Marese" → Marrese --> illustrate this.

## Introduction to Inter-System Preclusion

We began to unpack the general situation in which the rendering court is from one jurisdiction and the subsequent court is from another. We identified at least four scenarios:

| Rendering Court | Subsequent Court |
|---|---|
| State | State |
| State | Federal |
| Federal | State |
| Federal | Federal |

The sources of governing law:

- **Article IV, § 1 of the Constitution** (Full Faith and Credit Clause) speaks only to the first scenario: state rendering court, state subsequent court.
- **28 U.S.C. § 1738** speaks to the first two scenarios: state rendering court followed by state subsequent court, and state rendering court followed by federal subsequent court.
- When the **rendering court is federal** — whether the subsequent court is state or federal — neither the Constitution nor § 1738 applies by their plain terms. Despite Rule 41 <!-- corrected: "Rule 51" → Rule 41 --> purporting to apply, the better view is that it is not a rule of preclusion.

Because leaving federal court judgments without any preclusive framework would be disastrous — parties would be free to relitigate anything decided by a federal court — we need **federal common law** to fill the gap. Federal common law will insist on full faith and credit for federal court judgments in both subsequent federal courts and subsequent state courts.

What remains to be unpacked is: (1) what does full faith and credit mean in this context, and (2) what is the content of the federal common law, and will it vary according to the basis of subject-matter jurisdiction in lawsuit one? The answer is that it will vary, and that will be addressed in the next class.
