## Property Rights and the First *Mathews* Factor

We emphasized two fact patterns that implicate different aspects of the *Mathews v. Eldridge* test.

The first concerned the significance of property rights on both sides of a dispute. Property rights in an object or piece of real estate can be split: one party may hold legal title (sometimes evidenced by a physical document, such as the title to a house or car) while another party holds possession and is paying off what amounts to a loan. In that situation, both parties have property interests at stake. If one party files an emergency request for repossession, the court may note that the party in possession has a property right — they hold possession of the item and have some equity in it — but so does the filing party, who holds legal title. The existence of property rights on both sides is relevant to Part One of the *Mathews v. Eldridge* test: the private interests at issue.

## Litigation Bonds and Procedural Due Process

We also discussed how bonds work and how they bear on the *Mathews* analysis.

A litigation bond is a promise from an insurance company or a financially solvent third party to pay if the plaintiff is ordered to pay damages due to a wrongful seizure action and is unable to do so. If a scheme requires the plaintiff to post a bond before a court will issue an emergency seizure order, that bond serves two constitutional functions:

**First**, it protects the defendant's private interest by ensuring that if the seizure turns out to be wrongful, the defendant can actually recover damages — even if the plaintiff later becomes insolvent.

**Second**, it reduces the risk of erroneous determination. The insurance company or other bond issuer will factor the plaintiff's likelihood of success on the merits into the price of the bond. If the plaintiff has a strong likelihood of success, the bond price will be low (because the chance the bond will be invoked is small). If the plaintiff's likelihood of success is low, the price will be higher — and if the price is too high, the plaintiff may either be unable to pay it or decide it is not worthwhile. The court can thus treat a plaintiff's inability to obtain a bond as a market signal that the action may lack merit.

That market signal can be distorted, however, by the plaintiff's financial limitations. If a plaintiff simply does not have the resources to obtain a bond regardless of the merits, we have an access-to-justice problem.

## The District Court Opinion's Minimum Factors and When to Use Them

We discussed how useful it would be to have a list of minimum factors we would expect to see in any procedural due process scheme. The district court opinion in *Vitek v. Jones* <!-- corrected: "VTEC"/"VTECT" → *Vitek v. Jones* --> is one of the first attempts to provide such a minimum scheme. It identifies basics including:

- Adequate notice
- A neutral decision maker
- An opportunity to present evidence
- An opportunity to see the other side's evidence
- A hearing
- A written statement of findings

The plurality would have added the assistance of a lawyer; Justice Powell said help of some kind.

**When are these minimum factors useful versus when is the *Mathews v. Eldridge* test useful?**

The minimum-factors list is particularly useful when constructing a new scheme from scratch — for instance, when a court must address a situation in which no procedural due process is being provided at all, or when a newly created right or benefit scheme springs into existence and we need to design an adjudicatory process for determining eligibility.

The *Mathews v. Eldridge* test is much more useful in adjudicating a case-specific attack upon an existing scheme — when a party has identified an existing scheme and argues it should have some feature it currently lacks. The reason is that *Mathews* demands a comparison between an existing scheme and an alternative. Until you have identified both the existing scheme and the specific alternative the challenger proposes, you cannot really use *Mathews*, because the test requires that comparison. When there is no existing scheme to compare anything to, or when you have not yet defined your proposed alternative, *Mathews* cannot operate. That is what the minimum-factors approach is useful for.

## Right to Counsel in Civil Cases

Finally, we discussed *Lassiter v. Department of Social Services* at some length. The key takeaway:

In all civil cases, there is ordinarily a presumption that no counsel will be provided at state expense. Any effort to argue that the government must provide counsel at state expense will be adjudicated using the *Mathews v. Eldridge* factors — exactly as in *Lassiter* itself. That presumption against state-provided counsel applies across civil cases, including cases not involving a constitutional right of heightened importance. We will return to this when we discuss *Turner v. Rogers* later in the semester.
