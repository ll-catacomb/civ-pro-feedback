## Overview

Class 8 concluded our discussion of jurisdiction over persons and property. We resolved questions about quasi in rem jurisdiction, the in-state tag, and the challenges posed by internet-based disputes.

---

## *Shaffer v. Heitner*: Does *International Shoe* Apply to Quasi In Rem?

[00:00:00] We resumed where class 7 ended: whether the evolution of in personam jurisdiction doctrine under *International Shoe* and its progeny carries over to quasi in rem.

**The quasi in rem mechanism**: A plaintiff asks a court to seize a piece of property located in the forum that has no relationship to the subject matter of the lawsuit. The purpose is to secure a source of recovery if the plaintiff wins, since the court's power over the seized property is limited to the value of the property.

**Under *Harris v. Balk*** (the old rule): Once the court determined that a debt was physically located in the forum (because the debtor was present), seizing the debt for quasi in rem purposes was permissible regardless of any connection between the property and the litigation.

**In *Shaffer v. Heitner***: The Delaware statute at issue authorized seizure of Delaware stock (held by non-Delaware directors and officers) in essentially any lawsuit, and if the defendant came in to retrieve the seized stock, the defendant would be subject to the court's full in personam jurisdiction — not just quasi in rem jurisdiction limited to the property's value. The Supreme Court was troubled by the statute's breadth.

### Two Readings of *Shaffer*

**Reading 1 — Narrow**: The Court was reacting specifically to the extraordinary breadth of the Delaware statute. Ordinary quasi in rem jurisdiction (where the amount of recovery is genuinely capped at property value) is probably still fine as long as the long-arm statute authorizing it is not as sweeping. When Delaware later amended its statute to limit it to directors-and-officers litigation, that narrower statute was likely valid on this reading.

**Reading 2 — Broad**: *Shaffer* says all assertions of jurisdiction over property must be analyzed under the *International Shoe* framework because a quasi in rem action is really a lawsuit against the **owner** of the property, not against the property itself. If so, the standard specific jurisdiction analysis applies: long-arm statute, minimum contacts linking the defendant, the subject matter, and the forum, and fair-and-reasonable. Most traditional quasi in rem actions would fail this analysis because the whole point of quasi in rem is to sue in a place where the defendant has property but where there are no minimum contacts linking the defendant to the litigation and the forum.

The Court's language in *Shaffer* supports the broad reading, but the result of the case is arguably consistent with the narrow reading. We genuinely do not know which interpretation is correct.

## *Burnham v. Superior Court*: The In-State Tag

[00:25:00] Does physical service on a defendant while present in the forum state — the tag that was the cornerstone of *Pennoyer* — remain sufficient to establish personal jurisdiction after *International Shoe*?

The result in *Burnham* was unanimous (jurisdiction upheld), but the reasoning split the Court exactly 4–4:

- **Four justices** (Scalia plurality) held that the in-state tag is sufficient standing alone, full stop, as it has been historically and as it should remain.
- **Four justices** (Brennan concurrence) held that the in-state tag is *one relevant factor* in the overall *International Shoe* framework — it is relevant to fair-and-reasonable analysis, but it does not automatically suffice.

This means we still do not know whether the tag stands alone or is merely one factor. On any exam or in practice, **argue both sides**.

## Internet Jurisdiction

[00:40:00] The internet creates structural difficulties for a personal jurisdiction doctrine built around territorial contacts. Contracts form electronically without parties knowing where the counterpart is located. Identities exist in cyberspace without a geographic address. Torts can be committed instantly across every state boundary simultaneously.

None of this maps easily onto a framework that focuses heavily on geographic connections between a defendant's conduct and a specific forum.

*Griffis v. Luban* <!-- corrected: "Griffiths versus Leuven" → *Griffis v. Luban* --> is assigned not because its specific holding matters, but because it provides fact patterns for practicing the application of the framework — long-arm statute analysis, Step 2A (minimum contacts/purposeful availment), Step 2B (fair and reasonable) — in a setting that challenges the framework's premises and forces careful thinking about what "purposeful availment" means when the defendant never physically set foot in the forum.

## Summary: What We Know and Do Not Know

After eight classes on personal jurisdiction, here is an honest accounting of open questions:

| Issue | Status |
|---|---|
| In-state tag for general jurisdiction | Unresolved (4–4 in *Burnham*) |
| Quasi in rem post-*Shaffer* | Two plausible readings; unresolved |
| Implied consent revival (*J. McIntyre* plurality) | Possible future direction; not yet law |
| Internet purposeful availment | No settled framework |

For exam purposes: wherever the law is genuinely unsettled, present the analysis under both possible rules and argue both sides.
