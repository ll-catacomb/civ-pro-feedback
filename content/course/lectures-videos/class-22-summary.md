## Overview

Class 22 dealt primarily with inter-system preclusion. A portion of the class also addressed class actions; the black-letter law on class actions is covered in the summary for Class 23.

## Inter-System Preclusion: Four Scenarios

There are at least four scenarios to care about when the rendering and subsequent adjudicators are courts:

| Rendering Court | Subsequent Court | Governing Law |
|---|---|---|
| State | State | Article IV, § 1 of the Constitution + 28 U.S.C. § 1738 |
| State | Federal | 28 U.S.C. § 1738 |
| Federal | State | Federal common law |
| Federal | Federal | Federal common law |

## The Full Faith and Credit Obligation

Under all of those sources of law — Article IV, § 1, § 1738, and federal common law — the obligation of the subsequent court is to give **full faith and credit** to the decision of the rendering court. That means the subsequent court must give at least as much preclusive effect to the rendering court's judgment as the rendering court's own system would give.

- If the rendering court system would preclude (claim preclusion or issue preclusion), the subsequent court **must** also preclude.
- If the rendering court system would not preclude, the subsequent court may have some discretion — it might choose to preclude anyway for reasons of judicial economy, or it might not.

## When the Rendering Court Is a State Court

The straightforward application, illustrated in *Migra v. Warren City School District Board of Education*, <!-- corrected: "Negroes" → *Migra v. Warren City School District Board of Education* --> is that where the rendering court was a state court and the subsequent court is a federal court, § 1738 governs and the Supreme Court says you consult the law of the state to determine the preclusive effect of the state court judgment in federal court.

## When the Rendering Court Is a Federal Court

### Arising-Under Jurisdiction

When the rendering federal court is exercising arising-under (federal-question) jurisdiction, and the subsequent court is also a federal court exercising arising-under jurisdiction, cases like *Parklane Hosiery* <!-- corrected: "Park Lane hosiery" → Parklane Hosiery --> establish that the Supreme Court does not consult state law. The federal common law rule is **uniform** regardless of the geographic location of the district courts involved, and that uniformity holds regardless of the nature of the subsequent court.

### Diversity Jurisdiction

When the rendering court is a federal court sitting in **diversity**, the content of the federal common law full faith and credit rule is different. In *Semtek International Inc. v. Lockheed Martin Corp.* <!-- corrected: "SimTech" and "SymTech" and "CEMTECH" → Semtek --> the Supreme Court held that a subsequent state court must give a diversity federal court's judgment the preclusive effect that would be given by the courts of the state in which the rendering federal court sits.

In *Semtek* itself, the rendering court was a federal district court in California sitting in diversity. Therefore, the subsequent state court (in Maryland) had to give as much preclusive effect as a California state court would have given in the same circumstances — California state law governed.

The reason, as the *Semtek* Court explains, is essentially Erie concerns or Erie policies, even though the Erie tests are not directly applied: we want uniformity between the result that would have obtained had the rendering court been a California state court and the result when the rendering court is a California federal court sitting in diversity.

The prevailing view in the civil procedure scholarly community, and the rule to use for this class, is that the *Semtek* principle extends regardless of the identity of the subsequent court or its basis of jurisdiction. Any time a rendering court is a federal court sitting in diversity, the preclusive effect of that judgment is determined by the law of the state in which it sits.

## Open Questions

Several situations are not clearly resolved:

1. **Dual-basis jurisdiction:** What if both diversity and arising-under jurisdiction are present?
2. **Supplemental claims:** What if there is an arising-under anchoring claim with supplemental state-law claims involved?
3. **Transferred cases in diversity:** When the sole basis of jurisdiction is diversity and the case has been transferred, do we use the transferor state's preclusion law or the transferee state's preclusion law?

On an exam, if one of these unresolved questions is presented, argue the policy arguments on both sides: policy arguments for applying a uniform federal common law rule regardless of geography, and policy arguments for applying the preclusion law of the state in which the rendering federal district court sits.

**Important reminder:** The whole inter-system preclusion analysis is only relevant when the rendering court's preclusion law and the subsequent court's preclusion law actually differ. If they are the same, there is nothing to worry about.

## Ways Preclusion Law Can Differ Among Jurisdictions

We have already encountered at least three:

1. Whether a statute-of-limitations dismissal is on the merits for claim preclusion purposes.
2. Whether a general jury verdict with multiple independent possible bases means all those bases are considered decided in favor of the prevailing party (the *Russell v. Place* question).
3. Whether mutuality is required at all — New York generally requires none; some jurisdictions require strict mutuality (same parties, same procedural posture); others require same parties but allow different procedural postures; and under federal common law (*Parklane Hosiery*) there is a distinction between offensive and defensive non-mutual collateral estoppel.

## Agency Adjudications

When the rendering adjudicatory body is an agency rather than a court, federal common law governs (there is no statute). The Restatement (Second) of Judgments provides the general common law rule: a subsequent court (or agency) will ordinarily afford preclusive effect — both issue-preclusive and claim-preclusive — to an adjudicatory judgment from an agency, but only if that agency proceeding complies with minimum due process requirements.

The Restatement identifies some minimal requirements:

- Notice to the relevant parties
- An opportunity to present evidence and arguments
- A neutral decision-maker
- A clear rule of finality articulating when the decision is final

Things that are **not** required for minimum due process in this context include: court-appointed or lawyer assistance, a right of appeal, discovery, and a live hearing (paper submissions suffice).

This minimal due process standard is even less demanding than that articulated in *Vitek v. Jones*, <!-- corrected: "VTEC" → Vitek v. Jones --> which is itself less demanding than the procedural protections available in federal court.

The Restatement notes that its list is not exhaustive: in some circumstances, we might require more before affording preclusive effect to an agency determination. To assess what additional process might be required, we would consider the *Mathews v. Eldridge* <!-- corrected: "Matthews versus Eldridge" → Mathews v. Eldridge --> factors:

- The importance of the private interest at stake (the higher the stakes, the more process we want)
- The ease and cost of adding process
- The reduction in risk of erroneous determination that additional process would provide
- The government's interest in minimizing cost

## Limits on Agency Preclusion: Scope of Agency Authority

A critical warning: agency adjudicators frequently have very limited decision-making authority, confined to particular narrow issues or claims. For example, a Social Security Administration ALJ (administrative law judge) may only determine whether a claimant is disabled — not whether the agency's own processes are constitutional or whether its decisions reflect a pattern of discriminatory decision-making.

Because of the rule of *Marrese v. American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons*, <!-- corrected: "MADESE" → Marrese --> things the agency adjudicator lacks the power to adjudicate are not claim-precluded in a subsequent lawsuit by a prior agency adjudication. There was no opportunity to litigate those matters before the agency, so preclusion cannot run as to them.
