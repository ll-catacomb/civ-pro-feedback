## Overview

Class 5 covered horizontal choice of law (which state's substantive law governs when more than one state's law could apply) and introduced the foundational concepts of personal jurisdiction.

---

## Horizontal Choice of Law: The Basic Framework

[00:00:00] When attacking a choice-of-law problem — on an exam, in practice, or on an assignment — begin with a threshold question:

**Is the issue procedural or substantive?**

- **Procedural issues**: Apply the law of the **forum** — the state in which the lawsuit is occurring. This applies to all procedural questions without further analysis.
- **Substantive issues**: Conduct a choice-of-law analysis to determine which state's law governs.

---

## Procedure vs. Substance: Key Examples

Some issues fall clearly on one side:

- **Procedural**: Paper size, filing deadlines, response deadlines.
- **Limitations periods**: For our purposes, treat limitations periods as procedural. This means the forum state applies its own limitations period — even if the events occurred in another state with a shorter or longer period. A plaintiff who can find a forum with a longer limitations period may file there even if the period in the state where the events occurred has already run. Conversely, if the forum's limitations period is shorter than the period in the state where the events arose, the forum will apply its own shorter period and the claim may be time-barred.

## Tort Choice of Law: Two Approaches

We studied tort choice of law only. Contracts, property, and family law choice of law are not covered in this course. On any exam or assignment, you will be told whether the relevant jurisdiction follows the First or Second Restatement.

### First Restatement: *Lex Loci Delicti*

Apply the law of the **place of the injury** — where the damage occurred and the tort was complete. For personal injury, identify where the blood is. For intangible injuries like defamation (an injury to reputation), you must work out where the reputation is located, which can be harder.

Once you identify the place of injury, that state's law governs **all** substantive tort issues: damages caps, liability rules, culpability standards, and relationship-based defenses like guest statutes and fellow-servant rules.

**Strengths of the First Restatement approach:**
- Hard-and-fast rules that are easy and cheap to administer
- Predictability — parties can reckon the applicable law in advance
- Internal coherence — a single jurisdiction's complete body of tort law applies

### Second Restatement

Two key differences from the First Restatement:

1. **Issue-by-issue analysis**: The lawsuit is disaggregated into individual issues, and different states' laws may govern different issues. New York law might govern one issue; South Carolina law another; Texas law a third.

2. **Presumption with escape valve**: For each issue, begin with a presumption in favor of *lex loci delicti* (the First Restatement rule). Then examine the **§ 6 factors** — including the relevant policies of the forum, the relevant policies of other interested states, the parties' reasonable expectations, and the needs of the interstate judicial system — to decide whether to override the presumption.

*Babcock v. Jackson*: The tort occurred in Ontario, which had a guest statute. Under *lex loci delicti*, Ontario law would govern. But the court concluded that New York had the dominant interest in the guest-statute issue because the driver, passenger, and their insurance were all New York-based. Ontario's purpose in enacting the guest statute — protecting drivers' insurers against passenger-driver fraud — was a New York concern when the parties were New York residents with New York insurance. The court therefore applied New York law on that issue. However, had the issue been whether the driver's conduct was sufficiently careless to constitute negligence, Ontario law would govern because Ontario has a strong interest in regulating conduct within its borders.

**Danger of the Second Restatement**: *Frankenstein law* — when too many pieces of different states' laws are stitched together, the resulting legal scheme may be one that no jurisdiction would ever have adopted as a whole.

## Introduction to Personal Jurisdiction

[00:50:00] We largely reviewed the pre-recorded video on personal jurisdiction (circulated previously), so this summary is brief.

The basic typology of jurisdiction over persons and property:

- **In personam jurisdiction**: Jurisdiction over a person.
- **In rem jurisdiction**: Jurisdiction over a thing itself (a property dispute).
- **Quasi in rem jurisdiction**: Jurisdiction over the relationship between a person and a piece of property — typically used when the plaintiff cannot obtain in personam jurisdiction but wants to satisfy a judgment using property located in the forum.

We will develop these concepts in depth in the coming classes.
