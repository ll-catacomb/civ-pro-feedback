## Overview

Class 4 covered removal procedure and the doctrine of abstention, with particular focus on *Colorado River* abstention.

---

## Removal: Basic Mechanics

[00:00:00] Removal is the mechanism by which a defendant moves a case originally filed in state court to federal district court.

**Automatic removal**: Removal takes effect the instant a defendant files the removal papers in federal court. What we are really examining, therefore, is whether a **motion to remand** will be granted — i.e., whether the case will be sent back to state court.

**Remand for lack of subject matter jurisdiction**: At any time, and on the court's own initiative (*sua sponte*), a district court that determines it lacks subject matter jurisdiction must remand the case. This is an extension of *Motley*: when a removed case lacks a jurisdictional basis, the court remands rather than dismisses.

---

## Procedural Defects in Removal: Grounds for Remand

[00:08:00] Beyond the subject-matter jurisdiction ground, a plaintiff may file a **prompt motion to remand** — within 30 days of removal — based on any of the following procedural defects:

### 1. In-State Defendant Rule (Diversity Cases Only)

In a case where subject matter jurisdiction rests solely on § 1332 (diversity), if **any defendant is a citizen of the state in which the lawsuit was filed**, removal is improper. The rationale: diversity jurisdiction is designed to protect against potential bias against out-of-state parties. If a defendant is from the forum state, there is no basis to fear bias against defendants, and the plaintiff's choice of that state court is entitled to respect.

### 2. Late Removal (More Than 30 Days After the Removable Event)

Removal must occur within 30 days of the **removable event** — ordinarily the filing of a complaint that facially establishes a basis for federal jurisdiction. The rule promotes prompt decision-making about forum.

Sometimes, events during litigation *create* removability where none existed before. Two primary examples:
- Dismissal of non-diverse parties in a potential diversity case
- State court permission to add a federally-created cause of action to a complaint that previously lacked one

### 3. Failure of All Defendants to Join the Removal Petition

All defendants must sign the removal petition. The federal court system is intended to be the exception, not the rule, for trial-level litigation. If any defendant wants to remain in state court, the case stays there.

### 4. The One-Year Rule (Diversity Cases Only)

In diversity-only cases, if the case has been pending in state court for **more than one year** (measured from filing and service, which we treat as occurring on the same day), removal is untimely. This applies only when § 1332 is the sole jurisdictional basis.

## The Shamrock Oil Rule

Sheets* --> **Plaintiffs cannot remove.** Counterclaims and cross-claims are irrelevant to removability. We look only to the plaintiff's complaint. Even if a defendant asserts an arising-under counterclaim and the plaintiff attempts to remove, the district court will remand on prompt motion.

## Abstention: When a Federal Court Declines to Exercise Jurisdiction

[00:25:00] Abstention doctrines govern circumstances in which a federal district court, despite having all necessary jurisdictional predicates (subject matter jurisdiction, and the personal jurisdiction and venue we will study in the coming weeks), nonetheless refuses to go forward. When, if ever, is that legitimate?

**Standing categorical exceptions** (not subject to a balancing test): Federal district courts will not issue divorce decrees and will not probate wills. The stated rationale is state court expertise, though one could argue circularity — the federal courts' longstanding refusal to hear these matters has created the very state court expertise invoked to justify the refusal.

## Colorado River Abstention

The abstention doctrine we must know is the **fourth branch** of abstention doctrine, from *Colorado River Water Conservation District v. United States*. <!-- corrected: "Colorado River" → *Colorado River Water Conservation District v. United States* -->

**Threshold requirement**: Simultaneous state court and federal court litigation must be pending between substantially the same parties over substantially the same subject matter.

**Default rule — the unflagging obligation**: Even when that condition is met, federal district courts are under an **unflagging obligation** to exercise the jurisdiction Congress has conferred on them. Absent exceptional circumstances, they must proceed.

**When abstention is permitted**: Only in **exceptional circumstances** may a district court stay (ordinarily not dismiss, but stay) the federal litigation. The analysis is a totality-of-the-circumstances inquiry. Factors the *Colorado River* Court found relevant included:
- Presence of a congressional statute suggesting congressional comfort with state court adjudication
- Relatively rapid progress of the state court litigation
- The state court's investment of time and resources in a complex multi-party proceeding
- State court expertise developed through that investment
- Geographic convenience of the state court for parties and evidence

**Circuit split on "exceptional circumstances"**: The circuits are split. In the Seventh Circuit, following *Clark v. Lacy*, <!-- corrected: "Clark v. Lacey" → *Clark v. Lacy* --> Colorado River abstention stays are relatively easy to obtain — parallel state and federal litigation is treated as presumptively appropriate for abstention. In other circuits, such as the Fourth, it is very difficult to obtain a stay. The Supreme Court has not resolved this split.

**Background effect**: When parallel state and federal trial court litigation is simultaneously pending, the first one to reach a trial court judgment will ordinarily trigger preclusion doctrines that terminate the other lawsuit.
