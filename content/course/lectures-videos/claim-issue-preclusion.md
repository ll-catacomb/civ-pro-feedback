## Claim Preclusion: Finality and the Merits Requirement

[00:00:00] We finished the black letter law of claim preclusion, focusing on element three: a judgment that is both **final** and **on the merits**.

**Finality** for claim preclusion purposes attaches at the trial court judgment — even if the case is on appeal, the trial court judgment is sufficient to trigger preclusion.

**"On the merits"** is a policy concept rather than a literal description. It reflects a judgment about when we want to prevent a second lawsuit on the same transaction or occurrence by the same party. The following are *on the merits*:
- Judgment after trial
- Grant of summary judgment
- Successful motion to dismiss (ordinarily)
- Dismissal for failure to prosecute (because we do not want to allow the plaintiff to refile as a matter of policy)
- In some jurisdictions: limitations-based dismissal

The following are *not* on the merits:
- Dismissal for lack of subject matter jurisdiction
- Dismissal for lack of personal jurisdiction
- Dismissal for improper venue
- In some jurisdictions: limitations-based dismissal

## Additional Claim Preclusion Doctrines

### The *Martino* Rule <!-- corrected: "Mahreze" → "Martino" -->

When the rendering court in lawsuit number one lacks the **capacity** — especially the jurisdictional capacity — to adjudicate a particular theory of relief, that theory will *not* be claim precluded in a subsequent suit. In other words, a court cannot preclude what it could not have adjudicated. We saw this where the rendering court lacked subject matter jurisdiction over the theory asserted in the second lawsuit; that theory was not barred.

### Court Limitations on Preclusive Effect

A rendering court may limit the claim-preclusive effect of its own judgment by specifying that the adjudication is **without prejudice** to a refiling of a particular theory of relief.

## Issue Preclusion

[00:15:00] We then moved to issue preclusion. The following structural points govern:

- **Finality requirement**: Ordinarily a trial court judgment is needed for issue preclusion to run.
- **Insufficient opportunity or incentive**: If a party was prevented from fully litigating an issue in lawsuit one — due to limits on witness testimony, document access, or because the amount at stake was too small to justify full investment — the court has discretion to deny issue preclusion.
- **Due process constraint**: We only preclude parties who were present in lawsuit one or who were in **privity** with those who were.
- **Only losers are precluded**: Issue preclusion runs only against the party who *lost* the issue in lawsuit one. This is easy to forget as fact patterns grow complex.

---

## Elements of Issue Preclusion

### Element 1: Same Issue

Issues are defined **narrowly**. In *Cromwell*, for example, the issues were: Did Mr. Cromwell buy the bonds before maturity? Did he pay value for them? Did he buy them without knowledge of any underlying fraud? Each is a discrete factual question. Issues are narrower than theories of relief and much narrower than transaction occurrences.

### Element 2: Actually Litigated

Issue preclusion runs only if the issue was **actually litigated** in lawsuit one. Unlike compulsory counterclaims under Rule 13, parties are not required to litigate all possible issues. If an issue was not raised and contested, issue preclusion will not run even if the party had the opportunity to raise it.

### Element 3: Necessary to the Decision

[00:25:00] We examined two scenarios from *Russell v. Place*. <!-- corrected: "Russell versus Place" → *Russell v. Place* --> When a jury verdict rests on **multiple independent grounds** and it is impossible to determine which ground the jury relied upon:

- **Federal rule**: The party seeking issue preclusion cannot prove the issue was "necessary to the decision" and issue preclusion will not run.
- **Some state jurisdictions**: Courts will deem *all* independent grounds to have been decided against the losing party, so issue preclusion runs on each.

In *Russell v. Place* itself, the jury could have based its finding on the fat-liquor process or the tanned lambskin; <!-- corrected: "tan lamb skin" → tanned lambskin --> since it was impossible to say which, the federal rule precluded issue preclusion.

## Preview: Mutuality (Tomorrow's Topic)

[00:35:00] We close with a preview of mutuality for issue preclusion — the question of whether the parties in lawsuit two must bear some required relationship to the parties in lawsuit one.

**Four frameworks to be covered:**

1. **Strict mutuality**: Both the plaintiff and defendant in lawsuit two must be the same parties, in the same procedural roles, as in lawsuit one.

2. **Relaxed mutuality**: The parties must be the same, but procedural roles may be reversed — the plaintiff in lawsuit one may be the defendant in lawsuit two.

3. **Federal common law rule**: **Defensive non-mutual issue preclusion** is generally permitted. When plaintiff sues defendant A, loses on an issue, and then sues defendant B, defendant B may invoke issue preclusion against the plaintiff on that same issue. However, **offensive non-mutual issue preclusion** — where plaintiff two uses a prior loss by the defendant against the defendant — is generally *not* permitted under federal common law, unless plaintiff two could not have joined lawsuit one.

4. **New York rule** (*Hart*): There is no mutuality requirement at all.
