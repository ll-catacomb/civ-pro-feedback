## Overview

This is a summary for Class 20, which dealt entirely with claim preclusion.

---

## The Elements of Claim Preclusion

Claim preclusion has **three stated elements**, plus two that are usually unstated:

**Stated elements:**
1. Same party
2. Same claim
3. A judgment that is final and on the merits

**Unstated ("stealth") elements:**
4. The rendering court in Lawsuit 1 has not explicitly limited the claim-preclusive effect of its own judgment
5. The rendering court in Lawsuit 1 had jurisdiction over the theory of relief to be precluded in Lawsuit 2

---

## Policy: Why Claim Preclusion Exists

Claim preclusion requires a Lawsuit 1 that has gone to final judgment, and then a Lawsuit 2 in which a party that was — or should have been — in the posture of asserting theories of relief in Lawsuit 1 is now asserting or withholding them. The doctrine's purpose is efficiency: we want parties to bring all related theories of relief in one lawsuit. Claim preclusion achieves this by extinguishing theories of relief that were not brought in Lawsuit 1, even if they were never actually asserted. The threat of extinguishment is the incentive to consolidate.

This distinguishes claim preclusion from issue preclusion, which covers only things actually litigated and decided. Claim preclusion does not care whether the theory was asserted or not — typically it applies precisely because it was not.

Another key distinction: claim preclusion is indifferent to who won. It applies equally whether the plaintiff or the defendant prevailed in Lawsuit 1. (Issue preclusion, by contrast, does care who won.)

---

## Element 1: Final Judgment on the Merits

**Finality** for claim preclusion purposes is met once the trial court enters a final judgment, even if the case is still on direct appeal. A party in Lawsuit 2 may plead claim preclusion as soon as Lawsuit 1 reaches final judgment in the trial court, provided the other elements are met.

**"On the merits"** — some examples:

*Clearly not on the merits (no claim preclusion):*
- Dismissal for lack of subject matter jurisdiction, personal jurisdiction, or venue

*Clearly on the merits (claim preclusion applies):*
- Cases decided at trial
- Cases resolved by summary judgment followed by final judgment
- Cases dismissed for failure to state a claim under Rule 12(b)(6) followed by final judgment

*Harder cases:*
- **Dismissal for failure to prosecute, or as a discovery sanction**: typically treated as on the merits as a policy matter, to deter repetitive litigation.
- **Dismissal based on the statute of limitations**: more complex. Whether a limitations-based dismissal has claim-preclusive effect can vary by jurisdiction.

Example: A buyer of a defective truck has two theories — a products liability tort claim (short limitations period) and a breach of written contract claim (longer limitations period). If the buyer sues only on the tort after the limitations period has run and the case is dismissed, does that dismissal preclude the buyer from later suing for breach of the written contract, which is still timely? States disagree on this, and the federal system has its own view, which we will address next week.

## Element 2: Same Party

The general rule is that "same party" means the same legal entity. Spouses are not the same party. Business partners are not the same party. Corporations that hold interests in each other are not the same legal entity — absent a special relationship.

That special relationship is **privity**. For purposes of this course, parties are in privity in two circumstances only:

1. **Substantial control**: The party in Lawsuit 2 exercised substantial control over the conduct of the party in Lawsuit 1. Example: a sole shareholder of a corporation, where the corporation was the plaintiff in Lawsuit 1 and the shareholder is the plaintiff in Lawsuit 2.

2. **Successor in interest**: The party in Lawsuit 2 is a successor in interest to the party in Lawsuit 1. Example: a party sells the right to collect a debt to another party after filing a lawsuit on that debt. The successor, having acquired the interest, is bound by the prior judgment.

If neither of these applies, the parties are legally distinct and the ordinary rule governs — different legal entities are different parties.

## Element 3: Same Claim

"Claim" for claim preclusion purposes means **transaction or occurrence** — not a legal theory. This is the same broad transaction-or-occurrence test we encountered in *Heyward-Robinson* <!-- corrected: "Hayward Robinson" → Heyward-Robinson --> in the context of joinder. It is a deliberately broad and encompassing concept.

Ask: Are the two theories of relief related in time, space, and logic? Is the evidence needed to prove each similar? Would there be efficiency gains from requiring a plaintiff to bring both together? If yes, they are part of the same claim for preclusion purposes — regardless of whether they rest on different legal theories.

The court in *Gonzalez v. Banco Central Corp.* <!-- corrected: "Gonzalez" → Gonzalez v. Banco Central Corp.; retained as is --> states the law correctly. The dictum in that case says the same claim rule applies to the individual plots of land that were subject to the overall fraud, and each plot would ordinarily be the same transaction or occurrence. But the instructor finds the application troubling, because treating all plots as one "claim" would undermine the ability to sell individual parcels separately — which brings us to the next point.

### Exceptions Based on Market Structure

Sometimes the policy consequences of treating two things as the same claim are intolerable. In those cases, courts carve out exceptions by definition. The main example:

When we want to maintain a **functioning market** in individually tradeable items, we treat each item as a separate claim. Even if 2,000 bonds were sold in a single transaction, we define each bond — and each coupon on each bond — as a separate claim, so they can be separated and sold individually. The same logic applies when we want real property parcels to be individually alienable.

### Installment Contracts

For installment contracts (contracts with repeated payments over time):
- All **past-due and unpaid** payment obligations, as of the time the lawsuit is filed, are part of the same claim — we want one lawsuit for all past-due amounts.
- **Future payments** not yet owed are not part of the same claim — we do not yet know whether they will be missed.

## The "Stealth" Elements

### Limitation by the Rendering Court

A court in Lawsuit 1 may reduce the claim-preclusive effect of its own judgment by expressly dismissing a claim **"without prejudice."** A court may not, however, increase the claim-preclusive effect beyond what the law would otherwise provide simply by using the phrase "with prejudice." Use of that phrase means a subsequent court will examine the circumstances to determine whether claim preclusion is warranted.

### Jurisdiction of the Rendering Court: *Marrese v. American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons* <!-- corrected: "Marese" and "Roland Maresen" → Marrese v. American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons -->

In order for claim preclusion to extinguish a particular theory of relief in Lawsuit 2, the rendering court in Lawsuit 1 must have had the legal **ability** to adjudicate that theory. In *Marrese*, a state court was the rendering court in Lawsuit 1. The plaintiffs had sued on state antitrust causes of action. The state court could not hear federal antitrust claims because Congress had made federal antitrust jurisdiction exclusive to the federal courts — not because the state court was unwilling, but because it lacked authority. When the plaintiffs later brought federal antitrust claims in federal court based on the same facts, they were not claim precluded, even though they were the same parties, the same transaction or occurrence was involved, and the state court judgment was final on the merits. The rendering court simply could not have adjudicated the federal antitrust claim.
