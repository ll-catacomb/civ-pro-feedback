## Burdens of Production and Persuasion

Justice Brennan distinguishes two types of burdens:

**The burden of production** is the burden to produce evidence of something. If you fail to produce sufficient evidence, you will typically lose a pending motion — whether you filed it or it was filed against you. What counts as "sufficient" depends on the context, discussed below.

**The burden of persuasion** is the risk of non-persuasion — what happens if the evidence is in perfect equipoise, perfectly equal. If there is no evidence on an issue, or insufficient evidence to tilt the decision maker one way or the other, the evidence is said to be in equipoise, and the party bearing the burden of persuasion on that issue loses.

Burdens of persuasion are assigned by law before litigation starts and they never move. The plaintiff bears the burden of persuasion on each element of each cause of action asserted:

- For negligence: duty, breach, causation, damages
- For breach of contract: existence of a contract, breach, and damages

If the plaintiff fails to meet the burden of persuasion on any element, the plaintiff loses on that claim (though the plaintiff may prevail on other claims). Defendants bear the burden of persuasion on affirmative defenses.

## Summary Judgment: The Analytical Framework

The summary judgment record is constructed from discovery materials, which the parties attach to their memoranda in support of or in opposition to the motion. Once the record is complete, the judge applies the analytical framework Justice Brennan articulates.

### When the Moving Party Bears the Burden of Persuasion at Trial

If the moving party bears the burden of persuasion at trial on the issue as to which summary judgment is sought, the moving party must produce evidence that, if unrebutted, shows:

1. There is no genuine issue of material fact; and
2. The moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law — meaning a rational trier of fact could only find that the moving party has met its burden of persuasion by a preponderance of the evidence.

If the moving party fails to meet that burden of production, the judge denies the motion without consulting the other party's evidence. The motion is simply denied.

If the moving party meets that burden, the burden of production shifts to the non-moving party to produce evidence showing there is a genuine issue of material fact requiring a trial. If the non-moving party meets that burden, the judge denies the motion and the case goes to trial. If the non-moving party fails, the judge grants the motion.

### When the Moving Party Does Not Bear the Burden of Persuasion at Trial

If the moving party does not bear the burden of persuasion at trial, Justice Brennan identifies two options:

**Option One: Affirmatively knock out an element.** The moving party produces evidence that affirmatively negates some element of the opposing party's claim or defense. For example, in a negligence case, a defendant might produce evidence that the allegedly negligent act could not have caused the plaintiff's injuries — say, that the substance at issue does not cause the type of cancer the plaintiff suffers from. If the judge concludes the moving party has not produced enough evidence to affirmatively knock out the element (leaving no genuine issue of material fact), the judge denies the motion. If the judge concludes the moving party has done so, the burden of production shifts to the party with the burden of persuasion to demonstrate that a genuine issue of material fact exists.

**Option Two: Show absence of sufficient evidence.** The moving party tries to show that the opposing party has insufficient evidence to meet its burden of persuasion at trial. The example from class was *Jones v. Clinton*: Clinton's lawyers argued there was no evidence of causation between Jones's refusal to engage in sex with President Clinton and the adverse employment consequences she alleged. To show the absence of something, the moving party must take discovery, then present — from the discovery record — whatever the other side is likely to use to meet its burden, and argue why it is insufficient. You cannot simply file a conclusory brief asserting they have no evidence. You must anticipate what they will produce and demonstrate it falls short. If the judge is not satisfied that the moving party has made this showing, the motion is denied immediately. If satisfied, the burden of production again shifts to the non-moving party to come forward with evidence showing a genuine issue of material fact.

## "Genuine Issue of Material Fact"

Two important qualifiers:

**Genuine**: it must be an issue that actually requires a trial to resolve — not a manufactured or marginal dispute.

**Material**: the fact must be material to the ultimate disposition of the case. A fact that would be interesting to prove but would not affect the litigation's outcome is not material.

We applied this framework to a range of hypotheticals, and that was the black letter law for the day.
