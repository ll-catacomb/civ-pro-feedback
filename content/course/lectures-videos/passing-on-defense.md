## Overview

This is a short supplemental video explaining the passing-on defense — a doctrine that defendants asserted in Sherman Antitrust Act § 1 cases during the early and mid-twentieth century. Understanding the passing-on defense is necessary background for one of the appellate jurisdiction rulings covered in the next class period.

The Supreme Court ultimately ruled that the passing-on defense is not available in response to a Sherman Act § 1 lawsuit. But for a period before that ruling, the question was unsettled, and two appellate jurisdiction cases in the casebook arose out of that uncertainty.

---

## How Horizontal Price-Fixing Works

Section 1 of the Sherman Antitrust Act prohibits agreements among competitors that restrain trade. A classic example is horizontal price-fixing: competitors at the same level of the supply chain agree, rather than competing, to charge a single fixed price.

The scenario used in class: several suppliers of natural gas agree among themselves on the price they will charge a utility. Instead of competing and driving the price down, they form an illegal horizontal agreement — "horizontal" because they are all on the same plane in the supply chain, selling the same product to the same buyer. As a result, the utility must pay artificially inflated prices for gas. The utility then burns the gas, generates electricity, and sells the electricity to customers.

In a diagram:

- **Suppliers 1–4** (connected by arrows representing the illegal agreement) → sell gas to
- **The utility** (at artificially high prices) → which sells electricity to
- **Customers A, B, C, D...**

## The Passing-On Defense

When the utility sues the suppliers for violating Sherman Act § 1, the suppliers might assert that the utility was not actually harmed — arguing as follows:

The utility is itself a regulated monopoly. State governments recognize that because of the enormous fixed costs of building and operating utility infrastructure, it makes sense to have only one utility serving a given area. Customers cannot go elsewhere. So when the suppliers charged the utility inflated prices for gas, the utility simply passed those higher costs along to its customers in the form of higher electricity rates. The utility's profits were unaffected. The customers bore the real harm.

Under this argument, the utility has no damages and no standing to sue — or at least no recoverable injury. The customers are the ones harmed, but they are not parties to the utility's lawsuit. The suppliers would wait for the customers to sue, but in the meantime would defend the utility's claim on the ground that the utility suffered no injury because it passed the overcharge on.

That argument — that a direct purchaser was not injured because it passed its higher costs downstream to its own customers — is called the **passing-on defense**.

Note: In antitrust litigation, damages are sometimes trebled ("treble damages"), multiplying the award by three. This magnifies the stakes when the passing-on defense is at issue.

## Why the Passing-On Defense Created Appellate Jurisdiction Questions

Once a utility sued suppliers and the suppliers wished to assert the passing-on defense, they would seek discovery from the utility on the question of whether it actually passed the higher gas prices on to customers. The suppliers would want documents such as the utility's pricing policies, its customer margins, how it sets rates, and its internal economic records.

The utility would resist this discovery, arguing that the passing-on defense is legally unavailable, making any discovery on the question outside the permissible scope. The suppliers would respond that the defense is available and the discovery is relevant.

This discovery dispute forces the trial judge to rule on a threshold legal question: Is the passing-on defense legally available? The ruling has asymmetric consequences:

- If the judge rules the defense **is available**, the suppliers may take broad, burdensome, expensive discovery from the utility.
- If the judge rules the defense **is not available**, there is no discovery burden at all — at least with respect to this defense.

This legal question, arising in the context of a discovery ruling, is the kind of ruling that — before the Supreme Court settled the merits question — could give rise to appellate jurisdiction issues. Those cases are the subject of the next class period.
