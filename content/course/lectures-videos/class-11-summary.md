## Overview

Class 11 concluded our discussion of the Erie doctrine and began the motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6).

## Erie: The York Outcome-Determinative Test

[00:00:00] We briefly reviewed the *York* test: ask whether the difference between the federal and state rule is **outcome determinative**. If applying the federal rule rather than the state rule would affect the outcome of the litigation, use state law. If not, use federal law.

As noted, this test almost always points toward state law — nearly anything can be outcome determinative by the time a case is before a court.

---

## Erie: The Hanna Majority Test

[00:05:00] The Hanna majority test has two versions — the **Scalia version** and the **Ginsburg version** — a distinction that will matter when we later read *Shady Grove*.

**Step 1: Identify the federal law at issue and its source.** Is it a constitutional provision, a statute, a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure, or federal common law?

**Step 2: Apply the appropriate sub-test.**

### Constitutional Provisions

Presume validity — the people have spoken. Ask only whether the provision is **applicable**: does it provide an answer to the question at issue? Constitutional provisions are rarely interpreted narrowly; just apply standard constitutional interpretation and follow the provision if it applies.

### Federal Statutes

To measure validity, identify a source of congressional power in the Constitution. For Erie purposes, the relevant powers are typically: the Commerce Clause, the power to create the lower federal courts, the Full Faith and Credit Clause, and the Necessary and Proper Clause augmenting the others. If Congress had the power to enact the statute, it is valid.

Applicability is a standard statutory interpretation exercise. There is slightly more room to interpret a statute narrowly to avoid displacing state law — but not much.

*Example*: In *Stewart v. Ricoh*, <!-- corrected: "Stewart versus Rico" → *Stewart v. Ricoh* --> the transfer statutes were enacted pursuant to Congress's power to create the lower federal courts and the Necessary and Proper Clause, and were therefore valid.

### Federal Rules of Civil Procedure

**Validity** here is a **recitation exercise**, not an analytical one. Recite: the validity of a federal rule is measured by the Rules Enabling Act, which provides that a rule cannot abridge, enlarge, or modify a substantive right. As interpreted in *Sibbach*, <!-- corrected: "Sib Act" → *Sibbach* --> this means asking whether the rule really regulates procedure — does it concern itself with process? It does. Declare the rule valid and move on. Courts are essentially unwilling to strike down a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure on Rules Enabling Act grounds.

**Applicability** is where Scalia and Ginsburg diverge:

- **Scalia's approach**: Apply the federal rule as written without creative interpretation. If the rule applies to the question at hand, use it. If it does not apply, do not use it. Avoid consulting state law at all if the federal rule answers the question.

- **Ginsburg's approach**: Interpret the Federal Rule of Civil Procedure creatively, if possible, to allow the state law policy to receive effect. If the federal rule can be read narrowly enough that it does not answer the specific question at issue, there is no conflict and state law governs.

*Walker* illustrates the Ginsburg structural approach: the Court held that the federal rules' grace period for service did not speak to the question of what *tolls* the statute of limitations. That left room for a prior Supreme Court precedent (rather than state policy) to govern. The key structural move — reading the federal rule as not applicable — is the Ginsburg technique regardless of what it is avoiding.

### Federal Common Law (Judge-Made Law)

When the federal law at issue is federal common law, the valid-and-applicable framework does not apply. Instead, use the **twin aims of Erie** test:

1. Prevent forum shopping — would the use of a federal rule rather than the state rule give plaintiffs' attorneys an incentive to file in federal court?
2. Prevent inequitable administration of the laws.

In practice, this is the outcome-determinative test by another name: if the difference between state and federal law would affect outcomes, attorneys will factor it into forum selection decisions, creating forum shopping incentives. Use the state rule to eliminate the incentive.

### Justice Harlan's Concurrence in *Hanna*

The fourth test defines substantive law as any rule that bears on **primary conduct** — what clients do before they ever file a lawsuit. Harlan does not focus on forum shopping. He focuses on whether the rule at issue affects decisions like where to incorporate or what investments to make.

*Example from *Cohen*: A litigation bond requirement deters frivolous lawsuits, and deterring frivolous lawsuits affects the investment strategies of companies (primary conduct). Therefore it is substantive, and state law should govern. By contrast, the size of a filing paper or the length of a service grace period affects only what lawyers do inside litigation — not primary conduct — so federal law may be used for uniformity.

## Rule 12(b)(6): Motion to Dismiss for Failure to State a Claim

[00:40:00] There are three independent grounds for granting a 12(b)(6) motion:

### Ground 1: Fantastical Facts

The facts alleged are simply too implausible on their face to be credited. Classic example: aliens from Mars land a spaceship and commit some wrong. The complaint is dismissed because no reasonable trier of fact could credit those allegations.

### Ground 2: No Viable Cause of Action Applied to These Facts

The cause of action the plaintiff invokes does not provide a right to recover as applied to the stated facts. Example: a § 1983 claim against fellow students. Section 1983 requires state action, and students are not state actors. No amount of factual development will fix the legal mismatch.

### Ground 3: Insufficient Specificity of Allegations

[00:50:00] The facts are not alleged with sufficient particularity. Two competing frameworks:

**Code pleading**: The plaintiff must plead the underlying facts that, if proved, would establish each element of the claim — not merely the ultimate legal conclusion. A bare allegation of "conspiracy in restraint of trade" or "race discrimination" is insufficient; the complaint must allege the specific conduct that would support that conclusion (statements showing animus, documented patterns of disparate treatment, etc.).

**Notice pleading**: The complaint need not plead the specific underlying facts. It must contain enough information to put the defendant on notice of the nature of the allegations so the defendant can identify where to look for evidence and begin building a defense. In *Gillespie*, even an oddly-worded complaint satisfied this standard because it provided a date, time, party identification, and a basic description of the wrong — enough for defense counsel to begin an investigation and identify the applicable legal theory.

**What comes next**: Modern Supreme Court doctrine — *Twombly* and *Iqbal* — purports to reject notice pleading and instead requires **plausibility pleading**. The argument to be developed next class is that plausibility pleading is functionally just code pleading renamed. The Court introduced a structured two-step analysis, but the underlying demand for specific underlying facts is the same. The Federal Rules were designed to move *away* from code pleading, and the Court is walking that back while avoiding the label.
