## Overview

Class 12 focuses on preliminary injunctions through a simulated oral argument. This recording explains the format, roles, and how to prepare.

---

## Format and Structure

[00:00:00] Class 12 will simulate an oral argument in which students are assigned roles as advocates or judges. A Google document linked in the class email shows each student's assignment.

**The case**: *Thane Co.* <!-- corrected: "Thane code" → *Thane Co.* --> is the plaintiff and moving party seeking a preliminary injunction. The **Department of Commerce** (the government) is the defendant in opposition.

**The five advocate roles on each side:**
1. General themes — a broad overview of the side's case, previewing the specific-issue arguments
2. Likelihood of success on the merits
3. Irreparable harm
4. Balance of the equities
5. Public interest

Most students have been assigned one role on one side. A few have been assigned as judges, who should sit in the bottom row of the classroom for sight lines and audibility.

---

## The Four-Factor Preliminary Injunction Standard

The four factors are both the doctrinal test and the organizing structure of the argument:

1. **Likelihood of success on the merits**
2. **Irreparable harm**
3. **Balance of the equities**
4. **Public interest**

---

## How Class Will Run

[00:10:00] The sequence will be:

1. Randomized selection from those assigned to plaintiff's general themes → two-minute argument
2. Randomized selection from those assigned to defendant's general themes → two-minute argument
3. Judges ask a question of each side
4. Plaintiff's likelihood of success on the merits → two minutes
5. Defendant's likelihood of success on the merits → two minutes
6. Judges' questions
7. Repeat for balance of the equities, then irreparable harm
8. Judges confer and announce a ruling (odd number of judges ensures a majority)

Because more than ten students are assigned to each position, multiple students share each slot. The randomizer selects one per slot. Every student should prepare a two-minute argument regardless of whether they expect to be called.

---

## Preparation Instructions

**Reading**: Carefully read through all the briefs — both the Thane Co. moving brief and the Department of Commerce opposition brief. Then focus closely on the pages corresponding to your side and your assigned issue.

**Judges**: Read through both sides on all issues and prepare at least one question per argument segment.

**Argument preparation**: Do not write out your argument verbatim. Instead, identify the two or three points you want to emphasize and practice delivering them in approximately two minutes, once or twice, before class.

---

## Oral Argument Basics

For those who have not done an oral argument before:

- Begin with the conventional opening: "**May it please the court. My name is [your name], and I represent [your client].**"
- Maintain eye contact with the judges as much as possible.
- In this simulation, judges will not interrupt advocates mid-argument (they will ask questions between segments). In actual practice, interruptions from the bench are common and welcome — questions from judges reveal what concerns them and allow you to address those concerns directly. Here we skip interruptions given that many students are doing this for the first time.

Questions about the format can be raised at the beginning of class on Thursday.
