## Overview

This is a summary for Class 16, which focused on the distribution of power between judge and jury — primarily by examining the historical distinction between law and equity and its continuing effects in the modern era, and then by addressing the law/fact distinction.

---

## The Basic Framework

Judges do a great deal of fact-finding in the federal courts. They hear evidence and testimony and conduct much of the business of fact-finding. In order for either side to demand a jury trial, two conditions must both be true:

1. The issue must be on the **law side** of the law/equity distinction, as derived from history; and
2. The issue must be on the **fact side** of the law/fact distinction.

If either condition is not satisfied, there will be no jury trial — the judge will conduct all fact-finding.

---

## The Seventh Amendment and the Law/Equity Distinction

The law/equity distinction applies in civil cases in federal courts. The right to a jury trial under the Seventh Amendment does not apply to the states. The Seventh Amendment "preserves" the right to a jury trial in cases in which that right historically existed, when the amount in controversy exceeds $20 — which is to say, effectively all cases.

In most cases, determining whether a jury trial right attaches is straightforward: look for the historical analog to the claim being asserted, and the answer is usually clear. It is also often easy to tell from the relief requested. In a breach of contract lawsuit, for instance:

- If the plaintiff requests **damages**, there is a right to a jury trial (law side).
- If the plaintiff requests **specific performance** (which is an injunction), there is no right to a jury trial (equity side).

## Newly Created Causes of Action: The *Chauffeurs, Teamsters & Helpers Local 391 v. Terry* Two-Part Test

The difficulty arises when Congress creates new causes of action that did not exist in the 1790s, or when Congress creates new decision makers (typically administrative law judges within agencies).

For newly created causes of action, courts apply a two-part test from *Chauffeurs, Teamsters & Helpers Local 391 v. Terry*: <!-- corrected: "Schoffers v. Terry" and "Schauphers v. Terry" → Chauffeurs, Teamsters & Helpers Local 391 v. Terry -->

**Part One: Find the historical analog.** Look at the cause of action and try to find its best historical analog in terms of the types of parties at issue and their legal relationship. In *Chauffeurs v. Terry*, the Court found a strong analogy between a union member's suit for violation of the duty of fair representation and a historical suit by beneficiaries against a trustee for breach of fiduciary duty.

But the Court also taught us that it is not just the cause of action as a whole that matters — it is the particular issues embedded within the cause of action. In *Chauffeurs v. Terry*, two issues were embedded within the claim:

- **Whether there was a breach of the collective bargaining agreement** — analogous to a garden-variety breach of contract, which was historically on the law side, giving rise to a jury trial right.
- **Whether there was a breach of the duty of fair representation** — analogous to breach of fiduciary duty, which was historically equitable.

The Court found itself in equipoise on the first part — roughly one to one — and so the test depends heavily on Part Two.

**Part Two: The type of remedy requested.** This is, according to the Court, the more important part. Some remedies will be new, with no direct historical analog, in which case we find the closest one. In *Chauffeurs v. Terry*, the back pay the plaintiffs requested was more like damages than restitution: the ill-gotten gains subject to a restitutionary theory were held by the employer, who was not a party to the lawsuit. So the remedy could not be analogized to a restitutionary cause of action, and was instead treated as legal — consistent with a damages award on the law side.

## The Public Rights Exception and Administrative Decision Makers

Even when a cause of action would ordinarily carry a right to a jury trial, the absence of that right can be justified by the identity of the decision maker. The **public rights exception** provides that when the government is a party and is acting as a sovereign to enforce public rights — protecting worker safety, enforcing federal regulations, asserting forfeiture rights — adjudication may proceed before an administrative law judge without a jury. This exception is narrow: the government cannot simply be acting as an ordinary economic actor (purchasing goods or services). It must be exercising sovereign functions.

This exception saves much of the modern administrative state, since agencies generally cannot conduct jury trials.

**Note**: Sometimes you must apply both the *Chauffeurs v. Terry* two-part test and the public rights exception. If Congress creates a new cause of action and lodges adjudication in an agency, first determine whether a jury trial right attaches to the cause of action at all. If it does not, the inquiry ends. Only if a jury trial right would otherwise attach do you need to ask whether the public rights exception removes it.

## The Law/Fact Distinction

The law/fact distinction often resolves itself through historical practice, precedent, or sheer obviousness:

- Disputes about historical facts — contradictory witness testimony about whether a light was green — go to the jury.
- Statutory interpretation is a legal issue for the judge.

When cases fall near the line and lack historical analog or clear precedent, courts consult **function**: who is the better decision maker?

**Juries** are considered better at:
- Untangling contradictions in testimony and assessing witness credibility
- Representing community standards, given the cross-section of perspectives they bring

**Judges** are considered better at:
- Providing uniformity through precedent, so parties can plan their conduct accordingly
- Construing written instruments, given their training and practice
- Overseeing long-running structural injunction cases

In *Markman v. Westview Instruments*, <!-- corrected: "Markman" reference confirmed --> the Court found no direct historical practice to guide the law/fact distinction for construing the scope of a patent claim. It therefore consulted functional factors and held that claim construction is a matter of law — because judges are better both at providing uniformity and at construing written instruments like patent applications.
