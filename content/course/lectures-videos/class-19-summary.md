## 28 U.S.C. § 1292(b): The "Double Discretion" Doctrine

28 U.S.C. § 1292(b) allows a district court to certify an important but unresolved legal issue to the court of appeals before final judgment. It is called the "double discretion" doctrine because two actors must each exercise discretion in the party's favor.

For certification, the district court must state **in writing** that:

1. The order involves a **controlling question of law** (an important question material to the case);
2. There is a **substantial ground for difference of opinion** on that question — it cannot be an obvious or settled issue; and
3. An immediate appeal would **materially advance the ultimate termination of the litigation**.

Once the district court makes those findings in writing, the party seeking the appeal has **10 days** to petition the court of appeals for permission to appeal. The court of appeals has essentially unbridled discretion to accept or deny the petition.

Hence "double discretion": the district court's willingness to certify, and the circuit court's willingness to accept.

## Petitions for Writ of Mandamus

Another way to get a district court ruling before the circuit court is to petition for a writ of mandamus. Courts are generally reluctant to grant these petitions, in part because of how the proceeding begins: the petitioner files a new lawsuit in the circuit court, with the district judge as the nominal respondent. The district judge must then either defend the ruling personally or rely on the opposing party to do so (which the opposing party may or may not do).

The authorizing statute is the All Writs Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1651, which simply authorizes courts to issue writs. The circumstances under which mandamus may issue are governed by historical practice and developed doctrine.

**Two categories of mandamus:**

**Garden-variety writ**: The petitioning party must show:
1. The right to relief is **clear and indisputable** — the circuit court must be quite confident the petitioner is correct on the merits (a high bar, since presumably the district court disagreed);
2. There is some **exigent circumstance** — a reason to act now rather than waiting for final judgment; and
3. Issuing the writ would be in **equity and good conscience and in accordance with sound judicial administration**.

These factors are functionally similar to the four preliminary injunction factors from Rule 65, though Rule 65 does not apply in the court of appeals.

**Supervisory writ**: When a district court has been doing the same problematic thing repeatedly, the circuit court may issue a supervisory writ of mandamus to correct the pattern. The circuit court does not label the writ as "supervisory" — it simply issues the writ — but the supervisory nature of the situation is an argument the petitioner can make to the circuit court in support of the petition.

## Joinder: Adding Parties and Claims Beyond the Basic Case

A single plaintiff suing a single defendant on a single theory of relief requires no special authorization under the Federal Rules. Any additional complexity requires both:

1. **Authorization under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure** (permissive or compulsory joinder rules); and
2. **Subject matter jurisdiction** over each new claim or party.

The analysis is a two-step inquiry for each new claim or party:
- Step 1: Is it permitted under the applicable federal rule?
- Step 2: Is there subject matter jurisdiction?

For subject matter jurisdiction: first check whether the claim arises under federal law (either because Congress created the cause of action, or under the *Smith v. Kansas City Title* <!-- corrected: "Smith-Grabel" → Smith v. Kansas City Title and Grable & Sons Metal Products v. Darue Engineering --> /*Grable* framework). If not, check for diversity jurisdiction (complete diversity + amount in controversy over $75,000). If neither applies, check supplemental jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1367, but be careful about claims by plaintiffs against persons made parties under Rule 14, 19, 20, or 24 when the anchoring claim is a diversity case.

**The transaction or occurrence test** is central to many joinder rules:
- **Compulsory counterclaims** (Rule 13(a)): must be asserted if they arise from the same transaction or occurrence
- **Permissive counterclaims** (Rule 13(b)): may be asserted even if from a different transaction or occurrence
- **Cross-claims** (Rule 13(g)): allowed when they arise from the same transaction or occurrence
- **Impleader of a third-party defendant** (Rule 14): an exception — the test is not transaction or occurrence but rather whether the original defendant seeks to shift liability to the third party

Courts tend to define "same transaction or occurrence" broadly, asking whether the actions are similar in time, space, legal issues, and evidentiary overlap. The policy favors efficiency: litigating related claims together is more efficient than separate lawsuits. If the resulting case becomes too unwieldy, Rule 42 allows the district court to sever claims, control the order of trial, and otherwise manage the proceeding.

## Intervention

Two types of intervention exist — intervention as of right and permissive intervention — but you are not responsible for distinguishing between them. The court of appeals in *Smuck v. Hobson* <!-- corrected: "SMAC" → *Smuck v. Hobson* --> did not think much of the distinction either.

When a non-party seeks to intervene in a lawsuit, courts consider:

1. Does the proposed intervenor have a **legally cognizable, protectable interest** at stake in the litigation?
2. Is there already a party in the lawsuit **adequately protecting** that interest?
3. Would adding the new party make the lawsuit **too unwieldy to administer**?

- No cognizable interest → no intervention
- Interest already adequately represented → no intervention
- Addition would make the case unmanageable → no intervention
