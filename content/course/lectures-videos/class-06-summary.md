## Overview

Class 6 was the first full class devoted to personal jurisdiction. We worked through *Pennoyer v. Neff*, the transition to *International Shoe*, general jurisdiction doctrine, and began specific jurisdiction with *McGee v. International Life Insurance Co.*

---

## *Pennoyer v. Neff* and the Territorial Vision

[00:00:00] In *Pennoyer*, the Court articulates a strong territorial theory of personal jurisdiction: a state may exercise personal jurisdiction only over (1) a citizen of that state, (2) a corporation incorporated or resident there, or (3) an individual physically tagged with process **while present in the state**.

The dual rationale: state sovereigns should not encroach on each other's territorial authority, and a defendant tagged in-state could not complain too loudly about the inconvenience of litigating there — in an era of difficult travel, if you got there at all, it probably was not too burdensome to stay.

---

## The Pressure on *Pennoyer*: Cars, Contracts, and Corporations

As the national economy developed — contracts formed by mail and telegraph, corporations operating across state lines, railroads, telephones, and eventually automobiles — the in-state tag requirement became untenable. Courts developed fictions: treating state officials as appointed agents for service, deeming corporations "present" in a state simply by virtue of doing business there.

*International Shoe Co. v. Washington* casts aside those fictions and replaces them with a focus on the **nature and quality of the defendant's contacts** with the forum as the measure of whether jurisdiction is permissible.

---

## *International Shoe*: Two Types of Personal Jurisdiction

The *International Shoe* Court recognized two distinct bases of personal jurisdiction:

1. **General jurisdiction**: Jurisdiction over the defendant for any claim whatsoever, regardless of whether the subject matter of the litigation has any connection to the forum.
2. **Specific jurisdiction**: Jurisdiction that depends on the relationship among the defendant's activities, the subject matter of the litigation, and the forum.

*International Shoe* was itself a **specific jurisdiction** case. The company sent salespeople into Washington to solicit shoe orders. The litigation concerned unpaid unemployment insurance taxes on exactly that activity. The connection between what the defendant did in the forum and what the lawsuit was about was direct.

The majority opinion focuses almost exclusively on the defendant's conduct and contacts. It does not address the state's interest in providing a forum, the plaintiff's interest in a convenient place to litigate, the location of evidence, or which law will apply. Those factors will enter the analysis later.

## General Personal Jurisdiction

[00:20:00] Three bases (with a possible fourth):

### 1. "Really at Home" — Domicile and Daimler

An individual can be sued in the state of their **domicile** for any claim at all. For corporations, the parallel is **principal place of business** and **state of incorporation** — a corporation can be sued on any basis in either of those states.

This requires no long-arm statute because the defendant is at home and the court's power is not contested on due process grounds.

### 2. Daimler "At Home" Jurisdiction

From *Daimler AG v. Bauman*: a defendant's activities in a state may be so substantial relative to its total activities that the forum is **highly analogous** to the defendant's actual home. Because the defendant is not literally at home, a **long-arm statute** authorizing the court to hear the case is required, plus the exercise must satisfy the constitutional standard.

Long-arm statutes may: (a) enumerate specific circumstances in which jurisdiction is authorized; (b) authorize jurisdiction to the constitutional limits; or (c) both. If a case falls outside the enumerated circumstances and the statute does not authorize jurisdiction to constitutional limits, the court lacks statutory authority regardless of the constitutional analysis.

After *Daimler*, this form of general jurisdiction is rare. What is required is that a **large fraction of the defendant's total activities** (not merely a large absolute volume) occur in the forum state.

### 3. Consent

A defendant can waive personal jurisdiction and consent to it. Consent is established when:
- The defendant **fails to object promptly** — typically in the first responsive filing (a motion to dismiss or in the answer); a defendant who does not timely raise the objection forfeits it.
- The **plaintiff** consents to personal jurisdiction — in the forum where plaintiff filed — by virtue of filing there. A plaintiff cannot then object to personal jurisdiction over them for a counterclaim filed in that forum, even a counterclaim with no relationship to the forum and one that could not have been filed there originally.
- A **forum selection clause** constitutes advance consent. Unless the chosen forum is extremely oppressive, courts enforce forum selection clauses. A party who agreed to a clause cannot later object to being sued in the designated forum.

### (Possible Fourth Basis: The In-State Tag)

In *Pennoyer*, in-state physical service was sufficient to establish general personal jurisdiction. After *Burnham v. Superior Court*, four justices take this seriously and four do not. The status of the in-state tag for general jurisdiction remains unresolved, and you should be prepared to argue both ways.

## Specific Jurisdiction: Introduction and *McGee*

[00:50:00] Specific personal jurisdiction requires:
1. A **long-arm statute** (because we will be dealing with an objecting out-of-state defendant)
2. Constitutionality under the **Fourteenth Amendment Due Process Clause** (for state courts; and by operation of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 4(k)(1)(A), the same constitutional standard also limits federal courts)

*McGee v. International Life Insurance Co.*: <!-- corrected: "McGee case" → *McGee v. International Life Insurance Co.* --> A Texas insurance company sold a single life insurance policy to a California resident. When the insured died, the company refused to pay. The insured's estate sued in California. The Court found California jurisdiction valid.

The relationship is tight: the defendant (Texas insurer) sold the policy in California; the subject matter (the policy and the insured's death) is California-based; the forum is California. The defendant purposefully availed itself of the California market — even if only once — derived economic benefit, and was entitled to the protection of California law while conducting that business.

The Court goes further, however, discussing California's interest in providing a forum for its residents to litigate against out-of-state defendants, and the plaintiff's interest in a convenient forum. We do not yet know how these additional factors fit together with the purposeful availment analysis. Further cases will clarify.
