## Standards of Review

An appellate court applies one of four standards when reviewing rulings that a trial judge made and that a losing party alleges were incorrect.

### De Novo

*De novo* (Latin: "anew") means the appellate court gives no deference whatsoever to the trial court's decision. It considers the trial court's reasoning the way it would a party's brief — persuasive if well-reasoned, but entitled to no special weight. De novo review applies to the trial court's **rulings on questions of law**.

### Clearly Erroneous and Abuse of Discretion

These two standards are functionally the same. Under either, the appellate court gives the trial judge some discretion and deference, but not unlimited deference. If the appellate court can see what the trial judge did and why, and concludes it was reasonable — even if the appellate court would have decided differently — it will uphold the decision and find no error. If the appellate court concludes the trial judge's decision was far outside the bounds of reasonable judgment, it will find error (though not necessarily reverse, for reasons discussed below).

- **Clearly erroneous**: used to review a trial judge's **findings of fact** when the judge is sitting as the trier of fact (as in an equity lawsuit).
- **Abuse of discretion**: applies to almost everything else — discovery rulings, applications of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (including Rule 59 motions for new trial), evidentiary rulings, and the like. The party must have objected to the ruling or requested what the judge refused to give; if not, only plain error review is available.

### Plain Error

Plain error review applies when a party argues that the trial judge made a mistake the party did not object to, or failed to do something the party did not ask for. The appellate court reviews only for error that is so obviously and egregiously wrong that no rational trial judge would have committed it. Plain error findings are extraordinarily rare.

## Harmless Error

Even if an appellate court concludes there was error, it does not automatically reverse or order a new trial. The appellate court must also find that the error was not harmless — meaning there is a reasonable probability the error affected the outcome at trial. If the court concludes the error was harmless, it affirms, even though there was error.

Courts sometimes use harmless error doctrine strategically: rather than deciding whether there was error, the appellate court assumes error arguendo and then concludes that even so, the error would be harmless. In that case, the court can affirm without reaching the merits of the alleged error.

This applies to almost all issues on appeal (with a few exceptions we will not cover in this first-year course).

## Jury Instructions

Jury instructions are typically given at the end of evidence and testimony. The judge asks the parties to submit proposed instructions, which are served on each other. The parties may then object to the opposing party's proposals, and the court gives the final instructions.

Key rules:
- If you want a particular jury instruction and do not ask for it, and the judge does not give it, you get only plain error review on appeal.
- If you fail to object to the opposing party's proposed instruction and the judge gives it, any appeal is likewise limited to plain error.

The *Kennedy* case holds that in at least some circuits, if a party proposes an instruction on a reasonably important issue that is close but not quite legally correct, the judge has an obligation to correct it and either give a corrected version or provide another instruction on the same subject. Failure to do so may constitute an abuse of discretion. This is not the rule in all circuits; in some, a judge commits no error by refusing to give a legally incorrect proposed instruction.

## Rule 52: Findings of Fact by the Judge

Rule 52 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure requires that when a judge sits as the trier of fact (as in an equity case), the judge must make findings of fact before entering judgment. The findings need not be in writing — they may be stated orally on the record. The judge may also adopt one party's proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law in their entirety.

In *Lansford-Coaldale Joint Water Authority v. Tonolli Corp.* <!-- corrected: "Lance for Caldell" → *Lansford-Coaldale Joint Water Authority v. Tonolli Corp.* --> the Court held that the standard of appellate review does not change when a judge wholesale adopts one party's proposed findings: the clearly erroneous standard applies regardless.

**Reasons for requiring findings of fact:**
1. Forcing the judge to articulate reasons tends to improve the quality of decision-making.
2. It helps the appellate court understand the trial judge's reasoning and exercise meaningful review for clear error.
3. It helps with preclusion doctrine — knowing what was decided and why, which will matter when we study issue preclusion.

## Rule 50: Judgment as a Matter of Law

Rule 50 applies when a jury is the trier of fact. It replaced the former directed verdict (pre-verdict) and judgment notwithstanding the verdict, or JNOV (post-verdict), motions.

**Procedure**: A party seeking judgment as a matter of law must:
1. File a **pre-verdict motion** before the jury deliberates. The judge does not rule on it immediately — the judge defers ruling until after the verdict, subject to renewal.
2. **Renew the motion** after the jury returns its verdict.

If the party does not make both the pre-verdict and post-verdict motions, an appellate court will not entertain any argument that the trial court erred in failing to grant judgment as a matter of law.

Why require the pre-verdict motion? To give the opposing party an opportunity to cure any evidentiary gap the motion has identified. Why require the post-verdict renewal? To formally remind the judge that the motion was deferred and still needs a ruling.

**Standard**: The judge makes all reasonable inferences in the light most favorable to the non-moving party — including resolving credible credibility disputes in the non-moving party's favor — and then asks: could any rational jury find for the non-moving party? If yes, deny the motion. If no rational jury could so find, grant the motion.

"All reasonable inferences" does not mean all conceivable inferences. Under *Scott v. Harris*, if objective evidence — such as a video or indisputably accurate records — clearly refutes a witness's testimony, the judge need not credit that testimony simply because the non-moving party relies on it.

Judgment as a matter of law is a legal determination, so the appellate court reviews a grant or denial de novo.

## Rule 59: Motion for New Trial

A Rule 59 motion for new trial may be filed at any time up to 28 days after the trial judge enters judgment.

**Grounds for a new trial:**
1. **Non-harmless trial error**: The judge made a mistake that you can show was not harmless, and you are asking the judge to spare everyone the trouble of an appeal by granting a new trial now.
2. **Verdict against the great weight of the evidence** (from *Yates v. United States* <!-- corrected: "Yates" → Yates v. United States; general principle confirmed -->): Even though the jury's verdict was rational (if it were not, Rule 50 would be the remedy), the judge believes it runs contrary to the great weight of the evidence. One example: the trial judge suspects a witness who testified in favor of the prevailing party was lying. When adjudicating a Rule 50 motion, the judge must assume that testimony is true; when adjudicating a Rule 59 motion for new trial, the judge need not. That is a key distinction between the two motions.
