## Overview

This is a detailed technical summary of class 9 for Section 2 (Monday) and Section 7 (Wednesday). It is recorded in advance of class 10 to create more time for that class's more challenging material. Topics: venue in federal court, forum non-convenience, and the transfer statutes.

---

## Venue: Orientation

[00:00:00] We study **federal court venue** only. State court venue statutes exist but are not covered in this course. Venue is concerned entirely with convenience — the convenient and expeditious resolution of disputes. It has no constitutional dimension and no power dimension. It is governed entirely by statute and determines which **federal district** (not which state) a case may be filed in.

Venue operates like personal jurisdiction in one important respect: if venue is improper and the defendant objects, the district court **must dismiss or transfer** and cannot proceed. Like personal jurisdiction, venue can be **waived** by failing to object promptly.

---

## The Federal Venue Statute: 28 U.S.C. § 1391

**B1**: A plaintiff may lay venue in any district where **any defendant resides**, provided **all defendants reside in the same state**. If even one defendant resides in a different state, B1 is unavailable for any district.

**Residency**:
- For a natural person: **domicile**
- For a corporation and unincorporated association: any district in which the entity is **subject to personal jurisdiction**

**B2**: A plaintiff may lay venue where **a substantial part of the events or omissions giving rise to the claim** occurred, or where the relevant property is located (in rem or quasi in rem proceedings).

**B3**: Available only when neither B1 nor B2 provides any usable district. B3 allows filing wherever any defendant is subject to personal jurisdiction. In practice, B3 is almost exclusively relevant when events occurred entirely outside the United States or when defendants are scattered across multiple states with no overlapping district.

## Forum Non-Convenience: When to Use It

[00:15:00] Forum non-convenience is a doctrine that operates by **dismissal** — the court dismisses the case with the expectation that the plaintiff will refile elsewhere.

Use forum non-convenience when:
1. A case is pending in a **state court** and the suggestion is to move it to another state or to a foreign court abroad, or
2. A case is pending in **federal court** and the suggestion is to move it to a **foreign court abroad**

Do **not** use forum non-convenience to move a case between federal courts — that is done through the transfer statutes. Do not use it to move a case from federal court to state court — that cannot be done (absent a forum selection clause directing the case to state court).

### The Forum Non-Convenience Analysis

1. **Presumption**: The court presumes the plaintiff's choice of forum is correct — unless the plaintiff is from abroad, in which case no such presumption applies (per *Piper Aircraft*).
2. **Private interest factors** (*Gulf Oil* factors): Location of witnesses who can be compelled to testify, location of documents and evidence, relative expense of litigation in each location, ability to implead third parties.
3. **Public interest factors** (*Gulf Oil* factors): Court congestion, the relationship between the forum community and the dispute (so that any required jury is drawn from a community with a genuine connection to the case), local interest in having the controversy decided at home.

If the combination of private and public interest factors overwhelms the presumption, the court dismisses.

**The defendant's promise**: In a forum non-convenience dismissal, the defendant may promise not to raise defenses it would otherwise have in the alternative forum — including personal jurisdiction, venue, or statute of limitations objections. This allows the defendant to effectively manufacture a viable forum to which the case can go.

## The Transfer Statutes: 28 U.S.C. §§ 1404, 1406, 1631

[00:35:00] The transfer statutes govern movement of cases within the **federal court system**. Do not try to distinguish among them for this course — treat their collective effect as the governing law.

**Key difference from forum non-convenience**: A defendant **cannot** create a transferee court by promising to waive personal jurisdiction and venue defenses there. The transferee court must be a court in which the case "**could have been brought**" — which under *Hoffman v. Blaski* means a **happy court**: one in which the plaintiff could have independently established both personal jurisdiction over the defendants and proper venue as of the time the suit was filed.

### The Four-Question Transfer Analysis

**Question 1: Must I transfer?**
If the current court lacks either personal jurisdiction or venue, the case must be transferred or dismissed. It cannot proceed over the defendant's objection.

**Question 2: May I transfer?**
A transfer is permissible only to a happy court — one where both personal jurisdiction and venue are independently available. If no such court exists, the case must be dismissed (not transferred).

**Question 3: Should I transfer?**
When the present court is a happy court and there is another happy court available, whether to transfer is a forum non-convenience analysis: presume the plaintiff's choice of forum is correct, then weigh the private and public *Gulf Oil* interest factors.

**Question 4: What happens if I do?**

This is the **Greiner Happy Court Rule** — governing horizontal choice of law after a transfer.

Federal courts sitting in diversity use the choice-of-law rules of the state in which they sit (*Klaxon*). After a transfer, the question is which state's choice-of-law rules apply.

- **If the transferor court was a happy court**: The choice-of-law analysis follows the transfer. The transferee court pretends it is sitting in the transferor court's state. Limitations periods and all other substantive law that would have applied under the transferor state's choice-of-law rules continue to apply. The transfer is a change of courtroom, nothing more.

- **If the transferor court was an unhappy court**: The choice-of-law analysis of the transferor court does not follow the transfer. The transferee court applies its own state's choice-of-law rules as if the lawsuit had been filed there from the beginning — including the transferee state's limitations period.

## Forum Selection Clauses and *Atlantic Marine*

[00:55:00] A forum selection clause changes the transfer analysis in three ways:

1. **No presumption in favor of plaintiff's choice of forum.** Instead, the court presumes it should transfer to the forum specified in the clause.

2. **Private interest factors are disregarded.** The parties encapsulated all private interest concerns when they negotiated the contract. Only public interest factors are considered to overcome the presumption in favor of transfer.

3. **The Greiner Happy Court Rule does not apply.** If the case is transferred to comply with the forum selection clause, the transferee court's choice-of-law analysis governs — regardless of whether the transferor court was happy or unhappy.

As a practical matter, the combination of these three changes means that a case subject to a forum selection clause will almost always be transferred to the clause's designated forum.

## *Stewart v. Ricoh* and Erie <!-- corrected: "Stewart v. Rico" → *Stewart v. Ricoh* -->

*Stewart v. Ricoh* is assigned as an Erie case. When a state law invalidates forum selection clauses and a defendant moves to transfer under a federal transfer statute, which governs? The answer (which we will fully develop in class 10 or 11) is the federal transfer statute, because the statutory law on transfers is valid and applicable federal law under the Erie framework. The rules governing transfer motions — including the effect of forum selection clauses — are part and parcel of the federal statutory transfer scheme.

Note: *Stewart v. Ricoh*'s specific statements about how to handle forum selection clause transfer motions are **not good law**. The controlling authority on that question is *Atlantic Marine*.
