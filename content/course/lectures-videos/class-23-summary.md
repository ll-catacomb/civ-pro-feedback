## Overview

Class 23 focused almost entirely on class actions, and also covered the rule that emerges from *Beacon Theaters v. Westover*. <!-- corrected: "Beacon Theaters" — full case name is Beacon Theatres, Inc. v. Westover; flagged for review -->

## Subject Matter Jurisdiction in Class Actions

### Arising-Under Claims

If a class action is filed in federal court and all class members assert arising-under (federal-question) causes of action — as was the case in *Parklane Hosiery*, <!-- corrected: "Park Lane Hosiery" → Parklane Hosiery --> a private class action where each member asserted claims under federal securities law — there is no subject-matter jurisdiction problem. 28 U.S.C. § 1331 covers each unnamed class member and the named plaintiff alike.

### Diversity Jurisdiction in Class Actions

If the only possible basis for jurisdiction is 28 U.S.C. § 1332, you need to work through both the complete-diversity requirement and the amount-in-controversy requirement in the class action context.

**Complete diversity:** Under *Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur v. Cauble* (a pre-Rule 23 case incorporated into Rule 23 jurisprudence), <!-- corrected: "Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur" — flagged; full cite is Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur v. Cauble, 255 U.S. 356 (1921) --> you look to diversity only of the named plaintiff(s) and the defendant. Unnamed class members are not considered.

**Amount in controversy:** As long as one named plaintiff is completely diverse and satisfies the amount-in-controversy requirement, the supplemental jurisdiction statute — 28 U.S.C. § 1367 — can be used to assert supplemental jurisdiction over unnamed class members (and even other named plaintiffs) who do not meet the amount-in-controversy requirement. This is the holding of *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Services, Inc.* <!-- corrected: "Exxon" → Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Services, Inc. --> That approach works only if the class action is against a **single defendant**; it does not work against multiple defendants.

### The Exxon Rationale

The reasoning in *Exxon Mobil v. Allapattah* applies equally to simple joinder of plaintiffs suing a single defendant and to class actions. The Court's logic:

1. As long as there is an anchoring claim — a diversity claim over which there is original jurisdiction under § 1332 (a completely diverse named plaintiff who meets the amount in controversy) — § 1367(a) authorizes jurisdiction to the constitutional limit.
2. The presence of amount-in-controversy (AIC) deficient claims does not contaminate the anchoring claim, because those claims do not reduce the importance of the $75,000+ anchoring claim.
3. By contrast, the presence of **non-diverse** parties would contaminate the anchoring claim, because the entire rationale for diversity jurisdiction — protecting against state-court bias toward the in-state party — disappears once there are parties from the same state on both sides of the case.

Therefore, AIC-deficient claims are permissible under § 1367(a) so long as the anchoring claim retains original jurisdiction.

**Why the single-defendant limit matters under § 1367(b):** Section 1367(b) ousts supplemental jurisdiction over claims by plaintiffs against persons made parties under Rule 20. If there are multiple defendants, Rule 20 was necessary to join them, so plaintiffs' claims against those additional defendants fall within § 1367(b)'s ouster. A single defendant, however, was not joined via Rule 20; only the multiple plaintiffs were joined (via Rule 20 in simple joinder or Rule 23 in a class action). Thus the § 1367(b) ouster does not apply.

Complete diversity must be preserved at all times under *Strawbridge v. Curtiss*. <!-- corrected: "Strawberry Justice Curtis" → Strawbridge v. Curtiss -->

### Illustrative Hypothetical

In the class hypothetical, there was complete diversity between the named plaintiff and the single defendant, and the named plaintiff was AIC-sufficient. Therefore it did not matter that unnamed class members were both non-diverse and AIC-deficient: under *Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur*, diversity is assessed only as to named plaintiffs; and under *Exxon Mobil v. Allapattah*, the supplemental jurisdiction statute permits jurisdiction over even non-diverse unnamed class members whose claims are AIC-deficient.

## Personal Jurisdiction in Class Actions

**Unnamed plaintiff class members:** As long as sufficient procedural protections are in place — as required for class certification under Rule 23 — it is permissible for a court to exercise personal jurisdiction over unnamed plaintiff class members. That is the rule from *Phillips Petroleum Co. v. Shutts*. <!-- corrected: "shuts" → Phillips Petroleum Co. v. Shutts -->

**Open question — specific personal jurisdiction:** An open question remains when only some class members' claims have minimum contacts relating the subject matter, the forum, and the defendant. We know from *Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Court* <!-- corrected: "Bristol-Myers Quib" → Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Court --> that, in a **simple joinder** context, a state court cannot exercise specific personal jurisdiction over claims by plaintiffs who lack the necessary minimum contacts merely because other plaintiffs in the same case do have those contacts. Because Rule 4(k)(1)(A) limits a federal court's personal jurisdiction to what a state court of the same state could exercise, this rule applies in federal court in simple joinder cases as well.

Whether *Bristol-Myers Squibb* applies to **class actions** is unresolved. The answer turns on whether a certified class is:

- An **aggregation of individual claims** (class action as joinder on steroids), in which case *Bristol-Myers Squibb* would prohibit specific personal jurisdiction as to individual class members whose claims lack minimum contacts; or
- A **new legal entity**, in which case that single entity has been harmed in many locations and could likely assert specific personal jurisdiction wherever any of its members (at least the named plaintiffs) suffered injury.

The Supreme Court has not yet decided which characterization is correct.

**General personal jurisdiction** remains straightforward: if you are suing a single defendant at its principal place of business or its state of incorporation, general personal jurisdiction is available for all class members' claims.

## Venue in Class Actions

For a class action against a single defendant that is a corporation, 28 U.S.C. § 1391(c) and (b)(1) together provide that venue can be laid wherever the defendant is subject to personal jurisdiction (since a corporation resides, for venue purposes, wherever it is subject to personal jurisdiction in that district). It may also be possible to lay venue under § 1391(b)(2) in a district where a key event common to all class members occurred — for example, the district where the alleged computer programming that swept money from plaintiffs' accounts was carried out.

## Pleading

The class action complaint must of course comply with *Twombly* and *Iqbal*. <!-- corrected: "Trombone and Eccle" → Twombly and Iqbal -->

## Rule 23 Class Certification

### The Four Prerequisites: Rule 23(a)

To certify a class, all four Rule 23(a) prerequisites must be satisfied, and the class must fit within at least one Rule 23(b) category.

#### Numerosity — Rule 23(a)(1)

- Generally satisfied if there are more than 40 plaintiffs.
- Generally not satisfied if there are fewer than 20 plaintiffs.
- Between 20 and 40, courts are more likely to certify if plaintiffs are geographically dispersed (making simple joinder impractical) or if their individual amounts in controversy are small (making individual suits not worth filing).

#### Commonality — Rule 23(a)(2)

Commonality is the most demanding prerequisite, especially after *Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes*. <!-- corrected: "Walmart" → Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes --> *Wal-Mart* requires **common answers** to common questions — ordinarily some specific practice or policy that the defendant has engaged in with respect to each and every class member. The mere existence of subjectivity in individual employment decisions (as in *Wal-Mart* itself, where there was no common company-wide policy for hiring and promotion decisions) is not itself a policy. You must be able to point to something concrete that the defendant did in common to all class members.

**Horizontal choice of law and commonality:** Choice-of-law issues can defeat commonality. Each class member's claim is treated as a separate claim for choice-of-law purposes. Under *Klaxon Co. v. Stentor Electric Manufacturing Co.* <!-- corrected: "Claxon" → *Klaxon* --> and *Ferens v. John Deere Co.*, <!-- corrected: "John Deere" → *Ferens v. John Deere Co.* --> a federal court applies the choice-of-law rules of the state in which it sits. That means different states' substantive law may apply to different class members' claims, and those differences can affect elements of the cause of action or available defenses — potentially defeating commonality.

State courts have sometimes tried to solve this problem by applying a single state's law to all class members. The Supreme Court held in *Phillips Petroleum Co. v. Shutts* that a state court cannot apply its own law to class members' claims unless there is some contact relating the defendant, the subject matter of the litigation, and the forum. The constitutional floor for choice-of-law contacts is lower than for personal jurisdiction, but some contact is required. In *Shutts*, Kansas had no contact whatsoever with the oil leases in Texas and Oklahoma, so Kansas could not apply Kansas law.

In the federal court context, even if a state's choice-of-law rules would seem to authorize applying a single state's law, a federal court may be constitutionally prevented from doing so unless there are contacts relating all unnamed class members to that state.

The result may be that many different states' laws apply to different class members' claims, which could defeat commonality — though not necessarily, since the trial judge always retains discretion about how much commonality is enough. The bar from *Wal-Mart* is high but not impossible. *Shady Grove Orthopedic Associates v. Allstate Insurance Co.* is a solid case for finding commonality. <!-- corrected: "Shady grow" → Shady Grove Orthopedic Associates v. Allstate Insurance Co. -->

#### Typicality and Adequacy of Representation — Rule 23(a)(3) and (4)

Typicality and representativeness tend to merge in practice. The inquiry is whether the named plaintiff's theory of relief is sufficiently close to the theories of relief of the unnamed class members such that if the named plaintiff pushes hard on its claim, all unnamed class members will necessarily benefit.

For **adequacy of representation**, the key question is whether there are conflicts of interest among class members. If some class members want immediate money (for treatment) while others want money preserved for later (in case they become sick), there may be a conflict requiring separate subclasses and separate representatives.

This issue arose in *Amchem Products, Inc. v. Windsor*, <!-- corrected: "ANCAM" → Amchem Products, Inc. v. Windsor --> where a large class of asbestos-exposure plaintiffs included both people who were currently sick and those who were showing signs but were not yet sick. The Court held that the class failed on adequacy of representation because of that conflict.

### Rule 23(b) Categories

The class must also satisfy at least one Rule 23(b) category:

**Rule 23(b)(1)(A) and Rule 23(b)(2)** are essentially the same: they apply when the defendant's course of conduct is common to all class members such that injunctive relief is the appropriate remedy and that is all that is sought. Neither of these categories applied in the class hypothetical.

**Rule 23(b)(1)(B) — Limited fund:** Applies when a limited fund (such as an insurance policy at its limits) cannot pay all class members. Rather than allowing individual plaintiffs to race to judgment and exhaust the fund, a mandatory class (no opt-out) is certified so that all class members receive a proportional share. This is analogous to a bankruptcy proceeding. Also not applicable in the hypothetical.

**Rule 23(b)(3) — Damages class:** Requires two additional elements:
1. **Predominance:** The common issues of law or fact must predominate over the non-common issues. This is a higher bar than commonality — if a class satisfies predominance, it necessarily satisfies commonality.
2. **Superiority:** The class action device must be superior to other methods of adjudication. The baseline assumption is that individual plaintiffs should ordinarily retain control over their own claims, so superiority must be affirmatively shown — for example, that individual lawsuits are economically impractical given small per-plaintiff recoveries.

Note: There is much more to class actions — notice, opt-out rights, settlement approval — that was not covered and will not be tested on the exam. Further study is available in advanced civil procedure coursework.

## Beacon Theaters and the Right to a Jury Trial

The rule from *Beacon Theatres, Inc. v. Westover* is straightforward.

**Background principle:** Under general preclusion principles and *Parklane Hosiery*, if lawsuit one is an equity lawsuit tried to the court (with the judge as fact-finder), the judge's factual findings can serve as the basis for issue preclusion in lawsuit two — even if lawsuit two has a right to a jury trial and issue preclusion would thereby deprive a party of having a jury determine those factual issues. The Supreme Court in *Parklane Hosiery* says that is permissible.

**The Beacon Theaters rule:** However, given the flexible modern rules of civil procedure — broad joinder, counterclaims, and so forth — if an equity claim and a law claim (the latter carrying a right to a jury trial) are in the **same** lawsuit and present common issues, a judge may **not** use scheduling power to try the equity claim first, make factual findings without a jury, and thereby deprive a party of its right to have a jury determine those same common factual issues. That is an abuse of discretion.

In *Beacon Theatres* itself, the court scheduled the old theater's equity claim (seeking an anti-lawsuit injunction) before the new theater's damages claim (which was on the law side and subject to a jury trial right), and would thereby have prevented the new theater from getting a jury trial on common factual issues. The Supreme Court held that scheduling power must be used to **preserve** the right to jury trial, not to circumvent it.

The general implication is that federal courts should schedule trials in a way that makes juries available on law claims whenever possible. There is additional case law beyond this course that adds nuance, but that is the framework for this class.
