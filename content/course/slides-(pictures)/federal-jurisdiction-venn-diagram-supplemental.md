# Constitutional And Statutory Bases for Federal District Court Jurisdiction (with Supplemental Jurisdiction)

<!-- page: 1 -->

> **Diagram type:** Venn diagram with three nested/overlapping ovals plus an additional oval representing supplemental jurisdiction.

## Text Extracted from Diagram

**Outside all ovals (four corners):** State courts only

**Orange oval label (top):** U.S. Const. art. 3, sec. 2, cl. 1

**Orange oval interior (left):** any federal issue

**Green oval interior:** "arising under," sec. 1331 / well-pleaded complaint

**Blue oval interior:** "diversity," sec. 1332 / complete diversity

**Blue oval label (upper right, outside blue but inside orange):** minimal diversity

**Dark green ellipse (center, overlap of green and blue):** Exclusive federal court juris.

**Black oval (bottom center, partially outside orange):** "supplemental," sec. 1367

## Description

This diagram builds on the basic federal jurisdiction Venn diagram by adding **supplemental jurisdiction** under **28 U.S.C. § 1367**.

The **orange oval** represents the full scope of federal jurisdiction permitted by the **U.S. Constitution, art. 3, sec. 2, cl. 1** — the outermost boundary of what federal courts *can* hear. It encompasses any case involving a federal issue, minimal diversity, and everything inside the green and blue ovals. Anything outside the orange oval is state courts only.

Within the orange oval, the **green oval** represents federal question jurisdiction under **28 U.S.C. § 1331** — cases "arising under" federal law. It requires a **well-pleaded complaint**, meaning the federal issue must appear on the face of the plaintiff's complaint, not just as a defense.

Also within the orange oval, the **blue oval** represents diversity jurisdiction under **28 U.S.C. § 1332** — cases between citizens of different states. It requires **complete diversity** (no plaintiff and defendant share the same state citizenship). The blue oval does not fill the entire orange oval because the Constitution only requires **minimal diversity** — § 1332's complete diversity requirement is stricter than the constitutional floor.

Where the **green and blue ovals overlap**, a case qualifies under both federal question and diversity jurisdiction. The dark ellipse at the center of that overlap marks cases of **exclusive federal court jurisdiction** — where only federal courts can hear the matter, with no concurrent state court jurisdiction.

The **black oval at the bottom** represents supplemental jurisdiction under **28 U.S.C. § 1367**. It overlaps with the green oval and extends partially outside the orange oval, illustrating that supplemental jurisdiction allows federal courts to hear related state-law claims that would not independently qualify for federal jurisdiction — so long as they form part of the same case or controversy as a claim that does.
