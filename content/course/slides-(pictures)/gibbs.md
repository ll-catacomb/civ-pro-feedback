## Description

This diagram illustrates the facts and legal framework from *United Mine Workers v. Gibbs*, the foundational supplemental jurisdiction case.

**The claims:** The mine supervisor brought two claims against the international union — one under the **Labor Management Relations Act** (federal law, black arrow) and one for **tortious interference with contractual relations** (state law, pink arrow). The federal claim is the anchoring claim; the state law claim is the would-be supplemental claim.

**The power + discretion test:**

*Power* — the court has constitutional power to hear the supplemental claim if both claims arise from the **same transaction or occurrence** (i.e., a common nucleus of operative fact).

*Discretion* — even if power exists, the court may decline to exercise supplemental jurisdiction based on:
- Whether state law claims predominate over federal claims
- Whether the state law issue requires a definitive interpretation by a state court
- How far the case has progressed if the anchoring federal claim is later dismissed
- Whether hearing both claims together risks jury confusion
- Whether Congress intended to limit jurisdiction in this area
