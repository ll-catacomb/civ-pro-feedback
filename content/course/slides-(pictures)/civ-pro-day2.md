## Overview: Subject Matter Jurisdiction

We are continuing our discussion of subject matter jurisdiction of the federal courts. Subject matter jurisdiction doctrine determines when a case can begin in federal court — in other words, when a plaintiff has the choice of whether to proceed in federal court or state court.

## The Venn Diagram: Constitutional Power vs. Statutory Power

- **State court only** — some cases fall outside the constitutional power entirely and must proceed in state court.
- **Constitutional power** — a broad but not unlimited grant of power from Article III to Congress. Congress may use or decline to use this power by passing a statute.
- **Statutory ovals** — Congress has passed two statutes that we now know:
  - **28 U.S.C. § 1331** — arising under jurisdiction
  - **28 U.S.C. § 1332** — diversity jurisdiction

Both statutory ovals sit entirely within the larger constitutional oval. To properly lie in federal district court, a case must fall within one of the statutory ovals. It is not enough to fall within the broader constitutional power alone.

Despite the fact that both statutes track the language of the Constitution, courts have interpreted the statutes more narrowly than the Constitution.

## Arising Under Jurisdiction (§ 1331): The Well-Pleaded Complaint Rule

Under § 1331, for almost all cases, courts apply the **well-pleaded complaint rule**: there is arising under jurisdiction only if the plaintiff's cause of action is a federally created cause of action.

The constitutional standard is broader — any federal issue embedded in a case would suffice — but that is not enough under the statute as interpreted in *Mottley*. <!-- corrected: "Motley" → "Mottley" -->

## The Smith-Grable Exception

An important exception to the rule that only a federally created cause of action will do under § 1331 is the **Smith-Grable exception**.

The doctrine has two steps:

**Step 1 — Power:** Does a federal issue exist that is embedded within the state law cause of action, and is that issue both *substantial* and *disputed*?

**Step 2 — Discretion:** If power exists, should the federal court exercise arising under jurisdiction? This is a multifactor balancing inquiry.

### *Smith*

In *Smith*, the case was a shareholder's derivative lawsuit. The theory was that directors and officers had caused the corporation to invest in federal government bonds that were in fact worthless because the federal government lacked the constitutional authority to issue them. The constitutionality of the bonds was embedded within the state law cause of action — there was no way to prove the claim without resolving whether the bonds were invalid.

The federal issue was:
- **Embedded** within the state law cause of action
- **Substantial** — federal bond issuance is critical to how the federal government finances itself
- **Disputed** — the parties would fight over it

On the discretion factors: the constitutionality of federal bonds would not arise frequently enough in shareholders' derivative suits to sweep excessive business from the state courts into the federal system. It was also a clean issue of law, not entangled with specific facts.

### *Grable*

In *Grable*, the cause of action was one for quiet title. The prior landowner's theory depended on the claim that the IRS's tax sale to the new landowner was invalid due to a failure to follow federal statutory notice requirements. There was no way to litigate the quiet title claim without adjudicating that federal statute.

The federal issue was:
- **Embedded** within the quiet title cause of action
- **Substantial** — the case would turn on it, and it implicates IRS tax collection and administration
- **Disputed** — a central issue in the case

On the discretion factors: the IRS notice question would not arise frequently enough in quiet title actions to flood the federal courts. It was again a clean issue of law — whether the IRS was required to serve process in a particular manner.

The court held that both *Smith* and *Grable* were appropriate for the exception.

### *Merrell Dow* — A Contrast <!-- corrected: "Merrill Dow" → "Merrell Dow" -->

In *Merrell Dow*, the theory was that a violation of federal law formed the basis of a state law negligence action (the concept you will encounter in Torts as *negligence per se*). Even if the federal issue were embedded within the state negligence claim, allowing any violation of federal law to form the basis for a state negligence action would sweep far too many cases into the federal court system. The best way to understand *Merrell Dow* is that exercising arising under jurisdiction would have upset the balance of federal-state business too dramatically — the discretion step fails.

## Declaratory Judgment Statute

The federal declaratory judgment statute is straightforward if you remember one thing: **a declaratory judgment is a remedy, not a source of jurisdiction.**

The analytical approach is to imagine the *coercive action* — the lawsuit that would have been filed in the absence of the declaratory judgment statute. Ask: would that imaginary lawsuit have fallen within one of the two statutory ovals (§ 1331 or § 1332)?

- If yes, the declaratory judgment action may proceed in federal court.
- If no, there is no subject matter jurisdiction.

Note: Smith-Grable is available as a basis for § 1331 jurisdiction in the declaratory judgment context if needed.

**Example — "Mr. Schmoe":** The coercive action would have been a breach of contract lawsuit by Mr. Schmoe against the bank, in which the federal issue arose only by way of defense. This is the identical posture to *Mottley* — a breach of contract claim with a federal issue arising defensively, which does not support § 1331 jurisdiction. Accordingly, when the bank tried to seize the forum by filing a declaratory judgment action claiming it was not liable, the court dismissed for lack of subject matter jurisdiction.

## Diversity Jurisdiction (§ 1332)

### Complete Diversity

Under *Strawbridge v. Curtiss*, the diversity statute ordinarily requires **complete diversity**: all plaintiffs must be citizens of different states from all defendants.

The Constitution requires only *minimal diversity* (at least one plaintiff diverse from at least one defendant), but that is not sufficient under the statute.

**Examples:**
- Two plaintiffs from South Carolina suing one defendant from Virginia and one from North Carolina → **complete diversity** ✓
- Two plaintiffs from South Carolina suing one defendant from Virginia and one from South Carolina → **no complete diversity** ✗ (South Carolina appears on both sides)

### Citizenship Rules

**Corporations** — Citizens of:
1. Their state of incorporation, *and*
2. Their **principal place of business** — the nerve center where corporate decision-making is conducted (not the location of primary manufacturing, sales, or other activity)

**Unincorporated associations** — Citizens of every state in which *any* of their members are citizens. This often makes unincorporated associations citizens of a large number of states.

**Individuals** — The relevant concept is **domicile**:
- Peg domicile at birth, then move forward chronologically.
- Domicile changes only if the person is (1) residing in a new state *and* (2) has the intention to remain there **indefinitely** (no definite leaving point).
- Evidence of domicile: driver's license, tax filings, marriage, relationships, purchase of property, etc.
- Classic non-changes: students and employees with a definite end date do not change domicile.

### Amount in Controversy

Diversity jurisdiction requires the amount in controversy to exceed **$75,000**. There is no amount in controversy requirement for § 1331 arising under jurisdiction.

The court accepts the plaintiff's stated amount unless it can conclude to a **legal certainty** that the claim cannot exceed $75,000. Various tests apply when the plaintiff does not seek a specific monetary amount — these are secondary issues best addressed through practice.
